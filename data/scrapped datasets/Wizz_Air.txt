As of 2020, the airline has its largest bases at <a href="/wiki/Budapest_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Budapest Airport">Budapest Airport</a> and <a href="/wiki/Luton_Airport" title="Luton Airport">Luton Airport</a> and flies to 164 airports.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc" role="navigation" href="/w/index.php?title=Wizz_Air&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The airline was established in September 2003. The lead investor is <a href="/wiki/Indigo_Partners" title="Indigo Partners">Indigo Partners</a>, an American private equity firm<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> specialising in transportation investments. The first flight was made from <a href="/wiki/Katowice_International_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Katowice International Airport">Katowice International Airport</a> on 19 May 2004.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The airline\'s <a href="/wiki/CEO" class="mw-redirect" title="CEO">CEO</a> is <a href="/wiki/J%C3%B3zsef_V%C3%A1radi" title="J\xc3\xb3zsef V\xc3\xa1radi">J\xc3\xb3zsef V\xc3\xa1radi</a>, former CEO of <a href="/wiki/Mal%C3%A9v_Hungarian_Airlines" title="Mal\xc3\xa9v Hungarian Airlines">Mal\xc3\xa9v Hungarian Airlines</a>. The company is registered in <a href="/wiki/Pest_County" title="Pest County">Pest County</a> (Hungary).<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>On 25 February 2015, Wizz Air shares started to be traded on the <a href="/wiki/London_Stock_Exchange" title="London Stock Exchange">London Stock Exchange</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In November 2017, Wizz Air announced that they were planning to launch a British division called <a href="/wiki/Wizz_Air_UK" title="Wizz Air UK">Wizz Air UK</a>. The airline is based at <a href="/wiki/London_Luton" class="mw-redirect" title="London Luton">London Luton</a>, mainly to take advantage of a number of take-off and landing slots acquired from <a href="/wiki/Monarch_Airlines" title="Monarch Airlines">Monarch Airlines</a> when the latter entered administration in 2017.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The airline applied successfully to the <a href="/wiki/Civil_Aviation_Authority_(United_Kingdom)" title="Civil Aviation Authority (United Kingdom)">CAA</a> for an <a href="/wiki/Air_Operator_Certificate" class="mw-redirect" title="Air Operator Certificate">AOC</a> and a Type A Operating Licence. The airline launched operations in March 2018 using British registered aircraft. Wizz Air UK will start to take over the flights to the UK that are currently operated by Wizz Air. Wizz Air said that the airline will employ up to 100 staff by the end of 2018.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In November 2018, it was reported that Wizz Air had announced plans to reactivate its <a href="/wiki/Wizz_Air_Ukraine" title="Wizz Air Ukraine">Wizz Air Ukraine</a> subsidiary, approximately three years after it was shut down. Under the plan, Wizz Air Ukraine will seek to complete certification in 2019 following the acquisition of twenty <a href="/wiki/Airbus_A320neo_family" title="Airbus A320neo family">A320/321 neo jets</a>. Bases will be developed in Kyiv as well as other cities across the country. By 2025, it aims to have a passenger throughput of 6 million passengers per annum.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>By early 2020, the <a href="/wiki/COVID-19_pandemic" title="COVID-19 pandemic">COVID-19 pandemic</a> had created a case of <a href="/wiki/Force_majeure" title="Force majeure">force majeure</a> in European aviation, gradually forcing airline fleets to land, including the majority of Wizz Air aircraft.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Although it was announced in March that no redundancies were planned, one-fifth of the staff was redeemed when it became clear that air travel across the continent was shutting down.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> In April 2020, based on passenger numbers, Wizz Air became Europe\'s largest low-cost airline with 78,000 passengers.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> By mid-June, they had reached 40 percent of their previous year\'s normal weekly revenue, while the proportion of no-shows fell from 80 percent in April to 30 percent.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In July 2020, the airline announced that it will form a joint venture with the Abu Dhabi Developmental Holding Company.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> In October 2020 the airline announced that its first Scandinavian base would be opened at Oslo\'s <a href="/wiki/Oslo_Airport,_Gardermoen" title="Oslo Airport, Gardermoen">Gardermoen</a> Airport in November 2020: the two aircraft based there would also undertake domestic flights within Norway.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>On 3 February 2021, Wizz Air announced the opening of its second base in Bosnia and Herzegovina, after Tuzla; the airline will open a base at Sarajevo with one Airbus A320. The airline announced nine new destinations from Sarajevo with 21 weekly departures. New destinations from Sarajevo announced were: Basel/Mulhouse, Brussels-Charleroi, Copenhagen, Dortmund, Eindhoven, Gothenburg, London-Luton, Memmingen and Paris-Beauvais.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Corporate_affairs">Corporate affairs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span href="/wiki/Budapest" title="Budapest">Budapest</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> since March 2015.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a Ferenc Liszt International Airport</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-AirportHQ_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AirportHQ-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a The airline occupied over 2,000 square metres (22,000&#160;sq&#160;ft) of space in an office building refurbished after the airline\'s arrival. The facility, with open-plan offices, housed about 150 employees.<sup id="cite_ref-AirportHQ_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AirportHQ-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> Before the time its head office was at the airport, it was in the Airport Business Park C2 in <a href="/wiki/Vecs%C3%A9s" title="Vecs\xc3\xa9s">Vecs\xc3\xa9s</a>, close to the airport.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Operations">Operations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wizz_Air&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Operations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>As is common with all European low-cost carriers, Wizz Air prefers to use smaller or secondary airports to reduce costs and fees incurred by service usage. It also has a <a href="/wiki/Buy-on-board" class="mw-redirect" title="Buy-on-board">buy-on-board</a> food service called Wizz Caf\xc3\xa9 as well as a second service called Wizz Boutique, which is for other items.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Subsidiaries">Subsidiaries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wizz_Air&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Subsidiaries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<dl><dt>Current subsidiaries</dt></dl>\n<ul><li><b><a href="/wiki/Wizz_Air_Abu_Dhabi" title="Wizz Air Abu Dhabi">Wizz Air Abu Dhabi</a></b> was founded on 12 December 2019 as Wizz Air\'s <a href="/wiki/United_Arab_Emirates" title="United Arab Emirates">UAE</a> subsidiary. The airline is a joint venture with state-owned Abu Dhabi Development Holding, ADDH, which owns 51 per cent.<sup id="cite_ref-launch_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-launch-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> Flights are operated from <a href="/wiki/Abu_Dhabi_International_Airport" title="Abu Dhabi International Airport">Abu Dhabi International Airport</a> to destinations in Europe, Asia and Africa.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li><b><a href="/wiki/Wizz_Air_UK" title="Wizz Air UK">Wizz Air UK</a></b><sup id="cite_ref-wizzuk_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wizzuk-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> was founded on 18 October 2017 as Wizz Air\'s <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">UK</a> unit, following <a href="/wiki/Civil_Aviation_Authority_(United_Kingdom)" title="Civil Aviation Authority (United Kingdom)">CAA</a> approval the subsidiary commenced operations with 10 registered aircraft initially. The unit is currently operating flights from and to Luton on behalf of its Hungarian parent and has been set up to ensure Wizz Air retains full market access to the United Kingdom following <a href="/wiki/Brexit" title="Brexit">Brexit</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-wizzuk_28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wizzuk-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>\n<dl><dt>Former subsidiaries</dt></dl>\n<ul><li><b><a href="/wiki/Wizz_Air_Bulgaria" title="Wizz Air Bulgaria">Wizz Air Bulgaria</a></b><sup id="cite_ref-wizzbulgaria_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wizzbulgaria-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> was Wizz Air\'s <a href="/wiki/Bulgaria" title="Bulgaria">Bulgarian</a> unit set-up in 2005 and based at <a href="/wiki/Sofia_Airport" title="Sofia Airport">Sofia Airport</a> with a fleet of 3 aircraft. It ceased operations on 31 March 2011, all flights merged back into Wizz Air Hungary Ltd.<sup id="cite_ref-wizzbulgaria_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wizzbulgaria-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li><b>Wizz Air Romania</b><sup id="cite_ref-wizzromania_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wizzromania-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> was a planned <a href="/wiki/Romania" title="Romania">Romanian</a> unit to be based at <a href="/wiki/Timi%C8%99oara_Traian_Vuia_International_Airport" title="Timi\xc8\x99oara Traian Vuia International Airport">Timi\xc8\x99oara Traian Vuia International Airport</a>. However, this sub-unit never started operations and a base was established there instead of under Wizz Air Hungary Ltd.<sup id="cite_ref-wizzromania_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wizzromania-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li><b><a href="/wiki/Wizz_Air_Ukraine" title="Wizz Air Ukraine">Wizz Air Ukraine</a></b> founded in 2008 was the <a href="/wiki/Ukraine" title="Ukraine">Ukrainian</a> unit of Wizz Air, which had its own air operator\'s certificate and operated from <a href="/wiki/Kyiv_Zhuliany_International_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Kyiv Zhuliany International Airport">Kyiv Zhuliany International Airport</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lviv_International_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Lviv International Airport">Lviv International Airport</a> with a fleet of 4 aircraft.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> As a result of the economical crisis following <a href="/wiki/Russian_military_intervention_in_Ukraine_(2014%E2%80%93present)" class="mw-redirect" title="Russian military intervention in Ukraine (2014\xe2\x80\x93present)">Russian military intervention in Ukraine</a>, Wizz Air Ukraine was terminated on 19 April 2015. Some routes to and from Kyiv were taken over by Wizz Air Hungary Ltd while all others ceased at the time, the airline has since begun to expand once again in Ukraine.<sup id="cite_ref-wizzair.com1_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wizzair.com1-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Wizz_Air_Ukraine_expansion_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wizz_Air_Ukraine_expansion-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> As of October 2016, the company operated flights to 13 cities in 7 countries from Kyiv.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Destinations">Destinations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wizz_Air&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Destinations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_Wizz_Air_destinations" title="List of Wizz Air destinations">List of Wizz Air destinations</a></div>\n<p>Wizz Air started new services between <a href="/wiki/Katowice_Airport" title="Katowice Airport">Katowice</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gatwick_Airport" title="Gatwick Airport">London\xe2\x80\x93Gatwick</a> in 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> In January 2008, flights started from Gdansk to <a href="/wiki/Gothenburg" title="Gothenburg">Gothenburg</a>, Bournemouth and Coventry. In summer 2008, Wizz Air restarted summer seasonal services from Katowice and Budapest to <a href="/wiki/Girona" title="Girona">Girona</a>, as well as a new weekly service to Girona from <a href="/wiki/Gda%C5%84sk" title="Gda\xc5\x84sk">Gda\xc5\x84sk</a>. It also restarted its three-times-weekly service from <a href="/wiki/Luton_Airport" title="Luton Airport">London\xe2\x80\x93Luton</a> to Burgas. class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_protection">Environmental protection</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wizz_Air&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Environmental protection">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In November 2019, Wizz Air dismissed concerns about the damage the airline may be causing to the environment, raised by the "flightshame" movement. This dismissal was based on the airline\'s per-passenger emission level. The company said that it would reduce emissions per capita by an additional 30 percent by 2030. At the same time, Wizz Air condemned inefficient airlines - such as <a href="/wiki/Lufthansa" title="Lufthansa">Lufthansa</a> - offering business class and using outdated technologies, which cause far more specific environmental damage than Wizz Air.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>One year later, in November 2020, among the European airlines, Wizz Air was able to show the lowest CO<sub>2</sub> emissions per passenger / kilometre and underlined their commitment to further reducing their environmental footprint. As part of their strategy, all fuel-saving flight phases of take-off and landing are continuously monitored for maximum environmental optimization, which has a significant impact on further continuous reductions in CO<small>2</small> emissions.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Incidents">Incidents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wizz_Air&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Incidents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<ul><li>On 8 June 2013, Wizz Air Flight 3141, an <a href="/wiki/Airbus_A320-232" class="mw-redirect" title="Airbus A320-232">Airbus A320-232</a> (registration HA-LWM) from <a href="/wiki/Henri_Coand%C4%83_International_Airport" title="Henri Coand\xc4\x83 International Airport">Bucharest - Henri Coand\xc4\x83 Airport</a>, <a href="/wiki/Romania" title="Romania">Romania</a> to <a href="/wiki/Ciampino%E2%80%93G._B._Pastine_International_Airport" title="Ciampino\xe2\x80\x93G. B. Pastine International Airport">Rome-Ciampino</a>, <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>, made an emergency landing<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup> at <a href="/wiki/Leonardo_da_Vinci%E2%80%93Fiumicino_Airport" title="Leonardo da Vinci\xe2\x80\x93Fiumicino Airport">Leonardo da Vinci\xe2\x80\x93Fiumicino Airport</a> when the crew encountered problems lowering one of the main <a href="/wiki/Landing_gear" title="Landing gear">undercarriages</a> and locking it into position. The aircraft diverted to Fiumicino because of the longer runway, and firefighters applied foam after landing as a precautionary measure. The aircraft was evacuated using slides.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup> Initial reports of injured passengers were denied by both Wizz Air and Rome Fiumicino Airport, who said some passengers requested medical checkups but reported no injuries.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wizz_Air&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Wizz Air is well known for a strong opposition against any activities of its employees in the trade unions.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup> Because of that approach to its employees the company is facing many accusations. One of the first cases was already closed by The Romanian supreme court in 2019 with a verdict that Wizz Air discriminated against its workers.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup> Other cases blaming Wizz Air for a similar attitude against its employees are still open in Ukraine and other countries.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup> Recently the Norwegian Prime minister has supported the boycott of Wizz Air when the company announced it will start domestic operations over the country.<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup>
