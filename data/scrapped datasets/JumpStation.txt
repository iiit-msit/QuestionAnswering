class="infobox-data category">Defunct/Closed 1994</td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p><b>JumpStation</b> was the first <a href="/wiki/Web_search_engine" class="mw-redirect" title="Web search engine">WWW search engine</a> that behaved, and appeared to the user, the way current web search engines do.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> It started indexing on 12 December 1993<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> and was announced on the <a href="/wiki/Mosaic_(web_browser)" title="Mosaic (web browser)">Mosaic</a> "What\'s New" webpage on 21 December 1993.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> It was hosted at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Stirling" title="University of Stirling">University of Stirling</a> in <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>.\n</p><p>It was written by Jonathon Fletcher, from <a href="/wiki/Scarborough,_North_Yorkshire" title="Scarborough, North Yorkshire">Scarborough</a>, England,<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> who graduated from the University with a first class honours degree in Computing Science in the summer of 1992<sup id="cite_ref-Scotland_on_Sunday_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Scotland_on_Sunday-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> and has subsequently been named "father of the search engine".<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>He was subsequently employed there as a systems administrator. JumpStation\'s development discontinued when he left the University in late 1994, having failed to get any investors, including the University of Stirling, to financially back his idea.<sup id="cite_ref-Scotland_on_Sunday_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Scotland_on_Sunday-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> At this point the database had 275,000 entries spanning 1,500 servers.<sup id="cite_ref-matrix_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-matrix-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>JumpStation used document titles and headings to index the web pages found using a simple linear search, and did not provide any ranking of results.<sup id="cite_ref-matrix_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-matrix-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> However, <b>JumpStation</b> had the same basic shape as <a href="/wiki/Google_Search" title="Google Search">Google Search</a> in that it used an index solely built by a <a href="/wiki/Web_robot" class="mw-redirect" title="Web robot">web robot</a>, searched this index using keyword queries entered by the user on a <a href="/wiki/Web_form" class="mw-redirect" title="Web form">web form</a> whose location was well-known,<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> and presented its results in the form of a list of <a href="/wiki/URL" title="URL">URLs</a> that matched those keywords.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nominations">Nominations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=JumpStation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Nominations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><b>JumpStation</b> was nominated for a <a href="/wiki/Best_of_the_Web_awards" title="Best of the Web awards">"Best Of The Web" award</a> in 1994<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> and the story of its origin and development written up, using interviews with Fletcher, by Wishart and Bochsler.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span
