rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.ft.com">www<wbr />.ft<wbr />.com</a></span></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p>The <i><b>Financial Times</b></i> (<i><b>FT</b></i>)<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;nb 1&#93;</a></sup> is a  <a href="/wiki/Newspaper" title="Newspaper">daily newspaper</a> printed in <a href="/wiki/Broadsheet" title="Broadsheet">broadsheet</a> and published digitally that focuses on business and economic <a href="/wiki/Current_affairs_(news_format)" title="Current affairs (news format)">current affairs</a>. Based in <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>, England, the paper is owned by the Japanese holding company, <a href="/wiki/Nikkei,_Inc." title="Nikkei, Inc.">Nikkei</a>, with core editorial offices across Britain, the United States, and continental Europe. In July 2015, <a href="/wiki/Pearson_plc" title="Pearson plc">Pearson</a> sold the publication to Nikkei for <a href="/wiki/Pound_sign" title="Pound sign">\xc2\xa3</a>844 million (<a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">US$</a>1.32 billion) after owning it since 1957. In Spring 2019, it reported one million paying subscriptions, three-quarters of which are digital subscriptions.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Guardian20190419_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian20190419-5">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The newspaper has a prominent focus on <a href="/wiki/Business_journalism" title="Business journalism">financial journalism</a> and economic analysis over <a href="/wiki/News_media" title="News media">generalist reporting</a>, drawing both criticism and acclaim. The daily sponsors an <a href="/wiki/Financial_Times_and_McKinsey_Business_Book_of_the_Year_Award" title="Financial Times and McKinsey Business Book of the Year Award">annual book award</a> and publishes a "<a href="/wiki/Financial_Times_Person_of_the_Year" title="Financial Times Person of the Year">Person of the Year</a>" feature.\n</p><p>The paper was founded in 1888 as the <i><b>London Financial Guide</b></i> before rebranding a year later as the <i>Financial Times</i>. It was first circulated around metropolitan London by <a href="/wiki/Financial_Times#History" title="Financial Times">James Sheridan</a>, who, along with his brother and <a href="/wiki/Horatio_Bottomley" title="Horatio Bottomley">Horatio Bottomley</a>, sought to report on city business opposite the <i><a href="/wiki/Financial_News_(1884%E2%80%931945)" title="Financial News (1884\xe2\x80\x931945)">Financial News</a></i>. The succeeding half-century competition between the two papers would eventually culminate in a <a href="/wiki/Financial_News_(1884%E2%80%931945)#History" title="Financial News (1884\xe2\x80\x931945)">1945 merger</a>, led by <a href="/wiki/Brendan_Bracken,_1st_Viscount_Bracken" class="mw-redirect" title="Brendan Bracken, 1st Viscount Bracken">Brendan Bracken</a>, which established it as <a href="/wiki/List_of_business_newspapers#Top_circulation" title="List of business newspapers">one of the largest business newspapers</a> in the world. Globalisation from the late-19th to mid-20th century facilitated editorial expansion for the <i>FT</i>, with the paper adding opinion columns, special reports, <a href="/wiki/Political_cartoon" title="Political cartoon">political cartoons</a>, <a href="/wiki/Letter_to_the_editor" title="Letter to the editor">reader letters</a>, book reviews, technology articles, and global politics features. The paper is often characterised by its light-pink (salmon) newsprint. It is supplemented by its lifestyle magazine (<i><a href="/wiki/FT_Magazine" title="FT Magazine">FT Magazine</a>)</i>, weekend edition (<i><a href="/wiki/Financial_Times#FT_Weekend" title="Financial Times">FT Weekend</a></i>), and a small portfolio of industry publications.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Financial_Times#Editorial_stance" title="Financial Times">editorial stance of the <i>Financial Times</i></a> centres on <a href="/wiki/Economic_liberalism" title="Economic liberalism">economic liberalism</a>, particularly free trade and free markets. Since its founding it has supported <a href="/wiki/Liberal_democracy" title="Liberal democracy">liberal democracy</a>, favouring <a href="/wiki/Classical_liberalism" title="Classical liberalism">classically liberal</a> politics and policies from international governments. Its newsroom is independent from its editorial board. Due to its history of economic commentary, the <i>FT</i> publishes a <a href="/wiki/Financial_Times#Indices" title="Financial Times">variety of financial indices</a>, primarily the <a href="/wiki/FTSE_All-Share_Index" title="FTSE All-Share Index">FTSE All-Share Index</a>. Its typical depth of coverage has, since the late-20th century, linked the paper with a <a href="/wiki/White-collar_worker" title="White-collar worker">white-collar</a> and educated readership.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The <i>Financial Times</i> is headquartered in <a href="/wiki/Bracken_House,_London" title="Bracken House, London">Bracken House</a> at <a href="/wiki/Cannon_Street" title="Cannon Street">10 Cannon Street</a>, near the city\'s financial centre, where it maintains its <a href="/wiki/Publishing" title="Publishing">publishing house</a>, corporate centre, and main editorial office.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc" href="/wiki/File:Financial_Times_1888_front_page.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Financial_Times_1888_front_page.jpg/174px-Financial_Times_1888_front_page.jpg" decoding="async" width="174" height="247" class="thumbimage" class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Financial_Times_1888_front_page.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The front page of the <i>Financial Times</i> on 13 February 1888.</div></div></div>\n<p>The <i>FT</i> was launched as the <i>London Financial Guide</i> on 10 January 1888, renaming itself the <i>Financial Times</i> on 13 February the same year. Describing itself as the friend of "The Honest Financier, the Bona Fide Investor, the Respectable Broker, the Genuine Director, and the Legitimate Speculator", it was a four-page journal. The readership was the financial community of the <a href="/wiki/City_of_London" title="City of London">City of London</a>, its only rival being the more daring and slightly older (founded in 1884) <i>Financial News</i>. On 2 January 1893 the <i>FT</i> began printing on light pink paper to distinguish it from the similarly named <i>Financial News</i>: at the time it was also cheaper to print on unbleached paper (several other more general newspapers, such as <i><a href="/wiki/The_Sporting_Times" title="The Sporting Times">The Sporting Times</a></i>, had the same policy), but nowadays it is more expensive as the paper has to be dyed specially.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>After 57 years of rivalry the <i>Financial Times</i> and the <i>Financial News</i> were merged in 1945 by <a href="/wiki/Brendan_Bracken,_1st_Viscount_Bracken" class="mw-redirect" title="Brendan Bracken, 1st Viscount Bracken">Brendan Bracken</a> to form a single six-page newspaper. The <i>Financial Times</i> brought a higher circulation while the <i>Financial News</i> provided much of the editorial talent. The <i>Lex</i> column was also introduced from <i>Financial News</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Pearson_PLC" class="mw-redirect" title="Pearson PLC">Pearson</a>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Gordon_Newton" title="Gordon Newton">Gordon Newton</a>, a Cambridge graduate, took over as Editor in 1949, and immediately introduced a policy (then most unusual in <a href="/wiki/Fleet_Street" title="Fleet Street">Fleet Street</a>) of direct recruitment of new university graduates, mainly from Oxbridge, as its trainee journalists. Many of them proceeded to have distinguished careers elsewhere in journalism and British public life, and became the mainstay of the paper\'s own editorial strengths until the 1990s. The first such \'direct recruit\' was (the future leading British economist) Andrew Shonfield; the second was (later Sir) William Rees-Mogg who went on, via <a href="/wiki/The_Sunday_Times" title="The Sunday Times">The Sunday Times</a>, to edit <a href="/wiki/The_Times" title="The Times">The Times</a> in 1967 following its acquisition by Roy Thomson. Other FT Oxbridge recruits included the future <a href="/wiki/Chancellor_of_the_Exchequer" title="Chancellor of the Exchequer">Chancellor of the Exchequer</a> <a href="/wiki/Nigel_Lawson" title="Nigel Lawson">Nigel Lawson</a>. The FT\'s distinctive recruitment policy for Fleet Street journalists was never popular with the <a href="/wiki/National_Union_of_Journalists" title="National Union of Journalists">National Union of Journalists</a> and ceased in 1966 following the recruitment of Richard Lambert from Oxford, himself a future Editor of the FT. Meanwhile, Pearson had bought the paper in 1957.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Over the years the paper grew in size, readership and breadth of coverage. It established correspondents in cities around the world, reflecting a renewed impetus in the <a href="/wiki/World_economy" title="World economy">world economy</a> towards <a href="/wiki/Globalisation" class="mw-redirect" title="Globalisation">globalisation</a>. As cross-border trade and capital flows increased during the 1970s, the <i>FT</i> began international expansion, facilitated by developments in technology and the growing acceptance of English as the international language of business. On 1 January 1979 the first <i>FT</i> (Continental Europe edition) was printed outside the UK, in Frankfurt; printing in the U.S. began in July 1985.<sup id="cite_ref-npius_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-npius-11">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Since then, with increased international coverage, the <i>FT</i> has become a global newspaper, printed in 22 locations with five international editions to serve the UK, continental Europe, the U.S., Asia and the Middle East.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The European edition is distributed in continental Europe and Africa. It is printed Monday to Saturday at five centres across Europe, reporting on matters concerning the European Union, the <a href="/wiki/Euro" title="Euro">euro</a> and European corporate affairs.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> In 1994 <i>FT</i> launched a luxury lifestyle magazine, <i>How To Spend It</i>. In 2009 it launched a standalone website for the magazine.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> On 13 May 1995 the <i>Financial Times</i> group made its first foray into the online world with the launch of FT.com. This provided a summary of news from around the globe, which was supplemented in February 1996 with stock price coverage. The second-generation site was launched in spring 1996. The site was funded by advertising and contributed to the online advertising market in the UK in the late 1990s. Between 1997 and 2000 the site underwent several revamps and changes of strategy, as the FT Group and Pearson reacted to changes online. <i>FT</i> introduced subscription services in 2002.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> FT.com is one of the few UK news sites successfully funded by individual subscription.\n</p><p>In 1997 the <i>FT</i> launched a U.S. edition, printed in New York, Chicago, Los Angeles, San Francisco, Dallas, Atlanta, Orlando and Washington, D.C., although the newspaper was first printed outside New York City in 1985. In September 1998 the <i>FT</i> became the first UK-based newspaper to sell more copies internationally than within the UK. In 2000 the <i>Financial Times</i> started publishing a German-language edition, <i><a href="/wiki/Financial_Times_Deutschland" title="Financial Times Deutschland">Financial Times Deutschland</a></i>, with a news and editorial team based in Hamburg. Its initial circulation in 2003 was 90,000. It was originally a joint venture with a German publishing firm, <a href="/wiki/Gruner_%2B_Jahr" title="Gruner + Jahr">Gruner + Jahr</a>. In January 2008 the <i>FT</i> sold its 50% stake to its German partner.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> <i>FT Deutschland</i> never made a profit and is said to have accumulated losses of \xe2\x82\xac250 million over 12 years. It closed on 7 December 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> The <i>Financial Times</i> launched a new weekly supplement for the fund management industry on 4 February 2002. <i>FT fund management</i> (FTfm) was and still is distributed with the paper every Monday. FTfm is the world\'s largest-circulation fund management title.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> Since 2005 the <i>FT</i> has sponsored the annual Content">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The <i>FT</i> is split into two sections. The first section covers domestic and international news, editorial commentary on politics and economics from <i>FT</i> journalists such as <a href="/wiki/Martin_Wolf" title="Martin Wolf">Martin Wolf</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gillian_Tett" title="Gillian Tett">Gillian Tett</a> and <a href="/wiki/Edward_Luce" title="Edward Luce">Edward Luce</a>, and opinion pieces from globally renowned leaders, policymakers, academics and commentators. About 110 of its 475 journalists are outside the United Kingdom.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Lex_column">The <i>Lex</i> column</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Financial_Times&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: The Lex column">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The <i>Lex</i> column is a daily feature on the back page of the first section. While it raised concerns over hints of <a href="/wiki/Protectionism" title="Protectionism">protectionism</a>, it praised his ability to "engage the country\'s attention," his calls for a bipartisan politics, and his plans for "<a href="/wiki/Health_care_reform_in_the_United_States" class="mw-redirect" title="Health care reform in the United States">comprehensive health-care reform</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> The <i>FT</i> favoured Obama again in 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup> The <i>FT</i> endorsed Democratic candidate <a href="/wiki/Hillary_Clinton" title="Hillary Clinton">Hillary Clinton</a> in the run-up for the 2016 U.S. presidential election and <a href="/wiki/Joe_Biden" title="Joe Biden">Joe Biden</a> in the 2020 U.S. presidential election.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ownership_and_related_publications">Ownership and related publications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Financial_Times&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Ownership and related publications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:262px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Otemachi_Conference_Center.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/Otemachi_Conference_Center.jpg/260px-Otemachi_Conference_Center.jpg" decoding="async" width="260" height="210" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/Otemachi_Conference_Center.jpg/390px-Otemachi_Conference_Center.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/Otemachi_Conference_Center.jpg/520px-Otemachi_Conference_Center.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2500" data-file-height="2023" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Otemachi_Conference_Center.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <i>FT</i> has been owned by <a href="/wiki/Nikkei,_Inc." title="Nikkei, Inc.">Nikkei</a> since 2015; the Japanese holding company purchased the paper for <a href="/wiki/Pound_sign" title="Pound sign">\xc2\xa3</a>844m (<a href="/wiki/Dollar_sign" title="Dollar sign">$</a>1.32 billion).</div></div></div>\n<p>On 23 July 2015, <a href="/wiki/Nikkei,_Inc." title="Nikkei, Inc.">Nikkei, Inc.</a> agreed to buy the Financial Times Group, a division of <a href="/wiki/Pearson_plc" title="Pearson plc">Pearson PLC</a> since 1957, for \xc2\xa3844m ($1.32 billion)<sup id="cite_ref-BBC20150723_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC20150723-29">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Guardian20150724_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian20150724-30">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> and the acquisition was completed on 30 November 2015.<sup id="cite_ref-Nikkei20151130_31-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nikkei20151130-31">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> Until August 2015 the FT group had a 50% shareholding in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Economist" title="The Economist">The Economist</a></i>, which was sold to the <a href="/wiki/Agnelli_family" title="Agnelli family">Agnelli family</a> for \xc2\xa3469 million.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup> Related publications include the <i>Financial Times</i>, FT.com, FT Search Inc., the publishing imprint FT Press and numerous joint ventures. In November 2013 it agreed to sell <a href="/wiki/Mergermarket" class="mw-redirect" title="Mergermarket">Mergermarket</a>, an online intelligence reporting business, to the London private equity investor BC Partners.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup> In addition, the FT Group has a unit called FT Specialist, which is a provider of specialist information on retail, personal and institutional finance segments. It publishes <i><a href="/wiki/The_Banker" title="The Banker">The Banker</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Money_Management" title="Money Management">Money Management</a></i> and <i>Financial Adviser</i> (a publication targeted at professional advisers), <i>fDi Intelligence</i> and <i>Professional Wealth Management</i> (PWM).<sup id="cite_ref-aboutus_62-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aboutus-62">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Financial Times Group announced the beta launch of newssift,<sup id="cite_ref-Newssift.com_63-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Newssift.com-63">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup> part of FT Search, in March 2009. Newssift.com is a next-generation search tool for business professionals that indexes millions of articles from thousands of global business news sources, not just the FT. The Financial Times Group acquired Money Media<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup> (an <a href="/wiki/Online_news" class="mw-redirect" title="Online news">online news</a> and commentary site for the industry) and Exec-Appointments<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup> (an online recruitment specialist site for the executive jobs market). The FT Group once had a 13.85% stake in Business Standard Ltd of India, the publisher of the <i><a href="/wiki/Business_Standard" title="Business Standard">Business Standard</a></i>. It sold this stake in April 2008 and has entered into an agreement with <a href="/wiki/Network_18" class="mw-redirect" title="Network 18">Network 18</a> to launch the <i>Financial Times</i> in India,<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup> though it is speculated that they may find it difficult to do so, as the brand \'Financial Times\' in India is owned by <a href="/wiki/The_Times_Group" title="The Times Group">The Times Group</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup> the publisher of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Times_of_India" title="The Times of India">The Times of India</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Economic_Times" title="The Economic Times">The Economic Times</a></i>. The group also publishes <a href="/wiki/America%27s_Intelligence_Wire" title="America&#39;s Intelligence Wire">America\'s Intelligence Wire</a>, a daily general <a href="/wiki/Newswire" class="mw-redirect" title="Newswire">newswire</a> service.<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <i>Financial Times\'</i> Financial Publishing division (formerly FT Business) provides print and online content for retail, personal and institutional finance audiences.  Examples of publications and services include: <i><a href="/wiki/Investors_Chronicle" title="Investors Chronicle">Investors Chronicle</a></i>, a personal finance magazine and website; "FT Money", a weekly personal finance supplement in "FT Weekend"; <i>FT Wealth</i>, a magazine for the global high-net-worth community and FTfm, a weekly review of the global fund management industry, <i><a href="/wiki/Money_Management" title="Money Management">Money Management</a></i> and <i>Financial Adviser</i> (a publication targeted at professional advisers). The institutional segment includes: <i><a href="/wiki/The_Banker" title="The Banker">The Banker</a></i>,  <i><a href="/wiki/This_Is_Africa" title="This Is Africa">This Is Africa</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/FDi_magazine" class="mw-redirect" title="FDi magazine">fDi</a></i> <a href="/wiki/FDi_magazine" class="mw-redirect" title="FDi magazine"><i>Intelligence</i></a> and <i>Professional Wealth Management</i> (PWM).<sup id="cite_ref-aboutus2_70-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aboutus2-70">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup> Money-Media, a separate arm of Financial Publishing, delivers a range of digital information services for fund management professionals around the globe, including: Ignites, Ignites Europe, Ignites Asia, FundFire and BoardIQ. Financial Publishing includes publications (<i><a href="/wiki/Pensions_Expert" title="Pensions Expert">Pensions Expert</a></i> and <i>Deutsche Pensions &amp; Investmentnachrichten</i>) and events (<i>Investment Expert</i>) for the European pensions industry. The group also publishes MandateWire, a financial information company that provides sales and market intelligence for investment professionals in North America, Europe and Asia.<sup id="cite_ref-aboutus_62-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aboutus-62">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>FT Knowledge is an associated company which offers educational products and services. FT Knowledge has offered the "Introducing the City" course (which is a series of Wednesday night lectures and seminars, as well as weekend events) during each autumn and spring since 2000. FT Predict is a <a href="/wiki/Prediction_market" title="Prediction market">prediction market</a> contest hosted by the <i>Financial Times</i> that allows users to buy and sell contracts based on future financial, political, and news-driven events by spending fictional Financial Times Dollars (FT$). Based on the assumptions displayed in James Surowiecki\'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wisdom_of_Crowds" title="The Wisdom of Crowds">The Wisdom of Crowds</a></i>, this contest allows people to use prediction markets to observe future occurrences while competing for weekly and monthly prizes.\n</p><p>The <i>Financial Times</i> also ran a business-related game called "In the Pink" (a phrase meaning "in good health", also a reference to the colour of the newspaper and to the phrase "in the red" meaning to be making a loss). Each player was put in the virtual role of Chief Executive and the goal was to have the highest <a href="/wiki/Profit_(accounting)" title="Profit (accounting)">profit</a> when the game closes. The winner of the game (the player who makes the highest profit) was to receive a real monetary prize of \xc2\xa310,000. The game ran from 1 May to 28 June 2006.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Indices">Indices</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Financial_Times&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Indices">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:171px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Indices_Financial_Times.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/Indices_Financial_Times.jpg/169px-Indices_Financial_Times.jpg" decoding="async" width="169" height="225" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/Indices_Financial_Times.jpg/253px-Indices_Financial_Times.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/Indices_Financial_Times.jpg/337px-Indices_Financial_Times.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1512" data-file-height="2016" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Indices_Financial_Times.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A selection of <i>FT</i> <a href="/wiki/Stock_market_index" title="Stock market index">market indices</a>, 2019</div></div></div>\n<p>The <i>Financial Times</i> collates and publishes a number of <a href="/wiki/Stock_market_index" title="Stock market index">financial market indices</a>, which reflect the changing value of their constituent parts. The longest-running of these was the former <i>Financial News Index</i>, started on 1 July 1935 by the <i>Financial News</i>. The <i>FT</i> published a similar index; this was replaced by the <i>Financial News Index</i>\xe2\x80\x94which was then renamed the <i>Financial Times (FT) Index</i>\xe2\x80\x94on 1 January 1947. The index started as an index of industrial shares, and companies with dominant overseas interests were excluded, such as the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Iranian_Oil_Company" class="mw-redirect" title="Anglo-Iranian Oil Company">Anglo-Iranian Oil Company</a> (later <a href="/wiki/BP" title="BP">BP</a>), <a href="/wiki/British-American_Tobacco" class="mw-redirect" title="British-American Tobacco">British-American Tobacco</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lever_Brothers" title="Lever Brothers">Lever Brothers</a> (later <a href="/wiki/Unilever" title="Unilever">Unilever</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Royal_Dutch_Shell" title="Royal Dutch Shell">Shell</a>. The oil and financial sectors were included decades later.<sup id="cite_ref-Littlewood_71-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Littlewood-71">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/FTSE_All-Share_Index" title="FTSE All-Share Index">FTSE All-Share Index</a>, the first of the FTSE series of indices, was created in 1962, comprising the largest 594 UK companies by market capitalisation.<sup id="cite_ref-Littlewood_71-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Littlewood-71">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup> The letters F-T-S-E represented that FTSE was a joint venture between the Financial Times (<b>F-T</b>) and the <a href="/wiki/London_Stock_Exchange" title="London Stock Exchange">London Stock Exchange</a> (<b>S-E</b>). On 13 February 1984 the <a href="/wiki/FTSE_100" class="mw-redirect" title="FTSE 100">FTSE 100</a> was introduced, representing about eighty percent of the London Stock Exchange\'s value.<sup id="cite_ref-Littlewood_71-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Littlewood-71">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup> In 1995 <a href="/wiki/FTSE_Group" title="FTSE Group">FTSE Group</a> was made an independent company. The first of several overseas offices was opened in New York City in 1999; Paris followed in early 2000, Hong Kong, <a href="/wiki/Frankfurt" title="Frankfurt">Frankfurt</a> and San Francisco in 2001, <a href="/wiki/Madrid" title="Madrid">Madrid</a> in 2002 and Tokyo in 2003.\n</p><p>Other well-known FTSE indices include the <a href="/wiki/FTSE_350_Index" title="FTSE 350 Index">FTSE 350 Index</a>, the <a href="/wiki/FTSE_SmallCap_Index" title="FTSE SmallCap Index">FTSE SmallCap Index</a>, the <a href="/wiki/FTSE_AIM_UK_50_Index" title="FTSE AIM UK 50 Index">FTSE AIM UK 50 Index</a> and <a href="/wiki/FTSE_AIM_100_Index" title="FTSE AIM 100 Index">FTSE AIM 100 Index</a> as well as the <a href="/wiki/FTSE_AIM_All-Share_Index" title="FTSE AIM All-Share Index">FTSE AIM All-Share Index</a> for stocks, and the FTSE UK Gilt Indices for government bonds.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="People">People</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Financial_Times&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: People">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In July 2006 the <i>FT</i> announced a "New Newsroom" project to integrate the newspaper more closely with FT.com. At the same time it announced plans to cut the editorial staff from 525 to 475. In August 2006 it announced that all the required job cuts had been achieved through voluntary layoffs. A number of former <i>FT</i> journalists have gone on to high-profile jobs in journalism, politics and business. <a href="/wiki/Robert_James_Thomson" class="mw-redirect" title="Robert James Thomson">Robert Thomson</a>, previously the paper\'s US managing editor, was the editor of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Times" title="The Times">The Times</a></i> and is now the publisher of the <i>Wall Street Journal</i>. <a href="/wiki/William_Lewis_(journalist)" title="William Lewis (journalist)">Will Lewis</a>, a former New York correspondent and News Editor for the <i>FT</i>, is the current editor of the <i><a href="/wiki/Daily_Telegraph" class="mw-redirect" title="Daily Telegraph">Daily Telegraph</a></i>. <a href="/wiki/Dominic_Lawson" title="Dominic Lawson">Dominic Lawson</a> went on to become editor of the <i><a href="/wiki/Sunday_Telegraph" class="mw-redirect" title="Sunday Telegraph">Sunday Telegraph</a></i> until he was sacked in 2005. <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Adonis" class="mw-redirect" title="Andrew Adonis">Andrew Adonis</a>, a former education correspondent, became an adviser on education to the then British Prime Minister, <a href="/wiki/Tony_Blair" title="Tony Blair">Tony Blair</a>, and was given a job as an education minister and a seat in the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Lords" title="House of Lords">House of Lords</a> after the 2005 election. <a href="/wiki/Ed_Balls" title="Ed Balls">Ed Balls</a> became chief economic adviser to the Treasury, working closely with <a href="/wiki/Gordon_Brown" title="Gordon Brown">Gordon Brown</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Chancellor_of_the_exchequer" class="mw-redirect" title="Chancellor of the exchequer">chancellor of the exchequer</a> (or finance minister), before being elected a Member of Parliament in 2005, and became <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_for_Children,_Schools_and_Families" title="Secretary of State for Children, Schools and Families">Secretary of State for Children, Schools and Families</a> in July 2007. <a href="/wiki/Bernard_Gray" title="Bernard Gray">Bernard Gray</a>, a former defence correspondent and Lex columnist, was chief executive of the publishing company CMP before becoming chief executive of TSL Education, publisher of the <i><a href="/wiki/Times_Educational_Supplement" class="mw-redirect" title="Times Educational Supplement">Times Educational Supplement</a></i>. David Jones, at one time the FT Night Editor, then became Head of IT. He was a key figure in the newspaper\'s transformation from hot metal to electronic composition and then onto full-page pagination in the 1990s. He went on to become Head of Technology for the Trinity Mirror Group.\n</p><p>Sir Geoffrey Owen was the editor of the <i>Financial Times</i> from 1981 to 1990. He joined the Centre for Economic Performance (CEP) at the <a href="/wiki/London_School_of_Economics" title="London School of Economics">London School of Economics</a> as Director of Business Policy in 1991 and was appointed Senior Fellow, Institute of Management, in 1997. He continues his work there.<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">&#91;71&#93;</a></sup> During his tenure at the FT he had to deal with rapid technological change and issues related to it, for example repetitive strain injury (RSI), which affected dozens of FT journalists, reporters and staff in the late 1980s.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Editors">Editors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Financial_Times&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Editors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r998391716">.mw-parser-output .div-col{margin-top:0.3em;column-width:30em}.mw-parser-output .div-col-small{font-size:90%}.mw-parser-output .div-col-rules{column-rule:1px solid #aaa}.mw-parser-output .div-col dl,.mw-parser-output .div-col ol,.mw-parser-output .div-col ul{margin-top:0}.mw-parser-output .div-col li,.mw-parser-output .div-col dd{page-break-inside:avoid;break-inside:avoid-column}</style><div class="div-col" style="column-width: 20em;">\n<dl><dd>1889: Douglas MacRae</dd>\n<dd>1890: <a href="/wiki/William_Ramage_Lawson" class="mw-redirect" title="William Ramage Lawson">William Ramage Lawson</a></dd>\n<dd>1892: Sydney Murray</dd>\n<dd>1896: A.
