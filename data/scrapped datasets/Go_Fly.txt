class="infobox-label">Website</th><td class="infobox-data">go-fly.com</td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p><b>Go Fly</b> (styled and trading as <b>Go</b>) was the name of a British low cost <a href="/wiki/Airline" title="Airline">airline</a>, founded by <a href="/wiki/British_Airways" title="British Airways">British Airways</a> (BA) in May 1998. It operated flights between London Stansted Airport and destinations in Europe. The airline was purchased from BA, in a management buy out backed by the <a href="/wiki/Private_equity_firm" class="mw-redirect" title="Private equity firm">private equity firm</a> <a href="/wiki/3i" title="3i">3i</a> in June 2001. In May 2002, it was bought by its rival <a href="/wiki/EasyJet" title="EasyJet">EasyJet</a>, and was merged into the airline\'s operations by 2005.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Its head office was in the <i>Enterprise House</i>, in <a href="/wiki/London_Stansted_Airport" title="London Stansted Airport">London Stansted Airport</a> in <a href="/wiki/Stansted_Mountfitchet" title="Stansted Mountfitchet">Stansted Mountfitchet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Essex" title="Essex">Essex</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="/wiki/Bob_Ayling" class="mw-redirect" title="Bob Ayling">Bob Ayling</a>, <a href="/wiki/CEO" class="mw-redirect" title="CEO">CEO</a> of <a href="/wiki/British_Airways" title="British Airways">British Airways</a>, approached <a href="/wiki/EasyJet" title="EasyJet">EasyJet</a> founder, <a href="/wiki/Stelios_Haji-Ioannou" title="Stelios Haji-Ioannou">Stelios Haji-Ioannou</a>, to ask whether he could visit claiming that he was fascinated by how the Greek entrepreneur had made the budget airline formula work. Haji-Ioannou not only agreed, but allegedly showed Ayling his business plan.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In November 1997, BA announced that, under the project name of Operation Blue Sky, it would launch its own low cost airline in order to meet the changing demand for air travel in Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> According to Ayling, the new airline would, "quickly become a favourite with the budget traveller" via its pricing scheme and available flights.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Barbara_Cassani" title="Barbara Cassani">Barbara Cassani</a>, who had been British Airways\' General Manager in New York and had been credited with turning around the airline\'s flagging fortunes on the transatlantic market in the beginning of the 1990s, was chosen by Ayling to set up the new venture.\n</p><p>The new airline would be run separately from British Airways as a wholly owned subsidiary, and would compete in the European <a href="/wiki/Low-cost_carrier" title="Low-cost carrier">low-cost carrier</a> market, dominated by <a href="/wiki/Ryanair" title="Ryanair">Ryanair</a>, EasyJet and <a href="/wiki/Debonair_(airline)" title="Debonair (airline)">Debonair</a>.\n</p><p>The airline was named Go Fly Limited in February 1998, following a lengthy debate over how to choose a name that would best suit the positioning of this new airline, which was already facing challenges from EasyJet,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Virgin_Atlantic" title="Virgin Atlantic">Virgin Atlantic</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> among others. The fleet consisted of <a href="/wiki/Boeing_737" title="Boeing 737">Boeing 737s</a>. The first two, 737-300s G-IGOC and G-IGOE,<sup id="cite_ref-CassaniKemp_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CassaniKemp-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> were initially acquired via lease from <a href="/wiki/Philippine_Airlines" title="Philippine Airlines">Philippine Airlines</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>A fourth plane, a 737-3Q8 G-IGOF was leased in May 1998.<sup id="cite_ref-CassaniKemp_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CassaniKemp-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> On Friday 22 May 1998, Go flew for the first time from its base at <a href="/wiki/London_Stansted_Airport" title="London Stansted Airport">London Stansted Airport</a> to <a href="/wiki/Rome_Ciampino" class="mw-redirect" title="Rome Ciampino">Rome Ciampino</a>. The following afternoon saw the airline\'s inaugural flight to <a href="/wiki/Milan_Malpensa_Airport" title="Milan Malpensa Airport">Milan Malpensa Airport</a>. The Italian start up, and launch of Go\'s operation, was managed by former <a href="/wiki/American_Airlines" title="American Airlines">American Airlines</a> executive Luca Berti.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>As part of their campaign against Go, Stelios Haji-Ioannou and nine other EasyJet staff booked tickets for the flight, arriving in orange <a href="/wiki/Boiler_suit" class="mw-redirect" title="Boiler suit">boiler suits</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-CassaniKemp_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CassaniKemp-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Other early routes were to <a href="/wiki/Copenhagen" title="Copenhagen">Copenhagen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bologna" title="Bologna">Bologna</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lisbon" title="Lisbon">Lisbon</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-CassaniKemp_8-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CassaniKemp-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> In July flights to <a href="/wiki/Glasgow" title="Glasgow">Glasgow</a> were started, then to <a href="/wiki/Munich" title="Munich">Munich</a> in November and <a href="/wiki/Venice" title="Venice">Venice</a> in December 1998, followed by <a href="/wiki/M%C3%A1laga" title="M\xc3\xa1laga">M\xc3\xa1laga</a>, <a href="/wiki/Faro,_Portugal" title="Faro, Portugal">Faro</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bilbao" title="Bilbao">Bilbao</a> in April 1999 and <a href="/wiki/Madrid" title="Madrid">Madrid</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-CassaniKemp_8-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CassaniKemp-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>After leasing three further planes, including one former <a href="/wiki/Air_Libert%C3%A9" title="Air Libert\xc3\xa9">Air Libert\xc3\xa9</a> plane and another ex <a href="/wiki/British_Midland" class="mw-redirect" title="British Midland">British Midland</a> jet, between November 1998 and November 1999, six additional planes were delivered direct from Boeing, leased from <a href="/wiki/GE_Commercial_Aviation_Services" class="mw-redirect" title="GE Commercial Aviation Services">GE Commercial Aviation Services</a> bringing the total to 13. Although this was four more than had originally been envisaged, the additional routes helped to spread the <a href="/wiki/Overhead_(business)" title="Overhead (business)">overheads</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-CassaniKemp_8-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CassaniKemp-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In a successful attempt to stem mounting losses, on 7 June 1999, a significant change in strategy was announced, cutting back on the existing schedules in favour of new routes tapping into the <a href="/wiki/Summer_vacation" title="Summer vacation">summer holiday</a> market to <a href="/wiki/Alicante" title="Alicante">Alicante</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ibiza" title="Ibiza">Ibiza</a> and <a href="/wiki/Palma,_Majorca" class="mw-redirect" title="Palma, Majorca">Palma</a>. This was followed by winter schedules targeting <a href="/wiki/Skiing" title="Skiing">skiers</a> travelling to <a href="/wiki/Lyon" title="Lyon">Lyon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Zurich" class="mw-redirect" title="Zurich">Zurich</a>, together with a new route to <a href="/wiki/Prague" title="Prague">Prague</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-CassaniKemp_8-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CassaniKemp-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In March 2001, Go expanded its operations in the United Kingdom, and opened a second base at <a href="/wiki/Bristol_Airport" title="Bristol Airport">Bristol Airport</a>, bringing low cost travel to <a href="/wiki/South_West_England" title="South West England">South West England</a> for the first time.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Go was very much the brain child of Bob Ayling, and when he left British Airways, Go\'s future was rendered shaky.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> In November 2000, BA announced it planned to sell Go, as part of efforts to improve profit levels, coupled with concerns that Go was attracting customers from BA\'s own services.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Estimated gains from the sale fluctuated, and in January 2001, British Airways admitted that its initial estimates may have been incorrect and that they now estimated Go to be worth only \xc2\xa3200m.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> With the revised forecasts, there seemed to be an increase in pressure to sell Go as soon as possible, possibly within two months.\n</p><p>Go was sold in June 2001, in a management buyout led by Cassani with the cooperation of private equity firm <a href="/wiki/3i" title="3i">3i</a> for \xc2\xa3100 million.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> The airline\'s management team remained in place.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> In December 2001, Go reported that it had carried 57.3% more passengers in December 2001 than in the previous year.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/EasyJet" title="EasyJet">EasyJet</a> announced that it would buy Go on 16 May 2002, for \xc2\xa3374 million, to expand its own operations.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>By December 2002, Go operated under the easyJet Air Operators Certificate (AOC) and in April 2003, the airline was absorbed into the easyJet brand. Although it was initially reported that Cassani might seek to block the deal with EasyJet, that did not happen.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
