<b>ATR 42</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Regional_airliner" title="Regional airliner">regional airliner</a> produced by <a href="/wiki/ATR_(aircraft_manufacturer)" title="ATR (aircraft manufacturer)">ATR</a>, with final assembly in <a href="/wiki/Toulouse" title="Toulouse">Toulouse</a>, France.\nOn 4 November 1981, the aircraft was launched with ATR, as a <a href="/wiki/Joint_venture" title="Joint venture">joint venture</a> between French <a href="/wiki/A%C3%A9rospatiale" title="A\xc3\xa9rospatiale">A\xc3\xa9rospatiale</a> (now <a href="/wiki/Airbus" title="Airbus">Airbus</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Aeritalia" title="Aeritalia">Aeritalia</a> (now <a href="/wiki/Leonardo_S.p.A." title="Leonardo S.p.A.">Leonardo S.p.A.</a>).\nThe <i>ATR 42-300</i> performed its <a href="/wiki/Maiden_flight" title="Maiden flight">maiden flight</a> on 16 August 1984 and <a href="/wiki/Type_certification" class="mw-redirect" title="Type certification">type certification</a> was granted during September 1985.\nLaunch customer <a href="/wiki/Air_Littoral" title="Air Littoral">Air Littoral</a> operated its first revenue-earning flight in December of that year.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/High_wing" class="mw-redirect" title="High wing">high wing</a> airliner is powered by two <a href="/wiki/Turboprop" title="Turboprop">turboprop</a> engines, <a href="/wiki/Pratt_%26_Whitney_Canada_PW120" class="mw-redirect" title="Pratt &amp; Whitney Canada PW120">Pratt &amp; Whitney Canada PW120s</a>. \nCapacity varies between 40 and 52 passengers, hence the "42" in its name.\nLater variants are upgraded with new <a href="/wiki/Avionics" title="Avionics">avionics</a> like a <a href="/wiki/Glass_cockpit" title="Glass cockpit">glass cockpit</a>, and newer engine versions.\nThe ATR 42 is the basis for the stretched <a href="/wiki/ATR_72" title="ATR 72">ATR 72</a>, introduced in October 1989.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc" role="navigation" links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Development">Development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=ATR_42&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>During the 1960s and 1970s, European aircraft manufacturers had, for the most part, undergone considerable corporate restructuring, including <a href="/wiki/Merger" class="mw-redirect" title="Merger">mergers</a> and consolidations, as well as moved towards collaborative multi-national programmes, such as the newly launched <a href="/wiki/Airbus_A300" title="Airbus A300">Airbus A300</a>. In line with this trend towards intra-European cooperation, French aerospace company <a href="/wiki/A%C3%A9rospatiale" title="A\xc3\xa9rospatiale">A\xc3\xa9rospatiale</a> and Italian aviation conglomerate <a href="/wiki/Aeritalia" title="Aeritalia">Aeritalia</a> commenced discussions on the topic of working together to develop an all-new <a href="/wiki/Regional_airliner" title="Regional airliner">regional airliner</a>. Prior to this, both companies had been independently conducting studies for their own aircraft concepts, the <i>AS 35</i> design in the case of Aerospatiale and the <i>AIT 230</i> for Aeritalia, to conform with demand within this sector of the market as early as 1978.<sup id="cite_ref-milestones_2017_2-0" class="reference"><a By 1983, ATR\'s customer services division has been set up, readying infrastructure worldwide to provide support for ATR\'s upcoming aircraft to any customer regardless of location.<sup December 1985, the first production aircraft, designated as the <i>ATR 42-300</i>, was delivered to French launch customer <a href="/wiki/Air_Littoral" title="Air Littoral">Air Littoral</a>; the first revenue service was performed later that same month.<sup id="cite_ref-milestones_arch_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-milestones_arch-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> During January 1986, already confident of the ATR 42\'s success and of the demand for an enlarged version of the aircraft, ATR announced that the launch of a programme to develop such an aircraft, which was designated as the <a href="/wiki/ATR_72" title="ATR 72">ATR 72</a> to reflect its increased passenger capacity.<sup id="cite_ref-milestones_2017_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-milestones_2017-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>By the end of 1986, the ATR 42 had accumulated a sizable backlog of orders, which in turn led to a ramping up of the type\'s rate of production.<sup id="cite_ref-milestones_arch_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-milestones_arch-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> During August 1988, ATR\'s marketing efforts in the lucrative <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North American</a> market resulted in the securing of a large order of 50 ATR-300s from US operator <a href="/wiki/Texas_Air_Corporation" title="Texas Air Corporation">Texas Air Corporation</a>; that same year, another American regional carrier, <a href="/wiki/Trans_World_Express" title="Trans World Express">Trans World Express</a>, received the 100th production aircraft.<sup id="cite_ref-milestones_arch_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-milestones_arch-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> On 1 July 1989, ATR opened their new global training centre for the type in Toulouse, which provided centralised and modern facilities for the training to airline staff and other personnel across the world. During June 1999, the ATR global training center became one of the first European institutions to be recognised as a Type Rating Training Organization (TRTO), as defined by the <a href="/wiki/Joint_Aviation_Authorities" title="Joint Aviation Authorities">Joint Aviation Authorities</a> (JAA).<sup id="cite_ref-milestones_2017_2-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-milestones_2017-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>During September 1989, it was announced that ATR had achieved its original target of 400 sales of the ATR.<sup id="cite_ref-milestones_arch_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-milestones_arch-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> That same year, deliveries of the enlarged ATR 72 commenced; shortly thereafter, it became common for both types to be ordered together.<sup id="cite_ref-milestones_2017_2-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-milestones_2017-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Since the smaller ATR 42 is assembled on the same production line as the ATR 72, along with sharing the majority of subsystems, components, and manufacturing techniques, the two types support each other to remain in production. This factor may have been crucial as, by 2015, the ATR 42 was the only 50-seat regional aircraft that was still being manufactured.<sup id="cite_ref-ain_nov_2015_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ain_nov_2015-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-lease_freight_ain_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lease_freight_ain-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="ATR_42-320">ATR 42-320</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=ATR_42&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: ATR 42-320">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:FedEx_ATR-42_EI-FXC.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/FedEx_ATR-42_EI-FXC.jpg/220px-FedEx_ATR-42_EI-FXC.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="150" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/FedEx_ATR-42_EI-FXC.jpg/330px-FedEx_ATR-42_EI-FXC.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/FedEx_ATR-42_EI-FXC.jpg/440px-FedEx_ATR-42_EI-FXC.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1200" data-file-height="816" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:FedEx_ATR-42_EI-FXC.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A freighter-configured ATR 42-300, operated by <a href="/wiki/FedEx_Feeder" class="mw-redirect" title="FedEx Feeder">FedEx Feeder</a></div></div></div>\n<p>In order to maintain a technological edge on the highly competitive market for regional airliners during the 1990s, several modifications and improved versions of the ATR 42 were progressively introduced. The initial ATR 42-300 model remained in production until 1996, while the first upgraded (and broadly similar) model, designated as the <i>ATR 42-320</i>, was also produced until 1996. The -320 variant principally differed in that it was powered by a pair of the more-powerful PW121 engines, giving it improved performance over the 300. Another variant, the <i>ATR 42-300QC</i>, was a dedicated <a href="/wiki/Combi_aircraft" title="Combi aircraft">\'quick-change\' (convertible)</a> freight/passenger version of the standard -300 series.<sup id="cite_ref-stats41_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stats41-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-milestones_arch_4-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-milestones_arch-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="ATR_42-500">ATR 42-500</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=ATR_42&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: ATR 42-500">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The next major production version was the <i>ATR 42-500</i> series, the development of which having been originally announced on 14 June 1993.<sup id="cite_ref-milestones_arch_4-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-milestones_arch-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Performing its maiden flight on 16 September 1994, and awarded certification by the British <a href="/wiki/Civil_Aviation_Authority_(United_Kingdom)" title="Civil Aviation Authority (United Kingdom)">Civil Aviation Authority</a> and France\'s (DGCA) during July 1995;<sup id="cite_ref-milestones_arch_4-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-milestones_arch-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> the -500 model was an upgraded aircraft, equipped with new PW127 engines, new six-bladed propellers, improved hot and high performance, increased weight capacity and an improved passenger cabin. On 31 October 1995, the first ATR 42-500 was delivered to Italian operator <a href="/wiki/Air_Dolomiti" title="Air Dolomiti">Air Dolomiti</a>; on 19 January 1996, the first revenue service to be performed by the type was conducted.<sup id="cite_ref-milestones_2017_2-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-milestones_2017-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\nOn 19 November 2000, 120 min <a href="/wiki/ETOPS" title="ETOPS">ETOPS</a> were approved.<sup id="cite_ref-TCDS_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TCDS-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In addition to new aircraft models, various organisational changes were also implemented. On 10 July 1998, ATR launched its new Asset Management Department.<sup id="cite_ref-milestones_arch_4-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-milestones_arch-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> In June 2001, <a href="/wiki/EADS" class="mw-redirect" title="EADS">EADS</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alenia_Aeronautica" title="Alenia Aeronautica">Alenia Aeronautica</a>, ATR\'s parent companies, decided to reinforce their partnership, regrouping all industrial activities related to regional airliners underneath the ATR consortium.<sup id="cite_ref-milestones_2017_2-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-milestones_2017-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> On 3 October 2003, ATR became one of the first aircraft manufacturers to be certified under <a href="/wiki/ISO_9000#2000_version" title="ISO 9000">ISO 9001-2000</a> and <a href="/wiki/AS9100" title="AS9100">EN/AS/JISQ 9100</a>, the worldwide quality standard for the aeronautics industry. During July 2004, ATR and <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazilian</a> aircraft manufacturer <a href="/wiki/Embraer" title="Embraer">Embraer</a> announced a cooperation agreement on the AEROChain Portal for the purpose of delivering improved customer service.<sup id="cite_ref-milestones_2017_2-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-milestones_2017-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> During April 2009, ATR announces the launch of its \'Door-2-Door\' service as a new option in its comprehensive customer services range.<sup id="cite_ref-milestones_2017_2-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-milestones_2017-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="ATR_42-600">ATR 42-600</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=ATR_42&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: ATR 42-600">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:ATR_42-600.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/ATR_42-600.jpg/220px-ATR_42-600.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/ATR_42-600.jpg/330px-ATR_42-600.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/ATR_42-600.jpg/440px-ATR_42-600.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1800" data-file-height="1199" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:ATR_42-600.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The ATR 42-600 was launched on 2 October 2007, first flew on 4 March 2010, and was first delivered in November 2012.</div></div></div>\n<p>The current production version is the <i>ATR 42-600</i> series. On 2 October 2007, ATR CEO St\xc3\xa9phane Mayer announced the launch of the -600 series aircraft; the ATR 42-600 and ATR 72-600 featured various improvements to increase efficiency, dispatch reliability, lower fuel burn and operating costs. While broadly similar to the earlier -500 model; differences include the adoption of improved PW127M engines, a new <a href="/wiki/Glass_cockpit" title="Glass cockpit">glass cockpit</a>, and a variety of other minor improvements. Using the test registration <i>F-WWLY</i>, the prototype ATR 42-600 first flew on 4 March 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The first aircraft was delivered to <a href="/wiki/Tanzania" title="Tanzania">Tanzanian</a> airline <a href="/wiki/Precision_Air" title="Precision Air">Precision Air</a> in November 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>As a consequence of strong demand for the -600 series, ATR decided to invest in the establishment of a second, more modern final assembly line and acquisition of more hangar space at their Toulouse site, along with a new large completion and delivery area; overall, the manufacturing operation expanded to four times the footprint that it had in 2005.<sup id="cite_ref-ain_nov_2015_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ain_nov_2015-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Speaking in October 2015, ATR CEO Patrick de Castelbajac stated that the firm was set to produce in excess of 90 aircraft that year, and that the new manufacturing facilities could support a production rate of up to 120 per year. At the time, the company had a backlog of orders for 300 aircraft, sufficient for three years\' of production.<sup id="cite_ref-ain_nov_2015_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ain_nov_2015-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> During 2017, a new in-house financing and leasing division was established by ATR in order to offer customers a greater degree of support and expand the company\'s range of services.<sup id="cite_ref-lease_freight_ain_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lease_freight_ain-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Considerable emphasis has been placed upon the continuous development of ATR\'s aircraft models.<sup id="cite_ref-lease_freight_ain_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lease_freight_ain-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\nAdditionally, during the mid-2010s, reports emerged that the development of a further stretched 90-seat ATR model was under consideration as well; allegedly, shareholder Airbus was relatively unenthusiastic on proceeding with such a development, while ATR CEO Fabrice Br\xc3\xa9gier favoured a focus on resolving manufacturing issues.<sup id="cite_ref-ain_nov_2015_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ain_nov_2015-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-chin_ain_2014_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chin_ain_2014-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="42-600S_STOL">42-600S STOL</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=ATR_42&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: 42-600S STOL">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>During the late-2010s, ATR conducted a feasibility study into developing the ATR 42\'s short takeoff and landing capabilities, potentially enabling the type to make using of runways as short as 800 meters (2,625 feet) via the adoption of enhanced brakes and a drastically modified tail unit; the company viewed this measure as expanding the aircraft\'s potential and opening new sales possibilities.<sup id="cite_ref-lease_freight_ain_6-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lease_freight_ain-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> It could introduce a steep approach capability and operating costs that compare to 30-seaters.<sup id="cite_ref-lease_freight_ain_6-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lease_freight_ain-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Short_takeoff_and_landing" class="mw-redirect" title="Short takeoff and landing">STOL</a> improvements should be launched in 2019 to reduce landing distance from 1,100 to 800&#160;m (3,600 to 2,600&#160;ft).<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>\nExisting <a href="/wiki/Flight_control_surface" class="mw-redirect" title="Flight control surface">control surfaces</a> would be reengineered with takeoff <a href="/wiki/Wing_flap" class="mw-redirect" title="Wing flap">flaps</a> extended to 25\xc2\xb0, and both spoilers deploying at landing and not only for inflight turns, adding an <a href="/wiki/Autobrake" title="Autobrake">autobrake</a> system, while the <a href="/wiki/Aircraft_rudder" class="mw-redirect" title="Aircraft rudder">rudder</a>\'s tab and upper section is modified to increase its maximum deflection to counter an <a href="/wiki/Turbine_engine_failure" title="Turbine engine failure">engine failure</a>, lowering the <a href="/wiki/Minimum_control_speed" class="mw-redirect" title="Minimum control speed">minimum control speed</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>ATR estimates a market for STOL 20\xe2\x80\x9342 seaters of 800 over 30 years, to serve islands and deliver cargo to short runways.\nThe ATR 42-600S empty weight will be reduced with lighter seats and galley fittings.<sup id="cite_ref-AvWeek9apr2019_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AvWeek9apr2019-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> The variant was officially announced at the June 2019 <a href="/wiki/Paris_Air_Show" title="Paris Air Show">Paris Air Show</a>. Irish lessor Elix Aviation Capital will be the launch customer, taking 10 aircraft from 2022 to 2024; commitments from <a href="/wiki/Air_Tahiti" title="Air Tahiti">Air Tahiti</a> and another undisclosed customer bring the total order book to 17.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>On 9 October 2019, ATR launched the ATR 42-600S at the <a href="/wiki/European_Regions_Airline_Association" title="European Regions Airline Association">ERA</a> General Assembly, with 20 commitments including from Elix Aviation Capital (10 aircraft) and Air Tahiti (two aircraft).\nCapable of operating from 800&#160;m (2,600&#160;ft) runways with up to 42 passengers, certification is expected for the second half of 2022 before first delivery.\nThe ATR 42-600S can access close to 500 airports with an 800\xe2\x80\x931,000&#160;m (2,600\xe2\x80\x933,300&#160;ft) runway, expanding its addressable market by 25%.<sup id="cite_ref-ATR9oct2019_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ATR9oct2019-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Design">Design</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span and a <a href="/wiki/T-tail" title="T-tail">T-tail</a>, certified in the <a href="/wiki/Transport_category" title="Transport category">transport category</a>, and powered by <a Power Unit">Auxiliary Power Unit</a> (APU), but can still be autonomous in ground operations, as it has a propeller brake on the starboard engine, allowing to keep the engine running to provide power on ground.\n</p><p>It has a circular, pressurized cabin with a circular cross-section, with a 2.57&#160;m (8&#160;ft 5&#160;in) inside width for four-abreast seating, allowing 48 seats at a 30&#160;in (760&#160;mm) seat pitch.\nIt has a 54.5&#160;m<sup>2</sup> (587&#160;sq&#160;ft) <a href="/wiki/Wing" title="Wing">wing</a> area and a 24.57&#160;m (80.6&#160;ft) wing span, for a 11.1 <a href="/wiki/Wing_aspect_ratio" class="mw-redirect" title="Wing aspect ratio">wing aspect ratio</a>.\nIt has a 18,600&#160;kg (41,000&#160;lb) <a href="/wiki/MTOW" class="mw-redirect" title="MTOW">MTOW</a>, for a 341&#160;kg/m<sup>2</sup> (70&#160;lb/sq&#160;ft) <a href="/wiki/Wing_loading" title="Wing loading">wing loading</a>.\nIt can reach a cruise speed of 300 Kn (556 km/h) <a href="/wiki/True_air_speed" class="mw-redirect" title="True air speed">TAS</a>, with a fuel flow of 811 kg/h (1,788 lb/h):<sup id="cite_ref-600-factsheet_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-600-factsheet-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> a <a href="/wiki/Fuel_economy_in_aircraft" title="Fuel economy in aircraft">fuel economy</a> of 1.46 kg/km (5.96 lb/nmi) or 3.8&#160;L/100&#160;km (62&#160;mpg<sub>&#8209;US</sub>) per seat with 48 seats and a <a href="/wiki/Jet_fuel" title="Jet fuel">jet fuel</a> density of 0.8.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" title="Envoy Air">American Eagle</a> placed a large order for 100 ATRs, composed of 41 ATR 42s and 59 ATR 72s, further consolidating ATR\'s position in <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-milestones_2017_2-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-milestones_2017-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> On 5 September 1997, American Eagle took delivery of the 500th ATR to be built. On 5 June 1998, <a href="/wiki/Tarom" class="mw-redirect" title="Tarom">Tarom</a>, the national carrier of <a href="/wiki/Romania" title="Romania">Romania</a>, accepted delivery of its first two ATR 42-500, of a batch of seven aircraft ordered a year earlier.<sup id="cite_ref-milestones_2017_2-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-milestones_2017-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> On 28 June 1998, ATR gains foothold in the <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuban</a> market following an order from airline <a href="/wiki/Cubana_de_Aviacion" class="mw-redirect" title="Cubana de Aviacion">Cubana de Aviacion</a> for the ATR 42.<sup id="cite_ref-milestones_2017_2-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-milestones_2017-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> During 2000, the combined global ATR fleet attained its 10,000,000th flight, during which a cumulative distance of approximately 4 billion kilometres (2.5 billion statute miles) had been traversed and around 450 million passengers had flown onboard ATR-built aircraft.<sup id="cite_ref-milestones_arch_4-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-milestones_arch-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> 2007 set a new record for the programme\'s sales, a total of 113 new ATR aircraft having been ordered during a single year.<sup id="cite_ref-milestones_2017_2-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-milestones_2017-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>2011 was another record-breaking year for sales at ATR.<sup id="cite_ref-record_2011_AIN_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-record_2011_AIN-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> According to ATR\'s CEO Filippo Bagnato, sales had continued to grow during the <a href="/wiki/Great_Recession" title="Great Recession">Great Recession</a> despite the downturn experienced by most aviation companies as "fuel consumption that can be half that of the alternatives and [with] lower maintenance costs". Bagnato noted the strength of Africa as a market for the type, as well at the firm\'s aircraft being capable of serve destinations that would otherwise be inaccessible with other aircraft due to the austere conditions of many airstrips and runways in the region, as well as the ability to operate autonomously without any reliance upon ground support equipment.<sup id="cite_ref-record_2011_AIN_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-record_2011_AIN-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> For 2013, ATR claimed a 48 per cent global market share for regional aircraft deliveries between 50 and 90 seats (comprising both turboprops and jets), making it the dominant manufacturer within this sector of the market.<sup id="cite_ref-chin_ain_2014_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chin_ain_2014-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> That same year, during which firm orders for 10 ATR 42-600s and 79 ATR 72-600s were recorded, <a href="/wiki/Lease" title="Lease">leasing</a> companies were responsible for 70 per cent of these; according to ATR\'s CEO Filippo Bagnato: "Years ago, we were not even considered by the lessors; now they see ATRs as a good investment".<sup id="cite_ref-chin_ain_2014_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chin_ain_2014-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>During May 1997, ATR had achieved its first breakthrough sale in China, placed by operator <a href="/wiki/China_Xinjiang_Airlines" title="China Xinjiang Airlines">China Xinjiang Airlines</a> and the <a href="/wiki/CAAC_Airlines" title="CAAC Airlines">CAAC</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-milestones_arch_4-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-milestones_arch-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> By 2013, while the Asia Pacific region had comprised the majority of ATR\'s sales when geographically ranked; however, orders from <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">Chinese</a> airlines remained elusive; Bagnato ascribed this anomaly to local market conditions dictating the typical use of larger aircraft, as well as a Chinese government policy of imposing high <a href="/wiki/Tariff" title="Tariff">tariffs</a> on the import of foreign-built fixed-wing aircraft.<sup id="cite_ref-chin_ain_2014_12-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chin_ain_2014-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> During late 2014, ATR set up a new office in <a href="/wiki/Beijing" title="Beijing">Beijing</a> and hired several former Airbus sales personnel with the aim of launching the type on the Chinese market. ATR believed that many of the already-flown routes did not suit larger 150-seat aircraft; however, of the roughly 2,600 commercial aircraft flying in China at that time, only 68 had a capacity of less than 90 seats and of these, fewer than 20 aircraft were powered by turboprop engines.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In response to airlines often wanting to phase out their early production ATR models to replace them with the latest generation ATR series, as well as to answer demand from cargo operators for the type, ATR has operated two separate dedicated freighter conversion programmes, known as the Bulk Freighter (tube version) and the ULD Freighter.<sup id="cite_ref-focus_rece_conv_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-focus_rece_conv-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Both conversions involve complete stripping of furnishings along with the addition of floor strengthening, new window plugs and 9g restraining nets, six additional longitudinal tracks for added flexibility, and an E-Class cabin; the ULD model can accommodate standard ULD-packaged cargo, such as LD3 containers or 88x108in (2.2x2.7m) <a href="/wiki/Pallets" class="mw-redirect" title="Pallets">pallets</a>, which were loaded via a large cargo door located on the port forward side. Undertaken by a range of companies, such as Alenia subsidiary <a href="/w/index.php?title=Aeronavali&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Aeronavali (page does not exist)">Aeronavali</a>, Texas-based <a href="/wiki/M7_Aerospace" title="M7 Aerospace">M7 Aerospace</a>; French firms <a href="/w/index.php?title=Indra%C3%A9ro_Siren&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Indra\xc3\xa9ro Siren (page does not exist)">Indra\xc3\xa9ro Siren</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Aeroconseil&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Aeroconseil (page does not exist)">Aeroconseil</a>, Canadian <a href="/w/index.php?title=Infinion_Certification_Engineering&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Infinion Certification Engineering (page does not exist)">Infinion Certification Engineering</a>, and Spanish company <a href="/w/index.php?title=Arrodisa&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Arrodisa (page does not exist)">Arrodisa</a>, by October 2012, in excess of one-fifth of all first-generation ATR 42 and ATR 72 aircraft had already been converted to freighters.<sup id="cite_ref-focus_rece_conv_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-focus_rece_conv-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>During January 2017, Japanese regional airliner <a href="/wiki/Japan_Air_Commuter" title="Japan Air Commuter">Japan Air Commuter</a> (JAC) has taken delivery of its first ATR 42-600, becoming the first owner-operator of the type in the nation. The aircraft was one of nine to have been ordered by JAC, along with options for a further 14 ATR 42s, as a replacement for its aging <a href="/wiki/Saab_340" title="Saab 340">Saab 340</a> fleet; reportedly, the airline is considering replacing its <a href="/wiki/Bombardier_Q400" class="mw-redirect" title="Bombardier Q400">Bombardier Q400s</a> with ATRs as well.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Silver_Airways" title="Silver Airways">Silver Airways</a> started to operate the ATR 42-600 on 22 April 2019, making them the first airline to fly the -600 variant in the USA. The carrier has 16 firm orders for ATR turboprops with options to purchase 30 additional aircraft from ATR through lessor <a href="/wiki/Nordic_Aviation_Capital" title="Nordic Aviation Capital">Nordic Aviation Capital</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Variants">Variants</span><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=ATR_42&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: ATR 42-200">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The -200 was the original ATR 42 prototype and only a few were built for testing purposes. It was powered by <a href="/wiki/Pratt_%26_Whitney_Canada" title="Pratt &amp; Whitney Canada">Pratt &amp; Whitney Canada</a> <a href="/wiki/Pratt_%26_Whitney_Canada_PW100" title="Pratt &amp; Whitney Canada PW100">PW120</a> engines rated at 1,800&#160;shp (1,300&#160;kW).<sup id="cite_ref-www.atraircraft.com_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-www.atraircraft.com-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="ATR_42-300"><span class="anchor" id="300"></span>ATR 42-300</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=ATR_42&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: ATR 42-300">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The -300 was the standard production version. This model was manufactured until 1996. It was powered by <a href="/wiki/Pratt_%26_Whitney_Canada" title="Pratt &amp; Whitney Canada">Pratt &amp; Whitney Canada</a> <a href="/wiki/Pratt_%26_Whitney_Canada_PW100" title="Pratt &amp; Whitney Canada PW100">PW120</a> engines rated at 2,000&#160;shp (1,500&#160;kW).\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="ATR_42-320_2">ATR 42-320</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=ATR_42&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: ATR 42-320">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The -320 was an improved version of the -300 powered by <a href="/wiki/Pratt_%26_Whitney_Canada_PW100" title="Pratt &amp; Whitney Canada PW100">PW121</a> engines (2100 shp). It was designed to have better performance especially in hot and high conditions.<sup id="cite_ref-www.atraircraft.com_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-www.atraircraft.com-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="ATR_42-400">ATR 42-400</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=ATR_42&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: ATR 42-400">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The ATR 42-400 is an upgraded version of the -320 using six-bladed propellers on otherwise identical PW121 engines. Three ATR 42-400 were delivered to the Government of Italy as the ATR 42 "Surveyor" version. The only two civilian ATR 42-400 produced (msn 487 &amp; 491) were delivered to CSA Czech Airlines in 1995/1996 as an interim upgrade prior to delivery of -500s. In 2006 these two aircraft were sold to <a href="/wiki/Conviasa" title="Conviasa">Conviasa</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="ATR_42-500_2">ATR 42-500</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=ATR_42&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: ATR 42-500">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The ATR 42-500 is a further upgraded version, the first delivery of which occurring during October 1995. It has many improvements for performance and passenger comfort including new engines, new propellers, a newly designed cabin, and increased weight. It has six-bladed propellers powered by <a href="/wiki/Pratt_%26_Whitney_Canada_PW100" title="Pratt &amp; Whitney Canada PW100">PW127E</a> engines rated at 2,400&#160;shp (1,800&#160;kW) for improved hot and high performance and increased cruise speed. The engines are flat rated for +45C. Propellers are electrically controlled and are made from composite. It has an increased maximum takeoff weight, allowing for more cargo and greater range. Due to the six-bladed propellers and better insulation, it has reduced noise levels. The newest version have CATII capability and dual Honeywell HT1000 FMS installation.<sup id="cite_ref-stats41_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stats41-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="ATR_42-600_2">ATR 42-600</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=ATR_42&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: ATR 42-600">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>During October 2007, the launch of the ATR 42-600 series was announced. Akin to the ATR 72-600, the -600 series featured the <a href="/wiki/Pratt_%26_Whitney_Canada_PW100" title="Pratt &amp; Whitney Canada PW100">PW127M</a> as its standard engine (providing 5% additional thrust, thus improving performance on short runways, in hot weather and on high altitude); the "boost function" activates the additional power as needed. It also features a <a href="/wiki/Glass_cockpit" title="Glass cockpit">Glass Cockpit</a> flight deck, complete with five <a href="/wiki/LCD" class="mw-redirect" title="LCD">wide LCD screens</a>, that replaced the previous <a href="/wiki/EFIS" class="mw-redirect" title="EFIS">EFIS</a> (Electronic Flight Instrument System); additionally, a Multi-Purpose Computer (MPC) enhances flight safety and operational capabilities. <a href="/wiki/Avionics" title="Avionics">Avionics</a> supplied by <a href="/wiki/Thales_Group" title="Thales Group">Thales</a> provides <a href="/wiki/Instrument_landing_system" title="Instrument landing system">CAT III</a> and <a href="/wiki/Required_Navigation_Performance" class="mw-redirect" title="Required Navigation Performance">RNP</a> capabilities. It also includes lighter, more comfortable seats and larger overhead baggage bins. According to the third issue of its EASA\'s Type Certificate,<sup id="cite_ref-TCDS_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TCDS-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> <i>ATR 42-600</i> is ATR\'s marketing designation for the -500 series when fitted with the NAS (New Avionic Suite) or \'Glass Cockpit\'. The <i>ATR 42-600</i> designation is not recognised by EASA as a certified aircraft model or variant, and is not used on ATR certified/approved documentation; instead, only "Mod 5948",  "ATR 42-500 with Mod 5948",  "ATR 42-500 fitted with NAS", or ATR 42-500 "600 version" is used.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_versions">Other versions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=ATR_42&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Other versions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:EIFXE_(7263831648).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/EIFXE_%287263831648%29.jpg/220px-EIFXE_%287263831648%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/EIFXE_%287263831648%29.jpg/330px-EIFXE_%287263831648%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/EIFXE_%287263831648%29.jpg/440px-EIFXE_%287263831648%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="683" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:EIFXE_(7263831648).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/FedEx_Express" title="FedEx Express">FedEx Express</a> ATR-42 cargo variant</div></div></div>\n<dl><dt>STOL variant</dt>\n<dd></dd></dl>\n<p>ATR launched the ATR 42-600S at the ERA General Assembly, with 20 commitments including from Elix Aviation Capital (10 aircraft) and Air Tahiti (two aircraft). The aircraft was written off in the accident.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>On 11 October 1999, an <a href="/wiki/1999_Air_Botswana_incident" class="mw-redirect" title="1999 Air Botswana incident">Air Botswana captain boarded an ATR 42-320 aircraft</a> at Gaborone Airport and took off. Once in the air, he asked by radio to speak to President <a href="/wiki/Festus_Mogae" title="Festus Mogae">Festus Mogae</a> (who was outside the country at the time), Air Botswana\'s general manager and others. In spite of all attempts to persuade him to land and discuss his grievances, he stated he was going to crash into aircraft parked on the airport apron. After a flying time of about two hours, he performed two loops and then crashed at 200 knots (230&#160;mph) into Air Botswana\'s two other ATR 42s parked on the apron. The captain was killed, but there were no other casualties. He had been grounded on medical reasons, refused reinstatement, and regrounded until February 2000.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>On 12 November 1999, <a href="/wiki/Si_Fly_Flight_3275" title="Si Fly Flight 3275">Si Fly Flight 3275</a>, an ATR 42-312 registration F-OHFV, was on a flight from <a href="/wiki/Rome_Ciampino_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Rome Ciampino Airport">Rome Ciampino Airport</a> in Italy to <a href="/wiki/Pristina_International_Airport_Adem_Jashari" class="mw-redirect" title="Pristina International Airport Adem Jashari">Pristina International Airport Adem Jashari</a> in <a href="/wiki/Kosovo" title="Kosovo">Kosovo</a>, operating on behalf of the UN.
