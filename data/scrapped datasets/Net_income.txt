.reflist-lower-roman{list-style-type:lower-roman}</style><div class="reflist">\n</div><p>In <a href="/wiki/Business" title="Business">business</a> and <a href="/wiki/Accountancy" class="mw-redirect" title="Accountancy">accounting</a>, <b>net income</b> (also <b>total comprehensive income</b>, <b>net earnings</b>, <b>net profit</b>, <b>bottom line</b>, <b>sales profit</b>, or <b>credit sales</b>) is an entity\'s <a href="/wiki/Income" title="Income">income</a> minus <a href="/wiki/Cost_of_goods_sold" title="Cost of goods sold">cost of goods sold</a>, expenses, <a href="/wiki/Depreciation" title="Depreciation">depreciation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Amortization_(business)" title="Amortization (business)">amortization</a>, <a href="/wiki/Interest" title="Interest">interest</a>, and taxes for an <a href="/wiki/Accounting_period" title="Accounting period">accounting period</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ias1_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ias1-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>It is computed as the residual of all revenues and gains less all expenses and losses for the period,<sup id="cite_ref-swsf_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-swsf-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> and has also been defined as the net increase in <a href="/wiki/Equity_(finance)" title="Equity (finance)">shareholders\' equity</a> that results from a company\'s operations.<sup id="cite_ref-nnp_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nnp-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> It is different from <a href="/wiki/Gross_income" title="Gross income">gross income</a>, which only deducts the cost of goods sold from revenue.\n</p><p>For households and individuals, <b>net income</b> refers to the (gross) income minus taxes and other deductions (e.g. mandatory <a href="/wiki/Pension" title="Pension">pension</a> contributions).\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc" role="navigation" aria-labelledby="mw-toc-heading"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="mw-headline" id="Definition">Definition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Net_income&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Net income can be distributed among holders of common stock as a <a href="/wiki/Dividend" title="Dividend">dividend</a> or held by the firm as an addition to <a href="/wiki/Retained_earnings" title="Retained earnings">retained earnings</a>. As <a href="/wiki/Profit_(accounting)" title="Profit (accounting)">profit</a> and <a href="/wiki/Earnings" title="Earnings">earnings</a> are used synonymously for <a href="/wiki/Income" title="Income">income</a> (also depending on UK and US usage), <b>net earnings</b> and <b>net profit</b> are commonly found as synonyms for net income. Often, the term <b>income</b> is substituted for net income, yet this is not preferred due to the possible ambiguity. Net income is informally called the <b>bottom line</b> because it is typically found on the last line of a company\'s <a href="/wiki/Income_statement" title="Income statement">income statement</a> (a related term is <a href="/wiki/Top_line" class="mw-redirect" title="Top line">top line</a>, meaning <a href="/wiki/Revenue" title="Revenue">revenue</a>, which forms the first line of the account statement).\n</p><p>In simplistic terms, net profit is the money left over after paying all the expenses of an endeavor. In practice this can get very complex in large organizations. The <a href="/wiki/Bookkeeper" class="mw-redirect" title="Bookkeeper">bookkeeper</a> or <a href="/wiki/Accountant" title="Accountant">accountant</a> must itemise and allocate revenues and expenses properly to the specific working scope and context in which the term is applied.\n</p><p>Net income is usually calculated per annum, for each <a href="/wiki/Fiscal_year" title="Fiscal year">fiscal year</a>. The items deducted will typically include <a href="/wiki/Tax_expense" title="Tax expense">tax expense</a>, financing expense (<a href="/wiki/Interest_expense" title="Interest expense">interest expense</a>), and minority interest. Likewise, \n<a href="/wiki/Preferred_stock" title="Preferred stock">preferred stock</a> dividends will be subtracted too, though they are not an expense. For a <a href="/wiki/Merchandising_(play)" title="Merchandising (play)">merchandising</a> company, subtracted costs \nmay be the <a href="/wiki/Cost_of_goods_sold" title="Cost of goods sold">cost of goods sold</a>, sales discounts, and sales returns and allowances. For a product company, <a href="/wiki/Advertising" title="Advertising">advertising</a>, \n<a href="/wiki/Manufacturing" title="Manufacturing">manufacturing</a>, &amp; design and development costs are included. Net income can also be calculated by adding a company\'s operating income to non-operating income and then subtracting off taxes.<sup id="cite_ref-nbp_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nbp-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The net <a href="/wiki/Profit_margin" title="Profit margin">profit margin</a> percentage is a related ratio. This figure is calculated by dividing net profit by revenue or turnover, and it represents profitability, as a percentage.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="An_equation_for_net_income">An equation for net income</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Net_income&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: An equation for net income">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Net profit: To calculate net profit for a venture (such as a company, division, or project), subtract all costs, including a fair share of total corporate overheads, from the gross revenues or turnover.\n</p>\n<dl><dd>Net profit = sales revenue \xe2\x88\x92 total costs</dd></dl>\n<p>Net profit is a measure of the fundamental profitability of the venture. "It is the revenues of the activity less the costs of the activity. The main complication is . . . when needs to be allocated" across ventures. "Almost by definition, overheads are costs that cannot be directly tied to any specific" project, product, or division. "The classic example would be the cost of headquarters staff." "Although it is theoretically possible to calculate profits for any sub-(venture), such as a product or region, often the calculations are rendered suspect by the need to allocate overhead costs." Because overhead costs generally don\'t come in neat packages, their allocation across ventures is not an exact science.<sup id="cite_ref-Marketing_Metrics2_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Marketing_Metrics2-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Example">Example</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span taxes">Earnings before interest and taxes</a> (EBIT) - <a href="/wiki/Interest_expense" title="Interest expense">Interest expense</a> (cost of borrowing money) = <a href="/wiki/Earnings_before_taxes" class="mw-redirect" title="Earnings before taxes">Earnings before taxes</a> (EBT) - <a href="/wiki/Tax_expense" title="Tax expense">Tax expense</a> = Net income (EAT)\n</p><p>Another equation to calculate net income:\n</p>\n<dl><dd><dl><dd><a href="/wiki/Net_sales" class="mw-redirect" title="Net sales">Net sales</a> = <a href="/wiki/Gross_sales" class="mw-redirect" title="Gross sales">gross 100%.</dd>\n<dd><a href="/wiki/Operating_profit" class="mw-redirect" title="Operating profit">Operating profit</a> = gross profit \xe2\x80\x93 total <a href="/wiki/Operating_expense" title="Operating
