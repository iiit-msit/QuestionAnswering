navigation-not-searchable">Not to be confused with <a href="/wiki/Subsidiarity" title="Subsidiarity">Subsidiarity</a> or <a href="/wiki/Subsidy" title="Subsidy">Subsidy</a>.</div>\n<p>A <b>subsidiary</b>, <b>subsidiary company</b> or <b>daughter company</b><sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> is a <a href="/wiki/Company_(law)" class="mw-redirect" title="Company (law)">company</a> owned or controlled by another company, which is called the <a href="/wiki/Parent_company" title="Parent company">parent company</a>, parent, or <a href="/wiki/Holding_company" title="Holding company">holding company</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The subsidiary can be a company, <a href="/wiki/Corporation" title="Corporation">corporation</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Limited_liability_company" title="Limited liability company">limited liability company</a>. In some cases, it is a government or <a href="/wiki/State-owned_enterprise" title="State-owned enterprise">state-owned enterprise</a>.\n</p><p>Subsidiaries are a common feature of business life<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (January 2021)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> and most <a href="/wiki/Multinational_corporation" title="Multinational corporation">multinational corporations</a> organize their operations in this way.<sup id="cite_ref-Drucker_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Drucker-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Examples include holding companies such as <a href="/wiki/Berkshire_Hathaway" title="Berkshire Hathaway">Berkshire Hathaway</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Jefferies_Financial_Group" title="Jefferies Financial Group">Jefferies Financial Group</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Walt_Disney_Company" title="The Walt Disney Company">The Walt Disney Company</a>, <a href="/wiki/WarnerMedia" title="WarnerMedia">WarnerMedia</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Citigroup" title="Citigroup">Citigroup</a>; as well as more focused companies such as <a href="/wiki/IBM" title="IBM">IBM</a>, <a href="/wiki/Xerox" title="Xerox">Xerox</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Microsoft" title="Microsoft">Microsoft</a>. These, and others, organize their businesses into national and functional subsidiaries, often with multiple levels of subsidiaries.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc" role="navigation" class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Subsidiary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Details">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Subsidiaries are separate, distinct <a href="/wiki/Commercial_law" title="Commercial law">legal</a> entities for the purposes of <a href="/wiki/Tax" title="Tax">taxation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Regulation" title="Regulation">regulation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Legal_liability" title="Legal liability">liability</a>. For this reason, they differ from <a href="/wiki/Division_(organization)" class="mw-redirect" title="Division (organization)">divisions</a>, which are businesses fully integrated within the main company, and not legally or otherwise distinct from it.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> In other words, a subsidiary can sue and be sued separately from its parent and its obligations will not normally be the obligations of its parent. However, creditors of an insolvent subsidiary may be able to obtain a judgment against the parent if they can <a href="/wiki/Piercing_the_corporate_veil" title="Piercing the corporate veil">pierce the corporate veil</a> and prove that the parent and subsidiary are mere <a href="/wiki/Alter_egos" class="mw-redirect" title="Alter egos">alter egos</a> of one another, therefore any copyrights, trademarks, and patents remain with the subsidiary until the parent shuts down the subsidiary.\n</p><p>One of the ways of controlling a subsidiary is achieved through the ownership of <a href="/wiki/Share_(finance)" title="Share (finance)">shares</a> in the subsidiary by the parent. These shares give the parent the necessary votes to determine the composition of the board of the subsidiary, and so exercise control. This gives rise to the common presumption that 50% plus one share is enough to create a subsidiary. There are, however, other ways that control can come about, and the exact rules both as to what control is needed, and how it is achieved, can be complex (see below). A subsidiary may itself have subsidiaries, and these, in turn, may have subsidiaries of their own. A parent and all its subsidiaries together are called a <a href="/wiki/Corporate_group" title="Corporate group">corporate</a>, although this term can also apply to cooperating companies and their subsidiaries with varying degrees of shared ownership.\n</p><p>A parent company does not have to be the larger or "more powerful" entity; it is possible for the parent company to be smaller than a subsidiary, such as <a href="/wiki/Danjaq" title="Danjaq">DanJaq</a>, a closely held family company, which controls <a href="/wiki/Eon_Productions" title="Eon Productions">Eon Productions</a>, the large corporation which manages the <a href="/wiki/James_Bond" title="James Bond">James Bond</a> franchise. Conversely, the parent may be larger than some or all of its subsidiaries (if it has more than one), as the relationship is defined by control of ownership shares, not the number of employees. \n</p><p>The parent and the subsidiary do not necessarily have to operate in the same locations or operate the same businesses. Not only is it possible that they could conceivably be competitors in the marketplace, but such arrangements happen frequently at the end of a <a href="/wiki/Hostile_takeover" class="mw-redirect" title="Hostile takeover">hostile takeover</a> or voluntary merger. Also, because a parent company and a subsidiary are separate entities, it is entirely possible for one of them to be involved in legal proceedings, bankruptcy, tax delinquency, indictment or under investigation while the other is not.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tiered_subsidiaries">Tiered subsidiaries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Subsidiary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Tiered subsidiaries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In descriptions of larger corporate structures, the terms "first-tier subsidiary", "second-tier subsidiary", "third-tier subsidiary", etc., are often used to describe multiple levels of subsidiaries. A first-tier subsidiary means a subsidiary/daughter company of the ultimate parent company,<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;note 1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Chron_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chron-10">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> while a second-tier subsidiary is a subsidiary of a first-tier subsidiary: a "granddaughter" of the main parent company.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Consequently, a third-tier subsidiary is a subsidiary of a second-tier subsidiary\xe2\x80\x94a "great-granddaughter" of the main parent company.\n</p><p>The ownership structure of the small British specialist company Ford Component Sales, which sells Ford components to specialist car manufacturers and <a href="/wiki/OEM" class="mw-redirect" title="OEM">OEM</a> manufacturers, such as <a href="/wiki/Morgan_Motor_Company" title="Morgan Motor Company">Morgan Motor Company</a> and <a href="/wiki/Caterham_Cars" title="Caterham Cars">Caterham Cars</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> illustrates how multiple levels of subsidiaries are used in large corporations:\n</p>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Ford_Motor_Company" title="Ford Motor Company">Ford Motor Company</a> \xe2\x80\x93 U.S. parent company based in <a href="/wiki/Dearborn,_Michigan" title="Dearborn, Michigan">Dearborn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Michigan" title="Michigan">Michigan</a>\n<ul><li>Ford International Capital LLC \xe2\x80\x93 First-tier subsidiary (U.S. holding company located in Dearborn, Michigan, but registered in <a href="/wiki/Delaware" title="Delaware">Delaware</a>)<sup id="cite_ref-SEC_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SEC-13">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>\n<ul><li>Ford Technologies Limited \xe2\x80\x93 Second-tier subsidiary (British holding company, located at the Ford UK head office in <a href="/wiki/Brentwood,_Essex" title="Brentwood, Essex">Brentwood</a>, <a href="/wiki/Essex" title="Essex">Essex</a>, with five employees)<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Ford_of_Britain" title="Ford of Britain">Ford Motor Company Limited</a> \xe2\x80\x93 Third-tier subsidiary (the main British Ford company, with head office in Brentwood, with 10,500 employees)<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup></li></ul></li></ul></li></ul></li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Control">Control</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Subsidiary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Control">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="General">General</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Subsidiary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: General">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The word "control" and its derivatives (subsidiary and parent) may have different meanings in different contexts. These concepts may have different meanings in various areas of law (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Corporate_law" title="Corporate law">corporate law</a>, <a href="/wiki/Competition_law" title="Competition law">competition law</a>, <a href="/wiki/Capital_markets_law" class="mw-redirect" title="Capital markets law">capital markets law</a>) or in <a href="/wiki/Accounting" title="Accounting">accounting</a>. For example, if Company A purchases shares in Company B, it is possible that the transaction is not subject to <a href="/wiki/Merger_control" title="Merger control">merger control</a> (because Company A had been deemed to already control Company B before the share purchase, under competition law rules), but at the same time Company A may be required to start consolidating Company B into its financial statements under the relevant accounting rules (because it had been treated as a <a href="/wiki/Joint_venture" title="Joint venture">joint venture</a> before the purchase for accounting purposes).\n</p><p>Control can be direct (e.g., an ultimate parent company controls the first-tier subsidiary directly) or indirect (e.g., an ultimate parent company controls second and lower tiers of subsidiaries indirectly, through first-tier subsidiaries).\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="European_Union">European Union</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Subsidiary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: European Union">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Recital 31 of Directive 2013/34/EU<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> stipulates that control should be based on holding a majority of voting rights, but control may also exist where there are agreements with fellow shareholders or members. In certain circumstances, control may be effectively exercised where the parent holds a minority or none of the shares in the subsidiary.\n</p><p>According to Article 22 of the directive 2013/34/EU an undertaking is a parent if it:\n</p>\n<ul><li>has a majority of the shareholders\' or members\' voting rights in another undertaking (a subsidiary undertaking);</li>\n<li>has the right to appoint or remove a majority of the members of the administrative, management or supervisory body of another undertaking (a subsidiary undertaking) and is at the same time a shareholder in or member of that undertaking;</li>\n<li>has the right to exercise a dominant influence over an undertaking (a subsidiary undertaking) of which it is a shareholder or member, pursuant to a contract entered into with that undertaking or to a provision in its memorandum or articles of association, where the law governing that subsidiary undertaking permits its being subject to such contracts or provisions.</li>\n<li>is a shareholder in or member of an undertaking, and:\n<ul><li>a majority of the members of the administrative, management or supervisory bodies of that undertaking (a subsidiary undertaking) who have held office during the financial year, during the preceding financial year and up to the time when the consolidated financial statements are drawn up, have been appointed solely as a result of the exercise of its voting rights; or</li>\n<li>controls alone, pursuant to an agreement with other shareholders in or members of that undertaking (a subsidiary undertaking), a majority of shareholders\' or members\' voting rights in that undertaking.</li></ul></li></ul>\n<p>Additionally, control may arise when:\n</p>\n<ul><li>a parent undertaking has the power to exercise, or actually exercises, dominant influence or control over another undertaking (the subsidiary undertaking); or</li>\n<li>a parent undertaking and another undertaking (the subsidiary undertaking) are managed on a unified basis by the parent undertaking.</li></ul>\n<p>Under the international accounting standards adopted by the EU<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> a company is deemed to control another company only if it has all the following:\n</p>\n<ul><li>power over the other company;</li>\n<li>exposure, or rights, to variable returns from its involvement with the other company; and</li>\n<li>the ability to use its power over the other company to affect the number of the company\'s returns (<a href="/wiki/IFRS" class="mw-redirect" title="IFRS">IFRS</a> 10 para 7). Power generally arises when the parent has rights that give it the ability to direct the relevant activities, i.e. the activities that significantly affect the other subsidiary\'s returns.</li></ul>\n<p>A subsidiary can have only one parent; otherwise, the subsidiary is, in fact, a joint arrangement (joint operation or joint venture) over which two or more parties have joint control (IFRS 11 para 4). Joint control is the contractually agreed sharing of control of an arrangement, which exists only when decisions about the relevant activities require the unanimous consent of the parties sharing control.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Kingdom">United Kingdom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Subsidiary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: United Kingdom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Companies_Act_2006" title="Companies Act 2006">Companies Act 2006</a> contains two definitions: one of "subsidiary" and the other "subsidiary undertaking".\n</p><p>According to s.1159 of the Act, a company is a "subsidiary" of another company, its "holding company", if that other company:\n</p>\n<ul><li>holds a majority of the voting rights in it, or</li>\n<li>is a member of it and has the right to appoint or remove a majority of its board of directors, or</li>\n<li>is a member of it and controls alone, pursuant to an agreement with other members, a majority of the voting rights in it, or if it is a subsidiary of a company that is itself a subsidiary of that other company.</li></ul>\n<p>The second definition is broader. According to s.1162 of the Companies Act 2006, an undertaking is a parent undertaking in relation to another undertaking, a subsidiary undertaking, if:\n</p>\n<ul><li>it holds a majority of the voting rights in the undertaking, or</li>\n<li>it is a member of the undertaking and has the right to appoint or remove a majority of its board of directors, or</li>\n<li>it has the right to exercise a dominant influence over the undertaking\xe2\x80\x94\n<ul><li>by virtue of provisions contained in the undertaking\'s articles, or</li>\n<li>by virtue of a control contract, or</li></ul></li>\n<li>it is a member of the undertaking and controls alone, pursuant to an agreement with other shareholders or members, a majority of the voting rights in the undertaking.</li></ul>\n<p>An undertaking is also a parent undertaking in relation to another undertaking, a subsidiary undertaking, if:\n</p>\n<ul><li>it has the power to exercise, or actually exercises, dominant influence or control over it, or</li>\n<li>it and the subsidiary undertaking are managed on a unified basis.</li></ul>\n<p>The broader definition of "subsidiary undertaking" is applied to the accounting provisions of the Companies Act 2006, while the definition of "subsidiary" is used for general purposes.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Oceania">Oceania</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Subsidiary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Oceania">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In <a href="/wiki/Oceania" title="Oceania">Oceania</a>, the accounting standards defined the circumstances in which one
