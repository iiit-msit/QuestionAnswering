title="Statutory corporation">statutory corporation</a> which <a href="/wiki/Civil_aviation_authority" class="mw-redirect" title="Civil aviation authority">oversees and regulates all aspects of civil aviation</a> in the United Kingdom. Its areas of responsibility include:\n</p>\n<ul><li>Supervising the issuing of pilots\' licences, testing of equipment, calibrating of navaids, and many other inspections (Civil Aviation Flying Unit).</li>\n<li>Managing the regulation of security standards, including vetting of all personnel in the aviation industry (Directorate of Aviation Security).</li>\n<li>Overseeing the national protection scheme for customers abroad in the event of a travel company failure (<a href="/wiki/Air_Travel_Organisers%27_Licensing" title="Air Travel Organisers&#39; Licensing">Air Travel Organisers\' Licensing</a> \xe2\x80\x93 ATOL).</li></ul>\n<p>The CAA is a public corporation of the <a href="/wiki/Department_for_Transport" title="Department for Transport">Department for Transport</a>, liaising with the government via the Standards Group of the <a href="/wiki/Cabinet_Office" title="Cabinet Office">Cabinet Office</a>.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc" role="navigation" In some aspects of aviation it is the primary regulator. Prior to the UK\'s departure from the European Union, some areas, where the responsibility for regulation has passed to the <a href="/wiki/European_Aviation_Safety_Agency" title="European Aviation Safety Agency">European Aviation Safety Agency</a> (EASA), the CAA acted as EASA\'s local office, implementing the regulations.  Representatives from the CAA used to sit on EASA\'s advisory bodies, taking part in the Europe-wide regulation process. Following the end of the transition period of <a href="/wiki/Brexit" title="Brexit">Brexit</a> on the 1st of January 2021, the UK is no longer an EASA participant. Whilst there are some elements of continuity following the UK-EU trade agreement, they do not constitute a replication of the UK\xe2\x80\x99s regulatory arrangements as part of the EASA/EU framework.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The UK government requires that the CAA\'s costs are met entirely from its charges on those whom it regulates. Unlike many other countries, there is no direct government funding of the CAA\'s work. It is classed as a public corporation, established by statute, in the public sector. The connection it has with the government is via the <a href="/wiki/Machinery_of_government" title="Machinery of government">machinery of government</a> and the Standards Group of the <a href="/wiki/Cabinet_Office" title="Cabinet Office">Cabinet Office</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Responsibilities">Responsibilities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The CAA regulates (approximately):\n</p>\n<ul><li>Active professional and private <a href="/wiki/Pilot_(aeronautics)" class="mw-redirect" (206)</li>\n<li>Licensed <a href="/wiki/Aerodrome" title="Aerodrome">aerodromes</a> (241)</li>\n<li>Organisations involved in the design, production and maintenance of aircraft (950)</li>\n<li>ATOL holders (2,400)</li>\n<li>Aircraft registered in the UK (19,000)</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Alternative_Dispute_Resolution" class="mw-redirect" title="Alternative Dispute Resolution">Alternative Dispute Resolution</a> providers</li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="ATOL">ATOL</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Civil_Aviation_Authority_(United_Kingdom)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: ATOL">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The CAA also oversees the <a href="/wiki/Air_Travel_Organisers%27_Licensing" title="Air Travel Organisers&#39; Licensing">Air Travel Organisers\' Licensing</a> (ATOL).\n</p><p>By law, every UK travel company which sells air holidays and flights is required to hold an ATOL, which stands for Air Travel Organiser\'s Licence.\n</p><p>If a travel company with an ATOL ceases trading, the ATOL scheme protects customers who had booked holidays with the firm. It ensures they do not get stranded abroad or lose money.\n</p><p>The scheme is designed to reassure customers that their money is safe, and will provide assistance in the event of a travel company failure.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Civil_Aviation_Authority_(United_Kingdom)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Before 1972, regulation of aviation was the responsibility of the <b>Air Registration Board</b>.\n</p><p>The CAA was established in 1972, under the terms of the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Civil_Aviation_Act_1971&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Civil Aviation Act 1971 (page does not exist)">Civil Aviation Act 1971</a>, following the recommendations of a government committee chaired by Sir <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ronald_Edwards_(industrialist)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Ronald Edwards (industrialist) (page does not exist)">Ronald Edwards</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The CAA has been a public corporation of the Department for Transport since then.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/w/index.php?title=Civil_Aviation_Act_1982&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Civil Aviation Act 1982 (page does not exist)">Civil Aviation Act 1982</a> was an <a href="/wiki/Act_of_Parliament" title="Act of Parliament">Act of Parliament</a> to address evolving conditions, and currently governs air flight in the UK.\n</p><p>Responsibility for <a href="/wiki/Air_traffic_control" title="Air traffic control">air traffic control</a> in the UK passed to <a href="/wiki/National_Air_Traffic_Services" class="mw-redirect" title="National Air Traffic Services">NATS</a> in the run-up to the establishment of its <a href="/wiki/Public-private_partnership" class="mw-redirect" title="Public-private partnership">public-private partnership</a> in 2001.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs The new Directorate of Aviation Security within the CAA now manages rule-making and compliance to deliver proportionate and focussed regulation for UK aviation to ensure the highest standards of security across the civil aviation sector.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. 2009.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-dtc_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dtc-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Dame_Deirdre_Hutton" class="mw-redirect" title="Dame Deirdre Hutton">Dame Deirdre Hutton</a> (August 2009 - August 2020) was appointed to chair the CAA in 2009 by Transport Secretary <a class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Chief_Executive">Chief Executive</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Civil_Aviation_Authority_(United_Kingdom)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Chief Executive">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Andrew Haines was Chief Executive until 2018 when his term of office was allowed to expire normally.<sup id="cite_ref-caap_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-caap-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>On 30 November 2017, the board appointed Richard Moriarty as Chief Executive. He acceded the job in summer 2018.<sup id="cite_ref-caap_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-caap-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Civil_Aviation_Authority_(United_Kingdom)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The CAA head office is located in Aviation House on the grounds of <a href="/wiki/Gatwick_Airport" title="Gatwick Airport">Gatwick Airport</a> in <a href="/wiki/Crawley" title="Crawley">Crawley</a>, Sussex. The Authority relocated from its previous London head office in early 2019, moving its head office functions to its existing office at Aviation House, as well as opening a new London branch office at Westferry Circus, Canary Wharf by that July.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="GA_regulation">GA regulation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Civil_Aviation_Authority_(United_Kingdom)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: GA regulation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><a href="/wiki/General_aviation" title="General aviation">General aviation</a> is an official category that covers a wide range of unscheduled air activity such as flying clubs and training establishments. In 2013 the CAA announced a new approach to regulating GA which will be more proportionate. A new dedicated GA unit was established in 2014 www.caa.co.uk/ga\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="CAA_Flying_Unit">CAA Flying Unit</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Civil_Aviation_Authority_(United_Kingdom)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: CAA Flying Unit">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:19870830-EGSU-DH104-GALFU.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/19870830-EGSU-DH104-GALFU.jpg/220px-19870830-EGSU-DH104-GALFU.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="145" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/19870830-EGSU-DH104-GALFU.jpg/330px-19870830-EGSU-DH104-GALFU.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/19870830-EGSU-DH104-GALFU.jpg/440px-19870830-EGSU-DH104-GALFU.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1600" data-file-height="1054" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:19870830-EGSU-DH104-GALFU.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Preserved de Havilland Dove aircraft G-ALFU of CAA at Duxford Airfield, EGSU</div></div></div>\n<p>The CAA was also responsible for the calibration of navigation and approach aids until the Flight Calibration Services group was privatised and sold to Flight Precision Ltd in 1996.\n</p><p>The history of the Civil Aviation Flying Unit (CAFU) can be traced back to the Air Ministry\'s Civil Operations Fleet founded in 1944. The CAA and its predecessors have operated 49 aircraft of 13, primarily British, aircraft types including <a href="/wiki/De_Havilland_Tiger_Moth" title="De Havilland Tiger Moth">de Havilland Tiger Moths</a>, <a href="/wiki/Avro_Anson" title="Avro Anson">Avro Ansons</a>, <a href="/wiki/Airspeed_Consul" title="Airspeed Consul">Airspeed Consuls</a>, <a href="/wiki/Percival_Prince" title="Percival Prince">Percival Princes</a>, <a href="/wiki/De_Havilland_Dove" title="De Havilland Dove">de Havilland Doves</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Hawker_Siddeley_HS_748" title="Hawker Siddeley HS 748">Hawker Siddeley HS 748s</a><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/British_Aerospace_125" title="British Aerospace 125">Hawker Siddeley HS 125s</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The roles performed by CAFU aircraft included:\n</p>\n<ul><li>Calibration and testing of radio/radar navigational aids in the UK and overseas</li>\n<li>Flight testing of candidates for the initial issue of commercial pilots\' licences, instrument ratings and instructor ratings</li>\n<li>Training and testing of authorised instrument and type-rating examiners</li>\n<li>Carriage of Government Ministers, MEPs and other officials</li>\n<li>Charter flights for Dan-Air Services Ltd</li>\n<li>Radar target flying for the College of Air Traffic Control</li>\n<li>Ordnance Survey photographic flights</li>\n<li>Airport lighting inspections</li>\n<li>Aerodrome categorisation and evaluation flights</li>\n<li>Trials of new equipment and procedures, e.g. Microwave Landing Systems, ground proximity warning systems, Extended Range Twin-engine Operations (ETOPS)</li>\n<li>Refresher flying for Flight Operations Inspectors and other staff</li>\n<li>Educational flights for local schools,<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>\n<p>Beyond the privatisation of the calibration service in 1996, the Civil Aviation Authority operated two HS 125-700 aircraft successively up until 2002, providing conversion and continuation flying for professional CAA pilots, conducting radar trials for National Air Traffic Services (NATS) and serving the CAA, NATS and Highlands &amp; Islands Airports Ltd (HIAL) in the communications role.\n</p><p>Previous to the privatisation, Stansted Airport had been the home of Flight Calibration; however, in 1996 the department was moved to Teesside Airport in the North East of England with the photographic laboratory services contracted out to a local company, HighLight Photographics.\n</p>\n<h3><span id="CAA_Signals_Training_Establishment_.28STE.29_.E2.80.93_Bletchley_Park"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="CAA_Signals_Training_Establishment_(STE)_\xe2\x80\x93_Bletchley_Park">CAA Signals Training Establishment (STE) \xe2\x80\x93 Bletchley Park</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Civil_Aviation_Authority_(United_Kingdom)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: CAA Signals Training Establishment (STE) \xe2\x80\x93 Bletchley Park">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Based mainly in \'A\', \'B\' and \'E\' Blocks and with further Navigation Aid and Radar classrooms on the northwest corner of the park (now occupied by housing), the STE trained technicians to maintain airport and en-route telecommunications and navigational aids for UK airport and en-route services, including telecommunications, navigational aids and radar.\n</p><p>A two-to-three-year locally domiciled apprenticeship trained technicians who were then posted to airports or en-route centres for on-going employment. STE also provided training facilities for existing technicians to keep up to date with technological developments or to enhance their skills on a broader range of equipments.\n</p><p>Apprentices had exclusive use of the \'AT Club\' (Apprentice Technicians Club) and also to the Bletchley Park \'Radio Shack\' with a call-sign of \'G4BWD\' \xe2\x80\x93 \'Golf Four Building Works Department\', able to access the 2-metre band.\n</p><p>In 1974, STE developed a newer training course, reducing training to a one-to-two-year period for higher-qualified (\'A\'-level and beyond) entrants, nicknamed \'Super-ATs\' or \'Super-Techs\'.\n</p>\n<h3><span id="CAA_College_of_Telecommunications_Engineering_.28CTE.29_.E2.80.93_Bletchley"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="CAA_College_of_Telecommunications_Engineering_(CTE)_\xe2\x80\x93_Bletchley">CAA College of Telecommunications Engineering (CTE) \xe2\x80\x93 Bletchley</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Civil_Aviation_Authority_(United_Kingdom)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: CAA College of Telecommunications Engineering (CTE) \xe2\x80\x93 Bletchley">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In 1975/1976, the \'Signals Training Establishment\' was renamed the \'College of
