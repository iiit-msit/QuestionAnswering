href="https://www.wikidata.org/wiki/Q595453" class="extiw" title="d:Q595453">edit on Wikidata</a>]</div></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p>A <b>colostomy</b> is an opening (<a href="/wiki/Stoma_(medicine)" title="Stoma (medicine)">stoma</a>) in the <a href="/wiki/Large_intestine" title="Large intestine">large intestine</a> (colon), or the <a href="/wiki/Surgical_procedure" class="mw-redirect" title="Surgical procedure">surgical procedure</a> that creates one. The opening is formed by drawing the healthy end of the colon through an <a href="/wiki/Surgical_incision" title="Surgical incision">incision</a> in the anterior <a href="/wiki/Abdominal_wall" title="Abdominal wall">abdominal wall</a> and <a href="/wiki/Surgical_suture" title="Surgical suture">suturing</a> it into place. This opening, often in conjunction with an attached <a href="/wiki/Ostomy_pouching_system" class="mw-redirect" title="Ostomy pouching system">ostomy system</a>, provides an alternative channel for <a href="/wiki/Feces" title="Feces">feces</a> to leave the body. Thus if the natural <a href="/wiki/Anus" title="Anus">anus</a> is not available for that job (for example, in cases where it has been removed in the fight against <a href="/wiki/Colorectal_cancer" title="Colorectal cancer">colorectal cancer</a> or <a href="/wiki/Ulcerative_colitis" title="Ulcerative colitis">ulcerative colitis</a>), an artificial anus takes over. It may be <a href="/wiki/Colostomy_reversal" title="Colostomy reversal">reversible</a> or irreversible, depending on the circumstances.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc" role="navigation" class="toclevel-1 tocsection-9"><a href="#References"><span class="tocnumber">6</span> <span class="toctext">References</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Colostomy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>There are many reasons for this procedure.  Some common reasons are:\n</p>\n<ul><li>A part of the colon has been removed, e.g. due to <a href="/wiki/Colon_cancer" class="mw-redirect" title="Colon cancer">colon cancer</a> requiring a <a href="/wiki/Total_mesorectal_excision" title="Total mesorectal excision">total mesorectal excision</a>, <a href="/wiki/Diverticulitis" title="Diverticulitis">diverticulitis</a>, injury, etc., so that it is no longer possible for feces to exit via the <a href="/wiki/Anus" title="Anus">anus</a>.</li>\n<li>A part of the colon has been operated upon and needs to be \'rested\' until it is healed. In this case, the colostomy is often temporary and is usually reversed at a later date, leaving the patient with a small <a href="/wiki/Scar" title="Scar">scar</a> in place of the stoma.  Children undergoing surgery for extensive <a href="/wiki/Pelvic_tumor" title="Pelvic tumor">pelvic tumors</a> commonly are given a colostomy in preparation for surgery to remove the tumor, followed by <a href="/wiki/Colostomy_reversal" title="Colostomy reversal">reversal</a> of the colostomy.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Fecal_incontinence" title="Fecal incontinence">Fecal incontinence</a> that is not responsive to other treatments.</li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Colostomy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Blausen_0247_Colostomy.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Blausen_0247_Colostomy.png/220px-Blausen_0247_Colostomy.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="220" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Blausen_0247_Colostomy.png/330px-Blausen_0247_Colostomy.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Blausen_0247_Colostomy.png/440px-Blausen_0247_Colostomy.png 2x" data-file-width="1500" data-file-height="1500" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Blausen_0247_Colostomy.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Illustration depicting various types of colostomy</div></div></div>\n<p>Types of colostomy include:<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<ul><li><b>Loop colostomy:</b> This type of colostomy is usually used in emergencies and is a temporary and large <a href="/wiki/Stoma_(medicine)" title="Stoma (medicine)">stoma</a>. A loop of the bowel is pulled out onto the abdomen and held in place with an external device. The bowel is then sutured to the abdomen and two openings are created in the one stoma: one for stool and the other for mucus.</li>\n<li><b>End colostomy:</b> A stoma is created from one end of the bowel. The other portion of the bowel is either removed or sewn shut (<a href="/wiki/Hartmann%27s_operation" title="Hartmann&#39;s operation">Hartmann\'s procedure</a>).</li>\n<li><b>Double barrel colostomy:</b> The bowel is severed and both ends are brought out onto the abdomen. Only the proximal stoma is functioning. Most often, double-barrel colostomy is a temporary colostomy with two openings into the colon (distal and proximal). The elimination occurs through the proximal stoma.</li></ul>\n<p>Colostomy surgery that is planned usually has a higher rate of long-term success than surgery performed in an emergency situation.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Duration">Duration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Colostomy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Duration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>A colostomy may be temporary; and <a href="/wiki/Colostomy_reversal" title="Colostomy reversal">reversed</a> at a later date; or permanent.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Alternatives">Alternatives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Colostomy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Alternatives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>A man in the UK has been given a remote-controlled bowel.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Colostomy or ileostomy is now rarely performed for rectal cancer, with surgeons usually preferring primary <a href="/wiki/Bowel_resection" title="Bowel resection">resection</a> and internal <a href="/wiki/Anastomosis" title="Anastomosis">anastomosis</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-ASCRS_textbook_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ASCRS_textbook-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> e.g. an <a href="/wiki/Ileo-anal_pouch" title="Ileo-anal pouch">ileo-anal pouch</a>. In place of an external <a href="/wiki/Prosthesis" title="Prosthesis">appliance</a>, an internal ileo-anal pouch is constructed using a portion of the patient\'s lower intestine, to act as a new <a href="/wiki/Rectum" title="Rectum">rectum</a> to replace the removed original.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Procedure">Procedure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Colostomy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Procedure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Placement of the stoma on the <a href="/wiki/Abdomen" title="Abdomen">abdomen</a> can occur at any location along the colon, but the most common placement is on the lower left side near the <a href="/wiki/Sigmoid_colon" title="Sigmoid colon">sigmoid</a> where a majority of colon cancers occur. Other locations include the <a href="/wiki/Ascending_colon" title="Ascending colon">ascending</a>, <a href="/wiki/Transverse_colon" title="Transverse colon">transverse</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Descending_colon" title="Descending colon">descending</a> sections of the colon.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Routine_care">Routine care</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Colostomy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Routine care">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Ostomy_system" title="Ostomy system">Ostomy system</a></div>\n<p>Pouches and the stick-on appliances to which they attach must be changed regularly. Sometimes an odor neutralizer and lubricant is squirted into a new pouch before it is attached. Two types of pouches are available: one disposable and one drainable. Most pouches are opaque and filter out air through a charcoal filter. The recommended practice is to empty such pouches when one-third full. <sup id="cite_ref-Taylor_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Taylor-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Appliances, in contrast with pouches, are usually replaced every three to seven days except in cases where their seals have broken contact with the skin, when they should be replaced immediately.<sup id="cite_ref-Taylor_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Taylor-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Even as long ago as the 1940s, surgeons conducting a <a href="/wiki/Review_article" title="Review article">review</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Cleveland_Clinic" title="Cleveland Clinic">Cleveland Clinic</a> (Jones and Kehm, 1946)<sup id="cite_ref-pmid_20274022_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pmid_20274022-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> could summarize the routine care of the permanent colostomy as usually quite satisfactory, stating that after patients recover from the initial worry prompted by the need for a colostomy, most of them learn to manage their colostomy quite well.<sup id="cite_ref-pmid_20274022_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pmid_20274022-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> "These patients come from all walks of life and carry on their daily work as usual. One patient stated that he could see no advantage of the normal anus over a colostomy. While this may be somewhat overstated, it is true that most people with a permanent colostomy can live a useful, happy life."<sup id="cite_ref-pmid_20274022_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pmid_20274022-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> They found that, just as in anyone else, dietary indiscretion was the usual factor in occasional bowel habit disruption.<sup id="cite_ref-pmid_20274022_7-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pmid_20274022-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> This historical experience has been borne out, as today the conclusion still stands that most patients can successfully manage a colostomy as part of their <a href="/wiki/Activities_of_daily_living" title="Activities of daily living">activities of daily living</a>. \n</p><p>Jones and Kehm preferred tissue paper as a colostomy cover (held in place with a band or garment) rather than a colostomy bag.<sup id="cite_ref-pmid_20274022_7-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pmid_20274022-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> They found that irrigation of the colostomy varied with each patient\'s bowel habit but that most patients developed a routine of every-other-day irrigation, whereas a few needed no irrigation.<sup id="cite_ref-pmid_20274022_7-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pmid_20274022-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>People with colostomies must wear an <a href="/wiki/Ostomy_pouching_system" class="mw-redirect" title="Ostomy pouching system">ostomy pouching system</a> to collect intestinal waste.  Ordinarily, the pouch must be emptied or changed a couple of times a day depending on the frequency of activity; in general the further from the <a href="/wiki/Anus" title="Anus">anus</a> (i.e., the further \'up\' the intestinal tract) the ostomy is located the greater the output and more frequent the need to empty or change the pouch.<sup id="cite_ref-UOAA_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UOAA-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Colostomy_and_irrigation">Colostomy and irrigation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Colostomy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Colostomy and irrigation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>People with colostomies who have ostomies of the <a href="/wiki/Sigmoid_colon" title="Sigmoid colon">sigmoid colon</a> or <a href="/wiki/Descending_colon" title="Descending colon">descending colon</a> may have the option of irrigation, which allows for the person to not wear a pouch, but rather just a gauze cap over the stoma, and to schedule irrigation for times that are convenient.<sup id="cite_ref-ostomy_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ostomy-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> To irrigate, a <a href="/wiki/Catheter" title="Catheter">catheter</a> is placed inside the stoma, and flushed with water, which allows the feces to come out of the body into an irrigation sleeve.<sup id="cite_ref-webmed_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-webmed-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Most colostomates irrigate once a day or every other day, though this depends on the person, their food intake, and their health.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Complications">Complications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Colostomy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Complications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div
