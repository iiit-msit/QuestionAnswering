and price restrictions on airlines affecting, in particular, the carriers permitted to serve specific routes. In the United States, the term usually applies to the <a href="/wiki/Airline_Deregulation_Act" title="Airline Deregulation Act">Airline Deregulation Act</a> of 1978. A new form of regulation has been developed to some extent to deal with problems such as the allocation of the limited number of slots available at airports.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc" links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Introduction">Introduction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Airline_deregulation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Introduction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>As jets were integrated into the market in the late 1950s and early 1960s, the industry experienced dramatic growth. By the mid-1960s, airlines were carrying roughly 100 million passengers and by the mid-1970s, over 200 million Americans had traveled by air.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> This steady increase in air travel began placing serious strains on the ability of federal regulators to cope with the increasingly complex nature of air travel.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>The onset of high inflation, low economic growth, falling productivity, rising labor costs and higher fuel costs proved problematic to the airlines.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Although it is generally recognized that the purpose behind government regulation is to create a stable industry,<sup id="cite_ref-Bamber,_Gittell,_Kochan,_Nordanflycht_2011_4_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bamber,_Gittell,_Kochan,_Nordanflycht_2011_4-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Kaps_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kaps-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> in the decades leading up to deregulation many airline market analysts expressed concerns with the structure of the United States\' passenger air transport system. Concerns included high barriers to entry for fledgling airlines, slow government response to existing airlines entering to compete in city-pairings, and <a href="/wiki/Monopoly" title="Monopoly">monopolistic</a> practices by legacy airlines artificially inflating passenger ticket prices.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>In order to address these growing concerns airline deregulation began in the US in 1978. It was, and still is, a part of a sweeping experiment to ultimately reduce ticket prices and entry controls holding sway over new airline hopefuls. Airline deregulation had begun with initiatives by economist <a href="/wiki/Alfred_E._Kahn" title="Alfred E. Kahn">Alfred E. Kahn</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Presidency_of_Richard_Nixon" title="Presidency of Richard Nixon">Nixon administration</a>, carried through the <a href="/wiki/Gerald_Ford" title="Gerald Ford">Ford administration</a> and finally, at the behest of <a href="/wiki/Ted_Kennedy" title="Ted Kennedy">Ted Kennedy</a>, signed into law by President <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_Carter" title="Jimmy Carter">Jimmy Carter</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>Globally, state supported airlines are still relatively common, maintaining control over ticket prices and route entry, but many countries have since deregulated their own domestic airline markets. A similar but less <a href="/wiki/Laissez-faire" title="Laissez-faire">laissez-faire</a> approach has been taken by the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a>, Australia, United Kingdom, Scandinavia, Ireland and select South and Central American nations.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_the_United_States">In the United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Airline_deregulation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: In the United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The beginning of federal government regulation of the interstate airline industry can be traced to the Air Mail Act of 1925 and the Air Commerce Act of 1926. Additional federal regulation of commercial aviation was imposed with the passage of the Civil Aeronautics Act of 1938.<sup id="cite_ref-Millbrooke_2006_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Millbrooke_2006-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>That Act created the Civil Aeronautics Authority, which became the Civil Aeronautics Board (CAB), and gave the CAB the power to regulate airline routes, control entry to and exit from the market, and mandate service rates, to investigate accidents, certify aircraft and pilots, to create rules for air traffic control (ATC) and to recommend new rules to prevent repetition of previous accidents.<sup id="cite_ref-Millbrooke_2006_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Millbrooke_2006-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Additional airline safety regulation came later with the passage of the Federal Aviation Act of 1958,<sup id="cite_ref-Millbrooke_2006_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Millbrooke_2006-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> which created the Federal Aviation Administration (FAA) <sup id="cite_ref-Millbrooke_2006_6-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Millbrooke_2006-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Civil_Aeronautics_Board">Civil Aeronautics Board</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Airline_deregulation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Civil Aeronautics Board">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Civil_Aeronautics_Board" title="Civil Aeronautics Board">Civil Aeronautics Board</a></div>\n<p>In 1938 the U.S. government, through the Civil Aeronautics Board (CAB), regulated many areas of commercial aviation such as routes, fares and schedules.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\nThe CAB had three main functions: to award routes to airlines, to limit the entry of air carriers into new markets, and to regulate fares for passengers.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\nMuch of the established practices of commercial passenger travel within the US, went back even farther, to the policies of W. F. Brown,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\nthe US postmaster general in the 1920s and early 1930s in the administration of President H. Hoover.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\nBrown had changed the mail payments system to encourage the manufacture of passenger aircraft instead of mail carrying aircraft. His influence was crucial in awarding contracts so as to create four major domestic airlines: United, American, Eastern, and Transcontinental and Western Air (TWA).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\nSimilarly, Brown had also helped give Pan American a monopoly on international routes. (See also the US Centennial of Flight Commission <sup id="cite_ref-centennialofflight1_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-centennialofflight1-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>)\n</p><p>Typical regulatory thinking from the 1940s onward is evident in a Civil Aeronautics Board report. In the absence of particular circumstances presenting an affirmative reason for a new carrier, there appears to be no inherent desirability of increasing the present number of carriers merely for the purpose of numerically enlarging the industry.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Airline_Deregulation_Act">Airline Deregulation Act</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Airline_deregulation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Airline Deregulation Act">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Airline_Deregulation_Act" title="Airline Deregulation Act">Airline Deregulation Act</a></div>\n<p>The Airline Deregulation Act of 1978 removed many of the previously mentioned controls. Prior to deregulation, it was required that airlines first seek regulatory approval to serve any given route.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Thus incumbent airline operators could raise barriers to the challenge of new competition. This system was dismantled as a result of the Airline Deregulation Act. (See also the <a href="/wiki/Centennial_of_Flight_Commission" title="Centennial of Flight Commission">Centennial of Flight Commission</a><sup id="cite_ref-centennialofflight1_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-centennialofflight1-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>) It also dismantled the notion of a <a href="/wiki/Flag_carrier" title="Flag carrier">flag carrier</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Post-deregulation">Post-deregulation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Airline_deregulation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Post-deregulation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In the wake of deregulation, airlines have adopted new strategies and consumers are experiencing a new market. Below are the marquee effects of deregulation.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hub_and_spoke">Hub and spoke</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Airline_deregulation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Hub and spoke">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Airlines quickly moved to a <a href="/wiki/Hub-and-spoke" class="mw-redirect" title="Hub-and-spoke">hub-and-spoke</a> system, whereby an airline selected an airport, the hub, as the destination point for flights from a number of origination cities, the spokes. Because the size of the planes used varied according to the travel on that spoke, and since hubs allowed passenger travel to be consolidated in "transfer stations", capacity utilization increased. The hub-and-spoke model survives among the legacy carriers, but the <a href="/wiki/Low-cost_carriers" class="mw-redirect" title="Low-cost carriers">low-cost carriers</a> (LCCs), now 30 percent of the market, typically fly point to point. The network hubs model offers consumers more convenience for routes, but point-to-point routes have proven less costly for airlines to implement. Over time, the legacy carriers and the LCCs will likely use some combination of point to point and network hubs to capture both economies of scale and pricing advantages.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Price">Price</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Airline_deregulation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Price">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Base ticket prices have declined steadily since deregulation.<sup id="cite_ref-Maynard_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Maynard-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>  The inflation adjusted 1982 constant dollar yield for airlines has fallen from 12.3 cents in 1978 to 7.9 cents in 1997.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Along with rising US. populations<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> and the increasing demand of work-force-mobility, these trends were some of the catalysts for dramatic expansion in passenger miles flown, increasing from 250 million passenger miles in 1978 to 750 million passenger miles in 2005.<sup id="cite_ref-Bamber,_G.J.,_Gittell,_J.H.,_Kochan,_T.A._&amp;_von_Nordenflytch,_A._2009_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bamber,_G.J.,_Gittell,_J.H.,_Kochan,_T.A._&amp;_von_Nordenflytch,_A._2009-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> As such, some think tanks and the U.S. airline <a href="/wiki/Trade_organization" class="mw-redirect" title="Trade organization">trade organization</a>, <a href="/wiki/Airlines_for_America" title="Airlines for America">Airlines for America</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> argue that deregulation has provided benefits to the average air traveler.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Service_quality">Service quality</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Airline_deregulation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Service quality">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The quality of airline service can be measured in many different ways, including the number of aircraft departures, the total number of miles flown, seating comfort, punctuality of service, other programs and services, and various frills or amenities.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>Over the past several years the public\'s view of airline service quality has shown a significant drop.<sup id="cite_ref-chicagotribune.com_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chicagotribune.com-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> According to the 2008 American Customer Satisfaction Index, a University of Michigan study of 80,000 consumers\' expectations and preferences, the major US airlines ranked last among all the industries surveyed. In 2009, the airlines have moved up to being one point ahead of Cable &amp; Satellite TV and the newspaper industry (though results for all industries were not available at the time of this writing).<sup id="cite_ref-theacsi.org_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-theacsi.org-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 2011 Congress finally responded to repeated calls for the United States government to pass an "Air Passenger Bill of Rights" to provide specific requirements about what must happen to air passengers in certain conditions.<sup id="cite_ref-latimes.com_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-latimes.com-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> The push for the bill stemmed from several high-profile passenger strandings over the last several years. On April 25, 2011, the Enhancing Airline Passenger Protections rule, 76 Fed. Reg. 32,110, was enacted.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Amongst other items, the rule includes raising the minimum "denied boarding compensation" to customers with valid tickets yet still not allowed to board the aircraft. The legislation further penalizes airlines up to $27,500 a passenger if left stranded aboard an aircraft, on a tarmac for more than three hours.<sup id="cite_ref-bare_url_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bare_url-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>  In 2010, the largest <a href="/wiki/Trade_associations" class="mw-redirect" title="Trade associations">trade associations</a> representing airline management interests before Capitol Hill, <a href="/wiki/Airlines_for_America" title="Airlines for America">Airlines for America</a> and the Regional Airline Association, opposed this legislation stating that they could self-regulate themselves and they already had begun implementing systems by which to mitigate any tarmac delays.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>  Later American Eagle, an RAA airline member, was the first airline to be fined under the new legislation. A total settlement including fines and compensation paid to passengers totaled $800,000 for tarmac delays incurred in Chicago in May 2011.<sup id="cite_ref-bare_url_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bare_url-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Deregulation advocate <a href="/wiki/Alfred_E._Kahn" title="Alfred E. Kahn">Alfred Kahn</a> noted a deterioration in the quality of airline service following deregulation, including the "turmoil" of massive restructuring of airline routes, price wars, conflicts with airline employee unions, airline bankruptcies, and industry consolidation.<sup id="cite_ref-jstor.org_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jstor.org-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> He also noted unexpected congestion and delays "that have plagued air travelers in recent years".<sup id="cite_ref-jstor.org_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jstor.org-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> However, he also argued that such congestion and delays was also a sign of deregulation success (because it indicated a large increase in passenger volume due to decreases in the price of airfares).<sup id="cite_ref-jstor.org_23-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jstor.org-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> Kahn considered the turmoil, congestion, and delays to be unforeseen "surprises" from deregulation and continued to support deregulation in spite of these events.<sup id="cite_ref-jstor.org_23-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jstor.org-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Competition_between_carriers">Competition between carriers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span Tokyo Narita Airport, Japan</div></div></div>\n<p>A major goal of airline deregulation was to increase competition between airline carriers, leading to price decreases. As a result of deregulation, barriers to entry into the airlines industry for a potential new airline decreased significantly, resulting in many new airlines entering the market, thus increasing competition.<sup id="cite_ref-Maynard_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Maynard-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Industry_consolidation_and_reduction_in_competition_between_carriers">Industry consolidation and reduction in competition between carriers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Airline_deregulation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Industry consolidation and reduction in competition between carriers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>After airline deregulation, many airlines in the United States were purchased by other airlines and merged into fewer airlines "after thirty five years and hundreds of new startup airlines, hundreds of bankruptcies, liquidations, reorganizations, and mergers", wrote Richard Finger, <a href="/wiki/Equities_trader" class="mw-redirect" title="Equities trader">equities trader</a> and <a href="/wiki/Finance" title="Finance">finance</a> business analyst at <a href="/wiki/Forbes" title="Forbes">Forbes</a>.com.<sup id="cite_ref-forbes.com_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-forbes.com-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> This has resulted in just 4 major US airlines (<a href="/wiki/United_Airlines" title="United Airlines">United Airlines</a>, <a href="/wiki/American_Airlines" title="American Airlines">American Airlines</a>, <a href="/wiki/Delta_Air_Lines" title="Delta Air Lines">Delta Air Lines</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Southwest_Airlines" title="Southwest Airlines">Southwest Airlines</a>) controlling a near-monopoly (up to 90%) of both the domestic market and the <a href="/wiki/Gate_(airport)" title="Gate (airport)">gates</a> at some regional airports in the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-forbes.com_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-forbes.com-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> For example, <a href="/wiki/United_Airlines" title="United Airlines">United Airlines</a> (with its merger/purchase of <a href="/wiki/Continental_Airlines" title="Continental Airlines">Continental Airlines</a> in 2012)<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> now controls over 90 percent of domestic travel in and out of <a href="/wiki/Houston_Intercontinental_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Houston Intercontinental Airport">Houston Intercontinental Airport</a>; "many flights have been consolidated so travelers have fewer choices", resulting in increased fares. It is now under 80%.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>As such, the current situation in the airline industry benefits the airlines, by decreasing competition among airlines, increasing fares for airline passengers, decreasing airline employee pay and benefits (especially pensions), and creating an oligopoly of only four major airlines that was never intended (<a href="/wiki/Unintended_consequences" title="Unintended consequences">unintended consequences</a>) by the framers of airline deregulation act.<sup id="cite_ref-forbes.com_24-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-forbes.com-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects_on_airline_staff">Effects on airline staff</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Airline_deregulation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Effects on airline staff">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>A key indicator of the volatility of deregulation;<sup id="cite_ref-Bamber,_Gittell,_Kochan,_Nordanflycht_2011_4_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bamber,_Gittell,_Kochan,_Nordanflycht_2011_4-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Kaps_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kaps-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> from 1976 to 1986 the US. airlines saw a 39% increase in employees (according to <a href="/wiki/Alfred_E._Kahn" title="Alfred E. Kahn">Alfred Kahn</a>),<sup id="cite_ref-jstor.org_23-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jstor.org-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> and saw continued but less rapid growth throughout the 1990s.<sup id="cite_ref-Maynard_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Maynard-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Subsequently, between 2000 and 2008, the same industry shed 100,000 jobs - approximately 20% - and formerly busy hub airports (such as Pittsburgh and St. Louis) have reduced staffs due to a significantly decreased number of flights.<sup id="cite_ref-Maynard_11-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Maynard-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Immediately following the September 11th attacks, the <a href="/wiki/Air_Transportation_Safety_and_System_Stabilization_Act" class="mw-redirect" title="Air Transportation Safety and System Stabilization Act">Air Transportation Safety and System Stabilization Act</a> bestowed the US airlines with $15 billion in loans and an additional $5 billion in grants by the US. government. Despite these loans and grants, nearly every major carrier fired 20% of its staff, with United and American both cutting 20,000 jobs.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> This makes it very difficult to determine the precise job losses due to the effects of deregulation.  Then-retired former CEO of American Airlines <a href="/wiki/Robert_Crandall" title="Robert Crandall">Robert Crandall</a> stated, "I\'m not sure 9/11 by itself had any particular profound impact [on the industry], but it exacerbated the problems they had before 9/11."<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Although regular pay-cuts had become commonplace in the years following deregulation, of the employees remaining after September 11, 2001, the average pay cut has been 18%,<sup id="cite_ref-Bamber,_Gittell,_Kochan,_Nordanflycht_2011_4_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bamber,_Gittell,_Kochan,_Nordanflycht_2011_4-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> with many of the highest earners seeing as much as 40% reductions, virtually every regularly scheduled airline has shifted its pension obligations to its employees.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>According to a study by economist David Card, deregulation resulted in the shift of approximately 5,000 to 7,000 airline mechanic jobs from the major trunk airlines to smaller carriers between 1978 and 1984.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> Because such smaller carriers typically pay less than the major airlines, the average hourly wage of airline mechanics decreased by up to 5 percent; however, the author considered this decrease to be relatively small.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Open_Skies">Open Skies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Airline_deregulation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Open Skies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Open_skies" class="mw-redirect" title="Open skies">Open skies</a></div>\n<p>Beyond the domestic liberalization of the airlines in the US, Open Skies agreements are bilateral agreements between the US and other countries to open the aviation market to foreign access and remove barriers to competition. They give airlines the right to operate air services from any point in the US to any point in the other country, as well as to and from third countries.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> The first major Open Skies agreements were entered into in 1979.\n</p><p>The U.S. has Open Skies agreements with more than 60 countries, including fifteen of the 28 EU nations. Open Skies agreements have been successful at removing many of the government implemented barriers to competition and allowing airlines to have foreign partners,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> access to international routes to and from their home countries and freedom from many traditional forms of economic regulation.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticisms">Criticisms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Airline_deregulation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Criticisms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>With long standing companies like <a href="/wiki/Braniff_International_Airways" title="Braniff International Airways">Braniff</a>, <a href="/wiki/Trans_World_Airlines" title="Trans World Airlines">TWA</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Pan_American_World_Airways" class="mw-redirect" title="Pan American World Airways">Pan Am</a> disappearing through bankruptcy since 1978, the years since 2000 have seen every remaining <a href="/wiki/Legacy_carrier" title="Legacy carrier">legacy carrier</a> file for bankruptcy at least once. US Airways filed twice in the same number of years. During the same time period, <a href="/wiki/Southwest_Airlines" title="Southwest Airlines">Southwest Airlines</a> continued to expand its route structure, buy new airplanes, and hire more employees, while remaining profitable.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/JetBlue" title="JetBlue">JetBlue</a>, a new airline that started up in 1999, "was one of only a few U.S. airlines that made a profit during the sharp downturn in airline travel following the September 11, 2001 attacks. For many years, analysts had predicted that JetBlue\'s growth rate would become unsustainable. Despite this, the airline continued to add planes and routes to the fleet at a brisk pace. JetBlue is one of the largest airlines in the Northeast United States."<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Unions contend that airline management now uses bankruptcy as a tool to liquidate labor contracts. Progressives view this as union-busting, allowing management to throw out contracts already agreed upon while still receiving exorbitant bonuses themselves, regardless of work quality.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> In doing so, according to the labor union view, airline executive management has created what many are calling the great <a href="/wiki/Race_to_the_bottom" title="Race to the bottom">race to the bottom</a><sup id="cite_ref-The_Washington_Times_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_Washington_Times-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> with one company filing for bankruptcy after the next, leaving only those who have not filed for bankruptcy, paying their employees what was contractually agreed upon.<sup id="cite_ref-Associated_Press,_NBC_News_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Associated_Press,_NBC_News-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>One of the key elements deregulation sought to end were airline <a href="/wiki/Oligopoly" title="Oligopoly">oligopolies</a> and monopolies. However, since 2010 the number of major airlines has receded dramatically. With Delta merging with NorthWest, American merging with US Airways, United merging with Continental, SouthWest with AirTran, and Frontier being purchased by Republic who also owns Chautauqua and Midwest Express and bought Shuttle America in 2005,<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> it has been suggested that the old monopolies and oligopolies still exist regardless of regulation. Instead of using their political connections to keep competition out, now, larger airlines simply price-war new entrants out of business, or simply buy them.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> This leaves the weaker airlines to merge, eliminating market choices and creating an oligopoly market.\n</p><p>Various solutions have been proposed by labor unions, former management and industry analysts, including, for the first time since 1978, federal control over some of the prices charged and routes served by major airlines<sup id="cite_ref-cei.org_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cei.org-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> with a view of increasing price and cost competition.<sup id="cite_ref-Bamber,_G.J.,_Gittell,_J.H.,_Kochan,_T.A._&amp;_von_Nordenflytch,_A._2009_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bamber,_G.J.,_Gittell,_J.H.,_Kochan,_T.A._&amp;_von_Nordenflytch,_A._2009-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-cei.org_39-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cei.org-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In June 2008 former CEO of <a href="/wiki/American_Airlines" title="American Airlines">American Airlines</a>, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Crandall" title="Robert Crandall">Robert Crandall</a> stated,\n</p>\n<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r996844942">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>The consequences of deregulation have been very adverse. Our airlines, once world leaders, are now laggards in every category, including fleet age, service quality and international reputation. Fewer and fewer flights are on time. Airport congestion has become a staple of late-night comedy shows. An even higher percentage of bags are lost or misplaced. Last-minute seats are harder and harder to find. Passenger complaints have skyrocketed. Airline service, by any standard, has become unacceptable.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote><p>Crandall has also criticized deregulation for causing airlines to cut service to smaller airports, resulting in a "relatively unsatisfactory transportation network;" he argues that this "has accelerated the movement of people towards the big cities and has discouraged the creation of medium-sized cities."<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><h2><span
