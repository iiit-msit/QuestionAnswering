Airport in relation to Ennis and Limerick</div></div></div>\n<p><b>Shannon Airport</b> (<a href="/wiki/Irish_language" title="Irish language">Irish</a>: <i lang="ga">Aerfort na Sionainne</i>)<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> (<a href="/wiki/IATA_airport_code" title="IATA airport code">IATA</a>: <b>SNN</b>, <a href="/wiki/ICAO_airport_code" title="ICAO airport code">ICAO</a>: <b>EINN</b>) is an <a href="/wiki/International_airport" title="International airport">international airport</a> located in <a href="/wiki/County_Clare" title="County Clare">County Clare</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Republic of Ireland</a>. It is adjacent to the <a href="/wiki/Shannon_Estuary" title="Shannon Estuary">Shannon Estuary</a> and lies halfway between <a href="/wiki/Ennis" title="Ennis">Ennis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Limerick" title="Limerick">Limerick</a>. The airport is the third busiest airport in the Republic of Ireland, and the fifth busiest on <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">the island</a>. In 2018, Shannon Airport handled 1,864,762 passengers, a 6.5% annual increase.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>The airport has a history of pioneering in global aviation with the first transatlantic proving flight in 1945, the world\'s first <a href="/wiki/Duty-free" class="mw-redirect" title="Duty-free">duty-free</a> airport opening in 1947 and Europe\'s first <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> border preclearance facility opening in 1986. It was a busy refuelling stop for many international carriers in the 1960s, making it a gateway between Europe and the Americas.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> At 3,199 metres (10,495&#160;ft), Shannon has the longest runway in Ireland (which allowed it to be a <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle_abort_modes#Emergency_landing_sites" title="Space Shuttle abort modes">designated alternative landing site</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle" title="Space Shuttle">Space Shuttle</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc" role="navigation" <span class="toctext">External links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shannon_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Establishment">Establishment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shannon_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Establishment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In the late 1930s, <a href="/wiki/Transatlantic_flight" title="Transatlantic flight">transatlantic air traffic</a> was dominated by <a href="/wiki/Flying_boat" title="Flying boat">flying boats</a>, and a flying boat terminal was located at <a href="/wiki/Foynes" title="Foynes">Foynes</a> on the south side of the <a href="/wiki/Shannon_Estuary" title="Shannon Estuary">Shannon Estuary</a>. However, it was realised that changing technology would require a permanent <a href="/wiki/Runway" title="Runway">runway</a> and airport.\n</p><p>In 1936, the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Ireland" title="Government of Ireland">Government of Ireland</a> confirmed that it would develop a 3.1-square-kilometre (1.2&#160;sq&#160;mi) site at Rineanna for the country\'s first transatlantic airport. The land on which the airport was to be built was boggy, and on 8 October 1936 work began to drain the land. In July 1939, a <a href="/wiki/SABENA" class="mw-redirect" title="SABENA">SABENA</a> <a href="/wiki/Savoia-Marchetti_S.73" title="Savoia-Marchetti S.73">Savoia-Marchetti S.73</a> from <a href="/wiki/Brussels" title="Brussels">Brussels</a> via <a href="/wiki/Croydon_Airport" title="Croydon Airport">Croydon Airport</a> was the first commercial flight to use the Rineanna airfield.<sup id="cite_ref-GalAd80_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GalAd80-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> \n</p><p>By 1942 a serviceable airport had been established and was named Shannon Airport. <a href="/wiki/British_Overseas_Airways_Corporation" title="British Overseas Airways Corporation">BOAC</a> began scheduled service to <a href="/wiki/Bristol_Airport" title="Bristol Airport">Bristol</a> on February 21, 1942 to provide a land plane connection between England and the flying boat terminal at Foynes. <a href="/wiki/Aer_Lingus" title="Aer Lingus">Aer Lingus</a> began scheduled service to <a href="/wiki/Dublin_Airport" title="Dublin Airport">Dublin</a> in August 1942.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Transatlantic_service">Transatlantic service</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shannon_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Transatlantic service">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>By the end of <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> in 1945, the existing runways at Shannon were extended to allow <a href="/wiki/Transatlantic_flight" title="Transatlantic flight">transatlantic flights</a> to take off. The first Air Services Agreement between Ireland and the United States in 1945 only permitted U.S. airlines to serve Shannon, and only permitted Irish airlines to serve Boston, Chicago and New York.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2021)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>On 16 September 1945 the first transatlantic proving flight, a <a href="/wiki/Pan_American_World_Airways" class="mw-redirect" title="Pan American World Airways">Pan Am</a> <a href="/wiki/Douglas_DC-4" title="Douglas DC-4">DC-4</a>, landed at Shannon from <a href="/wiki/Gander_International_Airport" title="Gander International Airport">Gander</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> On 24 October 1945, the first scheduled transatlantic commercial flight using a land plane, an <a href="/wiki/American_Overseas_Airlines" title="American Overseas Airlines">American Overseas Airlines</a> DC-4, <i>Flagship New England</i>, stopped at the airport on the <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>\xe2\x80\x93<a href="/wiki/Gander,_Newfoundland_and_Labrador" title="Gander, Newfoundland and Labrador">Gander</a>\xe2\x80\x93Shannon\xe2\x80\x93<a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a> route.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Trans_World_Airlines" title="Trans World Airlines">Trans World Airlines</a> began service between New York and Paris via Gander and Shannon on February 5, 1946,<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> and began a Shannon-Dublin tag flight in 1971 after the <a href="/wiki/Civil_Aeronautics_Authority" class="mw-redirect" title="Civil Aeronautics Authority">US Civil Aeronautics Board</a> threatened to ban Aer Lingus from landing in New York. <a href="/wiki/Aer_Lingus" title="Aer Lingus">Aerlinte Eireann</a> began service from Dublin to New York via Shannon on April 28, 1958.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1947, the "Customs Free Airport Act" established Shannon as the world\'s first <a href="/wiki/Duty-free_shop" title="Duty-free shop">duty-free</a> airport, a move promoted by <a href="/wiki/Brendan_O%27Regan" title="Brendan O&#39;Regan">Brendan O\'Regan</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Luxury_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Luxury-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Shannon became a model for other duty-free facilities worldwide.<sup id="cite_ref-Luxury_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Luxury-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> \n</p><p>In 1969, it was announced that a new government agency, <a href="/wiki/Dublin_Airport_Authority" class="mw-redirect" title="Dublin Airport Authority">Aer Rianta</a> (now the Dublin Airport Authority), would be given responsibility for Shannon Airport. Passenger numbers at the airport reached 460,000 that year. With the increase in passengers and the introduction of the <a href="/wiki/Boeing_747" title="Boeing 747">Boeing 747</a>, it was decided that a new enlarged <a href="/wiki/Airport_terminal" title="Airport terminal">terminal</a> was needed. The first commercial operation of a 747 took place in April 1971, while the new terminal officially opened in May that year.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Aeroflot_service">Aeroflot service</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shannon_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Aeroflot service">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Aeroflot" title="Aeroflot">Aeroflot</a> began service to Shannon in September 1975 as a stop between the Soviet Union and other Communist bloc countries such as Cuba and Angola. Aeroflot kept its own fuel storage at Shannon and allowed the airport to sell the Soviet fuel to other airlines.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> \n</p><p>The United States demanded that Ireland suspend Aeroflot\'s Shannon operations following the shooting down of <a href="/wiki/Korean_Air_Lines_Flight_007" title="Korean Air Lines Flight 007">Korean Air Lines Flight 007</a> in 1983, and Ireland temporarily banned Aeroflot later that year.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Nonetheless, the Aeroflot operation returned and developed into a hub by the mid-1990s, with flights to New York, Chicago, Washington, Miami, and Havana, largely using <a href="/wiki/Ilyushin_Il-62" title="Ilyushin Il-62">Ilyushin Il-62</a> aircraft.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> \n</p><p>The cooperation between Aeroflot and Aer Rianta at Shannon also led to a joint venture between the two companies to open duty-free shops at <a href="/wiki/Sheremetyevo_International_Airport" title="Sheremetyevo International Airport">Sheremetyevo Airport</a> in 1987.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>On September 30, 1994 Shannon was the site of the "<a href="/wiki/Boris_Yeltsin_circling_over_Shannon_diplomatic_incident" title="Boris Yeltsin circling over Shannon diplomatic incident">circling over Shannon</a>" diplomatic incident involving Russian President <a href="/wiki/Boris_Yeltsin" title="Boris Yeltsin">Boris Yeltsin</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Decline_of_the_.22Shannon_stopover.22"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Decline_of_the_&quot;Shannon_stopover&quot;">Decline of the "Shannon stopover"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shannon_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Decline of the &quot;Shannon stopover&quot;">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Shannon introduced <a href="/wiki/United_States_border_preclearance" title="United States border preclearance">United States border preclearance</a> in 1986, allowing departing passengers to the US to complete US entry procedures at Shannon. These services were extended to cover private aircraft in 2010, making Shannon the first airport in the world to offer US border preclearance for private aircraft.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1990, the U.S.-Irish bilateral agreement was changed to allow Irish airlines to serve Los Angeles and additional U.S. airlines to serve Dublin via Shannon. An amendment in 1993 allowed airlines to provide direct transatlantic services to Dublin, but 50% of transatlantic flights had to either originate or stop over in Shannon. During the 1990s, the airport began to struggle. However, 1996 saw the beginning of <a href="/wiki/Continental_Airlines" title="Continental Airlines">Continental Airlines</a> flying between Dublin, Shannon and <a href="/wiki/Newark,_New_Jersey" title="Newark, New Jersey">Newark, New Jersey</a>. Shannon began to rebound in the late 1990s with the success of the <a href="/wiki/Economy_of_the_Republic_of_Ireland" title="Economy of the Republic of Ireland">Irish economy</a>, the improving situation in <a href="/wiki/The_Troubles" title="The Troubles">Northern Ireland</a> and an influx of American tourists. By the end of the decade Shannon had passenger numbers of 2.2 million and in the year 2000, a new <a href="/wiki/Irish_pound" title="Irish pound">\xc2\xa3</a>40 million terminal extension was opened.\n</p><p>In 2005, an agreement was reached regarding a transitional period. Beginning in November 2006 and ending in April 2008, the agreement gradually eliminated restrictions on cargo services. For passenger service, it reduced the stopover requirement and allowed Irish airlines to serve three additional U.S. destinations. Furthermore, it was agreed that at the end of this period, no restrictions would be placed on scheduled services between any airport in the one country to any airport in the other.\n</p><p>In 2007, the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> and the U.S. announced that an agreement had been reached on an <a href="/wiki/Freedoms_of_the_air" title="Freedoms of the air">open skies</a> aviation policy (<a href="/wiki/EU%E2%80%93US_Open_Skies_Agreement" title="EU\xe2\x80\x93US Open Skies Agreement">EU\xe2\x80\x93US Open Skies Agreement</a>). The agreement came into effect from 30 March 2008, leading to the complete abolition of the Shannon Stopover, although this would have happened under the 2005 agreement anyway.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/British_Airways" title="British Airways">British Airways</a> operated <a href="/wiki/Business_class" title="Business class">business class</a>-only flights between <a href="/wiki/London_City_Airport" title="London City Airport">London City Airport</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy_International_Airport" title="John F. Kennedy International Airport">John F. Kennedy International Airport</a> in New York between 2009 and 2020. Of these, the westbound flights stopped for fuel in Shannon to allow them to take off from the short runway of the London City Airport, which is located in the <a href="/wiki/London_Docklands" title="London Docklands">London Docklands</a> area. This stop also allowed passengers to clear US Customs and Immigration in Shannon rather than at JFK. Introduced in 2009, two flights per day, one of which carried the flight number BA1, were operated using <a href="/wiki/Airbus_A318" title="Airbus A318">A318</a> aircraft capable of operating steep approaches.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> The flights were halved in 2017, and in 2020, amid their suspension due to the <a href="/wiki/COVID-19_pandemic" title="COVID-19 pandemic">COVID-19 pandemic</a>, it was announced that the flights would not be resumed.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2000s">2000s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shannon_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 2000s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Ryanair" title="Ryanair">Ryanair</a> increased services and passenger numbers at the airport through 2008. In 2007, Shannon carried 3.2 million passengers. However, after a disagreement with the <a href="/wiki/Dublin_Airport_Authority" class="mw-redirect" title="Dublin Airport Authority">Dublin Airport Authority</a> (DAA) in 2008, Ryanair announced that the number of based aircraft would reduce from four to one and 150 jobs would be lost. Services were cut by 75% and 32 Ryanair routes from the airport were reduced to eight.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/CityJet" title="CityJet">CityJet</a> launched a twice-daily route to <a href="/wiki/Paris_Charles_de_Gaulle_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Paris Charles de Gaulle Airport">Paris Charles de Gaulle Airport</a> in 2008 when <a href="/wiki/Aer_Lingus" title="Aer Lingus">Aer Lingus</a> closed its London <a href="/wiki/Heathrow_Airport" title="Heathrow Airport">Heathrow</a> flights. The company based an <a href="/wiki/Avro_RJ" class="mw-redirect" title="Avro RJ">Avro RJ</a>85 at Shannon. More services were under consideration, including a route to London City Airport; however CityJet pulled out of Shannon in October 2009 after Aer Lingus reinstated its Heathrow flights.\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Independent_operation.2C_2012_to_present"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Independent_operation,_2012_to_present">Independent operation, 2012 to present</span><span On 31 December 2012 at 11:59&#160;pm, Shannon Airport became a publicly owned commercial airport and is now operated and run by the Shannon Airport Authority plc. Shannon announced a target in 2012 to grow its passenger numbers to 2.5 million annually within five years. However, Shannon has fallen short of its stated targeted figure with just 1.74 million flying through the airport in 2017.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In March 2013, the new company appointed <a href="/wiki/Neil_Pakey" title="Neil Pakey">Neil Pakey</a> as its first CEO. Traffic figures for June 2013 report an 8% increase on the previous year, the first time a traffic increase has been recorded in three years. On 21 March 2013, <a href="/wiki/Ryanair" title="Ryanair">Ryanair</a> announced a new twice-weekly route to <a href="/wiki/Alicante" title="Alicante">Alicante</a>, Spain to begin on 5 June for the summer months. That brought Shannon\'s total to 33 seasonal scheduled summer routes. In August 2013 Aer Lingus announced a 1x weekly service to <a href="/wiki/Lanzarote" title="Lanzarote">Lanzarote</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">Canary Islands</a>, Spain every Saturday during the winter months using an A320. In October 2013, United Airlines confirmed it will increase capacity by 88% on its Shannon-Chicago route for 2014. In late 2013, Aer Lingus announced 2 new routes to <a href="/wiki/M%C3%A1laga" title="M\xc3\xa1laga">M\xc3\xa1laga</a>, Spain (two weekly) and to <a href="/wiki/Bristol" title="Bristol">Bristol</a>, UK (one daily). Ryanair also announced 8 new routes from Shannon to continental Europe. The new routes began from the start of April 2014, and a second Boeing 737-800 was based at Shannon to accommodate the extra 300,000 passengers a year it would bring in. The destinations announced were <a href="/wiki/Berlin_Sch%C3%B6nefeld_Airport" title="Berlin Sch\xc3\xb6nefeld Airport">Berlin Schonefeld</a>, <a href="/wiki/Beauvais%E2%80%93Till%C3%A9_Airport" title="Beauvais\xe2\x80\x93Till\xc3\xa9 Airport">Beauvais</a>, <a href="/wiki/Memmingen_Airport" title="Memmingen Airport">Memmingen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Warsaw_Modlin_Airport" title="Warsaw Modlin Airport">Warsaw Modlin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Krak%C3%B3w_John_Paul_II_International_Airport" title="Krak\xc3\xb3w John Paul II International Airport">Krak\xc3\xb3w</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nice_C%C3%B4te_d%27Azur_Airport" title="Nice C\xc3\xb4te d&#39;Azur Airport">Nice</a>, <a href="/wiki/Faro_Airport" title="Faro Airport">Faro</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Fuerteventura_Airport" title="Fuerteventura Airport">Fuerteventura</a>.\n</p><p>On 4 July 2014, the "Bank of Ireland Runway Night Run" featured 1,200 people running along Shannon\'s runway to raise money for charity.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In late 2014, Aer Lingus Regional operator <a href="/wiki/Stobart_Air" title="Stobart Air">Stobart Air</a> said that they would close down their Shannon base in early 2015. They returned in June 2015 operating 6 flights weekly Birmingham service followed by 6 flights weekly <a href="/wiki/Edinburgh" title="Edinburgh">Edinburgh</a> service. In late 2015, they announced a new CEO for Shannon, Matthew Thomas. Ryanair announced that it will be ending its Paris and Memmingen routes in late 2016, and it also reduced its <a href="/wiki/Manchester" title="Manchester">Manchester</a> and <a href="/wiki/London_Stansted" class="mw-redirect" title="London Stansted">London Stansted</a> routes. Ryanair is aiming for 720,000 passengers in Summer 2017 even though that they were close to 800,000 in Summer 2016. In October 2016, SAS announced a new route to Stockholm from 1 August 2017 to 7 October  2017. Shortly after that, <a href="/wiki/Lufthansa" title="Lufthansa">Lufthansa</a> announced a weekly service to Frankfurt running from April to October in 2017. In September 2017 Ryanair announced a new Route to Reus. It will run through summer 2018 operating 2x weekly (Tuesdays and Saturdays) replacing the route to Berlin. The same day, Air Canada announced a new 4x weekly service to Toronto with the Boeing 737 MAX. In February 2018, Ryanair announced it would resume flights to Bristol and Liverpool from May 2018. In late 2018, Ryanair announced a new 2x weekly service to Ibiza, and the resumption of flights to East Midlands, both beginning in April 2019. The East Midlands, Bristol and Ibiza flights are all being ceased at the end of the 2019 summer season due to fears of Brexit and the 737 MAX groundings.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Due to the 737 MAX groundings, Air Canada and Norwegian Airlines suspended their routes to Shannon. This reduced the number of passenger by 120,000. On 23 October 2019, Lauda announced that they will launch a twice weekly flight to Shannon from their base in Vienna operating every Wednesday and Saturday. The airport handled 1,864,762 passengers in 2018. This number is the highest passenger numbers since gaining independence from the DAA.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Usage_for_military_stopovers">Usage for military stopovers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shannon_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Usage for military stopovers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Shannon Airport has a history of foreign military use. A large part of its business in recent years has been military stopovers, currently almost all American; however, the airport was also frequently used by the Soviet military until the 1990s, since Ireland, having a traditional policy of military neutrality, was not a member of <a href="/wiki/NATO" title="NATO">NATO</a>. There were some restrictions, such as being carrying no arms, ammunition, or explosives, and that the flights in question did not form part of military exercises or operations. Shannon saw military transports throughout the <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a> and during the first <a href="/wiki/Gulf_War" title="Gulf War">Gulf War</a>.\n</p><p>In the aftermath of 11 September attacks, the Irish government offered the use of Shannon to the <a href="/wiki/Federal_government_of_the_United_States" title="Federal government of the United States">US government</a>. When the United States invaded <a href="/wiki/Iraq" title="Iraq">Iraq</a> in 2003, the government still allowed <a href="/wiki/United_States_Armed_Forces" title="United States Armed Forces">United States Armed Forces</a> to use the airport. This caused much controversy and was the subject of protests and a challenge brought to the <a href="/wiki/High_Court_(Ireland)" title="High Court (Ireland)">High Court</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> As of November 2008, approximately 1.2 million troops have passed through Shannon since the beginning of the <a href="/wiki/Iraq_War" title="Iraq War">Iraq War</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> In 2012\xe2\x80\x932013, the military flight contracts are held by <a href="/wiki/Omni_Air_International" title="Omni Air International">Omni Air International</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>On 6 December 2005, the <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a> programme <i><a href="/wiki/Newsnight" title="Newsnight">Newsnight</a></i> alleged that Shannon was used on at least 33 occasions by United States <a href="/wiki/Central_Intelligence_Agency" title="Central Intelligence Agency">Central Intelligence Agency</a> (CIA) <a href="/wiki/Rendition_aircraft#Shannon_Airport,_Ireland" title="Rendition aircraft">flights</a>, thought to be part of a US policy called <a href="/wiki/Extraordinary_rendition_by_the_United_States" class="mw-redirect" title="Extraordinary rendition by the United States">extraordinary rendition</a>. <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> reported the number to be 33, though referring to "Ireland" rather than Shannon, while <a href="/wiki/Amnesty_International" title="Amnesty International">Amnesty International</a> has alleged the number of flights to be 50. <a href="/wiki/Casement_Aerodrome" title="Casement Aerodrome">Casement Aerodrome</a> has seen similar claims. The United States and Ireland have denied these allegations.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Facilities">Facilities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shannon_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Facilities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Shannon2.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/Shannon2.jpg/220px-Shannon2.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/Shannon2.jpg/330px-Shannon2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/Shannon2.jpg/440px-Shannon2.jpg 2x" data-file-width="800" data-file-height="533" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Shannon2.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The United States immigration booths at Shannon prior to the opening of the new Customs and Border Protection facilities (November 2008)</div></div></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shannon_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The current airport terminal was opened on 27 March 2000 by then Minister of Transport <a href="/wiki/Mary_O%27Rourke" title="Mary O&#39;Rourke">Mary O\'Rourke</a>. title="Galway">Galway</a>. <a href="/wiki/Dublin" title="Dublin">Dublin</a> is approximately 223 kilometres (139&#160;mi) away and <a href="/wiki/Cork_(city)" title="Cork (city)">Cork</a> is around All car parks operate 24 hours and are regularly patrolled by <a href="/wiki/Airport_Police_(Ireland)" title="Airport Police (Ireland)">Airport Police</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rail_link">Rail link</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shannon_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Rail link">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>For many years a rail link to the airport (as a spur from the nearby <a href="/wiki/Western_Railway_Corridor" title="Western Railway Corridor">Limerick\xe2\x80\x93Ennis</a> line) has been proposed,<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> but nothing has materialised. The nearest major stations (<a href="/wiki/Ennis_railway_station" title="Ennis railway station">Ennis railway station</a> and <a href="/wiki/Limerick_railway_station" class="mw-redirect" title="Limerick railway station">Limerick railway station</a>) require bus or taxi to connect with. <a href="/wiki/Sixmilebridge_railway_station" title="Sixmilebridge railway station">Sixmilebridge</a> is the nearest station to the airport. <sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Accidents_and_incidents">Accidents and incidents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shannon_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Accidents and incidents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Due to the location of Shannon, it receives a large number of emergency stopovers.\n</p>\n<ul><li>16 July 1943 \xe2\x80\x93 <a href="/wiki/British_Overseas_Airways_Corporation" title="British Overseas Airways Corporation">British Overseas Airways Corporation</a> de Havilland DH91 <i>Fortuna</i> crash-landed short of the Runway 14 threshold. There were no injuries.</li>\n<li>18 June 1946 \xe2\x80\x93 an <a href="/wiki/Aer_Lingus" title="Aer Lingus">Aer Lingus</a> <a href="/wiki/Douglas_DC-3" title="Douglas DC-3">Douglas DC-3</a>, <i>Charlie Alpha</i>, on a domestic flight from Rineanna to Dublin crashed shortly after takeoff with only minor injuries reported.</li>\n<li>28 December 1946 \xe2\x80\x93 <a href="/wiki/TWA" class="mw-redirect" title="TWA">TWA</a> <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_Constellation" title="Lockheed Constellation">Lockheed Constellation</a> NC86505 <a href="/wiki/TWA_Flight_6963" title="TWA Flight 6963">Flight 6963</a> crashed attempting to land at the airport.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>15 April 1948 \xe2\x80\x93 <a href="/wiki/Pan_Am_Flight_1-10" title="Pan Am Flight 1-10">Pan Am Flight 1-10</a>, a Lockheed Constellation crashed attempting to land at the airport. 30 Passengers and crew died in the post crash fire. There was 1 survivor.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>5 September 1954 \xe2\x80\x93 Lockheed Super Constellation PH-LKY, <a href="/wiki/KLM_Flight_633" title="KLM Flight 633">KLM Flight 633</a> from Amsterdam to New York City, using Shannon as a refuelling stop, crashed just after takeoff into a mudbank adjacent to the airport. 28 people on board died. There were 28 survivors.</li>\n<li>15 July 1956 \xe2\x80\x93 a <a href="/wiki/Swissair" title="Swissair">Swissair</a> Convair CV-440-11 crashed on approach to the airport due to pilot error. The aircraft was on its delivery flight from San Diego, California to Z\xc3\xbcrich via New York, Gander and Shannon. The crash killed all four crew on board.</li>\n<li>14 August 1958 \xe2\x80\x93 Lockheed Super Constellation <a href="/wiki/KLM_Flight_607-E" title="KLM Flight 607-E">KLM Flight 607-E</a> from Amsterdam to New York crashed into the Atlantic Ocean after a refuelling stop at Shannon, killing all 99 passengers and crew on board.</li>\n<li>22 June 1959 - Pan American DC-6B N5026K, Clipper Panama, caught fire and burned on the runway as it was preparing to take off. The aircraft was preparing for a flight to New York. The number 1 propeller blade on the number 4 engine detached due to metal fatigue.  The imbalance on the spinning propeller caused the engine to detach from the engine housing and a fire erupted on the wing. The 8 occupants escaped from the aircraft which was subsequently destroyed by fire.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>26 February 1960 \xe2\x80\x93 an <a href="/wiki/Alitalia-Linee_Aeree_Italiane" class="mw-redirect" title="Alitalia-Linee Aeree Italiane">Alitalia</a> <a href="/wiki/Douglas_DC-7" title="Douglas DC-7">Douglas DC-7</a>C I-DUVO crashed after the port wingtip struck the wall of a cemetery  after taking off from the airport. The aircraft had made an unscheduled refuelling stop en route from Rome to New York. The aircraft had not gained sufficient height to clear the hilltop where the cemetery is located. The aircraft then crashed in the field where it exploded and disintegrated, scattering wreckage over a wide area. 34 people out of 52 passengers and crew on board died as a result of the accident. One cabin crew member, a steward who was seated at the rear of the aircraft survived the accident.</li>\n<li>26 March 1961 - An Eagle Airways DC-6A G-APOM was damaged and subsequently destroyed by fire during a touch and go training exercise.  The aircraft had landed on runway 24 and was accelerating for takeoff when the landing gear was inadvertently raised before the aircraft became airborne. The DC-6 settled onto the runway, veered to the left off the paved surface and onto the grass.  A fire began on the port wing and eventually consumed and destroyed the aircraft.  There were no injuries among the 6 crew who evacuated the aircraft.</li>\n<li>10 September 1961 \xe2\x80\x93 a <a href="/wiki/1961_President_Airlines_Douglas_DC-6_crash" title="1961 President Airlines Douglas DC-6 crash">President Airlines Douglas DC-6 crashed</a> into the river Shannon after taking off from the airport on a flight to Gander. The disaster killed all 83 passengers and crew, one of the worst air disasters in Ireland\'s history. 1 cabin crew member survived the crash but died a few hours after being rescued.</li>\n<li>30 September 1977 \xe2\x80\x93 an Interconair Bristol 175 Britannia 253 crashed to the left of runway 24 after severe vibration on the approach. The left wing broke off and caught fire but none of the six crew were injured in the crash.</li>\n<li>20 June 1979 \xe2\x80\x93 <a href="/wiki/American_Airlines_Flight_293" title="American Airlines Flight 293">American Airlines Flight 293</a> landed in Shannon after being hijacked.</li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_issues">Environmental issues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shannon_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Environmental issues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>As Shannon Airport has been built adjacent to the <a href="/wiki/Shannon_Estuary" title="Shannon Estuary">Shannon Estuary</a>, it is likely to have problems with <a href="/wiki/Sea_level_rise" title="Sea level rise">rising sea levels</a> and high waters in the <a href="/wiki/River_Shannon" title="River Shannon">Shannon</a>. The government is working on plans to protect both the airport and <a href="/wiki/Shannon,_County_Clare" title="Shannon, County Clare">the town</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
