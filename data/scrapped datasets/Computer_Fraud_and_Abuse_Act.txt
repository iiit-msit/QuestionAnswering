href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>\n\t\t<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#searchInput">Jump to search</a>\n\t\t<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>The <b>Computer Fraud and Abuse Act</b> (<b>CFAA</b>) is a <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> <a href="/wiki/Cybersecurity" class="mw-redirect" title="Cybersecurity">cybersecurity</a> bill that was enacted in 1986 as an amendment to existing computer fraud law (<a href="/wiki/Title_18_of_the_United_States_Code" title="Title 18 of the United States Code">18&#160;U.S.C.</a>&#160;<a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/18/1030">\xc2\xa7&#160;1030</a>), which had been included in the <a href="/wiki/Comprehensive_Crime_Control_Act_of_1984" title="Comprehensive Crime Control Act of 1984">Comprehensive Crime Control Act of 1984</a>. The law prohibits accessing a computer without authorization, or in excess of authorization.<sup id="cite_ref-FirstEnacted_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FirstEnacted-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Prior to computer-specific criminal laws, computer crimes were prosecuted as <a href="/wiki/Mail_and_wire_fraud" title="Mail and wire fraud">mail and wire fraud</a>, but the applying law was often insufficient.\n</p><p>The original 1984 bill was enacted in response to concern that computer-related crimes might go unpunished.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The House Committee Report to the original computer crime bill characterized the 1983 techno-thriller film <i><a href="/wiki/WarGames" title="WarGames">WarGames</a></i>\xe2\x80\x94in which a young teenager (played by <a href="/wiki/Matthew_Broderick" title="Matthew Broderick">Matthew Broderick</a>) from <a href="/wiki/Seattle" title="Seattle">Seattle</a> breaks into a U.S. military supercomputer programmed to predict possible outcomes of nuclear war and unwittingly almost starts World War III\xe2\x80\x94as "a realistic representation of the automatic dialing and access capabilities of the personal computer."<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The CFAA was written to extend existing <a href="/wiki/Tort_law" class="mw-redirect" title="Tort law">tort law</a> to <a href="/wiki/Intangible_property" title="Intangible property">intangible property</a>, while, in theory, limiting federal jurisdiction to cases "with a compelling federal interest\xe2\x80\x94i.e., where computers of the federal government or certain financial institutions are involved or where the crime itself is interstate in nature.", but its broad definitions have spilled over into <a href="/wiki/Contract_law" class="mw-redirect" title="Contract law">contract law</a>. (see "Protected Computer", below). In addition to amending a number of the provisions in the original <i>section 1030</i>, the CFAA also criminalized additional computer-related acts. Provisions addressed the distribution of <a href="/wiki/Malware" title="Malware">malicious code</a> and <a href="/wiki/Denial-of-service_attack" title="Denial-of-service attack">denial of service attacks</a>. Congress also included in the CFAA a provision criminalizing trafficking in passwords and similar items.<sup id="cite_ref-FirstEnacted_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FirstEnacted-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Since then, the Act has been amended a number of times\xe2\x80\x94in 1989, 1994, 1996, in 2001 by the <a href="/wiki/USA_PATRIOT_Act" class="mw-redirect" title="USA PATRIOT Act">USA PATRIOT Act</a>, 2002, and in 2008 by the Identity Theft Enforcement and Restitution Act. With each amendment of the law, the types of conduct that fell within its reach were extended.\n</p><p>In January 2015 <a href="/wiki/Barack_Obama" title="Barack Obama">Barack Obama</a> proposed expanding the CFAA and the <a href="/wiki/RICO_Act" class="mw-redirect" title="RICO Act">RICO Act</a> in his <i>Modernizing Law Enforcement Authorities to Combat Cyber Crime</i> proposal.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/DEF_CON" title="DEF CON">DEF CON</a> organizer and <a href="/wiki/Cloudflare" title="Cloudflare">Cloudflare</a> researcher Marc Rogers, Senator <a href="/wiki/Ron_Wyden" title="Ron Wyden">Ron Wyden</a>, and Representative <a href="/wiki/Zoe_Lofgren" title="Zoe Lofgren">Zoe Lofgren</a> have stated opposition to this on the grounds it will make many regular Internet activities illegal, and moves further away from what they were trying to accomplish with <a href="#Aaron_Swartz">Aaron\'s Law</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The text near this tag is dated. (April 2015)">needs update</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc" role="navigation" aria-labelledby="mw-toc-heading"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Computer_Fraud_and_Abuse_Act&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Protected computers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The only computers, in theory, covered by the CFAA are defined as "<a href="/wiki/Protected_computer" title="Protected computer">protected computers</a>". They are defined under section <span class="plainlinksneverexpand"><a href="/wiki/Title_18_of_the_United_States_Code" title="Title 18 of the United States Code">18 U.S.C.</a>&#160;<a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/18/1030#e_2">\xc2\xa7&#160;1030(e)(2)</a></span> to mean a computer:\n</p>\n<ul><li>exclusively for the use of a <a href="/wiki/Financial_institution" title="Financial institution">financial institution</a> or the United States Government, or any computer, when the conduct constituting the offense affects the computer\'s use by or for the financial institution or the government; or</li>\n<li>which is used in or affecting interstate or foreign commerce or communication, including a computer located outside the United States that is used in a manner that affects interstate or foreign commerce or communication of the United States ...</li></ul>\n<p>In practice, any ordinary computer has come under the jurisdiction of the law, including cellphones, due to the interstate nature of most Internet communication.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criminal_offenses_under_the_Act">Criminal offenses under the Act</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span id="Notable_cases_and_decisions_referring_to_the_Act">Notable cases and decisions referring to the Act</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Computer_Fraud_and_Abuse_Act&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Notable cases and decisions referring to the Act">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The Computer Fraud and Abuse Act is both a criminal law and a statute that creates a <a href="/wiki/Private_right_of_action" class="mw-redirect" title="Private right of action">private right of action</a>, allowing <a href="/wiki/Damages" title="Damages">compensation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Injunction" title="Injunction">injunctive</a> or other <a href="/wiki/Equitable_relief" class="mw-redirect" title="Equitable relief">equitable relief</a> to anyone harmed by a violation of this law. These provisions have allowed private companies to sue disloyal employees for damages for the misappropriation of confidential information (<a href="/wiki/Trade_secret" title="Trade secret">trade secrets</a>).\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Criminal_cases">Criminal cases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Computer_Fraud_and_Abuse_Act&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Criminal cases">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<ul><li><i><a href="/wiki/United_States_v._Morris_(1991)" title="United States v. Morris (1991)">United States v. Morris (1991)</a></i>, 928 F.2d 504 (2d Cir. 1991), decided March 7, 1991. After the release of the <a href="/wiki/Morris_worm" title="Morris worm">Morris worm</a>, an early <a href="/wiki/Computer_worm" title="Computer worm">computer worm</a>, its creator was convicted under the Act for causing damage and gaining unauthorized access to "federal interest" computers. (December 2019)">full citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>In addition to Lofgren\'s efforts, Representatives <a href="/wiki/Darrell_Issa" title="Darrell Issa">Darrell Issa</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jared_Polis" title="Jared Polis">Jared Polis</a> (also on the <a href="/wiki/House_Judiciary_Committee" class="mw-redirect" title="House Judiciary Committee">House Judiciary Committee</a>) raised questions<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous.
