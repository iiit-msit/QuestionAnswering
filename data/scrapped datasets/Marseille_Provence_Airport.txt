id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In the 1920s and 1930s, Marignane was one of France\'s main points of operation for flying boats. It even briefly served as a terminal for <a href="/wiki/Pan_American_World_Airways" class="mw-redirect" title="Pan American World Airways">Pan American World Airways</a> <i>Clipper</i> flying boats.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Other flying boat operators were <a href="/wiki/A%C3%A9ropostale_(aviation)" title="A\xc3\xa9ropostale (aviation)">A\xc3\xa9ropostale</a> and <a href="/wiki/Air_Union" title="Air Union">Air Union</a>, the latter moving over from <a href="/wiki/Antibes" title="Antibes">Antibes</a> in 1931. Marignane was also a production site for hydroplanes by <a href="/wiki/Lior%C3%A9_et_Olivier" title="Lior\xc3\xa9 et Olivier">Lior\xc3\xa9 et Olivier</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Antoine_de_Saint-Exupery" class="mw-redirect" title="Antoine de Saint-Exupery">Antoine de Saint-Exupery</a> describes turning back to Marignane airport with a fuel leak in chapter 8 of \xe2\x80\x9c<a href="/wiki/Wind,_Sand_and_Stars" title="Wind, Sand and Stars">Wind, Sand and Stars</a>\xe2\x80\x9d, before setting out again for Tunis, and the fateful event that informed his later description of the crash-landing in his best-known book, \xe2\x80\x9c<a href="/wiki/The_Little_Prince" title="The Little Prince">The Little Prince</a>\xe2\x80\x9d.\n</p><p>In September 2006, the airport opened its new terminal MP2 for budget airlines.  In 2013 the airport expanded its shopping and dining options, with 30 new shops and restaurants, among which is the first <a href="/wiki/Burger_King" title="Burger King">Burger King</a> restaurant in France since 1997.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<ul><li>The airport is the main fixed wing base of <a href="/wiki/S%C3%A9curit%C3%A9_Civile" title="S\xc3\xa9curit\xc3\xa9 Civile">S\xc3\xa9curit\xc3\xa9 Civile</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Aerial_firefighting" title="Aerial firefighting">aerial firefighting</a> division of the <a href="/wiki/French_Ministry_of_the_Interior" class="mw-redirect" title="French Ministry of the Interior">French Ministry of the Interior</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>** This S\xc3\xa9curit\xc3\xa9 Civile base has now moved to nearby <a href="/wiki/N%C3%AEmes%E2%80%93Al%C3%A8s%E2%80%93Camargue%E2%80%93C%C3%A9vennes_Airport" class="mw-headline" id="Accidents_and_incidents">Accidents and incidents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<ul><li>On 4 February 1948, <a href="/wiki/SNCASE_Languedoc" class="mw-redirect" title="SNCASE Languedoc">SNCASE Languedoc P/7</a> F-BATK of <a href="/wiki/Air_France" title="Air France">Air France</a> was damaged beyond economical repair.<sup id="cite_ref-ASN040248_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ASN040248-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>On 30 July 1950, SNCASE Languedoc P/7 F-BCUI of Air France was damaged beyond economic repair when its undercarriage collapsed on landing.<sup id="cite_ref-ASN300750_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ASN300750-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>On 6 February 1989, Inter Cargo Service Flight 3132, operated by <a href="/wiki/Vickers_Vanguard" title="Vickers Vanguard">Vickers Vanguard</a> F-GEJE, crashed on takeoff.  Three crew died; no passengers were being carried.<sup id="cite_ref-ASN060289_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ASN060289-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>On 26 December 1994, <a href="/wiki/Air_France_Flight_8969" title="Air France Flight 8969">Air France Flight 8969</a>, with 236 people aboard, arrived in Marseille after being hijacked by four young men of the Armed Islamic Group (GIA) at <a href="/wiki/Houari_Boumediene_Airport" title="Houari Boumediene Airport">Houari Boumediene Airport</a> in <a href="/wiki/Algiers" title="Algiers">Algiers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Algeria" title="Algeria">Algeria</a> two days prior. After 15 hours on the ground and a breakdown in negotiations, the French special forces <a href="/wiki/GIGN" title="GIGN">GIGN</a> stormed the aircraft. In the ensuing firefight, all four hijackers were killed while 3 crew, 13 passengers, and 9 GIGN operatives were injured. The <a href="/wiki/Airbus_A300B2-1C" class="mw-redirect" title="Airbus A300B2-1C">Airbus A300B2-1C</a> F-GBEC was written off.</li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See
