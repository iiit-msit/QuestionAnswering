force</td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p>The <b>Spam Act 2003</b> (Cth) is an Act passed by the <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_Australia" title="Parliament of Australia">Australian Parliament</a> in 2003 to regulate commercial <a href="/wiki/E-mail" class="mw-redirect" title="E-mail">e-mail</a> and other types of commercial <a href="/w/index.php?title=Electronic_message_(disambiguation)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Electronic message (disambiguation) (page does not exist)">electronic messages</a>. The Act restricts <a href="/wiki/Spam_(electronic)" class="mw-redirect" title="Spam (electronic)">spam</a>, especially <a href="/wiki/E-mail_spam" class="mw-redirect" title="E-mail spam">e-mail spam</a> and some types of phone spam, as well as <a href="/wiki/E-mail_address_harvesting" class="mw-redirect" title="E-mail address harvesting">e-mail address harvesting</a>. However, there are broad exemptions.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The first portions of the Act came into effect on 12 December 2003, the day the act received <a href="/wiki/Royal_Assent" class="mw-redirect" title="Royal Assent">Royal Assent</a>, with the remaining sections of the Act coming into force on 10 April 2004. The Act was originally enforced by the Australian Communications Authority, which in 2005 merged into the <a href="/wiki/Australian_Communications_and_Media_Authority" title="Australian Communications and Media Authority">Australian Communications and Media Authority</a> (ACMA).\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc" role="navigation" aria-labelledby="mw-toc-heading"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="Outline">Outline</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spam_Act_2003&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Outline">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The key points of the act provide that:\n</p>\n<ul><li>Unsolicited commercial electronic messages must not be sent unless it is a designated commercial electronic message defined at Schedule 1 of the act.</li>\n<li>Commercial electronic messages must include information about the individual or organisation who authorised the sending of the message.</li>\n<li>Commercial electronic messages must contain a functional unsubscribe facility.</li>\n<li>Address\xe2\x80\x91harvesting software must not be supplied, acquired or used.</li>\n<li>An electronic address list produced using address\xe2\x80\x91harvesting software must not be supplied, acquired or used.</li>\n<li>The main remedies for breaches of this Act are civil penalties and injunctions.</li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Significant_cases">Significant cases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a Clarity1 (2006)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spam_Act_2003&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Australian Communications Authority v Clarity1 (2006)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In ACA v Clarity1 (2006),<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Robert_Nicholson_(judge)" title="Robert Nicholson (judge)">Robert Nicholson</a>, J looked at the respondent\xe2\x80\x99s key defence, being retrospective application of provisions under the Act relating to the acquisition and use of harvested address lists. He noted specifically that lists gathered or acquired prior to the Act coming into force are still subject to the legislation. He also struck down the respondent\'s defence that he had obtained consent to use the gathered addresses for the defined purpose, and also noted a lack of compliance with the provisions of the Act requiring the provision of a functional unsubscribe facility.\n</p>\n<ul><li><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.comcourts.gov.au/file/Federal/P/WAD155/2005/actions">Case
