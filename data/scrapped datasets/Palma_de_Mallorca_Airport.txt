language">Catalan</a>: <i lang="ca">Aeroport de Palma de Mallorca</i>, Spanish: <i lang="es">Aeropuerto de Palma de Mallorca</i>; <a href="/wiki/IATA_airport_code" title="IATA airport code">IATA</a>: <b>PMI</b>, <a href="/wiki/ICAO_airport_code" title="ICAO airport code">ICAO</a>: <b>LEPA</b>; also known as <b>Son Sant Joan Airport</b> or <i>Aeroport de Son Sant Joan</i>) is an international airport located 8&#160;km (5.0&#160;mi) east<sup id="cite_ref-AIP_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AIP-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> of <a href="/wiki/Palma,_Mallorca" class="mw-redirect" title="Palma, Mallorca">Palma, Mallorca</a>, Spain, adjacent to the village of Can Pastilla. The airport on the Balearic Islands is Spain\'s <a href="/wiki/List_of_the_busiest_airports_in_Spain" title="List of the busiest airports in Spain">third largest airport</a><sup id="cite_ref-aenapax_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aenapax-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> after <a href="/wiki/Adolfo_Su%C3%A1rez_Madrid%E2%80%93Barajas_Airport" title="Adolfo Su\xc3\xa1rez Madrid\xe2\x80\x93Barajas Airport">Madrid\xe2\x80\x93Barajas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Barcelona_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Barcelona Airport">Barcelona-El Prat</a>. Palma de Mallorca was used by 29.7&#160;million passengers in 2019.<sup id="cite_ref-aena.es_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aena.es-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The airport is the main base for the Spanish carrier <a href="/wiki/Air_Europa" title="Air Europa">Air Europa</a> and also a focus airport for <a href="/wiki/Ryanair" title="Ryanair">Ryanair</a>, <a href="/wiki/EasyJet" title="EasyJet">EasyJet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vueling" title="Vueling">Vueling</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jet2.com" title="Jet2.com">Jet2.com</a>. The airport shares runways with the nearby Son Sant Joan Air Force Base, operated by the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Air_Force" title="Spanish Air Force">Spanish Air Force</a>.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" href="/w/index.php?title=Palma_de_Mallorca_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_years">Early years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Palma_de_Mallorca_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The interest of the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Spain" title="Government of Spain">Spanish Government</a> in developing <a href="/wiki/Airmail" title="Airmail">airmail</a> during the first decades of the 20th century, led to a study of the possibility of establishing an air mail line to the <a href="/wiki/Balearic_Islands" title="Balearic Islands">Balearic Islands</a>. Finally, in 1921, the company Aeromar\xc3\xadtima Mallorquina established the postal line Barcelona - Palma, which used seaplanes in the port of Palma de Mallorca. Before the creation of this airline, trials were complete in two flat fields: <b>Son Sant Joan</b> and <a href="/wiki/Son_Bonet_Aerodrome" title="Son Bonet Aerodrome">Son Bonet</a>, both of which were later chosen for the construction of aerodromes.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1934, the company Aero-Taxi de Mallorca was created with the intention of starting tourist flights to the island, establishing a <a href="/wiki/Flight_training" title="Flight training">flight school</a> in Son Sant Joan. A year later, another one was founded in Son Bonet.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In May 1935 the company <a href="/wiki/LAPE" title="LAPE">LAPE</a>, L\xc3\xadneas A\xc3\xa9reas Postales Espa\xc3\xb1olas (Spanish Postal Airlines), a predecessor of <a href="/wiki/Iberia_(airline)" title="Iberia (airline)">Iberia</a>; was founded. A month later, in <a href="/wiki/August_1935" title="August 1935">August</a>, the first regular air route between <a href="/wiki/Madrid" title="Madrid">Madrid</a> and Palma, stopping at <a href="/wiki/Valencia" title="Valencia">Valencia</a>, was created using the Son Sant Joan aerodrome. A year later, this line was replaced by a new one connecting Palma and Barcelona. <a href="/wiki/1938" title="1938">Three years later</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lufthansa" title="Lufthansa">Lufthansa</a> and <a href="/wiki/Iberia_(airline)" title="Iberia (airline)">Iberia</a> established new lines in Son Bonet,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> while Son Sant Joan was beginning to be used by the military. Through the years, Son Bonet became the main civilian airport in the island, while the creation of Son Sant Joan Air Force Base limited further civilian enterprises at the aerodrome.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1954, the runway was enlarged and paved to enable the operation of <a href="/wiki/North_American_F-86_Sabre" title="North American F-86 Sabre">F-86 Sabre</a> fighters, which also meant the diversion of the Palma - <a href="/wiki/Llucmajor" title="Llucmajor">Llucmajor</a> road. During those years, the first paved taxiways and aprons were built, while Son Bonet received the first big groups of European tourists through the airlines <a href="/wiki/British_European_Airways" title="British European Airways">BEA</a>, <a href="/wiki/Air_France" title="Air France">Air France</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aviaco" title="Aviaco">Aviaco</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_creation_of_the_international_airport">The creation of the international airport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Palma_de_Mallorca_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: The creation of the international airport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The increase in traffic, and the inability to enlarge Son Bonet, led the authors of the 1958 National Airport Plan to propose building a large civilian airport near the Son Sant Joan airbase. The National Airport Council approved this plan the following year and commercial traffic was transferred from Son Bonet to Son Sant Joan. This was the birth of what today is known as the Palma de Mallorca Airport. During that year, a terminal and a civilian apron were built south of the military facilities, along with a VHF communication center. Also, a <a href="/wiki/VHF_omnidirectional_range" title="VHF omnidirectional range">VOR</a> was installed in the island.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Finally, on 7th July 1960, the airport was opened to both domestic and international traffic.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Just two weeks later, expansion of the airport was declared urgent by the government, and on summer 1961 the works of extension of the runway and taxiway were started. At the end of the year, more plans were made, including a power plant, a communications centre and fire and rescue facilities.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Growth_since_the_1960s">Growth since the 1960s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Palma_de_Mallorca_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Growth since the 1960s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>After reaching 1 million passengers for the first time in 1962, in 1965, a new terminal was constructed, and air navigation services were completed at the end of the following year. Also in 1965 <a href="/wiki/Air_Spain" title="Air Spain">Air Spain</a> (1965 - 1975) began operating from the airport<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> and a smaller terminal (today\'s Terminal B) was planned. Passenger numbers increased rapidly, reaching 2 million in 1965. Construction of a second runway, parallel to the existing one, was begun in 1970. Two years later, terminal B went into service, and the second runway (06L/24R) opened in 1974.\n</p><p>In 1980, the airport carried 7&#160;million passengers. However, this increased to nearly 10&#160;million in 1986. This led to the construction of yet another new terminal building, the current central terminal building. This building is now the airport\'s primary entrance and exit and houses the airport\'s checkin and baggage claim areas. Construction started in mid-1993 and it was designed by the Majorcan architect Pere Nicolau Bover. During the construction in 1995, passenger numbers exceeded 15 million. The new terminal finally opened in 1997.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Today">Today</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Palma_de_Mallorca_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Today">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Following a decline in passenger numbers at the airport following the <a href="/wiki/September_11_attacks" title="September 11 attacks">September 11 attacks</a> in 2001, passenger numbers rose steadily between 2002 and 2007 when traffic peaked at 23.2&#160;million passengers. From 2007 onward there was a decline in passenger numbers, with 21.1&#160;million using the airport in 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-aenapax_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aenapax-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Today, Palma de Mallorca airport carries over 29.7&#160;million passengers<sup id="cite_ref-aena.es_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aena.es-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> per year to their destinations, with 178,253 aircraft movements, mostly to mainland Spain, Germany and the United Kingdom.\n</p><p>In November 2015, <a href="/wiki/Air_Berlin" title="Air Berlin">Air Berlin</a> (1978 - 2017) announced that it would shut down its hub operations at the airport which it had maintained for over ten years. All seven domestic connection routes to the mainland, such as flights to <a href="/wiki/Valencia" title="Valencia">Valencia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bilbao" title="Bilbao">Bilbao</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sevilla" class="mw-redirect" title="Sevilla">Sevilla</a>, as well as the route to <a href="/wiki/Faro,_Portugal" title="Faro, Portugal">Faro</a> in Portugal ceased during spring 2016.<sup id="cite_ref-ABPMI_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ABPMI-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>During the Summer months the dual-runway airport handles as many movements as <a href="/wiki/Gatwick_Airport" title="Gatwick Airport">London\xe2\x80\x93Gatwick</a>. On the busiest day of the week it handles as many as 1,100 movements, almost as many as <a href="/wiki/Heathrow_Airport" title="Heathrow Airport">London\xe2\x80\x93Heathrow</a>, the busiest airport in Europe. According to the operational data provided by AENA, the airport can handle 66 movements per hour, or almost 1,600 movements over a 24-hour operational period.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" This main terminal was designed by local architect Pere Nicolau Bover and was officially opened on 12 April 1997. The airport now consists of four modules: Module A (the former Terminal A Building), Module B (the former Terminal B Building), Module C and Module D (the last two were completely new sets of buildings and gates that opened along with the new central terminal and check in area in 1997). The airport can handle 25 million passengers per year, with a capacity to dispatch 12,000 passengers per hour.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Module_A">Module A</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Palma_de_Mallorca_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Module A">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The former <i>Terminal A</i> Building is located in the north of the airport. It has 28 gates of which 8 have airbridges. This is the only Module that has double airbridges attached to gates. The Pier is mainly used by flights to non-Schengen destinations including the UK and Ireland. This part of the terminal building used to be closed during winter months and is only used in the summer. For winter 2018/2019 it will remain open.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Module_B">Module B</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Palma_de_Mallorca_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Module B">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The former <i>Terminal B</i> Building is the smallest module, located in the north east. It has eight gates located on the ground floor, of which none have airbridges. It is used by regional aircraft of <a href="/wiki/Air_Nostrum" title="Air Nostrum">Air Nostrum</a>, mainly operating flights to <a href="/wiki/Ibiza_Airport" title="Ibiza Airport">Ibiza Airport</a>, <a href="/wiki/Menorca_Airport" title="Menorca Airport">Menorca Airport</a>, <a href="/wiki/Valencia_Airport" title="Valencia Airport">Valencia Airport</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lleida_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Lleida Airport">Lleida Airport</a>, <a href="/wiki/Asturias_Airport" title="Asturias Airport">Asturias Airport</a> and <a href="/wiki/Santiago_de_Compostela_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Santiago de Compostela Airport">Santiago de Compostela Airport</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Module_C">Module C</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Palma_de_Mallorca_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Module C">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The largest of the Modules located in the east. It has 33 gates of which 9 have airbridges. It is used by <a href="/wiki/Condor_Flugdienst" class="mw-redirect" title="Condor Flugdienst">Condor</a> along with <a href="/wiki/EasyJet" title="EasyJet">EasyJet</a> and <a href="/wiki/Norwegian_Air_Shuttle" title="Norwegian Air Shuttle">Norwegian Air Shuttle</a> flights to Schengen destinations. The majority of airbridges have airberlin.com written on them. The southern area of the Module was worked on and reopened in May 2010. The refurbishment and expansion is so that the Module can handle more flights, and to improve ways to get into the pier as it is the longest walk from security control. There will also be a further eight gates with airbridges, but there will still be 33 in total.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Module_D">Module D</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Palma_de_Mallorca_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Module D">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Located in the south. It has 19 gates of which 10 have airbridges. All odd numbered gates are gates with a bus transfer. During the closure of the southern area of Module C, it was used mainly for flights to Europe.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_facilities">Other facilities</span><span
