Bidder\'s Edge</b></i>, 100 F. Supp. 2d 1058 (N.D. Cal. 2000), was a leading case applying the <a href="/wiki/Trespass_to_chattels" title="Trespass to chattels">trespass to chattels</a> doctrine to online activities.  In 2000, <a href="/wiki/EBay" title="EBay">eBay</a>, an <a href="/wiki/Online_auction" title="Online auction">online auction</a> company, successfully used the \'trespass to chattels\' theory to obtain a preliminary injunction preventing Bidder\'s Edge, an auction data aggregator, from using a \'<a href="/wiki/Web_crawler" title="Web crawler">crawler</a>\' to gather data from eBay\'s website.<sup id="cite_ref-case_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-case-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The opinion was a leading case applying \'trespass to chattels\' to online activities, although its analysis has been criticized in more recent jurisprudence.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc" role="navigation" aria-labelledby="mw-toc-heading"><input type="checkbox" role="button" class="tocnumber">5</span> <span class="toctext">External links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins_of_dispute">Origins of dispute</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=EBay_v._Bidder%27s_Edge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origins of dispute">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Bidder\'s Edge ("BE") was founded in 1997 as an "aggregator" of auction listings.<sup id="cite_ref-case_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-case-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>1061</span></sup> Its website provided a database of auction listings that BE automatically collected from various auction sites, including eBay.<sup id="cite_ref-case_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-case-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>1061</span></sup> Accordingly, BE\'s users could easily search auction listings from throughout the web rather than having to go to each individual auction site.<sup id="cite_ref-case_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-case-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>1061</span></sup>\n</p><p>In early 1998, eBay allowed BE to include <a href="/wiki/Beanie_Baby" class="mw-redirect" title="Beanie Baby">Beanie Babies</a> and <a href="/wiki/Furby" title="Furby">Furby</a> auction listings in BE\'s database.<sup id="cite_ref-case_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-case-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>1062</span></sup> It is unclear whether BE scraped these listings from eBay or linked to them in some other format. However, on April 24, 1999, eBay verbally approved BE automatically "crawling" the eBay web site for a period of 90 days.<sup id="cite_ref-case_1-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-case-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>1062</span></sup>\n</p><p>During this time, the parties contemplated striking a formal license agreement.<sup id="cite_ref-case_1-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-case-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>1062</span></sup> These negotiations did not conclude successfully because the parties could not agree on technical issues.<sup id="cite_ref-case_1-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-case-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Subsequently, in early 1999, BE added auction listings from many other sites in its database, including eBay\'s. Despite the integration of many websites\' listings, nearly 69% of the listings in BE\'s database were from eBay.<sup id="cite_ref-case_1-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-case-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>1063</span></sup>\n</p><p>eBay wanted BE to access the eBay system only when a BE user queried the BE system.<sup id="cite_ref-case_1-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-case-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>1062</span></sup> Doing so would increase the accuracy/currency of the data BE presented to its users  and impose a lighter load on eBay\'s network.<sup id="cite_ref-case_1-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-case-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> BE accessed eBay approximately 100,000 times a day, constituting about 1.53% of eBay\'s total daily requests.<sup id="cite_ref-case_1-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-case-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>1063</span></sup> BE wanted to periodically crawl eBay\'s entire website to compile its own auction database, which would increase the speed of BE\'s response to user queries and allow BE to notify its users when eBay auctions changed.<sup id="cite_ref-case_1-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-case-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>1063</span></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Further_developments_leading_to_suit">Further developments leading to suit</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=EBay_v._Bidder%27s_Edge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Further developments leading to suit">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Due to the disagreement regarding technical issues, at the end of the 90-day period, eBay notified BE that its activities were no longer permitted, but eBay offered again to license BE\'s activities. BE did not accept eBay\'s offer.<sup id="cite_ref-case_1-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-case-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>1068</span></sup>\n</p><p>In late August or early September 1999, eBay requested by telephone that BE cease posting eBay auction listings on its site.<sup id="cite_ref-case_1-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-case-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>1062</span></sup> BE agreed to do so. In October 1999, BE learned that other auction aggregations sites were including information about eBay auctions.<sup id="cite_ref-case_1-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-case-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>1062</span></sup>\n</p><p>On November 2, 1999, BE issued a press release indicating that it had resumed including eBay auction listings on its site. On November 9, 1999, eBay sent BE a letter reasserting that BE\'s activities were unauthorized, insisting that BE cease accessing the eBay site, alleging that BE\'s activities constituted a trespass of eBay\'s chattels and offering to license BE\'s activities.<sup id="cite_ref-case_1-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-case-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>1062</span></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="IP_address_blocking_and_proxy">IP address blocking and proxy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=EBay_v._Bidder%27s_Edge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: IP address blocking and proxy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>As a result, eBay attempted to block BE from accessing the eBay site; by the end of November 1999, eBay had blocked a total of 169 IP addresses it believed BE was using to query eBay\'s system.<sup id="cite_ref-case_1-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-case-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>1062</span></sup> BE continued crawling eBay\'s site by using <a href="/wiki/Proxy_server" title="Proxy server">proxy servers</a> to evade eBay\'s IP address blocks. Information requests sent through such servers cannot easily be traced back to the originating <a href="/wiki/IP_address" title="IP address">IP Address</a>, which allowed Bidder\'s Edge to evade eBay\'s attempts to block queries from the originating IP address.<sup id="cite_ref-case_1-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-case-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>1061</span></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Lawsuits">Lawsuits</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=EBay_v._Bidder%27s_Edge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Lawsuits">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>eBay sued Bidder\'s Edge on December 10, 1999, in the <a href="/wiki/United_States_District_Court_for_the_Northern_District_of_California" title="United States District Court for the Northern District of California">Northern District of California</a> federal court.<sup id="cite_ref-case_1-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-case-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>1065</span></sup> eBay moved for a preliminary injunction on the following causes of action:\n</p>\n<ol><li><a href="/wiki/Trespass_to_chattels" title="Trespass to chattels">Trespass to chattels</a>,</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Lanham_Act" title="Lanham Act">False advertising under the Lanham Act, 15 U.S.C. \xc2\xa7 1125(a)</a>,</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Trademark_dilution" title="Trademark dilution">Trademark dilution</a>,</li>\n<li>violation of the <a href="/wiki/Computer_Fraud_and_Abuse_Act" title="Computer Fraud and Abuse Act">Computer Fraud and Abuse Act, 18 U.S.C. \xc2\xa7 1030</a>,</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Unfair_competition" class="mw-redirect" title="Unfair competition">Unfair competition</a>,</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Misappropriation" title="Misappropriation">Misappropriation</a>,</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Tortious_interference" title="Tortious interference">Interference with prospective economic advantage</a> and</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Unjust_enrichment" title="Unjust enrichment">Unjust enrichment</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-case_1-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-case-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>1063</span></sup></li></ol>\n<p>BE filed antitrust counterclaims on February 7, 2000.<sup id="cite_ref-case_1-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-case-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>1073</span></sup> The counterclaims charged eBay with monopolization, attempted monopolization, unfair business practices and interference with contractual relations.<sup id="cite_ref-case_1-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-case-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>1063</span></sup> On May 24, 2000, District Court Judge Whyte found that eBay had established a sufficient likelihood of prevailing on the trespass claim to support eBay\'s requested injunctive relief. Because the court found eBay entitled to the relief requested based on its trespass claim, the court did not address the remaining claims.<sup id="cite_ref-case_1-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-case-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>1063</span></sup> The opinion first addressed the merits of the trespass claim, then BE\'s arguments regarding copyright preemption of the trespass claim, and finally the public interest.<sup id="cite_ref-case_1-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-case-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>1063</span></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Trespass_to_chattels">Trespass to chattels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=EBay_v._Bidder%27s_Edge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Trespass to chattels">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The court said that eBay\'s trespass to chattels claim required it to show that:\n</p>\n<ol><li>Bidder\'s Edge intentionally and without authorization interfered with eBay\'s possessory interest in the computer system; and</li>\n<li>Bidder\'s Edge\'s unauthorized use proximately resulted in damage to eBay.<sup id="cite_ref-case_1-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-case-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>1069\xe2\x80\x931070</span></sup></li></ol>\n<p>eBay argued that BE\'s use was unauthorized and intentional.<sup id="cite_ref-case_1-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-case-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>1061</span></sup> The court said that eBay had not permitted BE\'s activity simply by having a website available over the Internet.<sup id="cite_ref-case_1-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-case-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>1070</span></sup> BE had violated eBay\'s terms of use and ignored eBay\'s requests to stop using its crawlers.  BE responded that it was not causing eBay irreparable harm because its activity (80,000\xe2\x80\x93100,000 hits per day) represented only a small fraction (approximately 1&#8201;\xc2\xbd percent) of the overall activity on eBay\'s site. eBay acknowledged that BE\'s activity was only a relatively slight interference with eBay\'s servers.<sup id="cite_ref-case_1-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-case-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>1071</span></sup>\n</p><p>Nevertheless, the court found that although BE\'s interference was not substantial, "any intermeddling with or use of another\'s personal property" established BE\'s possessory interference with eBay\'s chattel.<sup id="cite_ref-case_1-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-case-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Further, BE\'s use of eBay\'s bandwidth and system resources, even though small, harmed eBay because other companies might follow BE\'s example: "If the court were to hold otherwise, it would likely encourage other auction aggregators to crawl the eBay site, potentially to the point of denying effective access to eBay\'s customers.  If preliminary injunctive relief were denied, and other aggregators began to crawl the eBay site, there appears to be little doubt that the load on eBay\'s computer system would qualify as a substantial impairment of condition or value."<sup id="cite_ref-case_1-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-case-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>1072</span></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_interest">Public interest</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=EBay_v._Bidder%27s_Edge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Public interest">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The parties argued that the Internet would cease to function if, according to eBay, personal and intellectual property rights were not respected, or, according to BE, if information published on the Internet could not be universally accessed and used.<sup id="cite_ref-case_1-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-case-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>1072</span></sup> The court suspected that the Internet would not only survive but continue to grow and develop regardless of its ruling.<sup id="cite_ref-case_1-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-case-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>1072</span></sup> The court noted that particularly on the limited record available at the preliminary injunction stage, it was unable to determine whether the general public interest factors favored or opposed a <a href="/wiki/Injunction" title="Injunction">preliminary injunction</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-case_1-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-case-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>1072</span></sup>\n</p><p>BE also argued that eBay engaged in anticompetitive behavior.<sup id="cite_ref-case_1-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-case-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>1072</span></sup> However, the district court was not obligated to consider the merits of any antitrust counterclaims once it decided that eBay had a likelihood of success on the merits.<sup id="cite_ref-case_1-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-case-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>1072</span></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Order">Order</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=EBay_v._Bidder%27s_Edge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Order">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Based on its findings, the court issued a preliminary injunction against BE from "using any automated query program, robot, or similar device to access eBay\'s computer systems or networks for the purpose of copying any part of eBay\'s auction database."<sup id="cite_ref-case_1-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-case-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>1073</span></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Subsequent_developments_and_negative_history">Subsequent developments and negative history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=EBay_v._Bidder%27s_Edge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Subsequent developments and negative history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>One day after it filed federal antitrust charges against eBay, Bidder\'s Edge announced it would be acquired by OpenSite, an auction software company. However, the deal fell through when <a href="/wiki/Siebel_Systems" title="Siebel Systems">Siebel Systems</a> bought OpenSite.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>eBay and Bidder\'s Edge settled their legal disputes in March 2001.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> As part of the settlement, Bidder\'s Edge paid eBay an undisclosed amount and agreed not to access and re-post eBay\'s auction information.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The settlement also required BE to drop its appeal of the preliminary injunction.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Meanwhile, Bidder\'s Edge shut down its website on February 21, 2001.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 2003, the California Supreme Court implicitly overruled the <i>eBay v. Bidder\'s Edge</i> opinion in <i><a href="/wiki/Intel_Corp._v._Hamidi" title="Intel Corp. v. Hamidi">Intel v. Hamidi</a></i>, a case interpreting California\'s common law trespass to chattels.\n</p><p>The <i>Hamidi</i> court considered the eBay court analysis, which stated that if BE\'s activity were allowed to continue unchecked, it would encourage other auction aggregators to engage in similar searching which would cause eBay irreparable harm. In analyzing this point, the Hamidi court stated,"[W]e do not read [eBay decision] as expressing the court\'s complete view of the issue. In isolation, moreover, [it] would not be a correct statement of California or general American law on this point." As a result, the opinion may be or may no longer be valid precedent.\n</p><p>Further, since eBay was issued, some courts have become more circumspect about the "slippery slope" argument that eBay successfully made about additional crawlers following BE\'s lead. For example, in <i>White Buffalo Ventures LLC v. University of Texas at Austin</i>, the Fifth Circuit said "Since the spider does not cause physical injury to the chattel, there must be some evidence that the use or utility of the computer (or computer network) being \'spiderized\' is adversely affected by the use of the spider. No such evidence is presented here. This court respectfully disagrees with other district courts\' finding that mere use of a spider to enter a publicly available web site to gather information, without more, is sufficient to fulfill the harm requirement for trespass to chattels."<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline"
