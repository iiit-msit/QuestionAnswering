template">e</abbr></a></li></ul></div></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p>A <b>cause of action</b>, in <a href="/wiki/Law" title="Law">law</a>, is a set of facts sufficient to justify suing to obtain money, property, or the enforcement of a <a href="/wiki/Legal_right" class="mw-redirect" title="Legal right">legal right</a> against another party. The term also refers to the legal theory upon which a plaintiff brings suit (such as <a href="/wiki/Breach_of_contract" title="Breach of contract">breach of contract</a>, <a href="/wiki/Battery_(tort)" title="Battery (tort)">battery</a>, or <a href="/wiki/False_imprisonment" title="False imprisonment">false imprisonment</a>). The legal document which carries a claim is often called a \'statement of claim\' in English law, or a \'<a href="/wiki/Complaint" title="Complaint">complaint</a>\' in U.S. federal practice and in many U.S. states. It can be any communication notifying the party to whom it is addressed of an alleged fault which resulted in damages, often expressed in amount of money the receiving party should pay/reimburse.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>To pursue a cause of action, a <a href="/wiki/Plaintiff" title="Plaintiff">plaintiff</a> <a href="/wiki/Pleading" title="Pleading">pleads</a> or <a href="/wiki/Allegation" title="Allegation">alleges</a> facts in a <a href="/wiki/Complaint" title="Complaint">complaint</a>, the pleading that initiates a lawsuit. A cause of action generally encompasses both the <a href="/wiki/Legal_theory" class="mw-redirect" title="Legal theory">legal theory</a> (the legal wrong the plaintiff claims to have suffered) and the <a href="/wiki/Legal_remedy" title="Legal remedy">remedy</a> (the relief a court is asked to grant). Often the facts or circumstances that entitle a person to seek judicial relief may create multiple causes of action. Although it is fairly straightforward to file a Statement of Claim in most jurisdictions, if it is not done properly, then the filing party may lose his case due to simple technicalities.\n</p><p>There are a number of specific causes of action, including: <a href="/wiki/Contract" title="Contract">contract</a>-based actions; statutory causes of action; <a href="/wiki/Torts" class="mw-redirect" title="Torts">torts</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Assault" title="Assault">assault</a>, <a href="/wiki/Battery_(tort)" title="Battery (tort)">battery</a>, <a href="/wiki/Invasion_of_privacy" class="mw-redirect" title="Invasion of privacy">invasion of privacy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fraud" title="Fraud">fraud</a>, <a href="/wiki/Defamation" title="Defamation">slander</a>, <a href="/wiki/Negligence" title="Negligence">negligence</a>, <a href="/wiki/Intentional_infliction_of_emotional_distress" title="Intentional infliction of emotional distress">intentional infliction of emotional distress</a>; and suits in <a href="/wiki/Equity_(law)" title="Equity (law)">equity</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Unjust_enrichment" title="Unjust enrichment">unjust enrichment</a> and <a href="/wiki/Quantum_meruit" title="Quantum meruit">quantum meruit</a>.\n</p><p>The points a plaintiff must prove to win a given type of case are called the "elements" of that cause of action. For example, for a claim of <a href="/wiki/Negligence" title="Negligence">negligence</a>, the elements are: the (existence of a) <a href="/wiki/Duty" title="Duty">duty</a>, breach (of that duty), proximate cause (by that breach), and <a href="/wiki/Damages" title="Damages">damages</a>. If a complaint does not allege facts sufficient to support every element of a claim, the court, upon motion by the opposing party, may dismiss the complaint for <a href="/wiki/Failure_to_state_a_claim" class="mw-redirect" title="Failure to state a claim">failure to state a claim</a> for which relief can be granted.\n</p><p>The defendant to a cause of action must file an "Answer" to the complaint in which the <i>claims</i> can be admitted or denied (including denial on the basis of insufficient information in the complaint to form a response). The answer may also contain counterclaims in which the "Counterclaim Plaintiff" states its own causes of action. Finally, the answer may contain <a href="/wiki/Affirmative_defense" title="Affirmative defense">affirmative defenses</a>. Most defenses must be raised at the first possible opportunity either in the answer or by motion or are deemed waived. A few defenses, in particular a court\'s lack of subject matter <a href="/wiki/Jurisdiction" title="Jurisdiction">jurisdiction</a>, need not be pleaded and may be raised at any time.\n</p>\n<h2><span
