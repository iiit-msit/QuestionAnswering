Avoid <i><a href="/wiki/Myth" title="Myth">myth</a></i> in its informal sense, and establish the scholarly context for any formal use of the term.\n</p><p>The prefix <i><a href="/wiki/Pseudo-" title="Pseudo-">pseudo-</a></i> indicates that something is false or spurious, which may be debatable. The suffix <i><a href="/wiki/List_of_scandals_with_%22-gate%22_suffix" class="mw-redirect" title="List of scandals with &quot;-gate&quot; suffix">\xe2\x80\x91gate</a></i> suggests the existence of a scandal. Use these in articles only when they are in wide use externally, e.g. <a href="/wiki/Gamergate_controversy" title="Gamergate controversy">Gamergate controversy</a>, with in-text attribution if in doubt. Rather than describing an individual using the subjective and vague term <i>controversial</i>, instead give readers information about relevant controversies. Make sure, as well, that reliable sources establish the existence of a controversy and that the term is not used to grant a <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Fringe_theories" title="Wikipedia:Fringe theories">fringe viewpoint</a> undue weight.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;b&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>With regard to the term "pseudoscience": per the policy <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Neutral_point_of_view" title="Wikipedia:Neutral point of view">Wikipedia:Neutral point of view</a>, pseudoscientific views "should be clearly described as such". Per the content guideline <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Fringe_theories" title="Wikipedia:Fringe theories">Wikipedia:Fringe theories</a>, the term "pseudoscience", when supported by reliable sources, may be used to distinguish fringe theories from mainstream science.\n</p><p><link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1015928993"/><span role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable crossreference selfref">For additional guidance on <span class="nowrap"><i>-ist/-ism</i></span> terms, see <a href="#Neologisms_and_new_compounds">\xc2\xa7&#160;Neologisms and new compounds</a>, below.</span>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Unsupported_attributions">Unsupported attributions</span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Embrace_weasel_words" title="Wikipedia:Embrace weasel words">Wikipedia:Embrace weasel words</a></div>\n<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1007196855"/><div class="module-shortcutanchordiv"><span id="MOS:WEASEL"></span><span id="MOS:AWW"></span></div><div class="module-shortcutboxplain plainlist noprint" role="note"><div class="module-shortcutlist"><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Shortcut" title="Wikipedia:Shortcut">Shortcuts</a></div><ul><li><span class="plainlinks"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=MOS:WEASEL&amp;redirect=no">MOS:WEASEL</a></span></li><li><span class="plainlinks"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=MOS:AWW&amp;redirect=no">MOS:AWW</a></span></li></ul></div>\n<div class="quotebox pullquote centered" style="width:70%; ; background-color: #FFFFF0;"><link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1012376600"/>\n<div class="quotebox-quote left-aligned" style=""><span style="font-size: 120%;">Words to watch: <strong><i>some people say</i>, <i>many scholars state</i>, <i>it is believed/regarded/considered</i>, <i>many are of the opinion</i>, <i>most feel</i>, <i>experts declare</i>, <i>it is often reported</i>, <i>it is widely thought</i>, <i>research has shown</i>, <i>science says</i>, <i>scientists claim</i>, <i>it is often said</i>, <i><var style="padding-right: 1px;">X</var> has been described as <var style="padding-right: 1px;">Y</var></i>&#160;...</strong> </span></div>\n</div>\n<div class="floatright"><a href="/wiki/File:Weasel_words.svg" class="image"><img alt="A weasel saying, &quot;Some people say that weasel words are great!&quot;" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/Weasel_words.svg/200px-Weasel_words.svg.png" decoding="async" width="200" height="200" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/Weasel_words.svg/300px-Weasel_words.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/Weasel_words.svg/400px-Weasel_words.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="500" data-file-height="500" /></a></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Weasel_word" title="Weasel word">Weasel words</a> are words and phrases aimed at creating an impression that something specific and meaningful has been said, when in fact only a vague or ambiguous claim has been communicated. A common form of weasel wording is through vague attribution, where a statement is dressed with authority, yet has no substantial basis. Phrases such as those above present the appearance of support for statements but can deny the reader the opportunity to assess the source of the viewpoint. They may disguise a biased view. Claims about what people say, think, feel, or believe, and what has been shown, demonstrated, or proved should be clearly attributed.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;c&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The examples of weasel words above <strong>may</strong> be used in the <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Lead_section" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Lead section">lead section</a> of an article or in a <a href="/wiki/Topic_sentence" title="Topic sentence">topic sentence</a> of a paragraph only when the article body or the rest of the paragraph can supply attribution and accurately support that statement.\n</p><p>When views that are <strong>properly attributed through a reference</strong> to a <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Identifying_reliable_sources" class="mw-redirect" title="Wikipedia:Identifying reliable sources">reliable source</a> use weasel words or similar expressions, (i.e. those expressions <strong><i>accurately represent the opinions of the source</i></strong>) it is better to also attribute the source in the article text. Attributing the source in the article text so that readers can immediately see who is saying/writing/thinking something will help them evaluate the statement.\n</p><p>Weasel words (even those properly attributed) <strong>may</strong> be an indication that the statement is not an accurate representation of reality or scientific consensus. For a reader it will be relevant to know if the statement is supported by a majority or only by a few, see <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Neutral_point_of_view" title="Wikipedia:Neutral point of view">Wikipedia:Neutral point of view</a>.\n</p><p>A writer of a reliable source may analyze and interpret a subject, but for a Wikipedia editor to do so would violate the <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:No_original_research" title="Wikipedia:No original research">Wikipedia:No original research</a> policy. Equally, editorial <a href="/wiki/Irony" title="Irony">irony</a> and damning with faint praise have no place in Wikipedia articles.\n</p><p>Articles including weasel words should ideally be rewritten such that they are supported by reliable sources and attribute the statement in the article text: alternatively, they may be tagged with the <code>&#123;&#123;<a href="/wiki/Template:Weasel" title="Template:Weasel">Weasel</a>&#125;&#125;</code>, <code>&#123;&#123;<a href="/wiki/Template:By_whom" title="Template:By whom">By whom</a>&#125;&#125;</code>, or similar templates to identify the problem to future readers (who may elect to fix the problem).\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Expressions_of_doubt">Expressions of doubt</span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote to watch: <strong><i>supposed</i>, <i>apparent</i>, <i>purported</i>, <i>alleged</i>, <i>accused</i>, <i>so-called</i>&#160;...</strong> &#160; Also, scare-quoting: <strong><i>a Yale <i>purported</i> can imply that a given point is inaccurate, although <i>alleged</i> and <i>accused</i> are appropriate when wrongdoing is asserted but undetermined, such as with people awaiting or named</i>, or <i>contentiously named</i>, and it can be difficult to tell these apart. Simply <i>called</i> is preferable for the first meaning; detailed and attributed explanations are preferable for the others.\n</p><p>Misused punctuation can also have similar effects. Quotation marks, when not marking an actual quotation, may be interpreted as "<a href="/wiki/Scare_quotes" title="Scare quotes">scare quotes</a>", indicating that the writer is distancing themselves from the otherwise common interpretation of the quoted expression. The use of <em><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Text_formatting#Emphasis" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Text formatting">emphasis</a></em> may turn an innocuous word into a loaded expression, so such occurrences should also be considered carefully.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Editorializing">Editorializing<span id="Editorialising"></span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">"WP:EDITORIAL" redirects here. ; background-color: #FFFFF0;"><link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1012376600"/>\n<div class="quotebox-quote left-aligned" style=""><span style="font-size: 120%;">Words to watch: <strong><i>notably</i>, <i>it should be noted</i>, <i>arguably</i>, <i>interestingly</i>, <i>essentially</i>, <i>utterly</i>, <i>actually</i>, <i>clearly</i>, <i>absolutely</i>, <i>of course</i>, <i>without a doubt</i>, <i>indeed</i>, <i>happily</i>, <i>sadly</i>, <i>tragically</i>, <i>aptly</i>, <i>fortunately</i>, <i>unfortunately</i>, <i>untimely</i>&#160;...</strong> </span></div>\n</div>\n<p>The use of adverbs such as <i>notably</i> and <i>interestingly</i>, and phrases such as <i>it should be noted</i>, to highlight something as particularly significant or certain without attributing that opinion should usually be avoided so as to maintain an <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Neutral_point_of_view#Impartial_tone" title="Wikipedia:Neutral point of view">impartial tone</a>. Words such as <i>fundamentally</i>, <i>essentially</i>, and <i>basically</i> can indicate particular interpretative viewpoints, and thus should also be attributed in controversial cases. Care should be used with <i>actually</i>, which implies that a fact is contrary to expectations; make sure this is <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">verifiable</a> and not just assumed. <i>Clearly</i>, <i>obviously</i>, <i>naturally</i>, and <i>of course</i> all presume too much about the reader\'s knowledge and perspective and often amount to verbiage. Wikipedia should not take a view as to whether an event was <i>fortunate</i> or not.\n</p><p>This kind of <a href="/wiki/Persuasive_writing" title="Persuasive writing">persuasive writing</a> approach is also against the <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:No_original_research" title="Wikipedia:No original research">Wikipedia:No original research</a> policy (Wikipedia does not try to "steer" the reader to a particular interpretation or conclusion), and the <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style#Instructional_and_presumptuous_language" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style">Instructional and presumptuous language</a> guideline (Wikipedia does not "break the <a href="/wiki/Fourth_wall" title="Fourth wall">fourth wall</a>" and write <em>at</em> the reader, other than with <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Hatnote" title="Wikipedia:Hatnote">navigational hatnotes</a>.)\n</p>\n<div class="quotebox pullquote centered" style="width:70%; ; background-color: #FFFFF0;"><link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1012376600"/>\n<div class="quotebox-quote left-aligned" style=""><span style="font-size: 120%;">Words to watch: <strong><i>but</i>, <i>despite</i>, <i>however</i>, <i>though</i>, <i>although</i>, <i>furthermore</i>, <i>while</i>&#160;...</strong> </span></div>\n</div>\n<p>More subtly, editorializing can produce <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:No_original_research#Synthesis_of_published_material" title="Wikipedia:No original research">implications that are not supported by the sources</a>. When used to link two statements, words such as <i>but</i>, <i>despite</i>, <i>however</i>, and <i>although</i> may imply a relationship where none exists, possibly unduly calling the validity of the first statement into question while giving <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Neutral_point_of_view#Due_and_undue_weight" title="Wikipedia:Neutral point of view">undue weight</a> to the credibility of the second.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Synonyms_for_said">Synonyms for <i>said</i><span id="Various_synonyms_for_said"></span></span></h3>\n<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1007196855"/><div class="module-shortcutanchordiv"><span id="MOS:SAID"></span><span id="MOS:SAY"></span><span id="MOS:CLAIM"></span></div><div class="module-shortcutboxplain plainlist noprint" role="note"><div class="module-shortcutlist"><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Shortcut" title="Wikipedia:Shortcut">Shortcuts</a></div><ul><li><span class="plainlinks"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=MOS:SAID&amp;redirect=no">MOS:SAID</a></span></li><li><span class="plainlinks"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=MOS:SAY&amp;redirect=no">MOS:SAY</a></span></li><li><span class="plainlinks"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=MOS:CLAIM&amp;redirect=no">MOS:CLAIM</a></span></li></ul></div>\n<div class="quotebox pullquote centered" style="width:70%; ; background-color: #FFFFF0;"><link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1012376600"/>\n<div class="quotebox-quote left-aligned" style=""><span style="font-size: 120%;">Words to watch: <strong><i>reveal</i>, <i>point out</i>, <i>clarify</i>, <i>expose</i>, <i>explain</i>, <i>find</i>, <i>note</i>, <i>observe</i>, <i>insist</i>, <i>speculate</i>, <i>surmise</i>, <i>claim</i>, <i>assert</i>, <i>admit</i>, <i>confess</i>, <i>deny</i>&#160;...</strong> </span></div>\n</div>\n<p>In some types of writing, repeated usage of <i>said</i> is considered tedious, and writers are encouraged to employ synonyms. However, on Wikipedia, it is more important to avoid language that makes undue implications.\n</p><p><i>Said</i>, <i>stated</i>, <i>described</i>, <i>wrote</i>, <i>commented</i>, and <i>according to</i> are almost always neutral and accurate. Extra care is needed with more <a href="/wiki/Loaded_term" class="mw-redirect" title="Loaded term">loaded terms</a>. For example, to write that a person <i>clarified</i>, <i>explained</i>, <i>exposed</i>, <i>found</i>, <i>pointed out</i>, or <i>revealed</i> something can imply it is true, instead of simply conveying the fact that it was <i>said</i>. To write that someone <i>insisted</i>, <i>noted</i>, <i>observed</i>, <i>speculated</i>, or <i>surmised</i> can suggest the degree of the person\'s carefulness, resoluteness, or access to evidence, even when such things are unverifiable.\n</p><p>To write that someone <i>asserted</i> or <i>claimed</i> something can call their statement\'s credibility into question, by emphasizing any potential contradiction or implying a disregard for evidence. Similarly, be judicious in the use of <i>admit</i>, <i>confess</i>, <i>reveal</i>, and <i>deny</i>, <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Biographies_of_living_persons" title="Wikipedia:Biographies of living persons">particularly for living people</a>, because these verbs can inappropriately imply <a href="/wiki/Culpability" title="Culpability">culpability</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Expressions_that_lack_precision">Expressions that lack precision</span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Euphemisms">Euphemisms</span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">"WP:EUPHEMISM" redirects here. For the essay advising bluntness, see <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Call_a_spade_a_spade" title="Wikipedia:Call a spade a spade">WP:Call a spade a spade</a>.</div>\n<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1007196855"/><div class="module-shortcutanchordiv"><span id="MOS:EUPHEMISM"></span><span id="MOS:EUPH"></span></div><div class="module-shortcutboxplain plainlist noprint" role="note"><div class="module-shortcutlist"><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Shortcut" title="Wikipedia:Shortcut">Shortcuts</a></div><ul><li><span class="plainlinks"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=MOS:EUPHEMISM&amp;redirect=no">MOS:EUPHEMISM</a></span></li><li><span class="plainlinks"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=MOS:EUPH&amp;redirect=no">MOS:EUPH</a></span></li></ul></div>\n<div class="quotebox pullquote centered" style="width:70%; ; background-color: #FFFFF0;"><link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1012376600"/>\n<div class="quotebox-quote left-aligned" style=""><span style="font-size: 120%;">Words to watch: <strong><i>passed away</i>, <i>gave his/her life</i>, <i>eternal rest</i>, <i>make love</i>, <i>an issue with</i>, <i>collateral damage</i>&#160;...</strong> </span></div>\n</div>\n<p>The word <i>died</i> is neutral and accurate; avoid <a href="/wiki/Euphemism" title="Euphemism">euphemisms</a> such as <i>passed away</i>. Likewise, <i>have sex</i> is neutral; the euphemism <i>make love</i> is presumptuous. Some words that are proper in many contexts also have euphemistic senses that should be avoided: do not use <i>issue</i> for <i>problem</i> or <i>dispute</i>; <i><a href="/wiki/Civilian_casualties" title="Civilian casualties">civilian casualties</a></i> should not be masked as <i><a href="/wiki/Collateral_damage" title="Collateral damage">collateral damage</a></i>.\n</p><p>If a person <em>has</em> an affliction, or <em>is</em> afflicted, say just that.  \n</p><p>Norms vary for expressions concerning disabilities and disabled people. The goal is to express ideas clearly and directly without causing unnecessary offense.  Do not assume that plain language is inappropriate.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Clich.C3.A9s_and_idioms"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Clich\xc3\xa9s_and_idioms">Clich\xc3\xa9s and idioms</span></h3>\n<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1007196855"/><div class="module-shortcutanchordiv"><span id="MOS:CLICHE"></span><span id="MOS:IDIOM"></span></div><div class="module-shortcutboxplain plainlist noprint" role="note"><div class="module-shortcutlist"><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Shortcut" title="Wikipedia:Shortcut">Shortcuts</a></div><ul><li><span class="plainlinks"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=MOS:CLICHE&amp;redirect=no">MOS:CLICHE</a></span></li><li><span class="plainlinks"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=MOS:IDIOM&amp;redirect=no">MOS:IDIOM</a></span></li></ul></div>\n<div class="quotebox pullquote centered" style="width:70%; ; background-color: #FFFFF0;"><link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1012376600"/>\n<div class="quotebox-quote left-aligned" style=""><span style="font-size: 120%;">Words to watch: <strong><i>lion\'s share</i>, <i>tip of the iceberg</i>, <i>white elephant</i>, <i>gild the lily</i>, <i>take the plunge</i>, <i>ace up the sleeve</i>, <i>bird in the hand</i>, <i>twist of fate</i>, <i>at the end of the day</i>&#160;...</strong> </span></div>\n</div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Clich%C3%A9" title="Clich\xc3\xa9">Clich\xc3\xa9s</a> and <a href="/wiki/Idiom" title="Idiom">idioms</a> are generally to be avoided in favor of direct, literal expressions. <i>Lion\'s share</i> is often misunderstood; instead use a term such as <i>all, most</i>, <i>two-thirds</i>, or whatever matches the context. The <i>tip of the iceberg</i> should be reserved for discussions of <a href="/wiki/Iceberg" title="Iceberg">icebergs</a>.  If something is seen as wasteful excess, do not refer to it as <i>gilding the lily</i> or a <i>white elephant</i>; instead, describe the wasteful endeavor in terms of the actions or events that led to the excess. Instead of writing that someone <i>took the plunge</i>, state their actions matter-of-factly. If a literal interpretation of a phrase makes no sense in the context of a sentence, then the sentence should be reworded. Some idioms are only common in certain parts of the world, and many readers are not native speakers of English; articles should not presume familiarity with particular phrases. <a href="/wiki/Wiktionary" title="Wiktionary">Wiktionary</a> has a lengthy <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Category:English_idioms" class="extiw" title="wikt:Category:English idioms">list of English idioms</a>, some of which should be avoided.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Relative_time_references">Relative time references</span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:As_of#Precise_language" title="Wikipedia:As of">Wikipedia:As of \xc2\xa7&#160;Precise language</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers">Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers \xc2\xa7&#160;Chronological items</a></div>\n<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1007196855"/><div class="module-shortcutanchordiv"><span id="MOS:RELTIME"></span><span id="MOS:REALTIME"></span></div><div class="module-shortcutboxplain plainlist noprint" role="note"><div class="module-shortcutlist"><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Shortcut" title="Wikipedia:Shortcut">Shortcuts</a></div><ul><li><span class="plainlinks"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=MOS:RELTIME&amp;redirect=no">MOS:RELTIME</a></span></li><li><span class="plainlinks"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=MOS:REALTIME&amp;redirect=no">MOS:REALTIME</a></span></li></ul></div>\n<div class="quotebox pullquote centered" style="width:70%; ; background-color: #FFFFF0;"><link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1012376600"/>\n<div class="quotebox-quote left-aligned" style=""><span style="font-size: 120%;">Words to watch: <strong><i>recently</i>, <i>lately</i>, <i>currently</i>, <i>today</i>, <i>presently</i>, <i>to date</i>, <i>15 years ago</i>, <i>formerly</i>, <i>in the past</i>, <i>traditionally</i>, <i>this/last/next (year/month/winter/spring/summer/fall/autumn)</i>, <i>yesterday</i>, <i>tomorrow</i>, <i>in the future</i>, <i>now</i>, <i>soon</i>, <i>since</i>&#160;...</strong> </span></div>\n</div>\n<p>Absolute specifications of time are preferred to relative constructions using <i>recently</i>, <i>currently</i>, and so on, because the latter may go out of date. "By May 2021 contributions had dropped" has the same meaning as "Recently, contributions have dropped" but the first sentence retains its meaning as time passes. And <i>recently</i>-type constructions may be ambiguous even at the time of writing: Was it in the last week? Month? Year?<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;d&#93;</a></sup> The information that "The current president, Cristina Fern\xc3\xa1ndez, took office in 2007", or "Cristina Fern\xc3\xa1ndez has been president since 2007", is better rendered "Cristina Fern\xc3\xa1ndez became president in 2007". Wordings such as "17 years ago" or "Jones is 65 years old" should be rewritten as "in 2004", "Jones was 65 years old at the time of the incident", or "Jones was born in 1956."\n</p><p>When material in an article may become out of date, follow the <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:As_of" title="Wikipedia:As of">Wikipedia:As of</a> guideline, which allows information to be written in a less time-dependent way.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;e&#93;</a></sup> There are also several templates for alerting readers to time-sensitive wording problems.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;f&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Expressions like "former(ly)", "in the past", and "traditional(ly)" lump together unspecified periods in the past. "Traditional" is particularly pernicious because it implies <a href="/wiki/Time_immemorial" title="Time immemorial">immemorial</a> established usage. It is better to use explicit dates supported by sources. Instead of "hamburgers are a traditional American food," say "the hamburger was invented in about 1900 and became widely popular in the United States in the 1930s."<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;g&#93;</a></sup> Because seasons differ between the northern and southern hemisphere, try to use months, quarters, or other non-seasonal terms such as <i>mid-year</i> unless the season itself is pertinent (<i>spring blossoms</i>, <i>autumn harvest</i>); see <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Seasons_of_the_year" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers">Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers \xc2\xa7&#160;Seasons of the year</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Unspecified_places_or_events">Unspecified places or events</span></h3>\n<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1007196855"/><div class="module-shortcutanchordiv"><span id="MOS:WHATPLACE"></span></div><div class="module-shortcutboxplain plainlist noprint" role="note"><div class="module-shortcutlist"><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Shortcut" title="Wikipedia:Shortcut">Shortcut</a></div><ul><li><span class="plainlinks"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=MOS:WHATPLACE&amp;redirect=no">MOS:WHATPLACE</a></span></li></ul></div>\n<div class="quotebox pullquote centered" style="width:70%; ; background-color: #FFFFF0;"><link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1012376600"/>\n<div class="quotebox-quote left-aligned" style=""><span style="font-size: 120%;">Words to watch: <strong><i>this country</i>, <i>here</i>, <i>there</i>, <i>somewhere</i>, <i>sometimes</i>, <i>often</i>, <i>occasionally</i>, <i>somehow</i>&#160;...</strong> </span></div>\n</div>\n<p>As in the previous section, prefer specific statements to general ones. It is better to use explicit descriptions, based on reliable sources, of when, where, or how an event occurred. Instead of saying "In April 2012, Senator Smith somehow managed to increase his approval rating by 10%", say "In April 2012, Senator Smith\'s approval rating increased by 10%, which respondents attributed to his new position on foreign policy.<sup>[1]</sup>" Instead of saying "Senator Smith often discusses foreign policy in his speeches", say "Senator Smith discussed foreign policy during his election campaign, and subsequently during his victory speech at the State Convention Center.<sup>[2]</sup>"\n</p><p>Remember that Wikipedia is a global encyclopedia, and does not assume particular places or times are the "default". We emphasize facts and viewpoints to the same degree that they are emphasized by the reliable sources. Terms like <i>this country</i> should not be used.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Survived_by"><i>Survived by</i></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:What_Wikipedia_is_not#Wikipedia_is_not_a_blog,_Web_hosting_service,_social_networking_service,_or_memorial_site" title="Wikipedia:What Wikipedia is not">Wikipedia:What Wikipedia is not \xc2\xa7&#160;Wikipedia is not a blog, Web hosting service, social networking service, or memorial site</a>; and <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:What_Wikipedia_is_not#Wikipedia_is_not_a_newspaper" title="Wikipedia:What Wikipedia is not">Wikipedia:What Wikipedia is not \xc2\xa7&#160;Wikipedia is not a newspaper</a></div>\n<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1007196855"/><div class="module-shortcutanchordiv"><span id="MOS:SURVIVEDBY"></span></div><div class="module-shortcutboxplain plainlist noprint" role="note"><div class="module-shortcutlist"><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Shortcut" title="Wikipedia:Shortcut">Shortcut</a></div><ul><li><span class="plainlinks"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=MOS:SURVIVEDBY&amp;redirect=no">MOS:SURVIVEDBY</a></span></li></ul></div>\n<div class="quotebox pullquote centered" style="width:70%; ; background-color: #FFFFF0;"><link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1012376600"/>\n<div class="quotebox-quote left-aligned" style=""><span style="font-size: 120%;">Words to watch: <strong><i>is/was survived by</i>, <i>[Name]\'s survivors include</i>, &#160;...</strong> </span></div>\n</div>\n<p>Phrasing such as "Smith died in 1982, survived by her husband Jack and two sons" should be avoided; this information can be made more complete and spread out through the article. The "survived by" phrasing is a common way to end newspaper obituaries and legal death notices, and is relevant at the time of death or for inheritance purposes. But an encyclopedia article covers the subject\'s entire life, not just the event of their death. Information about children and spouses might be presented in an infobox or in sections about the subject\'s personal life. Readers can generally infer which family members died after the subject. Usually this information is not worth highlighting explicitly, except for unusual situations (for example where children predecease their parents, or where the inheritance was disputed).\n</p><p>Even in a <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Stub" title="Wikipedia:Stub">stub article</a>, a different arrangement with more details sounds more like an encyclopedia and less like an obituary: "Smith married Jack in 1957. The couple had two sons, Bill and Ted. She died in 1982."\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Person_or_office.3F"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Person_or_office?">Person or office?</span></h3>\n<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1007196855"/><div class="module-shortcutanchordiv"><span id="MOS:PERSONOROFFICE"></span></div><div class="module-shortcutboxplain plainlist noprint" role="note"><div class="module-shortcutlist"><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Shortcut" title="Wikipedia:Shortcut">Shortcut</a></div><ul><li><span class="plainlinks"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=MOS:PERSONOROFFICE&amp;redirect=no">MOS:PERSONOROFFICE</a></span></li></ul></div>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Biography#Positions,_offices,_and_occupational_titles" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Biography">Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Biography \xc2\xa7&#160;Positions, offices, and occupational titles</a></div>\n<p>It is necessary for a reference work to distinguish carefully between an office (such as <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">president of the United States</a>) and an incumbent (such as <a href="/wiki/Joe_Biden" title="Joe Biden">Joe Biden</a>); a newspaper does not usually need to make this distinction, for a newspaper "President Biden" and "the President" are one and the same during his tenure.\n</p>\n<ul><li><span class="example deprecated-content example-bad" style="font-family: Georgia, &#39;DejaVu Serif&#39;, serif; color: #8B0000;">President Biden nominates new justices of the US Supreme Court</span> \xe2\x80\x93 No; whoever is president at the time does.</li>\n<li><span class="example" style="font-family: Georgia, &#39;DejaVu Serif&#39;, serif; color: #006400;">President George W. Bush nominated John Roberts as Chief Justice</span> \xe2\x80\x93 Yes, as this will always be true.</li>\n<li><span class="example" style="font-family: Georgia, &#39;DejaVu Serif&#39;, serif; color: #006400;">The President nominated John Roberts as Chief Justice in 2005</span> \xe2\x80\x93 Yes, as the year makes this clear.</li>\n<li><span class="example example-neutral" style="font-family: Georgia, &#39;DejaVu Serif&#39;, serif; color: initial;">The guest list included Charles, Prince of Wales</span> \xe2\x80\x93 This is usually acceptable, as a confusion with <a href="/wiki/Charles_I_of_England" title="Charles I of England">Charles I of England</a>, Prince of Wales until 1625, is highly unlikely. In any event, "<a href="/wiki/Charles,_Prince_of_Wales" title="Charles, Prince of Wales">Charles, Prince of Wales</a>" will usually be <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Linking" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Linking">linked</a>.</li>\n<li><span class="example deprecated-content example-bad" style="font-family: Georgia, &#39;DejaVu Serif&#39;, serif; color: #8B0000;">Former president Richard Nixon met with Mao Zedong in 1972</span> \xe2\x80\x93 This is incorrect because Nixon was not a <em>former</em> president at the time; he was still in office. Write <span class="example" style="font-family: Georgia, &#39;DejaVu Serif&#39;, serif; color: #006400;">President Nixon met with Mao in 1972.</span> The construction <span class="example example-neutral" style="font-family: Georgia, &#39;DejaVu Serif&#39;, serif; color: initial;">then-President Nixon</span> is often superfluous, unless the context calls for distinctions between periods of Nixon\'s career, other holders of the office, or between other people also named Nixon.</li></ul>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Neologisms_and_new_compounds">Neologisms and new compounds</span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Wikipedia_is_not_a_dictionary#Neologisms" title="Wikipedia:Wikipedia is not a dictionary">Wikipedia:Wikipedia is not a dictionary \xc2\xa7&#160;Neologisms</a></div>\n<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1007196855"/><div class="module-shortcutanchordiv"><span id="MOS:NEO"></span></div><div class="module-shortcutboxplain plainlist noprint" role="note"><div class="module-shortcutlist"><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Shortcut" title="Wikipedia:Shortcut">Shortcut</a></div><ul><li><span class="plainlinks"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=MOS:NEO&amp;redirect=no">MOS:NEO</a></span></li></ul></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Neologism" title="Neologism">Neologisms</a> are expressions coined recently or in isolated circumstances to which they have remained restricted. In most cases, they do not appear in general-interest dictionaries, though they may be used routinely within certain communities or professions. They should generally be avoided because their definitions tend to be unstable and many do not last. Where the use of a neologism is necessary to describe recent developments in a certain field, its meaning must be supported by reliable sources.\n</p><p>Adding common prefixes or suffixes such as <i>pre-</i>, <i>post-</i>, <i>non-</i>, <i>anti-</i>, or <span class="nowrap"><i>-like</i></span> to existing words to create new compounds can aid brevity, but make sure the resulting terms are not misleading or offensive, and that they do not lend <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Neutral_point_of_view#Due_and_undue_weight" title="Wikipedia:Neutral point of view">undue weight</a> to a point of view. For instance, adding <span class="nowrap"><i>-ism</i></span> or <span class="nowrap"><i>-ist</i></span> to a word may suggest that a tenuous belief system is well-established, that a belief\'s adherents are particularly dogmatic or ideological (as in <i>abortionism</i>), or that factual statements are actually a matter of doctrine (as in <i>evolutionism</i>).  Some words, by their structure, can suggest extended forms that may turn out to be contentious (e.g. <i>lesbian</i> and <i>transgender</i> imply the longer words <i>lesbianism</i> and <i>transgenderism</i>, which are sometimes taken as offensive for seeming to imply a belief system or agenda).\n</p><p><link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1015928993"/><span role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable crossreference selfref">For additional guidance on <span class="nowrap"><i>-ist/-ism</i></span> terms, see <a href="#Contentious_labels">\xc2\xa7&#160;Contentious labels</a>, above.</span>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Easily_confused_terms">Easily confused terms</span></h3>\n<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1007196855"/><div class="module-shortcutanchordiv"><span id="MOS:CONFUSE"></span><span id="MOS:ARAB"></span></div><div class="module-shortcutboxplain plainlist noprint" role="note"><div class="module-shortcutlist"><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Shortcut" title="Wikipedia:Shortcut">Shortcuts</a></div><ul><li><span class="plainlinks"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=MOS:CONFUSE&amp;redirect=no">MOS:CONFUSE</a></span></li><li><span class="plainlinks"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=MOS:ARAB&amp;redirect=no">MOS:ARAB</a></span></li></ul></div>\n<p>Do not use similar or related words in a way that blurs meaning or is incorrect or distorting.\n</p><p>For example, the adjective <i>Arab</i> refers to people and things of <a href="/wiki/Arabs" title="Arabs">ethnic Arab</a> origin. The term <i>Arabic</i> generally refers to the <a href="/wiki/Arabic" title="Arabic">Arabic</a> language or writing system, and related concepts. <i>Arabian</i> relates to the <a href="/wiki/Arabian_peninsula" class="mw-redirect" title="Arabian peninsula">Arabian peninsula</a> or <a href="/wiki/Arabia_(disambiguation)" class="mw-disambig" title="Arabia (disambiguation)">historical Arabia</a>. (These terms are all <a href="/wiki/Proper_and_common_nouns" title="Proper and common nouns">capitalized</a>, e.g. <a href="/wiki/Arabic_script" title="Arabic script"><span class="example" style="font-family: Georgia, &#39;DejaVu Serif&#39;, serif; color: #006400;">Arabic script</span></a> and <a href="/wiki/Arabian_horse" title="Arabian horse"><span class="example" style="font-family: Georgia, &#39;DejaVu Serif&#39;, serif; color: #006400;">Arabian horse</span></a>, aside from a few <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Capital_letters#Peoples_and_their_languages" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Capital letters">conventionalized exceptions</a> that have lost their cultural connection, such as <a href="/wiki/Gum_arabic" title="Gum arabic"><span class="example" style="font-family: Georgia, &#39;DejaVu Serif&#39;, serif; color: #006400;">gum arabic</span></a>.) Do not substitute these terms for <i><a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">Islamic</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Muslims" title="Muslims">Muslim</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Islamism" title="Islamism">Islamist</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Ethnic_groups_in_the_Middle_East" title="Ethnic groups in the Middle East">Middle-eastern</a></i>, etc.; a Muslim Arab is someone who is in both categories.\n</p><p>Similar concerns pertain to many cultural, scientific, and other topics and the terminology used about them. When in doubt about a term, consult major modern dictionaries.\n</p>\n<h2><span id="Vulgarities.2C_obscenities.2C_and_profanities"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Vulgarities,_obscenities,_and_profanities">Vulgarities, obscenities, and profanities</span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main page: <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Offensive_material" title="Wikipedia:Offensive material">Wikipedia:Offensive material</a></div>\n<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1007196855"/><div class="module-shortcutanchordiv"><span id="MOS:VULGAR"></span></div><div class="module-shortcutboxplain plainlist noprint" role="note"><div class="module-shortcutlist"><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Shortcut" title="Wikipedia:Shortcut">Shortcut</a></div><ul><li><span class="plainlinks"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=MOS:VULGAR&amp;redirect=no">MOS:VULGAR</a></span></li></ul></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:NOTCENSORED" class="mw-redirect" title="Wikipedia:NOTCENSORED">Wikipedia is not censored</a>, and the inclusion of material that might offend is part of its <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Five_pillars" title="Wikipedia:Five pillars">purpose as an encyclopedia</a>. Quotes should always be verbatim and as they appear in the <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">original source</a>. However, language that is <a href="/wiki/Vulgarity" title="Vulgarity">vulgar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Obscene" class="mw-redirect" title="Obscene">obscene</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Profanity" title="Profanity">profane</a> should be used only if its omission would make an article less accurate or relevant, and if there is no non-obscene alternative.
