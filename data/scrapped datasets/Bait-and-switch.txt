First, customers are "baited" by merchants\' <a href="/wiki/Advertising" title="Advertising">advertising</a> products or services at a low price, but when customers visit the store, they discover that the advertised goods are not available, or the customers are pressured by salespeople to consider similar, but higher-priced items ("switching").\n</p><p>Bait-and-switch techniques have a long and widespread history as a part of commercial culture. Many variations on the bait-and-switch appear, for example, in China\'s earliest book of stories about fraud, Zhang Yingyu\'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Book_of_Swindles" title="The Book of Swindles">The Book of Swindles</a></i> (c. 1617).<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc" role="navigation" links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Function">Function</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bait-and-switch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Function">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The intention of the bait-and-switch is to encourage purchases of substituted goods, making consumers satisfied with the available stock offered, as an alternative to a disappointment or inconvenience of acquiring no goods (or bait) at all, and reckoning on a seemingly partial recovery of <a href="/wiki/Sunk_costs" class="mw-redirect" title="Sunk costs">sunk costs</a> expended trying to obtain the bait. It suggests that the seller will not show the original product or service advertised but instead will demonstrate a more expensive product or a similar product with a higher margin.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legality">Legality</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bait-and-switch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Legality">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, <a href="/wiki/Court" title="Court">courts</a> have held that the purveyor using a bait-and-switch operation may be subject to a <a href="/wiki/Lawsuit" title="Lawsuit">lawsuit</a> by customers for <a href="/wiki/False_advertising#Bait-and-switch" title="False advertising">false advertising</a>, and can be sued for <a href="/wiki/Trademark_infringement" title="Trademark infringement">trademark infringement</a> by competing manufacturers, retailers, and others who profit from the sale of the product used as bait. However, no <a href="/wiki/Cause_of_action" title="Cause of action">cause of action</a> will exist if the purveyor is capable of actually selling the goods advertised, but aggressively pushes a competing product.\n</p><p>Likewise, advertising a sale while intending to stock a limited amount of, and thereby sell out, a <a href="/wiki/Loss_leader" title="Loss leader">loss-leading</a> item advertised is legal in the United States.  The purveyor can escape liability if they make clear in their advertisements that quantities of items for which a sale is offered are limited, or by offering a <a href="/wiki/Rain_check_(idiom)" class="mw-redirect" title="Rain check (idiom)">rain check</a> on sold-out items.\n</p><p>In England and Wales, bait and switch is banned under the <a href="/wiki/Consumer_Protection_from_Unfair_Trading_Regulations_2008" title="Consumer Protection from Unfair Trading Regulations 2008">Consumer Protection from Unfair Trading Regulations 2008</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Breaking this law can result in a criminal prosecution, an unlimited fine and two years in jail.\nIn Canada, this tactic is illegal under the <i>Competition Act.</i> In Australia, bait advertising is illegal under the Competition and Consumer Act 2010<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> (formerly known as the Trade Practices Act 1974).\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Non-retail_use">Non-retail use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bait-and-switch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Non-retail use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<ul><li>On-line sellers use bait-and-switch by showing a photograph of a desired item to get sales, then shipping a cheaper copy of the item or a picture of the item.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Rickrolling" title="Rickrolling">Rickrolling</a> is a famous <a href="/wiki/Prank" class="mw-redirect" title="Prank">prank</a> and <a href="/wiki/Internet_meme" title="Internet meme">internet meme</a>, a type of bait-and-switch using a disguised <a href="/wiki/Hyperlink" title="Hyperlink">hyperlink</a>.</li>\n<li>Car dealerships and auto brokers have also been known to use various forms of bait-and-switch or similar tactics, such as advertising vehicles online at what seems like a bargain price, only for the customer to discover that the specific vehicle is no longer available, as well as adding on a plethora of additional fees or even changing the sale price when coming close to closing the sale.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Politics">Politics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
