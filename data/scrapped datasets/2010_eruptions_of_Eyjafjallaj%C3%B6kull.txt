data-file-height="726" /></a><div>Composite map of the volcanic ash cloud spanning 14\xe2\x80\x9325 April 2010</div></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p>The <b>2010 eruptions of Eyjafjallaj\xc3\xb6kull</b> were a period of volcanic events at <a href="/wiki/Eyjafjallaj%C3%B6kull" title="Eyjafjallaj\xc3\xb6kull">Eyjafjallaj\xc3\xb6kull</a> in <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a> which, although relatively small for volcanic eruptions, caused <a href="/wiki/Air_travel_disruption_after_the_2010_Eyjafjallaj%C3%B6kull_eruption" title="Air travel disruption after the 2010 Eyjafjallaj\xc3\xb6kull eruption">enormous disruption to air travel</a> across western and northern Europe over an initial period of six days in April 2010. Additional localised disruption continued into May 2010, and eruptive activity persisted until June 2010. The eruption was declared officially over in October 2010, after 3 months of inactivity, when snow on the glacier did not melt. From 14\xe2\x80\x9320 April, ash from the volcanic eruption covered large areas of Northern Europe. About 20 countries closed their airspace to commercial jet traffic and it affected approximately 10 million travellers.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Seismic activity started at the end of 2009 and gradually increased in intensity until on 20 March 2010, a small <a href="/wiki/Volcanic_eruption" class="mw-redirect" title="Volcanic eruption">eruption</a> began, rated as a 1 on the <a href="/wiki/Volcanic_Explosivity_Index" title="Volcanic Explosivity Index">volcanic explosivity index</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Beginning on 14 April 2010, the eruption entered a second phase and created an <a href="/wiki/Eruption_column" title="Eruption column">ash cloud</a> that led to the closure of most of the European <a href="/wiki/Instrument_Flight_Rules" class="mw-redirect" title="Instrument Flight Rules">IFR</a> airspace from 15 until 20 April 2010. Consequently, a very high proportion of flights within, to, and from Europe were cancelled, creating the highest level of <a href="/wiki/Air_travel_disruption_after_the_2010_Eyjafjallaj%C3%B6kull_eruption" title="Air travel disruption after the 2010 Eyjafjallaj\xc3\xb6kull eruption">air travel disruption</a> since the Second World War. The second phase resulted in an estimated 250&#160;million cubic metres (330,000,000&#160;cu&#160;yd) (0.25&#160;km<sup>3</sup>) of ejected <a href="/wiki/Tephra" title="Tephra">tephra</a> and an ash plume that rose to a height around 9&#160;km (5.6&#160;mi), which rates the explosive power of the eruption as a 4 on the volcanic explosivity index.<sup id="cite_ref-smithsonianmag.com_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-smithsonianmag.com-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> By 21 May 2010, the second eruption phase had subsided to the point that no further lava or ash was being produced.\n</p><p>By the evening of 6 June 2010, a small, new crater had opened up on the west side of the main crater. Explosive activity from this new crater was observed with emission of small quantities of ash.<sup id="cite_ref-www2.hi.is_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-www2.hi.is-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Seismic data showed that the frequency and intensity of earth tremors still exceeded the levels observed before the eruption, therefore scientists at the Icelandic Meteorological Office<sup id="cite_ref-vedur3_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vedur3-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> (IMO) and the Institute of Earth Sciences, <a href="/wiki/University_of_Iceland" title="University of Iceland">University of Iceland</a><sup id="cite_ref-earthice2_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-earthice2-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> (IES) continued to monitor the volcano.\n</p><p>In October 2010, \xc3\x81rmann H\xc3\xb6skuldsson, a scientist at the University of Iceland Institute of Earth Sciences, stated that the eruption was officially over, although the area was still geothermally active and might erupt again.<sup id="cite_ref-icelandreview.com_7-0" class="reference"><a style="font-size:85%">(<span class="unicode haudio"><span class="fn"><span style="white-space:nowrap;margin-right:.25em;"><a href="/wiki/File:Is-Eyjafjallaj%C3%B6kull.oga" title="About this 2x" data-file-width="20" data-file-height="20" /></a></span><a href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/cf/Is-Eyjafjallaj%C3%B6kull.oga" class="internal" Situated to the north of <a href="/wiki/Sk%C3%B3gar" title="Sk\xc3\xb3gar">Sk\xc3\xb3gar</a> and to the west of the larger ice cap <a href="/wiki/M%C3%BDrdalsj%C3%B6kull" title="M\xc3\xbdrdalsj\xc3\xb6kull">M\xc3\xbdrdalsj\xc3\xb6kull</a>, Eyjafjallaj\xc3\xb6kull covers the <a href="/wiki/Caldera" title="Caldera">caldera</a> of a volcano 1,666&#160;m (5,466&#160;ft) high, which has erupted relatively frequently since the last <a href="/wiki/Ice_age" title="Ice age">ice age</a>. The most recent major eruptions occurred in 920, 1612, and from 1821 to 1823.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Previous eruptions of Eyjafjallaj\xc3\xb6kull have been followed by eruptions at its larger neighbour, <a href="/wiki/Katla_volcano" class="mw-redirect" title="Katla volcano">Katla</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> On 20 April 2010 Icelandic President <a href="/wiki/%C3%93lafur_Ragnar_Gr%C3%ADmsson" title="\xc3\x93lafur Ragnar Gr\xc3\xadmsson">\xc3\x93lafur Gr\xc3\xadmsson</a> said, "the time for Katla to erupt is coming close ... we [Iceland] have prepared ... it is high time for European governments and airline authorities all over the world to start planning for the eventual Katla eruption."<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The volcanic events starting in March 2010 were considered to be a single eruption divided into phases. The first eruption phase ejected <a href="/wiki/Olivine" title="Olivine">olivine</a> <a href="/wiki/Basaltic_andesite" title="Basaltic andesite">basaltic andesite</a> lava<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> several hundred metres into the air in what is known as an <a href="/wiki/Effusive_eruption" title="Effusive eruption">effusive eruption</a>. Ash ejection from this phase of the eruption was small, rising to no more than 4&#160;km (2.5&#160;mi) into the atmosphere.\n</p><p>On 14 April 2010, however, the eruption entered an <a href="/wiki/Explosive_eruption" title="Explosive eruption">explosive</a> phase and ejected fine glass-rich ash to over 8&#160;km (5.0&#160;mi) into the atmosphere. The second phase was estimated to be a <a href="/wiki/VEI" class="mw-redirect" title="VEI">VEI</a> 4 eruption, which was large, but not nearly the most powerful eruption possible by volcanic standards. By way of comparison, the <a href="/wiki/Mount_St._Helens" title="Mount St. Helens">Mount St. Helens</a> <a href="/wiki/1980_eruption_of_Mount_St._Helens" title="1980 eruption of Mount St. Helens">eruption of 1980</a> was rated as 5 on the VEI, and the <a href="/wiki/1991_eruption_of_Mount_Pinatubo" title="1991 eruption of Mount Pinatubo">1991 eruption</a> of <a href="/wiki/Mount_Pinatubo" title="Mount Pinatubo">Mount Pinatubo</a> was rated as a 6. This second phase erupted <a href="/wiki/Trachyandesite" title="Trachyandesite">trachyandesite</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>This volcanic activity so disruptive to air travel because of a combination of factors:<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p>\n<ol><li>The volcano is directly under the <a href="/wiki/Jet_stream" title="Jet stream">jet stream</a>.</li>\n<li>The direction of the jet stream was unusually stable at the time of the eruption\'s second phase, continuously southeast.</li>\n<li>The second eruptive phase happened under 200&#160;m (660&#160;ft) of glacial ice. The resulting meltwater flowed back into the erupting volcano, which created two specific phenomena:\n<ol><li>The rapidly vaporising water significantly increased the eruption\'s explosive power.</li>\n<li>The erupting lava cooled very fast, which created a cloud of highly abrasive, glass-rich ash.</li></ol></li>\n<li>The volcano\'s explosive power was enough to inject ash directly into the jet stream.</li></ol>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_observations">Public observations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a observing the first fissure at <a href="/wiki/Fimmv%C3%B6r%C3%B0uh%C3%A1ls" title="Fimmv\xc3\xb6r\xc3\xb0uh\xc3\xa1ls">Fimmv\xc3\xb6r\xc3\xb0uh\xc3\xa1ls</a></div></div></div>\n<p>"Volcano tourism" quickly sprang up in the wake of the eruption, with local tour companies offering day trips to see the volcano.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Civil_Protection_Department&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="The Civil Protection Department (page does not exist)">The Civil Protection Teams from the <a href="/wiki/Icelandic_Association_for_Search_and_Rescue" title="Icelandic Association for Search and Rescue">Icelandic Association for Search and Rescue</a> were stationed at the eruption site as part of standard safety measures and to assist in enforcing access restrictions.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Vodafone" title="Vodafone">Vodafone</a> and the Icelandic telecommunications company M\xc3\xadla installed <a href="/wiki/Webcams" class="mw-redirect" title="Webcams">webcams</a>, giving views of the eruption from Valahn\xc3\xbakur, <a href="/wiki/Hvolsv%C3%B6llur" title="Hvolsv\xc3\xb6llur">Hvolsv\xc3\xb6llur</a>, and <a href="/wiki/%C3%9E%C3%B3r%C3%B3lfsfell" title="\xc3\x9e\xc3\xb3r\xc3\xb3lfsfell">\xc3\x9e\xc3\xb3r\xc3\xb3lfsfell</a>. The view of the eruption from \xc3\x9e\xc3\xb3r\xc3\xb3lfsfel also includes a thermal imaging camera.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Scientific_observations">Scientific observations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2010_eruptions_of_Eyjafjallaj%C3%B6kull&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Scientific observations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The London Volcanic Ash Advisory Centre (VAAC),<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> part of the UK <a href="/wiki/Met_Office" title="Met Office">Met Office</a>, was responsible for forecasting the presence of volcanic ash in the north-east Atlantic. All ash dispersion models for this geographic region were produced by the VAAC in London.\n</p><p>A study by the Icelandic Meteorological Office published on December 2009 indicated an increase in <a href="/wiki/Seismic_activity" class="mw-redirect" title="Seismic activity">seismic activity</a> around the Eyjafjallaj\xc3\xb6kull area during the years 2006\xe2\x80\x932009. The study reported increased activity that occurred between June and August 2009 (200&#160;events), compared to a total of about 250 earthquakes recorded between September 2006 and August 2009. It further indicated that the locations of most of the earthquakes in 2009 occurred between 8 to 12&#160;km (5.0 to 7.5&#160;mi) depth east of the volcano\'s top crater.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> At the end of December 2009, seismic activity began around the Eyjafjallaj\xc3\xb6kull volcano area, with thousands of small earthquakes (mostly of magnitude 1\xe2\x80\x932&#160;<a href="/wiki/Moment_magnitude_scale" title="Moment magnitude scale">M<sub>w</sub></a>), 7 to 10&#160;km (4.3 to 6.2&#160;mi) beneath the volcano.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The radar stations of the Meteorological Institute of Iceland did not detect any appreciable amount of <a href="/wiki/Volcanic_ash" title="Volcanic ash">volcanic ashfall</a> during the first 24&#160;hours of the eruption.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> However, during the night of 22 March, they reported some volcanic ash fall reaching the Flj\xc3\xb3tshl\xc3\xad\xc3\xb0 area (20 to 25&#160;km (12 to 16&#160;mi) north-west of the eruption\'s location)<sup id="cite_ref-Fasteignaskra_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fasteignaskra-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> and Hvolsv\xc3\xb6llur town (40 kilometres (25&#160;mi) north-west of the eruption location)<sup id="cite_ref-Fasteignaskra_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fasteignaskra-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> leaving vehicles with a fine, grey layer of volcanic ash. At around 07:00 on 22 March, an explosion launched eruption columns as far as 4&#160;km (2.5&#160;mi) straight up into the air. This was the highest plume since the eruption started.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> On 23 March, a small vapour explosion took place, when hot magma came into contact with nearby snowdrifts, emitting a vapour plume which reached an altitude of 7&#160;km (4.3&#160;mi), and was detected on radar from the Meteorological Institute of Iceland. After that, many further vapour explosions occurred.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>By 26 February 2010, the <a href="/wiki/Global_positioning_system" class="mw-redirect" title="Global positioning system">global positioning system</a> (GPS) equipment used by the Iceland Meteorological Office<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> at \xc3\x9eorvaldseyri farm in the Eyjafj\xc3\xb6ll area (around 15&#160;km (9.3&#160;mi) south-east of the location of the recent eruption)<sup id="cite_ref-Fasteignaskra_19-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fasteignaskra-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> had shown 3&#160;cm of displacement of the local <a href="/wiki/Crust_(geology)" title="Crust (geology)">crust</a> in a southward direction, of which a 1-cm displacement had taken place within four days. (See the <i>GPS Time Series</i><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> page of the Nordic Volcanological Center\'s<sup id="cite_ref-Nordic_Volcanological_Institute_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nordic_Volcanological_Institute-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> website for detailed information on the degree of movement detected in the Earth\'s crust in the Eyjafjallaj\xc3\xb6kull locality.)\n</p><p>This unusual seismic activity, along with the rapid movement of the Earth\'s crust in the area, gave <a href="/wiki/Geophysicist" class="mw-redirect" title="Geophysicist">geophysicists</a> evidence that magma was flowing from underneath the crust into Eyjafjallaj\xc3\xb6kull\'s <a href="/wiki/Magma_chamber" title="Magma chamber">magma chamber</a> and that pressure stemming from the process caused (in geophysical terms) the huge crustal displacement at \xc3\x9eorvaldseyri farm.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> The seismic activity continued to increase, and from 3 to 5 March, close to 3,000&#160;earthquakes were measured having their epicentre at the volcano. Most of these were too small (magnitude&#160;2) to be interpreted as precursors to an eruption, but some could be detected in nearby towns.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The grounding of European flights avoided about 3.44<span style="margin:0 .15em 0 .25em">\xc3\x97</span>10<sup><span style="white-space:nowrap">8</span></sup>&#160;kg of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">CO<sub>2</sub></a> emissions per day, while the volcano emitted about 1.5<span style="margin:0 .15em 0 .25em">\xc3\x97</span>10<sup><span style="white-space:nowrap">8</span></sup>&#160;kg of CO<sub>2</sub> per day.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Phase_1:_Effusive_eruption">Phase 1: Effusive eruption</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2010_eruptions_of_Eyjafjallaj%C3%B6kull&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Phase 1: Effusive eruption">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The first phase of the eruption lasted from 20 March to 12 April 2010 and was characterised by olivine basaltic andesite lava flowing from various eruptive vents on the flanks of the mountain.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Evacuations">Evacuations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2010_eruptions_of_Eyjafjallaj%C3%B6kull&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Evacuations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>About 500 farmers and their families had to escape from the areas of <a href="/wiki/Flj%C3%B3tshl%C3%AD%C3%B0" title="Flj\xc3\xb3tshl\xc3\xad\xc3\xb0">Flj\xc3\xb3tshl\xc3\xad\xc3\xb0</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eyjafj%C3%B6ll" class="mw-redirect" title="Eyjafj\xc3\xb6ll">Eyjafj\xc3\xb6ll</a>, and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Landeyjar&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Landeyjar (page does not exist)">Landeyjar</a> were evacuated overnight (including a group of 30 schoolchildren and their three teachers<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> from <a postponed, but on the evening of 21 March, domestic and international air traffic was allowed again.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> Inhabitants of the risk zone of Flj\xc3\xb3tshl\xc3\xad\xc3\xb0, Eyjafj\xc3\xb6ll, and Landeyjar area were allowed to return to their farms and homes after an evening meeting with the <a href="/wiki/List_of_Icelandic_ministries" Instead, the police closed the road to <a href="/wiki/%C3%9E%C3%B3rsm%C3%B6rk" class="mw-redirect" title="\xc3\x9e\xc3\xb3rsm\xc3\xb6rk">\xc3\x9e\xc3\xb3rsm\xc3\xb6rk</a> and the four-wheel-drive trail from <a href="/wiki/Sk%C3%B3gar" title="Sk\xc3\xb3gar">Sk\xc3\xb3gar</a> village to the Fimmv\xc3\xb6r\xc3\xb0uh\xc3\xa1ls mountain pass, but these roads and trails were reopened on 29 March, though only for suitable four-wheel drive vehicles. When the second fissure appeared, the road was closed again because of the danger of <a href="/wiki/Flash_flood" title="Flash flood">flash floods</a>, which could have developed if the fissure had opened near big ice caps or other snow reservoirs, but the road was again opened at around noon on 1 April.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects_on_the_river">Effects on the river</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2010_eruptions_of_Eyjafjallaj%C3%B6kull&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Effects on the river">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>On 22 March, a <a href="/wiki/Flow_meter" class="mw-redirect" title="Flow meter">flow meter</a> device in the Kross\xc3\xa1 glacial river (which drains Eyjafjallaj\xc3\xb6kull and M\xc3\xbdrdalsj\xc3\xb6kull glaciers) in the \xc3\x9e\xc3\xb3rsm\xc3\xb6rk area (a few kilometres north-west of the erupting location) started to record a sudden rise in water level and water temperature \xe2\x80\x93 the total water temperature rose by 6&#160;\xc2\xb0C (11&#160;\xc2\xb0F) over a two-hour period, which had never happened so quickly in the Kross\xc3\xa1 river since measurements began. Shortly afterward, the water level returned to normal and water temperature decreased.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> This rise in water temperature was thought to be related to the eruption nearby and was affecting part of the Kross\xc3\xa1 <a href="/wiki/Drainage_basin" title="Drainage basin">drainage basin</a>. The temperature of Hrun\xc3\xa1 river, which flows through the narrow Hrun\xc3\xa1rgil canyon, into which part of the lava stream was flowing, was recently recorded by geologists to be between 50 and 60&#160;\xc2\xb0C (122 and 140&#160;\xc2\xb0F), indicating that the river was cooling the lava in that canyon.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fissure">Fissure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a north, on 2 April 2010</div></div></div>\n<p>The first phase of the 2010 eruption began late on the evening of 20 March at the Eyjafjallaj\xc3\xb6kull.\n</p><p>The initial visual report of the eruption was at 23:52&#160;GMT, when a red cloud was seen at the north slopes of <a href="/wiki/Fimmv%C3%B6r%C3%B0uh%C3%A1ls" title="Fimmv\xc3\xb6r\xc3\xb0uh\xc3\xa1ls">Fimmv\xc3\xb6r\xc3\xb0uh\xc3\xa1ls</a> <a href="/wiki/Mountain_pass" title="Mountain pass">mountain pass</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a The eruption was preceded by intense seismicity and high rates of <a href="/wiki/Deformation_(volcanology)" title="Deformation (volcanology)">deformation</a> in the weeks before the eruption, in association with magma recharging of the volcano. Immediately before the eruption, the depth of the seismicity had become shallow, but was not significantly enhanced from what it had been in the previous weeks. Deformation was occurring at rates up to a centimetre a day since 4 March at various <a href="/wiki/GPS" class="mw-redirect" title="GPS">GPS</a> sites installed within 12&#160;km (7.5&#160;mi) from the eruptive site.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>A <a href="/wiki/Fissure_vent" title="Fissure vent">fissure</a> opened up about 150 metres (490&#160;ft) in length running in a north-east to south-west direction, with 10 to 12 erupting <a href="/wiki/Lava" title="Lava">lava</a> craters ejecting lava at a temperature around 1,000&#160;\xc2\xb0C (1,800&#160;\xc2\xb0F) up to 150&#160;m (490&#160;ft) into the air. The lava was alkali olivine <a href="/wiki/Basalt" title="Basalt">basalt</a><sup id="cite_ref-earthice1_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-earthice1-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> and was relatively <a href="/wiki/Viscous" class="mw-redirect" title="Viscous">viscous</a>, causing the motion of the lava stream to the west and east of the fissure to be slow. The molten lava flowed more than 4,000&#160;m (13,000&#160;ft) to the north-east of the fissure and into Hrunagil canyon, forming a lava fall more than 200&#160;m (660&#160;ft) long and slowly approaching <a href="/wiki/%C3%9E%C3%B3rsm%C3%B6rk" class="mw-redirect" title="\xc3\x9e\xc3\xb3rsm\xc3\xb6rk">\xc3\x9e\xc3\xb3rsm\xc3\xb6rk</a>, but had not yet<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=2010_eruptions_of_Eyjafjallaj%C3%B6kull&amp;action=edit">&#91;update&#93;</a></sup> reached the <a href="/wiki/Flood_plain" class="mw-redirect" title="Flood plain">flood plains</a> of <a href="/wiki/Kross%C3%A1_(Markarflj%C3%B3t)" class="mw-redirect" title="Kross\xc3\xa1 (Markarflj\xc3\xb3t)">Kross\xc3\xa1</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>On 25 March 2010, while studying the eruption, <a href="/wiki/Volcanology" title="Volcanology">scientists</a> witnessed, for the first time in history, the formation of a <a href="/wiki/Pseudocrater" class="mw-redirect" title="Pseudocrater">pseudocrater</a> during a <a href="/wiki/Steam_explosion" title="Steam explosion">steam explosion</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> Crustal expansion continued at \xc3\x9eorvaldseyri for two days after the eruption began, but was slowly decreasing whilst the volcanic activity was increasing. This indicates that the rate at which magma was flowing into the magma chamber roughly equaled the rate at which it was being lost due to the eruption, giving evidence that this phase of volcanic activity reached equilibrium.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>A new fissure opened on 31 March, around 200&#160;m (660&#160;ft) north-west of the original fissure.<sup id="cite_ref-guardian.co.uk_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-guardian.co.uk-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> Many witnesses were present while the new fissure opened. It was a bit smaller, around 300&#160;m (980&#160;ft) long according to witnesses, and lava coming from it started to flow into Hvann\xc3\xa1rgil canyon. These two erupting fissures shared the same magma chamber, according to geophysicists. No unusual seismic activity was detected at the time the new fissure appeared, nor any crustal expansion according to many <a href="/wiki/Seismometer" title="Seismometer">seismometers</a> and GPS recorders situated in nearby areas.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Geophysicist Magn\xc3\xbas Tumi Einarsson said (at a press meeting in <a href="/wiki/Hvolsv%C3%B6llur" title="Hvolsv\xc3\xb6llur">Hvolsv\xc3\xb6llur</a> on 21 March) that this eruption was small compared to, for example, the eruption of <a href="/wiki/Hekla" title="Hekla">Hekla</a> in 2000. The eruption, rather than taking place under the ice cap of the glacier, occurred in the mountain pass between the Eyjafjallaj\xc3\xb6kull and <a href="/wiki/M%C3%BDrdalsj%C3%B6kull" title="M\xc3\xbdrdalsj\xc3\xb6kull">M\xc3\xbdrdalsj\xc3\xb6kull</a> glaciers. As long as the fissure was not near the glacier, the risk of flooding was minimal; however, the fissure could extend into the ice cap, thereby greatly increasing the risk of flooding.<sup Where ash fall was significant, all horses had to be sheltered indoors.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup> The thick layer of ash that had fallen on some Icelandic pastures and farms at Raufarfell had become wet and compact, making it very difficult to continue farming, <a href="/wiki/Harvest" title="Harvest">harvesting</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Livestock_feed" class="mw-redirect" title="Livestock feed">grazing livestock</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Timeline_of_the_second_eruption_phase">Timeline of the second eruption phase</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2010_eruptions_of_Eyjafjallaj%C3%B6kull&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Timeline of the second eruption phase">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Unlike the earlier eruption phase, the second phase occurred beneath glacial ice. The people who lived near the volcano had high levels of irritation symptoms, though their lung function was not lower than expected.<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a plume on air travel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2010_eruptions_of_Eyjafjallaj%C3%B6kull&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" Eyjafjallaj\xc3\xb6kull eruption</a></div>\n<p>Volcanic ash is a major hazard to aircraft.<sup id="cite_ref-RILEY_66-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RILEY-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup> Smoke and href="/wiki/Sandblast" class="mw-redirect" title="Sandblast">sandblast</a> windscreens and melt in the heat of aircraft <a href="/wiki/Turbine_engine" class="mw-redirect" title="Turbine engine">turbine engines</a>, damaging engines and making them shut down.<sup id="cite_ref-Guardian_ash_halts_flights_56-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian_ash_halts_flights-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-RILEY_66-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RILEY-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup> Many flights within, to, and from Europe were cancelled following 14 April 2010 eruption, and although no commercial aircraft were damaged, the engines of some military aircraft were harmed.<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup> The presence and location of the plume depends upon the state of the eruption and the winds. While some ash fell on uninhabited areas in Iceland, most had been carried by westerly winds resulting in the shutdown of large air space over Europe. The shutdown had a <a href="/wiki/Effects_of_the_April_2010_Eyjafjallaj%C3%B6kull_eruption" title="Effects of the April 2010 Eyjafjallaj\xc3\xb6kull eruption">knock on impact on the economy and cultural events</a> across Europe. The Icelandic flag carrier airline, <a href="/wiki/Icelandair" title="Icelandair">Icelandair</a>, seemed at first especially vulnerable, but managed to deal effectively with the eruption and subsequently published a detailed report about its actions and conclusions.<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Short-_and_long-term_weather_and_environmental_effects">Short- and long-term weather and environmental effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2010_eruptions_of_Eyjafjallaj%C3%B6kull&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Short- and long-term weather and environmental effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a 2010</div></div></div>\n<p>At the mouth of the crater, the gases, ejecta, and volcanic plume have created a rare <a href="/wiki/Meteorological_phenomenon" class="mw-redirect" title="Meteorological phenomenon">weather phenomenon</a> known as <a href="/wiki/Dirty_thunderstorm" class="mw-redirect" title="Dirty thunderstorm">volcanic lightning</a> (or a "<a href="/wiki/Dirty_thunderstorm" class="mw-redirect" title="Dirty thunderstorm">dirty thunderstorm</a>").<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup> When rocks and other ejecta collide with one another, they create static electricity. This, with the abundant water-ice at the summit, aids in making <a href="/wiki/Lightning" title="Lightning">lightning</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">&#91;71&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>High-fluoride <a href="/wiki/Hekla" title="Hekla">Hekla</a> eruptions pose a threat to foraging livestock, especially sheep. <a href="/wiki/Skeletal_fluorosis" title="Skeletal fluorosis">Fluoride poisoning</a> can start in sheep at a diet with fluorine content of 25&#160;ppm. At 250&#160;ppm, death can occur within a few days.<sup id="cite_ref-toxsci.oxfordjournals.org_53-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-toxsci.oxfordjournals.org-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup> In 1783, 79% of the <a href="/wiki/Icelandic_sheep" title="Icelandic sheep">Icelandic sheep</a> stock were killed, probably as a result of fluorosis caused by the <a href="/wiki/1783_eruption_of_Laki" class="mw-redirect" title="1783 eruption of Laki">eruption of Laki</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">&#91;72&#93;</a></sup> The effect also spread beyond Iceland.<sup id="cite_ref-lakibbc_73-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lakibbc-73">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup> Ash from the current Eyjafjallaj\xc3\xb6kull eruption contains one-third the concentration typical in Hekla eruptions, with a <a href="/wiki/Mean_value" class="mw-redirect" title="Mean value">mean value</a> of 104&#160;mg of fluoride per kg of ash. Large-scale release of sulphur dioxide into the troposphere also poses a potential health risk, especially to people with pre-existing breathing disorders.\n</p><p>As of 15 April, the eruption was not large enough to have an effect on global temperatures like that of <a href="/wiki/Mount_Pinatubo" title="Mount Pinatubo">Mount Pinatubo</a> and other major past volcanic eruptions.<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">&#91;74&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75">&#91;75&#93;</a></sup> One <a href="/wiki/Eyjafjallaj%C3%B6kull#1821_to_1823_eruptions" title="Eyjafjallaj\xc3\xb6kull">previous related sequence of eruptions of this volcano, beginning in 1821</a> is recorded as having lasted for over two years, but no single set of major eruptions is known to have lasted more than \'several days\'.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Comparison_to_past_eruptions">Comparison to past eruptions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2010_eruptions_of_Eyjafjallaj%C3%B6kull&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Comparison to past eruptions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The eruptions of Eyjafjallaj\xc3\xb6kull and the largest ash plume associated with the second eruption phase were not unparalleled in either volume or abundance; however, the location was the critical factor because it affected air travel across Europe. Neither phase of the eruption was unusually powerful.<sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76">&#91;76&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77">&#91;77&#93;</a></sup> Other notable volcanic eruptions include the eruption of Mount Pinatubo of 1991 of VEI&#160;6.<sup id="cite_ref-smithsonianmag.com_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-smithsonianmag.com-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> This eruption lasted eight days, from 7&#160;\xe2\x80\x93&#160;15 June of that year, with an ash cloud that would have required additional days to dissipate,<sup id="cite_ref-PinatuboComparison_78-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PinatuboComparison-78">&#91;78&#93;</a></sup> and resulted in worldwide abnormal weather and decrease in global temperature over the next few years. However, the second phase of Eyjafjallaj\xc3\xb6kull\'s eruption lasted longer than that of Mount Pinatubo.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span
