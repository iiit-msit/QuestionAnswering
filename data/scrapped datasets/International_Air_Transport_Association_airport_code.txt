flight heading to <a href="/wiki/Oral_Ak_Zhol_Airport" title="Oral Ak Zhol Airport">Oral Ak Zhol Airport</a>, whose IATA airport code is "URA".</div></div></div>\n<p>An <b>IATA airport code</b>, also known as an <b>IATA location identifier</b>, <b>IATA station code</b>, or simply a <b><a href="/wiki/Location_identifier" title="Location identifier">location identifier</a></b>, is a three-letter <a href="/wiki/Geocode" title="Geocode">geocode</a> designating many <a href="/wiki/Airport" title="Airport">airports</a> and <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_area" title="Metropolitan area">metropolitan areas</a> around the world, defined by the <a href="/wiki/International_Air_Transport_Association" title="International Air Transport Association">International Air Transport Association</a> (IATA). The characters prominently displayed on <a href="/wiki/Bag_tag" title="Bag tag">baggage tags</a> attached at airport check-in desks are an example of a way these codes are used.\n</p><p>The assignment of these codes is governed by IATA Resolution 763, and it is administered by the IATA\'s headquarters in <a href="/wiki/Montreal" title="Montreal">Montreal</a>. The codes are published semi-annually in the IATA Airline Coding Directory.\n</p><p>IATA also provides codes for <a href="/wiki/Railway" class="mw-redirect" title="Railway">railway</a> stations and for airport handling entities. <a href="/wiki/List_of_airports_by_IATA_and_ICAO_code" title="List of airports by IATA and ICAO code">Alphabetical lists of airports</a> sorted by IATA code are available. A <a href="/wiki/List_of_IATA-indexed_railway_stations" title="List of IATA-indexed railway stations">list of railway station codes</a>, shared in agreements between airlines and rail lines such as <a href="/wiki/Amtrak" title="Amtrak">Amtrak</a>, <a href="/wiki/SNCF" title="SNCF">SNCF French Railways</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Deutsche_Bahn" title="Deutsche Bahn">Deutsche Bahn</a>, is available. Many railway administrations have their own list of codes for their stations, such as the <a href="/wiki/List_of_Amtrak_stations" title="List of Amtrak stations">list of Amtrak station codes</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="List">List</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=IATA_airport_code&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: List">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Lists_of_airports" title="Lists of airports">List of airports</a> by <a href="/wiki/International_Air_Transport_Association_code" title="International Air Transport Association code">IATA airport code</a>: <a href="/wiki/List_of_airports_by_IATA_airport_code:_A" title="List of airports by IATA airport code: A">A</a> - Initially, pilots in the United States used the two-letter code from the <a href="/wiki/National_Weather_Service" title="National Weather Service">National Weather Service</a> (NWS) for identifying cities. This system became unmanageable for cities and towns without an NWS identifier, and the use of two letters allowed only a few hundred combinations; a three-letter system of airport codes was implemented. This system allowed for 17,576 permutations, assuming all letters can be used in conjunction with each other.<sup id="cite_ref-sky_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sky-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Naming_conventions">Naming conventions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=IATA_airport_code&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Naming conventions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Predominantly, airport codes are named after the first three letters of the city in which it is located\xe2\x80\x94<a href="/wiki/Hartsfield%E2%80%93Jackson_Atlanta_International_Airport" title="Hartsfield\xe2\x80\x93Jackson Atlanta International Airport">ATL</a> for <a href="/wiki/Atlanta" title="Atlanta">Atlanta</a>, <a href="/wiki/Singapore_Changi_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Singapore Changi Airport">SIN</a> for <a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">Singapore</a>, <a href="/wiki/Silvio_Pettirossi_International_Airport" title="Silvio Pettirossi International Airport">ASU</a> for <a href="/wiki/Asunci%C3%B3n" title="Asunci\xc3\xb3n">Asunci\xc3\xb3n</a>, <a href="/wiki/Benito_Ju%C3%A1rez_International_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Benito Ju\xc3\xa1rez International Airport">MEX</a> for <a href="/wiki/Mexico_City" title="Mexico City">Mexico City</a>, <a href="/wiki/Denver_International_Airport" title="Denver International Airport">DEN</a> for <a href="/wiki/Denver" title="Denver">Denver</a>; <a href="/wiki/Istanbul_Airport" title="Istanbul Airport">IST</a> for <a href="/wiki/Istanbul" title="Istanbul">Istanbul</a>; or a combination of the letters in its name, <a href="/wiki/Almaty_International_Airport" title="Almaty International Airport">ALA</a> for <a href="/wiki/Almaty" title="Almaty">Almaty</a> (formerly known as Alma-Ata), <a href="/wiki/Cork_Airport" title="Cork Airport">ORK</a> for <a href="/wiki/Cork_(city)" title="Cork (city)">Cork</a>, <a href="/wiki/Newark_Liberty_International_Airport" title="Newark Liberty International Airport">EWR</a> for <a href="/wiki/Newark,_New_Jersey" title="Newark, New Jersey">Newark</a>, <a href="/wiki/Miguel_Hidalgo_y_Costilla_International_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Miguel Hidalgo y Costilla International Airport">GDL</a> for <a href="/wiki/Guadalajara" title="Guadalajara">Guadalajara</a>, <a href="/wiki/O._R._Tambo_International_Airport" title="O. R. Tambo International Airport">JNB</a> for <a href="/wiki/Johannesburg" title="Johannesburg">Johannesburg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong_International_Airport" title="Hong Kong International Airport">HKG</a> for <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong" title="Hong Kong">Hong Kong</a>, <a href="/wiki/Salt_Lake_City_International_Airport" title="Salt Lake City International Airport">SLC</a> for <a href="/wiki/Salt_Lake_City" title="Salt Lake City">Salt Lake City</a> and <a href="/wiki/Warsaw_Chopin_Airport" title="Warsaw Chopin Airport">WAW</a> for <a href="/wiki/Warsaw" title="Warsaw">Warsaw</a>. Some airports in the United States retained their NWS codes and simply appended an X at the end, such as <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_International_Airport" title="Los Angeles International Airport">LAX</a> for <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Portland_International_Airport" title="Portland International Airport">PDX</a> for <a href="/wiki/Portland,_Oregon" title="Portland, Oregon">Portland</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Phoenix_Sky_Harbor_International_Airport" title="Phoenix Sky Harbor International Airport">PHX</a> for <a href="/wiki/Phoenix,_Arizona" title="Phoenix, Arizona">Phoenix</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-sky_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sky-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Sometimes the airport code reflects pronunciation, rather than spelling, such as <a href="/wiki/Nadi_International_Airport" title="Nadi International Airport">NAN</a>, which reflects the pronunciation of "Nadi" as <small></small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Fijian" title="Help:IPA/Fijian">[\xcb\x88nandi]</a></span> in <a href="/wiki/Fijian_language" title="Fijian language">Fijian</a>, where "d" is realized as the <a href="/wiki/Prenasalized_consonant" title="Prenasalized consonant">prenasalized stop</a> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[\xe2\x81\xbfd]</span>.\n</p><p>For many reasons, some airport codes do not fit the normal scheme described above. Some airports, for example, cross several municipalities or regions, and therefore, use codes derived from some of their letters, resulting in DFW for <b><a href="/wiki/Dallas/Fort_Worth_International_Airport" title="Dallas/Fort Worth International Airport">D</a></b><a href="/wiki/Dallas/Fort_Worth_International_Airport" title="Dallas/Fort Worth International Airport">allas/<b>F</b>ort <b>W</b>orth</a>, DTW for <b><a href="/wiki/Detroit_Metropolitan_Wayne_County_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Detroit Metropolitan Wayne County Airport">D</a></b><a href="/wiki/Detroit_Metropolitan_Wayne_County_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Detroit Metropolitan Wayne County Airport">etroi<b>t</b>&#8211;<b>W</b>ayne County</a>, LBA for <b><a href="/wiki/Leeds_Bradford_International_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Leeds Bradford International Airport">L</a></b><a href="/wiki/Leeds_Bradford_International_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Leeds Bradford International Airport">eeds&#8211;<b>B</b>radford (<b>A</b>irport)</a>, MSP for <b><a href="/wiki/Minneapolis%E2%80%93Saint_Paul_International_Airport" title="Minneapolis\xe2\x80\x93Saint Paul International Airport">M</a></b><a href="/wiki/Minneapolis%E2%80%93Saint_Paul_International_Airport" title="Minneapolis\xe2\x80\x93Saint Paul International Airport">inneapolis&#8211;<b>S</b>aint <b>P</b>aul</a>, and RDU for <b><a href="/wiki/Raleigh%E2%80%93Durham_International_Airport" title="Raleigh\xe2\x80\x93Durham International Airport">R</a></b><a href="/wiki/Raleigh%E2%80%93Durham_International_Airport" title="Raleigh\xe2\x80\x93Durham International Airport">aleigh&#8211;<b>DU</b>rham</a>.  Other airports\xe2\x80\x94particularly those serving cities with multiple airports\xe2\x80\x94have codes derived from the name of the airport itself, such as JFK for New York\'s <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy_International_Airport" title="John F. Kennedy International Airport"><b>J</b>ohn <b>F</b>. <b>K</b>ennedy</a> or CDG for Paris\' <a href="/wiki/Charles_de_Gaulle_International_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Charles de Gaulle International Airport"><b>C</b>harles <b>d</b>e <b>G</b>aulle</a> (see below).  In some cases, the code comes from the airport\'s unofficial name, such as <a href="/wiki/Kahului_Airport" title="Kahului Airport">Kahului Airport</a>\'s OGG (for local aviation pioneer Jimmy H<b>ogg</b>).\n</p><p>Canada\'s unusual codes\xe2\x80\x93which bear little to no similarity with any conventional abbreviation to the city\'s name\xe2\x80\x93such as <a href="/wiki/Montr%C3%A9al%E2%80%93Pierre_Elliott_Trudeau_International_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Montr\xc3\xa9al\xe2\x80\x93Pierre Elliott Trudeau International Airport">YUL</a> in <a href="/wiki/Montr%C3%A9al" class="mw-redirect" title="Montr\xc3\xa9al">Montr\xc3\xa9al</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Edmonton_International_Airport" title="Edmonton International Airport">YEG</a> in <a href="/wiki/Edmonton" title="Edmonton">Edmonton</a>, originated from the two-letter codes used to identify weather reporting stations in the 1930s. The letters preceding the two-letter code follow the following format:\n</p>\n<ul><li><b>Y</b> (meaning "<i>yes</i>") if the reporting station was co-located with an airport.</li></ul>\n<ul><li><b>W</b> (meaning "<i>without</i>") if the reporting station was not co-located with an airport.</li></ul>\n<ul><li><b>U</b> if the reporting station was co-located with a <a href="/wiki/Non-directional_beacon" title="Non-directional beacon">non-directional beacon</a>.</li></ul>\n<ul><li><b>X</b> if the last two letters of the code had already been taken by another Canadian airport.</li></ul>\n<ul><li><b>Z</b> if the resulting code had already been taken by a US airport.</li></ul>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cities_with_multiple_commercial_airports">Cities with multiple commercial airports</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=IATA_airport_code&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Cities with multiple commercial airports">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In large metropolitan areas, airport codes are often named after the airport itself instead of the city it serves, while another code is reserved which refers to the city itself. The original airport in Nashville, Tennessee, was built in 1936 as part of the Works Progress Administration and called Berry Field with the designation, BNA. A new facility known as Nashville International Airport was built in 1987 but still uses BNA. This is in conjunction to rules aimed to avoid confusion that seem to apply in the United States, which state that "the first and second letters or second and third letters of an identifier may not be duplicated with less than 200 <a href="/wiki/Nautical_mile" title="Nautical mile">nautical miles</a> separation."<sup id="cite_ref-sky_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sky-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Thus, Washington, D.C. area\'s three airports all have radically different codes: IAD for <a href="/wiki/Washington_Dulles_International_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Washington Dulles International Airport">Washington\xe2\x80\x93Dulles</a>, DCA for <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Reagan_Washington_National_Airport" title="Ronald Reagan Washington National Airport">Washington\xe2\x80\x93Reagan</a> (District of Columbia Airport), and BWI for <a href="/wiki/Baltimore_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Baltimore Airport">Baltimore</a> (Baltimore\xe2\x80\x93Washington International, formerly BAL).<sup id="cite_ref-sky_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sky-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Since HOU is used for <a href="/wiki/William_P._Hobby_Airport" title="William P. The airports of <a href="/wiki/Hamburg_Airport" title="Hamburg Airport">Hamburg</a> (HAM) and <a href="/wiki/Hannover_Airport" title="Hannover Airport">Hannover</a> (HAJ) are less than 100 nautical It is actually located in <a href="/wiki/Marcoola,_Queensland" title="Marcoola, Queensland">Marcoola</a> rather than Maroochydore</li></ul>\n<p>In Asia, codes that do not correspond with their city\'s names include <a href="/wiki/Niigata_(city)" title="Niigata (city)">Niigata</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/Niigata_Airport" title="Niigata Airport">KIJ</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nanchang" title="Nanchang">Nanchang</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/Nanchang_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Nanchang Airport">KHN</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pyongyang" title="Pyongyang">Pyongyang</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/Pyongyang_International_Airport" title="Pyongyang International Airport">FNJ</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Kobe" title="Kobe">Kobe</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/Kobe_Airport" title="Kobe Airport">UKB</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Multiple_codes_for_a_single_airport">Multiple codes for a single airport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=IATA_airport_code&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Multiple codes for a single airport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><a href="/wiki/EuroAirport_Basel_Mulhouse_Freiburg" title="EuroAirport Basel Mulhouse Freiburg">EuroAirport Basel Mulhouse Freiburg</a>, which serves three countries, has three airport codes: BSL, MLH, EAP\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Airport_codes_using_the_English_name_of_the_city">Airport codes using the English name of the city</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=IATA_airport_code&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Airport codes using the English name of the city">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Some European cities have a different name in their respective language than in English, yet the airport code represents <i>only</i> the English name. Examples include:\n</p>\n<ul><li>CGN - Cologne/K\xc3\xb6ln (Germany)</li>\n<li>CPH - Copenhagen/K\xc3\xb8benhavn (Denmark)</li>\n<li>FLR - Florence/Firenze (Italy)</li>\n<li>GVA - Geneva/Gen\xc3\xa8ve (Switzerland)</li>\n<li>OPO - Oporto/Porto (Portugal)</li>\n<li>PRG - Prague/Praha (Czechia)</li>\n<li>VCE - Venice/Venezia (Italy)</li>\n<li>VIE - Vienna/Wien (Austria)</li></ul>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Use_in_colloquial_speech">Use in colloquial speech</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=IATA_airport_code&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Use in colloquial speech">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Some airports are identified even in the colloquial speech by their airport code. The most notable examples are LAX and JFK.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2020)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="National_policies">National policies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=IATA_airport_code&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: National policies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=IATA_airport_code&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Since the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Navy" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Navy">U.S. Navy</a> reserved "N" codes and to prevent confusion with <a href="/wiki/Federal_Communications_Commission" title="Federal Communications Commission">Federal Communications Commission</a> <a href="/wiki/Call_signs_in_North_America" title="Call signs in North America">broadcast call signs</a> beginning with "W" and "K", certain U.S. cities which begin with these letters had to adopt "irregular" airport codes when their airports opened: EWR for <a href="/wiki/Newark_Liberty_International_Airport" title="Newark Liberty International Airport">Newark</a>, <a href="/wiki/Norfolk_International_Airport" title="Norfolk International Airport">ORF</a> for <a href="/wiki/Norfolk,_Virginia" title="Norfolk, Virginia">Norfolk, Virginia</a>, EYW for <a href="/wiki/Key_West,_Florida" class="mw-redirect" title="Key West, Florida">Key West, Florida</a>, OME for <a href="/wiki/Nome_Airport" title="Nome Airport">Nome</a> and <a href="/wiki/Napa_County_Airport" title="Napa County Airport">APC</a> for <a href="/wiki/Napa,_California" title="Napa, California">Napa, California</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-sky_1-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sky-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> This practice is not followed outside the United States: <a href="/wiki/Karachi" title="Karachi">Karachi</a> is KHI, <a href="/wiki/Warsaw" title="Warsaw">Warsaw</a> is WAW, and <a href="/wiki/Nagoya" title="Nagoya">Nagoya</a> is NGO. In addition, since <a href="/wiki/Q_code" title="Q code">three letter codes starting with Q</a> are widely used in radio communication, cities with "Q" beginning their name also had to find alternate codes, as in the case of <a href="/wiki/Qiqihar" title="Qiqihar">Qiqihar</a> (NDG), <a href="/wiki/Quetta" title="Quetta">Quetta</a> (UET), <a href="/wiki/Quito" title="Quito">Quito</a> (UIO), and <a href="/wiki/Quimper" title="Quimper">Quimper</a> (UIP).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2020)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>IATA codes should not be confused with the <a href="/wiki/Location_identifier#FAA_identifier" title="Location identifier">FAA identifiers</a> of US airports. Most FAA identifiers agree with the corresponding IATA codes, but some do not, such as <a href="/wiki/Saipan_International_Airport" title="Saipan International Airport">Saipan</a> whose FAA identifier is GSN and its IATA code is SPN, and some coincide with IATA codes of non-US airports.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2020)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Canada">Canada</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=IATA_airport_code&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Canada">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<table class="box-Unreferenced_section plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Unreferenced" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><a href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" decoding="async" width="50" height="39" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="512" data-file-height="399" /></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>does not <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">cite</a> any <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">sources</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=IATA_airport_code&amp;action=edit">improve this section</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Referencing_for_beginners" title="Help:Referencing for beginners">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. Unsourced material may be challenged and <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability#Burden_of_evidence" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">removed</a>.</span>  <small class="date-container"><i>(<span class="date">March 2021</span>)</i></small><small class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p>Most large airports in Canada have codes that begin with the letter "Y", although not all "Y" codes are Canadian (for example, YUM for <a href="/wiki/Yuma,_Arizona" title="Yuma, Arizona">Yuma, Arizona</a>, and YNT for <a href="/wiki/Yantai" title="Yantai">Yantai</a>, China), and not all Canadian airports start with the letter "Y" (for example ZBF for <a href="/wiki/Bathurst,_New_Brunswick" title="Bathurst, New Brunswick">Bathurst, New Brunswick</a>). Many Canadian airports have a code that starts with W, X or Z, but none of these are major airports. When the Canadian transcontinental railways were built, each station was assigned its own two-letter Morse code. VR stands for Vancouver, TZ Toronto, QB Quebec, WG Winnipeg, SJ Saint John, YC Calgary, OW Ottawa, EG Edmonton, etc. When the Canadian government established airports, it used the existing railway codes for them as well. If the airport had a weather station, authorities added a "Y" to the front of the code, meaning "Yes" to indicate it had a weather station or some other letter to indicate it did not. When international codes were created in cooperation with the United States, because "Y" was seldom used in the US, Canada simply used the weather station codes for its airports, changing the "Y" to a "Z" if it conflicted with an airport code already in use. The result is that most major Canadian airport codes start with "Y" followed by two letters in the city\'s name: <a href="/wiki/Ottawa_Macdonald%E2%80%93Cartier_International_Airport" title="Ottawa Macdonald\xe2\x80\x93Cartier International Airport">YOW</a> for <a href="/wiki/Ottawa" title="Ottawa">Ottawa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Winnipeg_James_Armstrong_Richardson_International_Airport" title="Winnipeg James Armstrong Richardson International Airport">YWG</a> for <a href="/wiki/Winnipeg" title="Winnipeg">Winnipeg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Calgary_International_Airport" title="Calgary International Airport">YYC</a> for <a href="/wiki/Calgary" title="Calgary">Calgary</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Vancouver_International_Airport" title="Vancouver International Airport">YVR</a> for <a href="/wiki/Vancouver" title="Vancouver">Vancouver</a>, whereas other Canadian airports append the two-letter code of the radio beacons that were the closest to the actual airport, such as <a href="/wiki/Gander_International_Airport" title="Gander International Airport">YQX</a> in <a href="/wiki/Gander,_Newfoundland_and_Labrador" title="Gander, Newfoundland and Labrador">Gander</a> and <a href="/wiki/Prince_George_Airport" title="Prince George Airport">YXS</a> in <a href="/wiki/Prince_George,_British_Columbia" title="Prince George, British Columbia">Prince George</a>.\n</p><p>Four of the ten provincial capital airports in Canada have ended up with codes beginning with YY, including YYZ for Toronto, Ontario, YYJ for Victoria, British Columbia, YYT for St. John\'s, Newfoundland, and YYG for Charlottetown, Prince Edward Island. Canada\'s largest airport is YYZ for <a href="/wiki/Toronto%E2%80%93Pearson_International_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Toronto\xe2\x80\x93Pearson International Airport">Toronto\xe2\x80\x93Pearson</a> (YTZ was used for Toronto City Airport, so YYZ is the station code for a village called Malton, which is where Toronto Pearson International Airport is actually located). YUL is used for <a href="/wiki/Montr%C3%A9al%E2%80%93Pierre_Elliott_Trudeau_International_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Montr\xc3\xa9al\xe2\x80\x93Pierre Elliott Trudeau International Airport">Montr\xc3\xa9al\xe2\x80\x93Trudeau</a> (UL was the ID code for beacon in the city of Kirkland, now the location of Montr\xc3\xa9al\xe2\x80\x93Trudeau). While these codes make it difficult for the public to associate them with a particular Canadian city, some codes have become popular in usage despite their cryptic nature, particularly at the largest airports. Toronto\'s code has entered pop culture in the form of <a href="/wiki/YYZ_(instrumental)" class="mw-redirect" title="YYZ (instrumental)">"YYZ"</a>, a song by the rock band <a href="/wiki/Rush_(band)" title="Rush (band)">Rush</a> which utilizes the Morse code signal as a musical motif. Some airports have started using their IATA codes as marketing brands. <a href="/wiki/Calgary_International_Airport" title="Calgary International Airport">Calgary International Airport</a> has begun using its airport code YYC as a marketing brand and name for the airport authority web site (yyc.com),<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> while <a href="/wiki/Vancouver_International_Airport" title="Vancouver International Airport">Vancouver International Airport</a> advertises as YVR (yvr.ca).\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="New_Zealand">New Zealand</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=IATA_airport_code&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: New Zealand">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Numerous New Zealand airports use codes which contain a letter Z, to distinguish them from similar airport names in other countries. Examples include <a href="/wiki/Hamilton_Airport_(New_Zealand)" title="Hamilton Airport (New Zealand)">HLZ</a> for <a href="/wiki/Hamilton,_New_Zealand" title="Hamilton, New Zealand">Hamilton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Queenstown_Airport" title="Queenstown Airport">ZQN</a> for <a href="/wiki/Queenstown,_New_Zealand" title="Queenstown, New Zealand">Queenstown</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Westport_Airport_(New_Zealand)" title="Westport Airport (New Zealand)">WSZ</a> for <a href="/wiki/Westport,_New_Zealand" title="Westport, New Zealand">Westport</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lack_of_codes">Lack of codes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=IATA_airport_code&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Lack of codes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>There are several airports with scheduled service that have not been assigned <b>ICAO</b> codes that do have IATA codes. For example, several airports in Alaska have scheduled commercial service, such as <a href="/wiki/Stebbins_Airport" title="Stebbins Airport">Stebbins</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nanwalek_Airport" title="Nanwalek Airport">Nanwalek</a>, which use <a href="/wiki/Location_identifier" title="Location identifier">FAA codes</a> instead. There are also airports with scheduled service for which there are ICAO codes but not IATA codes, such as Nkhotakota Airport/Tangole Airport in <a href="/wiki/List_of_airports_in_Malawi" title="List of airports in Malawi">Malawi</a> or <a href="/wiki/Ch%C5%8Dfu_Airport" title="Ch\xc5\x8dfu Airport">Ch\xc5\x8dfu Airport</a> in Tokyo, Japan. There are also several minor airports in <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a> (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Omsukchan_Airport" title="Omsukchan Airport">Omsukchan Airport</a>) which instead use <a href="/wiki/Location_identifier" title="Location identifier">internal Russian codes</a> for booking. Flights to these airports cannot be booked through the international air booking systems or have luggage transferred to there, and thus, they are booked instead through the airline or a domestic booking system. Thus, neither system completely includes all airports with scheduled service. Several <a href="/wiki/List_of_airports_in_Greenland" title="List of airports in Greenland">heliports in Greenland</a> have 3-letter codes used internally which might be IATA codes for airports in far away countries.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
