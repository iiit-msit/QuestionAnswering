data-file-height="803" /></a></div>\n</div>\n<p><b><a href="/wiki/Frances_Gertrude_McGill" title="Frances Gertrude McGill">Frances Gertrude McGill</a></b> (1882\xe2\x80\x931959) was a Canadian scientist who was a <a href="/wiki/Forensic_pathology" title="Forensic pathology">forensic pathologist</a>, nicknamed the "<a href="/wiki/Sherlock_Holmes" title="Sherlock Holmes">Sherlock Holmes</a> of <a href="/wiki/Saskatchewan" title="Saskatchewan">Saskatchewan</a>" for her deductive skills and public fame. McGill influenced the development of forensic pathology in Canadian police work. After receiving her medical degree in 1915, McGill moved to Saskatchewan, where she was hired first as the provincial <a href="/wiki/Bacteriology" title="Bacteriology">bacteriologist</a> and then as the provincial <a href="/wiki/Pathology" title="Pathology">pathologist</a>. She worked extensively with the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Canadian_Mounted_Police" title="Royal Canadian Mounted Police">Royal Canadian Mounted Police</a> (RCMP) and local police forces for more than thirty years, and was instrumental in establishing the first RCMP forensic laboratory. She directed the RCMP laboratory for three years, and trained new RCMP recruits in forensic detection methods. After retiring in 1946, McGill was appointed Honorary Surgeon for the RCMP by the <a href="/wiki/Minister_of_Justice_and_Attorney_General_of_Canada" title="Minister of Justice and Attorney General of Canada">Canadian minister of justice</a>, becoming one of its first official female members. McGill Lake in northern Saskatchewan was named in her honour. I listed buildings in Rhondda Cynon Taf">three Grade&#160;I listed buildings</a></b> in <a href="/wiki/Rhondda_Cynon_Taf" title="Rhondda Cynon Taf">Rhondda Cynon Taf</a>, a <a href="/wiki/County_borough" title="County borough">county borough</a> in <a href="/wiki/South_Wales" title="South Wales">South Wales</a>. In the United Kingdom, the term <a href="/wiki/Listed_building" title="Listed building">listed building</a> refers to a building or other structure officially designated as being of special architectural, historical, or cultural significance. Listing was begun by a provision in the <a href="/wiki/Town_and_Country_Planning_Act_1947" title="Town and Country Planning Act 1947">Town and Country Planning Act 1947</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a>, authority for listing or delisting, under the <a href="/wiki/Planning_(Listed_Buildings_and_Conservation_Areas)_Act_1990" title="Planning (Listed Buildings and Conservation Areas) Act 1990">Planning (Listed Buildings and Conservation Areas) Act 1990</a>, rests with the <a href="/wiki/Welsh_Government#Welsh_ministers" title="Welsh Government">Welsh ministers</a>, though these decisions are based on the recommendations of <a href="/wiki/Cadw" title="Cadw">Cadw</a>. The Grade&#160;I buildings are <a href="/wiki/Old_Bridge,_Pontypridd" title="Old Bridge, Pontypridd">a mid-18th-century bridge</a> <i>(pictured)</i> in <a href="/wiki/Pontypridd" title="Pontypridd">Pontypridd</a>, and two structures related to the <a href="/wiki/Coal_industry_in_Wales" title="Coal industry in Wales">coal-mining</a> heritage of the region: the <a href="/wiki/Engine_house" title="Engine house">engine house</a> (1875) and the <a href="/wiki/Headframe" title="Headframe">headframe</a> (1902) of the <a href="/wiki/Great_Western_Mine" title="Great Western Mine">Hetty Pit</a> near <a href="/wiki/Hopkinstown" title="Hopkinstown">Hopkinstown</a>. The growth of iron foundries in <a href="/wiki/Merthyr_Tydfil" title="Merthyr Tydfil">Merthyr Tydfil</a> led to the building of the <a href="/wiki/Glamorganshire_Canal" title="Glamorganshire Canal">Glamorganshire Canal</a> (1791\xe2\x80\x931795) and the <a href="/wiki/Taff_Vale_Railway" title="Taff Vale Railway">Taff Vale Railway</a> (1841) to connect the industry to the docks at Cardiff; both of these passed through Pontypridd, contributing to its development into a major market town.
