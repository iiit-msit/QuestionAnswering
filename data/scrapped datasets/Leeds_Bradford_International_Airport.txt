Authority</a><sup id="cite_ref-stats_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stats-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup></div></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p><b>Leeds Bradford Airport</b> (<a href="/wiki/IATA_airport_code" title="IATA airport code">IATA</a>: <b>LBA</b>, <a href="/wiki/ICAO_airport_code" title="ICAO airport code">ICAO</a>: <b>EGNM</b>) is located in <a href="/wiki/Yeadon,_West_Yorkshire" title="Yeadon, West Yorkshire">Yeadon</a>, in the <a href="/wiki/City_of_Leeds" title="City of Leeds">City of Leeds Metropolitan District</a> in <a href="/wiki/West_Yorkshire" title="West Yorkshire">West Yorkshire</a>, England, about 7 miles (11&#160;km) northwest of <a href="/wiki/Leeds_city_centre" title="Leeds city centre">Leeds city centre</a>, and about 9 miles (14&#160;km) northeast from <a href="/wiki/Bradford" title="Bradford">Bradford</a> city centre.<sup id="cite_ref-aip_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aip-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> It serves <a href="/wiki/Leeds" title="Leeds">Leeds</a> and Bradford and the wider <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire" title="Yorkshire">Yorkshire</a> region including <a href="/wiki/York" title="York">York</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wakefield" title="Wakefield">Wakefield</a> and <a href="/wiki/Harrogate" title="Harrogate">Harrogate</a>, and is the largest airport in Yorkshire. The airport was in public ownership until May 2007, when it was bought by <a href="/wiki/Bridgepoint_Capital" class="mw-redirect" title="Bridgepoint Capital">Bridgepoint Capital</a> for \xc2\xa3145.5&#160;million.<sup id="cite_ref-bridgepointaquires_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bridgepointaquires-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Bridgepoint sold it in 2017 to AMP Capital.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Leeds Bradford opened on 17 October 1931 when it was known as <i>Leeds and Bradford Municipal Aerodrome</i> or <i>Yeadon Aerodrome</i>;<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> locals still refer to it as Yeadon Airport. Largely used for <a href="/wiki/General_aviation" title="General aviation">general aviation</a> and training purposes early on, the first scheduled flights commenced on 8 April 1935. To accommodate passenger traffic, work commenced on the first <a href="/wiki/Airport_terminal" title="Airport terminal">terminal</a> in the late 1930s, although only the first wing was completed before the <a href="/wiki/Second_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Second World War">Second World War</a>. British aircraft manufacturer <a href="/wiki/Avro" title="Avro">Avro</a> constructed a <a href="/wiki/British_shadow_factories" title="British shadow factories">shadow factory</a> to the north of the aerodrome in the largest free-standing structure in Europe at that time. Avro produced around 5,515 aircraft before it closed in December 1946 and civil flights recommenced the following year.\n</p><p>In 1965, a new runway opened. After Yeadon\'s terminal was destroyed in a fire, a replacement was completed in 1968. In the early 1980s, runway extensions were completed that enabled it to be classified as a <a href="/wiki/Regional_airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Regional airport">regional airport</a>. On 4 November 1984, the day a runway extension was opened, <a href="/wiki/Wardair" title="Wardair">Wardair</a> commenced <a href="/wiki/Transatlantic_flight" title="Transatlantic flight">transatlantic flights</a> to <a href="/wiki/Toronto_Pearson_International_Airport" title="Toronto Pearson International Airport">Toronto</a>, using <a href="/wiki/Boeing_747" title="Boeing 747">Boeing 747s</a>. On 2 August 1986, an <a href="/wiki/Air_France" title="Air France">Air France</a> <a href="/wiki/Concorde" title="Concorde">Concorde</a> charter flight from Paris landed for the first time, drawing an estimated crowd of 70,000 people. More Concorde charter flights took place until 2000. In 1994, the airport\'s operational hour restrictions were removed, enabling flights at any time of day. Since 1996, the terminal has been expanded in the terms of size and facilities. In 2007, nearly 2.9&#160;million passengers passed through the airport, an 88% increase in seven years and more than twice as many compared with 1997 (1.2&#160;million).\n</p><p>Leeds Bradford has a <a href="/wiki/Civil_Aviation_Authority_(United_Kingdom)" title="Civil Aviation Authority (United Kingdom)">CAA</a> Public Use Aerodrome Licence (Number P800) that allows flights for passenger transport and flight training. The airport operates to many domestic and European destinations. It is the highest airport in England at an elevation of 681&#160;ft (208&#160;m).<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> By the number of passengers handled in 2018, Leeds Bradford was the <a href="/wiki/Busiest_airports_in_the_United_Kingdom_by_total_passenger_traffic" class="mw-redirect" title="Busiest airports in the United Kingdom by total passenger traffic">15th busiest airport in the UK</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> It is a base for <a href="/wiki/Jet2.com" title="Jet2.com">Jet2.com</a>.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc" role="navigation" aria-labelledby="mw-toc-heading"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leeds_Bradford_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Opening_and_early_operations">Opening and early operations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leeds_Bradford_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Opening and early operations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>What became Leeds Bradford Airport was built in the late 1920s and early 1930s on 60 acres of grassland next to the old Bradford to Harrogate road.<sup id="cite_ref-yorkpost_2011_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-yorkpost_2011-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> On 17 October 1931, the airport, which was interchangeably known as <i>Leeds and Bradford Municipal Aerodrome</i> or <i>Yeadon Aerodrome</i> in its early years, was officially opened.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Initially, the airport was operated by the Yorkshire Aeroplane Club on behalf of Leeds and Bradford Corporations. Its early operations were typified by training and <a href="/wiki/General_aviation" title="General aviation">general aviation</a> flights.<sup id="cite_ref-yorkpost_2011_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-yorkpost_2011-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1935, the aerodrome was expanded by 35 acres (140,000&#160;m<sup>2</sup>); the first scheduled flights commenced on 8 April 1935 with a service by North Eastern Airways from London (<a href="/wiki/Heston_Aerodrome" title="Heston Aerodrome">Heston Aerodrome</a>) to <a href="/wiki/Newcastle_upon_Tyne" title="Newcastle upon Tyne">Newcastle upon Tyne</a> (<a href="/wiki/Cramlington" title="Cramlington">Cramlington</a>). The service was extended to <a href="/wiki/Edinburgh" title="Edinburgh">Edinburgh</a> (<a href="/wiki/Turnhouse" title="Turnhouse">Turnhouse</a>). In June 1935, Blackpool and West Coast Air Services launched a service to the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Man" title="Isle of Man">Isle of Man</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> By 1936, the London/Yeadon/Newcastle/Edinburgh service was flying three times a week and also stopped at Doncaster and continued to Aberdeen (Dyce).\n</p><p>Seasonal flights between Yeadon and <a href="/wiki/Liverpool" title="Liverpool">Liverpool</a> commenced during the 1930s. To accommodate the expanding passenger numbers, work commenced on a <a href="/wiki/Airport_terminal" title="Airport terminal">terminal</a> building but progress was halted after a single wing had been completed.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> During this time, the <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">German</a> <a href="/wiki/Rigid_airship" title="Rigid airship">zeppelin</a> <a href="/wiki/LZ_129_Hindenburg" title="LZ 129 Hindenburg">LZ 129 Hindenburg</a> overflew the aerodrome and while the flight was claimed to be for publicity purposes, it was later found to have been engaged in <a href="/wiki/Espionage" title="Espionage">espionage</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-yorkpost_2011_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-yorkpost_2011-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wartime_use">Wartime use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leeds_Bradford_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Wartime use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>At the outbreak of the <a href="/wiki/Second_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Second World War">Second World War</a> in 1939, all civil aviation operations were halted. The aircraft manufacturer <a href="/wiki/Avro" title="Avro">Avro</a> constructed a <a href="/wiki/British_shadow_factories" title="British shadow factories">shadow factory</a> to the north of the aerodrome to manufacture <a href="/wiki/Military_aircraft" title="Military aircraft">military aircraft</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The factory was connected to the aerodrome by a taxiway from where the aircraft made their way to make their <a href="/wiki/Maiden_flight" title="Maiden flight">maiden flights</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> The Avro factory was <a href="/wiki/Camouflage" title="Camouflage">camouflaged</a>, its roof disguised as a field with dummy cattle and agricultural buildings so that from the air it resembled the surrounding fields.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Large numbers of houses were constructed nearby to house the workforce. The factory, which commenced production in 1941, was reportedly the largest free-standing structure in Europe at the time.<sup id="cite_ref-yorkpost_2011_9-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-yorkpost_2011-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>To better accommodate the large military aircraft, improvements were made to the aerodrome including two <a href="/wiki/Runway" title="Runway">runways</a>, more <a href="/wiki/Taxiway" title="Taxiway">taxiways</a> and extra <a href="/wiki/Hangar" title="Hangar">hangarage</a> enabling Yeadon to become an important site for military test flying.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> About 5,515 aircraft were produced at Yeadon of the following main types: <a href="/wiki/Avro_Anson" title="Avro Anson">Anson</a> (more than 4,500), <a href="/wiki/Bristol_Blenheim" title="Bristol Blenheim">Bristol Blenheim</a> (250), <a href="/wiki/Avro_Lancaster" title="Avro Lancaster">Lancaster bomber</a> (695), <a href="/wiki/Avro_York" title="Avro York">York</a> (45) and the <a href="/wiki/Avro_Lincoln" title="Avro Lincoln">Lincoln</a> (25).<sup id="cite_ref-:1_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Decreased demand at the end of the conflict saw the factory closed in December 1946.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_13-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> On <a href="/wiki/New_Year%27s_Day" title="New Year&#39;s Day">New Year\'s Day</a> 1947, the site was handed over to the <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_for_Transport" title="Secretary of State for Transport">Ministry of Civil Aviation</a>. Many of the airport\'s original hangars remain intact.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_13-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1947_to_1969">1947 to 1969</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leeds_Bradford_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 1947 to 1969">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:DH.84_Dragon_G-ACIT_AN%26T_Yeadon_21.05.56_edited-2.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/55/DH.84_Dragon_G-ACIT_AN%26T_Yeadon_21.05.56_edited-2.jpg/220px-DH.84_Dragon_G-ACIT_AN%26T_Yeadon_21.05.56_edited-2.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="144" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/55/DH.84_Dragon_G-ACIT_AN%26T_Yeadon_21.05.56_edited-2.jpg/330px-DH.84_Dragon_G-ACIT_AN%26T_Yeadon_21.05.56_edited-2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/55/DH.84_Dragon_G-ACIT_AN%26T_Yeadon_21.05.56_edited-2.jpg/440px-DH.84_Dragon_G-ACIT_AN%26T_Yeadon_21.05.56_edited-2.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="668" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:DH.84_Dragon_G-ACIT_AN%26T_Yeadon_21.05.56_edited-2.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A <a href="/wiki/De_Havilland_Dragon" title="De Havilland Dragon">de Havilland Dragon</a> of Air Navigation &amp; Trading at Yeadon Airport, May 1956</div></div></div>\n<p>In 1947, civil flights recommenced.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Local resident Geoff Rennard who had campaigned for Leeds and Bradford to have an aerodrome established an Aero Club. He was subsequently appointed airport manager, remaining for five years. In 1953, <i>Yeadon Aviation Ltd</i> was formed to manage and operate the airport and its Aero Club. In 1955, services to <a href="/wiki/Belfast" title="Belfast">Belfast</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jersey" title="Jersey">Jersey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ostend" title="Ostend">Ostend</a>, <a href="/wiki/Southend-on-Sea" title="Southend-on-Sea">Southend</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Wight" title="Isle of Wight">Isle of Wight</a> and <a href="/wiki/D%C3%BCsseldorf" title="D\xc3\xbcsseldorf">D\xc3\xbcsseldorf</a> were added to Yeadon\'s destination list.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Scheduled flights to London commenced in 1960; a route to <a href="/wiki/Dublin" title="Dublin">Dublin</a> by <a href="/wiki/Aer_Lingus" title="Aer Lingus">Aer Lingus</a> was added shortly after.<sup id="cite_ref-yorkpost_2011_9-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-yorkpost_2011-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> In 1965, a new runway was opened and in the same year the terminal building was accidentally destroyed in a fire. Its replacement was started shortly after and was operational by 1968.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span href="/wiki/Package_holiday" class="mw-redirect" title="Package holiday">package holiday</a> had become popular in the British Isles. During 1976, the first holiday charter flight to the <a href="/wiki/Iberian_Peninsula" title="Iberian Peninsula">Iberian Peninsula</a> by <a href="/wiki/Britannia_Airways" title="Britannia Airways">Britannia Airways</a> departed Leeds Bradford.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-yorkpost_2011_9-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-yorkpost_2011-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1978, it was recognised that, if the runway was extended, the airport could be upgraded to <a href="/wiki/Regional_airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Regional airport">regional airport</a> status.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_10-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> In 1982, construction commenced on the main runway necessitating the construction of a tunnel to take the <a href="/wiki/A658_road" title="A658 road">A658</a> Bradford to <a href="/wiki/Harrogate" title="Harrogate">Harrogate</a> road beneath the runway. On 4 November 1984, the runway was completed.<sup id="cite_ref-yorkpost_2011_9-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-yorkpost_2011-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Around this time, the airport\'s terminal building was extended and redeveloped. The first phase opened on 18 July 1985.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>On 4 November 1984, the day the runway extension was officially opened, <a href="/wiki/Wardair" title="Wardair">Wardair</a> commenced <a href="/wiki/Transatlantic_flight" title="Transatlantic flight">transatlantic flights</a> to <a href="/wiki/Toronto_Pearson_International_Airport" title="Toronto Pearson International Airport">Toronto</a>, using <a href="/wiki/Boeing_747" title="Boeing 747">Boeing 747s</a> but these flights were discontinued in 1989 when Wardair ceased operations. <a href="/wiki/Worldways_Canada" title="Worldways Canada">Worldways Canada</a>, <a href="/wiki/Odyssey_International" title="Odyssey International">Odyssey International</a>, <a href="/wiki/Air_Transat" title="Air Transat">Air Transat</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nationair" class="mw-redirect" title="Nationair">Nationair</a> and <a href="/wiki/Caledonian_Airways" title="Caledonian Airways">Caledonian</a> all operated transatlantic services from the airport well into the 1990s using <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_Tristar" class="mw-redirect" title="Lockheed Tristar">Lockheed Tristar</a> and <a href="/wiki/Boeing_757" title="Boeing 757">Boeing 757\xe2\x80\x93200</a> airliners.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:1_15-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>On 2 August 1986, an <a href="/wiki/Air_France" title="Air France">Air France</a> <a href="/wiki/Concorde" title="Concorde">Concorde</a> charter flight from Paris landed at the airport for the first time; an estimated 70,000 people were drawn to the airport to catch a glimpse of the <a href="/wiki/Supersonic" class="mw-redirect" title="Supersonic">supersonic</a> airliner.<sup id="cite_ref-yorkpost_2011_9-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-yorkpost_2011-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Occasional Concorde charter flights using <a href="/wiki/British_Airways" title="British Airways">British Airways</a> aircraft continued until June 2000, just one month before the <a href="/wiki/Air_France_Flight_4590" title="Air France Flight 4590">Concorde disaster</a> in Paris.\n</p><p>Leeds Bradford Airport had restricted operating hours that proved to be a deterrent to many <a href="/wiki/Charter_airline" class="mw-redirect" title="Charter airline">charter airlines</a>, whose cheap fares were heavily dependent on \'round-the-clock\' use of their aircraft. In 1994, the restrictions were removed, enabling planes to use the airport 24 hours per day, attracting more business.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_10-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1995_to_date">1995 to date</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leeds_Bradford_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 1995 to date">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Work on the airport terminal has been ongoing since 1996, and the result of this has been significant growth in terminal size and passenger facilities. The expanded terminal, along with new hangars, has accommodated the expanding use of airliners such as the <a href="/wiki/Boeing_737" title="Boeing 737">Boeing 737</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_15-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>On 6 October 2005, the original runway, 09/27, was permanently closed; it has since been redeveloped as a taxiway and to provide additional <a href="/wiki/Airport_ramp" class="mw-redirect" title="Airport ramp">apron</a> space.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 2007, nearly 2.9&#160;million passengers passed through the airport, an 88% increase in just seven years and more than twice as many compared with 1997 (1.2&#160;million). Long, medium and short-stay car parks provide 7,000 parking spaces at the airport, along with several drop off points.\n</p><p>The airport processes a small amount of freight from its two cargo sheds on site with a view to expanding this operation, a key focus of the business and masterplan objectives. The airport features a small runway of 2200&#160;m (7200&#160;ft) oriented as 14 and 32. Until recently, there was a second smaller runway oriented 9 and 27, however it was closed in 2005 and converted into a taxiway.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Future">Future</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leeds_Bradford_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Future">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Leeds Bradford Airport published a masterplan in March 2017.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> Planning permission was granted in January 2019 to redevelop the terminal to create additional departure gate access, extended seating areas, improved baggage reclaim facilities and enlarged immigration and customs facilities. Squadron was reformed and returned to Yeadon the following year.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> The squadron was equipped with <a href="/wiki/De_Havilland_Mosquito" title="De Havilland Mosquito">de Havilland Mosquito MK.XXX</a> aircraft which were difficult to operate because the runways were too short. Safety speed (at which the aircraft needs to be flown and controlled on a single engine) was not reached until over flying central Leeds when taking off in that direction\xe2\x80\x94with drastic results should things go wrong. The airfield sloped downhill and it was necessary to land at RAF Linton-on-Ouse (20 miles away) if the wind was coming from the wrong direction.<sup id="cite_ref-:5_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:5-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> Eventually, the Air Ministry re-equipped 609 with <a href="/wiki/Supermarine_Spitfire" title="Supermarine Spitfire">Spitfire LFXVIs</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:5_38-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:5-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> This was sufficient as a short-term measure, but the grass airstrip was not ideally suited to Spitfire operations, and it was decided that 609 Squadron should move to the hard runways of RAF Church Fenton in October 1950.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Yeadon was requisitioned by the Royal Air Force and became part of 13 Group and subsequently 12 Group. Once 609 (West Riding) Squadron left for Catterick, Yeadon served as a Flying Training School, bomber maintenance unit, and a scatter airfield. It was also used by Hawker Aircraft for development work on its Tornado design. The Royal Air Force remained a part of Yeadons life until 1957, operating Austers, Supermarine Spitfires, De Havilland Mosquitoes out of here. class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leeds_Bradford_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Airport ownership">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Leeds and Bradford councils jointly bought the airport site in 1930 and it opened as Yeadon Aerodrome in 1931. The airport became a limited company in 1987, and was shared between the five boroughs, Leeds (40%), Bradford (40%) and Wakefield, Calderdale and Kirklees (sharing the remaining 20%).\n</p><p>In October 2006, plans to privatise the airport were confirmed and on 4 April 2007 the five controlling councils announced that Bridgepoint Capital had been selected as the preferred bidder. On 4 May 2007, Bridgepoint Capital acquired the airport from Leeds, Bradford, Wakefield, Calderdale and Kirklees councils for \xc2\xa3145.5 million. Although Bridgepoint Capital owned the airport 100% financially, the councils hold a "special share" in the airport, to protect its name and continued operation as an air transport gateway for the Yorkshire region. In November 2017, Bridgepoint Capital sold the airport to AMP Capital who own several other airports around the world. AMP plans to expand the airport, improve the customer experience and secure more business flights.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a They are also the dedicated FBO at the airfield and provide helicopter and fixed wing charter flights as well as aircraft sales and management.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup> General aviation operations are confined to the south-side of the airport, in order to maintain separation from commercial traffic utilising the main terminal.\n</p><p>During 2005, a pair of new hangars capable of housing up to four Boeing 737-800s were constructed, as well as a new apron and direct taxiway to the runway. A dedicated southside fuel farm was also installed.<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Aviation Academy is located within a hangar at Leeds Bradford Airport. It is operated by <a href="/wiki/Craven_College" title="Craven College">Craven College</a> in conjunction with the <a href="/wiki/Open_University" title="Open University">Open University</a>. The academy trains and prepares students to work within the commercial aviation industry.<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Incidents_and_accidents">Incidents and accidents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leeds_Bradford_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Incidents and accidents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Prior to 1985, there were two recorded incidents of runway overruns at Leeds Bradford Airport, both involving <a href="/wiki/BMI_(airline)" class="mw-redirect" title="BMI (airline)">British Midland Airways</a> <a href="/wiki/Vickers_Viscount" title="Vickers Viscount">Viscount</a> aircraft, and both showing evidence of <a href="/wiki/Hydroplaning_(tires)" class="mw-redirect" title="Hydroplaning (tires)">hydroplaning</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-BBAI_65-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBAI-65">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Tristar_Incident_LBA.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Tristar_Incident_LBA.jpg/220px-Tristar_Incident_LBA.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="176" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Tristar_Incident_LBA.jpg/330px-Tristar_Incident_LBA.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Tristar_Incident_LBA.jpg/440px-Tristar_Incident_LBA.jpg 2x" data-file-width="971" data-file-height="777" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Tristar_Incident_LBA.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/British_Airtours" title="British Airtours">British Airtours</a> <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_Tristar" class="mw-redirect" title="Lockheed Tristar">Lockheed Tristar</a> at end of runway 14, 1985</div></div></div>\n<ul><li>On 27 May 1985, a <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_L-1011" class="mw-redirect" title="Lockheed L-1011">Lockheed Tristar</a> operated by <a href="/wiki/British_Airtours" title="British Airtours">British Airtours</a>, registration G-BBAI, <a href="/w/index.php?title=British_Airtours_Flight_101&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="British Airtours Flight 101 (page does not exist)">overran</a> the runway surface on landing from <a href="/wiki/Palma_(Majorca)" class="mw-redirect" title="Palma (Majorca)">Palma</a> after a rain shower. The aircraft was evacuated, with only minor injuries sustained by the 14 crew and 398 passengers. The nose <a href="/wiki/Landing_gear" title="Landing gear">landing gear</a> strut folded backwards during the overrun, leading to severe damage to the underside of the forward <a href="/wiki/Fuselage" title="Fuselage">fuselage</a>. The undersides of both wing-mounted engines were flattened and both engines suffered ingestion damage. The main wheels of the aircraft also dug deep troughs in the area beyond the end of the runway, damaging the buried airfield lighting cables. The accident report concluded that the overrun was caused by the inability of the aircraft to achieve the appropriate level of braking effectiveness and recommended that both the scheduled wet runway performance of the TriStar and the condition of the surface of runway 14 at Leeds Bradford Airport should be re-examined.<sup id="cite_ref-BBAI_65-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBAI-65">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>On 24 May 1995, an <a href="/wiki/Embraer_EMB_110_Bandeirante" title="Embraer EMB 110 Bandeirante">Embraer EMB 110 Bandeirante</a> aircraft, registration G-OEAA operated by Knight Air on a flight between Leeds Bradford and <a href="/wiki/Aberdeen" title="Aberdeen">Aberdeen</a> (see <a href="/wiki/Knight_Air_Flight_816" title="Knight Air Flight 816">Knight Air Flight 816</a>) entered a steeply descending <a href="/wiki/Aircraft_dynamic_modes#spiral_dive" title="Aircraft dynamic modes">spiral dive</a>, broke up in flight and crashed into farmland at <a href="/wiki/Dunkeswick_Moor" class="mw-redirect" title="Dunkeswick Moor">Dunkeswick Moor</a> near Leeds. All 12 occupants were killed. The probable cause of the accident was the failure of one or both <a href="/wiki/Attitude_indicator" title="Attitude indicator">artificial horizon</a> instruments. There was no standby artificial horizon installed (as there was no <a href="/wiki/Airworthiness" title="Airworthiness">airworthiness</a> requirement for one on this aircraft) and the accident report concluded that this left the crew without a single instrument available for assured attitude reference or simple means of determining which <a href="/wiki/Flight_instruments" title="Flight instruments">flight instruments</a> had failed. The aircraft entered a spiral dive from which the pilot, who was likely to have become spatially disoriented, was unable to recover.<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>On 18 May 2005, a Jordanian <a href="/wiki/Airbus_A320_family" title="Airbus A320 family">Airbus A320</a>, registration JY-JAR operating for Spanish charter airline <a href="/wiki/LTE_International_Airways" title="LTE International Airways">LTE</a> suffered a braking malfunction on landing at Leeds Bradford Airport following a flight from <a href="/wiki/Fuerteventura" title="Fuerteventura">Fuerteventura</a>. The aircraft touched down on runway 14 just beyond the touchdown zone, approximately 400&#160;m (1,300&#160;ft) beyond the aiming point. The pilots determined that the rate of deceleration was inadequate and applied full reverse thrust and full manual braking in an effort to stop the aircraft, however the normal braking system malfunctioned and the Captain turned the aircraft onto a level grassed area to the right of the runway where it came to rest. There were no injuries to the passengers or crew, however the <a href="/wiki/Air_Accidents_Investigation_Branch" title="Air Accidents Investigation Branch">Air Accidents Investigation Branch</a> made seven safety recommendations in the final accident report.<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
