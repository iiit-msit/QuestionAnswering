vulture to the windscreen at the 1952 Carrera Panamericana</div></div></div>\n<p>A <b>bird strike</b>\xe2\x80\x94sometimes called <b>birdstrike</b>, <b>bird ingestion</b> (for an engine), <b>bird hit</b>, or <b>bird aircraft strike hazard</b> (<b>BASH</b>)\xe2\x80\x94is a collision between an airborne animal (usually a <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> or <a href="/wiki/Bat" title="Bat">bat</a>)<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> and a moving vehicle, usually an <a href="/wiki/Aircraft" title="Aircraft">aircraft</a>. The term is also used for bird deaths resulting from collisions with structures such as power lines, towers and wind turbines (see <a href="/wiki/Bird%E2%80%93skyscraper_collisions" title="Bird\xe2\x80\x93skyscraper collisions">Bird\xe2\x80\x93skyscraper collisions</a> and <a href="/wiki/Towerkill" title="Towerkill">Towerkill</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Bird strikes are a significant threat to flight safety, and have caused a number of accidents with human casualties.<sup id="cite_ref-sodhi_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sodhi-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> There are over 13,000 bird strikes annually in the US alone.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> However, the number of major accidents involving civil aircraft is quite low and it has been estimated that there is only about 1 accident resulting in human death in one billion (10<sup>9</sup>) flying hours.<sup id="cite_ref-thorpe_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-thorpe-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The majority of bird strikes (65%) cause little damage to the aircraft;<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> however the collision is usually fatal to the bird(s) involved.\n</p><p>Particularly, the <a href="/wiki/Canada_goose" title="Canada goose">Canada goose</a> has been ranked as the third most hazardous wildlife species to aircraft with approximately 240 goose-aircraft collisions in the United States each year. 80% of all bird strikes go unreported.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Most accidents occur when a bird (or birds) collides with the windscreen or is sucked into the engine of jet aircraft. These cause annual damages that have been estimated at $400&#160;million<sup id="cite_ref-sodhi_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sodhi-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> within the United States alone and up to $1.2&#160;billion to commercial aircraft worldwide.<sup id="cite_ref-allan_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-allan-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> In addition to property damage, collisions between man-made structures and conveyances and birds is a contributing factor, among many others, to the worldwide decline of many avian species.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/International_Civil_Aviation_Organization" title="International Civil Aviation Organization">International Civil Aviation Organization</a> (ICAO) received 65,139 bird strike reports for 2011\xe2\x80\x9314, and the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Aviation_Authority" class="mw-redirect" title="Federal Aviation Authority">Federal Aviation Authority</a> counted 177,269 wildlife strike reports on civil aircraft between 1990 and 2015, growing 38% in seven years from 2009 to 2015. Birds accounted for 97%.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc" role="navigation" vehicle belonging to <a href="/wiki/Copenhagen_Airport" title="Copenhagen Airport">Copenhagen Airport</a> Kastrup, equipped with various tools</div></div></div>\n<p>Bird strikes happen most often during <a href="/wiki/Takeoff" title="Takeoff">takeoff</a> or <a href="/wiki/Landing" title="Landing">landing</a>, or during low altitude flight.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> However, bird strikes have also been reported at high altitudes, some as high as 6,000 to 9,000&#160;m (20,000 to 30,000&#160;ft) above the ground. <a href="/wiki/Bar-headed_goose" title="Bar-headed goose">Bar-headed geese</a> have been seen flying as high as 10,175&#160;m (33,383&#160;ft) above sea level. An aircraft over the <a href="/wiki/Ivory_Coast" title="Ivory Coast">Ivory Coast</a> collided with a <a href="/wiki/R%C3%BCppell%27s_vulture" title="R\xc3\xbcppell&#39;s vulture">R\xc3\xbcppell\'s vulture</a> at the altitude of 11,300&#160;m (37,100&#160;ft), the current record avian height.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The majority of bird collisions occur near or at <a href="/wiki/Airport" title="Airport">airports</a> (90%, according to the <a href="/wiki/International_Civil_Aviation_Organization" title="International Civil Aviation Organization">ICAO</a>) during takeoff, landing and associated phases. According to the FAA wildlife hazard management manual for 2005, less than 8% of strikes occur above 900&#160;m (3,000&#160;ft) and 61% occur at less than 30&#160;m (98&#160;ft).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>The point of impact is usually any forward-facing edge of the vehicle such as a wing leading edge, nose cone, jet engine cowling or engine inlet.\n</p><p>Jet engine ingestion is extremely serious due to the rotation speed of the engine fan and engine design. As the bird strikes a fan blade, that blade can be displaced into another blade and so forth, causing a <a href="/wiki/Cascading_failure" title="Cascading failure">cascading failure</a>. Jet engines are particularly vulnerable during the takeoff phase when the engine is turning at a very high speed and the plane is at a low altitude where birds are more commonly found.\n</p><p>The force of the impact on an aircraft depends on the weight of the animal and the speed difference and direction at the point of impact. The energy of the impact increases with the square of the speed difference. High-speed impacts, as with jet aircraft, can cause considerable damage and even <a href="/wiki/Catastrophic_failure" title="Catastrophic failure">catastrophic failure</a> to the vehicle. The <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a> of a 5&#160;kg (11&#160;lb) bird moving at a <a href="/wiki/Relative_velocity" title="Relative velocity">relative velocity</a> of 275&#160;km/h (171&#160;mph) approximately equals the energy of a 100&#160;kg (220&#160;lb) weight dropped from a height of 15 metres (49&#160;ft).<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> However, according to the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Aviation_Administration" title="Federal Aviation Administration">FAA</a> only 15% of strikes (ICAO 11%) actually result in damage to the aircraft.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>Bird strikes can damage vehicle components, or injure passengers. <a href="/wiki/Herd" title="Herd">Flocks</a> of birds are especially dangerous and can lead to multiple strikes, with corresponding damage. Depending on the damage, aircraft at low altitudes or during take-off and landing often cannot recover in time.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/US_Airways_Flight_1549" title="US Airways Flight 1549">US Airways Flight 1549</a> is a classic example of this. The engines on the <a href="/wiki/Airbus_A320_family#A320" title="Airbus A320 family">Airbus A320</a> used on that flight were torn apart by multiple bird strikes at low altitude. There was no time to make a safe landing at an airport, forcing a <a href="/wiki/Water_landing" title="Water landing">water landing</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Hudson_River" title="Hudson River">Hudson River</a>.\n</p><p>Remains of the bird, termed <i>snarge</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> are sent to identification centers where <a href="/wiki/Forensic" class="mw-redirect" title="Forensic">forensic</a> techniques may be used to identify the species involved. These samples need to be taken carefully by trained personnel to ensure proper analysis<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> and reduce the risks of infection (<a href="/wiki/Zoonoses" class="mw-redirect" title="Zoonoses">zoonoses</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Species">Species</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bird_strike&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Species">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Most bird strikes involve large birds with big populations, particularly <a href="/wiki/Geese" class="mw-redirect" title="Geese">geese</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gull" title="Gull">gulls</a> in the United States. In parts of the US, <a href="/wiki/Canada_geese" class="mw-redirect" title="Canada geese">Canada geese</a> and migratory <a href="/wiki/Snow_geese" class="mw-redirect" title="Snow geese">snow geese</a> populations have risen significantly<sup id="cite_ref-BSC-USA_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BSC-USA-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> while feral Canada geese and <a href="/wiki/Greylag_geese" class="mw-redirect" title="Greylag geese">greylag geese</a> have increased in parts of Europe, increasing the risk of these large birds to aircraft.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> In other parts of the world, large birds of prey such as <i><a href="/wiki/Gyps" title="Gyps">Gyps</a></i> vultures and <i><a href="/wiki/Milvus" title="Milvus">Milvus</a></i> kites are often involved.<sup id="cite_ref-thorpe_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-thorpe-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> In the US, reported strikes are mainly from <a href="/wiki/Wildfowl" class="mw-redirect" title="Wildfowl">waterfowl</a> (30%), <a href="/wiki/Gull" title="Gull">gulls</a> (22%), <a href="/wiki/Bird_of_prey" title="Bird of prey">raptors</a> (20%), and <a href="/wiki/Columbidae" title="Columbidae">pigeons and doves</a> (7%).<sup id="cite_ref-BSC-USA_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BSC-USA-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> The Smithsonian Institution\'s Feather Identification Laboratory has identified <a href="/wiki/Turkey_vultures" class="mw-redirect" title="Turkey vultures">turkey vultures</a> as the most damaging birds, followed by Canada geese and <a href="/wiki/American_white_pelican" title="American white pelican">white pelicans</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-wired_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wired-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> all of which are very large birds. In terms of frequency, the laboratory most commonly finds <a href="/wiki/Mourning_dove" title="Mourning dove">mourning doves</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shore_lark" class="mw-redirect" title="Shore lark">horned larks</a> involved in the strike.<sup id="cite_ref-wired_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wired-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The largest numbers of strikes happen during the spring and fall migrations. Bird strikes above 500 feet (150&#160;m) altitude are about 7 times more common at night than during the day during the bird migration season.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Large land animals, such as deer, can also be a problem to aircraft during takeoff and landing. Between 1990 and 2013, civil aircraft experienced more than 1,000 collisions with deer and 440 with <a href="/wiki/Coyote" title="Coyote">coyotes</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-BSC-USA_18-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BSC-USA-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>An animal hazard reported from <a href="/wiki/London_Stansted_Airport" title="London Stansted Airport">London Stansted Airport</a> in England is <a href="/wiki/Rabbit" title="Rabbit">rabbits</a>: they get run over by ground vehicles and planes, and they pass large amounts of droppings, which attract mice, which in turn attract <a href="/wiki/Owl" title="Owl">owls</a>, which then become another birdstrike hazard.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Countermeasures">Countermeasures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bird_strike&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Countermeasures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>There are three approaches to reduce the effect of bird strikes. The vehicles can be designed to be more <i>bird resistant</i>, the birds can be moved out of the way of the vehicle, or the vehicle can be moved out of the way of the birds.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Vehicle_design">Vehicle design</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bird_strike&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Vehicle design">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Most large commercial jet engines include design features that ensure they can shut-down after "ingesting" a bird weighing up to 1.8&#160;kg (4.0&#160;lb). The engine does not have to survive the ingestion, just be safely shut down. This is a \'stand-alone\' requirement, <i>i.e.</i>, the engine, not the aircraft, must pass the test. Multiple strikes (from hitting a bird <a href="/wiki/Flock_(birds)" title="Flock (birds)">flock</a>) on twin-engine jet aircraft are very serious events because they can disable multiple aircraft systems, requiring emergency action to land the aircraft, as in the January 15, 2009 forced ditching of <a href="/wiki/US_Airways_Flight_1549" title="US Airways Flight 1549">US Airways Flight 1549</a>.\n</p><p>Modern jet aircraft structures must be able to withstand one 1.8&#160;kg (4.0&#160;lb) collision; the empennage (tail) must withstand one 3.6&#160;kg (7.9&#160;lb) bird collision. Cockpit windows on jet aircraft must be able to withstand one 1.8&#160;kg (4.0&#160;lb) bird collision without yielding or <a href="/wiki/Spalling" class="mw-redirect" title="Spalling">spalling</a>.\n</p><p>At first, bird strike testing by manufacturers involved firing a bird carcass from a gas cannon and <a href="/wiki/Sabot_(firearms)" title="Sabot (firearms)">sabot</a> system into the tested unit. The carcass was soon replaced with suitable density blocks, often <a href="/wiki/Gelatin" title="Gelatin">gelatin</a>, to ease testing. Current testing is mainly conducted with <a href="/wiki/Computer_simulation" title="Computer simulation">computer simulation</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> although final testing usually involves some physical experiments (see <a href="/wiki/Birdstrike_simulator" class="mw-redirect" title="Birdstrike simulator">birdstrike simulator</a>).\n</p><p>Based on US <a href="/wiki/National_Transportation_Safety_Board" title="National Transportation Safety Board">NTSB</a> recommendation following the 2009 US Airways Flight 1549, the <a href="/wiki/European_Aviation_Safety_Agency" title="European Aviation Safety Agency">EASA</a> in 2017, followed a year after by the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Aviation_Administration" title="Federal Aviation Administration">FAA</a>, proposed that engines should sustain a bird strike not only on <a href="/wiki/Takeoff" title="Takeoff">takeoff</a> where turbofans are turning at their fastest, but also in <a href="/wiki/Climb_(aeronautics)" title="Climb (aeronautics)">climb</a> and <a href="/wiki/Descent_(aeronautics)" title="Descent (aeronautics)">descent</a> when they turn more slowly; new regulations could apply for the <a href="/wiki/Boeing_New_Midsize_Airplane" title="Boeing New Midsize Airplane">Boeing NMA</a> engines.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wildlife_management">Wildlife management</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bird_strike&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Wildlife management">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:B-6543_(7788321152).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/B-6543_%287788321152%29.jpg/220px-B-6543_%287788321152%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/B-6543_%287788321152%29.jpg/330px-B-6543_%287788321152%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/B-6543_%287788321152%29.jpg/440px-B-6543_%287788321152%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3416" data-file-height="2277" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:B-6543_(7788321152).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An <a href="/wiki/Airbus_A330" title="Airbus A330">Airbus A330</a> of <a href="/wiki/China_Eastern" class="mw-redirect" title="China Eastern">China Eastern</a> behind a flock of birds at <a href="/wiki/London_Heathrow" class="mw-redirect" title="London Heathrow">London Heathrow</a></div></div></div>\n<p>Though there are many methods available to wildlife managers at airports, no single method will work in all instances and with all species. Wildlife management in the airport environment can be grouped into two broad categories: non-lethal and lethal. Integration of multiple non-lethal methods with lethal methods results in the most effective airfield wildlife management strategy.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Non-lethal">Non-lethal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bird_strike&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Non-lethal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Non-lethal management can be further broken down into habitat manipulation, exclusion, visual, auditory, tactile, or chemical repellents, and relocation.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Habitat_manipulation">Habitat manipulation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bird_strike&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Habitat manipulation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>One of the primary reasons that wildlife is seen in airports is an abundance of food. Food resources on airports can be either removed or made less desirable. One of the most abundant food resources found on airports is turfgrass. This grass is planted to reduce runoff, control erosion, absorb jet wash, allow passage of emergency vehicles, and to be aesthetically pleasing (DeVault et al. 2013<sup id="cite_ref-L._DeVault,_B._F_2013_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-L._DeVault,_B._F_2013-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>) However, turfgrass is a preferred food source for species of birds that pose a serious risk to aircraft, chiefly the Canada goose (<i><a href="/wiki/Branta_canadensis" class="mw-redirect" title="Branta canadensis">Branta canadensis</a></i>). Turfgrass planted at airports should be a species that geese do not prefer (e.g. <a href="/wiki/St._Augustine_grass" title="St. Augustine grass">St. Augustine grass</a>) and should be managed in such a way that reduces its attractiveness to other wildlife such as small rodents and raptors (Commander, Naval Installations Command 2010,<sup id="cite_ref-Commander,_Naval_Installations_Command_2010_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Commander,_Naval_Installations_Command_2010-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> DeVault et al. 2013<sup id="cite_ref-L._DeVault,_B._F_2013_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-L._DeVault,_B._F_2013-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>). It has been recommended that turfgrass be maintained at a height of 7\xe2\x80\x9314 inches through regular mowing and fertilization (U.S. Air Force 2004<sup id="cite_ref-U.S._Air_Force_2004_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-U.S._Air_Force_2004-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>).\n</p><p>Wetlands are another major attractant of wildlife in the airport environment. They are of particular concern because they attract waterfowl which have a high potential to damage aircraft (Federal Aviation Administration 2013<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>). With large areas of impervious surfaces, airports must employ methods to collect runoff and reduce its flow velocity. These best management practices often involve temporarily ponding runoff. Short of redesigning existing runoff control systems to include non-accessible water such as subsurface flow wetlands (DeVault et al. 2013<sup id="cite_ref-L._DeVault,_B._F_2013_25-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-L._DeVault,_B._F_2013-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>), frequent drawdowns and covering of exposed water with floating covers and wire grids should be employed (International Civil Aviation Organization 1991<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>). The implementation of covers and wire grids must not hinder emergency services.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Exclusion">Exclusion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bird_strike&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Exclusion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Though excluding birds from the entire airport environment is virtually impossible, it is possible to exclude deer and other mammals that constitute a small percentage of wildlife strikes. Three-meter high fences made of chain link or woven wire, with barbed wire outriggers, are the most effective. When used as a perimeter fence, these fences also serve to keep unauthorized people off of the airport (Seamans 2001<sup id="cite_ref-Seamans,_T._W._2001_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Seamans,_T._W._2001-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>).  Realistically every fence must have gates.  Gates that are left open allow deer and other mammals onto the airport.  4.6 meter long <a href="/wiki/Cattle_guard" class="mw-redirect" title="Cattle guard">cattle guards</a> have been shown to be effective at deterring deer up to 98% of the time (Belant et al. 1998<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>).\n</p><p>Hangars with open superstructures often attract birds to nest and roost in. Hangar doors are often left open to increase ventilation, especially in the evenings. Birds in hangars are in proximity to the airfield and their droppings are both a health and damage concern. Netting is often deployed across the superstructure of a hangar denying access to the rafters where the birds roost and nest while still allowing the hangar doors to remain open for ventilation and aircraft movements. Strip curtains and door netting may also be used but are subject to improper use (e.g. tying the strips to the side of the door) by those working in the hangar. (U.S. Air Force 2004,<sup id="cite_ref-U.S._Air_Force_2004_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-U.S._Air_Force_2004-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> Commander, Naval Installations Command 2010<sup id="cite_ref-Commander,_Naval_Installations_Command_2010_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Commander,_Naval_Installations_Command_2010-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>).\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Visual_repellents">Visual repellents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bird_strike&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Visual repellents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>There have been a variety of visual repellent and harassment techniques used in airport wildlife management. They include using birds of prey and dogs, effigies, <a href="/wiki/Landing_lights" title="Landing lights">landing lights</a>, and lasers. Birds of prey have been used with great effectiveness at landfills where there were large populations of feeding gulls (Cook et al. 2008<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>). Dogs have also been used with success as visual deterrents and means of harassment for birds at airfields (DeVault et al. 2013<sup id="cite_ref-L._DeVault,_B._F_2013_25-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-L._DeVault,_B._F_2013-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>). However, airport wildlife managers must consider the risk of knowingly releasing animals in the airport environment. Both birds of prey and dogs must be monitored by a handler when deployed and must be cared for, when not deployed. Airport wildlife managers must consider the economics of these methods (Seamans 2001<sup id="cite_ref-Seamans,_T._W._2001_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Seamans,_T._W._2001-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>).\n</p><p>Effigies of both predators and conspecifics have been used with success to disperse gulls and vultures. The effigies of conspecifics are often placed in unnatural positions where they can freely move with the wind. Effigies have been found to be the most effective in situations where the nuisance birds have other options (e.g. other forage, loafing, and roosting areas) available. Time to habituation varies. (Seamans et al. 2007,<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> DeVault et al. 2013<sup id="cite_ref-L._DeVault,_B._F_2013_25-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-L._DeVault,_B._F_2013-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>).\n</p><p>Lasers have been used with success to disperse several species of birds. However, lasers are species-specific as certain species will only react to certain wavelengths. Lasers become more effective as ambient light levels decrease, thereby limiting effectiveness during daylight hours. Some species show a very short time to habituation (Airport Cooperative Research Program, 2011<sup id="cite_ref-Airport_Cooperative_Research_Program_2011_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Airport_Cooperative_Research_Program_2011-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>). The risks of lasers to aircrews must be evaluated when determining whether or not to deploy lasers on airfields.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> Southampton Airport utilizes a laser device which disables the laser past a certain <a href="/wiki/Elevation_(astronomy)" class="mw-redirect" title="Elevation (astronomy)">elevation</a>, eliminating the risk of the beam being shone directly at aircraft and air traffic control tower (Southampton Airport 2014).<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Auditory_repellents">Auditory repellents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bird_strike&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Auditory repellents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Auditory repellents are commonly used in both agricultural and aviation contexts. Devices such as propane exploders (cannons), pyrotechnics, and bioacoustics are frequently deployed on airports. Propane exploders are capable of creating noises of approximately 130 decibels (Wildlife Control Supplies<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>). They can be programmed to fire at designated intervals, can be remote controlled, or motion activated. Due to their stationary and often predictable nature, wildlife quickly becomes habituated to propane cannons. Lethal control may be used to extend the effectiveness of propane exploders (Washburn et al. 2006).\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:PyroLauncherFDO.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/PyroLauncherFDO.jpg/220px-PyroLauncherFDO.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="293" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/PyroLauncherFDO.jpg/330px-PyroLauncherFDO.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/PyroLauncherFDO.jpg/440px-PyroLauncherFDO.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2448" data-file-height="3264" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:PyroLauncherFDO.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Wireless specialized launcher mounted in an airport vehicle</div></div></div>\n<p>Pyrotechnics utilizing either an exploding shell or a screamer can effectively scare birds away from runways. They are commonly launched from a 12 gauge shotgun or a flare pistol, or from a wireless specialized launcher and as such, can be aimed to allow control personnel to "steer" the species that is being harassed. Birds show varying degrees of habituation to pyrotechnics. Studies have shown that lethal reinforcement of pyrotechnic harassment has extended its usefulness (Baxter and Allen 2008<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>). Screamer type cartridges are still intact at the end of their flight (as opposed to exploding shells that destroy themselves) constituting a foreign object damage hazard and must be picked up. The use of pyrotechnics is considered "take" by the U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service (USFWS) and USFWS must be consulted if federally threatened or endangered species could be affected. Pyrotechnics are a potential fire hazard and must be deployed judiciously in dry conditions (Commander, Naval Installations Command, 2010,<sup id="cite_ref-Commander,_Naval_Installations_Command_2010_26-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Commander,_Naval_Installations_Command_2010-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> Airport Cooperative Research Program 2011<sup id="cite_ref-Airport_Cooperative_Research_Program_2011_34-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Airport_Cooperative_Research_Program_2011-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>).\n</p><p>Bioacoustics, or the playing of conspecific distress or predator calls to frighten animals, is widely used. This method relies on the animal\'s evolutionary danger response (Airport Cooperative Research Program 2011<sup id="cite_ref-Airport_Cooperative_Research_Program_2011_34-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Airport_Cooperative_Research_Program_2011-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>).However, bioacoustics are species-specific and birds may quickly become habituated to them and they should not be used as a primary means of control (U.S. Air Force 2004,<sup id="cite_ref-U.S._Air_Force_2004_27-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-U.S._Air_Force_2004-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> Commander, Naval Installations Command 2010<sup id="cite_ref-Commander,_Naval_Installations_Command_2010_26-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Commander,_Naval_Installations_Command_2010-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>).\n</p><p>In 2012, operators at <a href="/wiki/Gloucestershire_Airport" title="Gloucestershire Airport">Gloucestershire Airport</a> in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> revealed that <a href="/wiki/Tina_Turner_discography" title="Tina Turner discography">songs by</a> the American-Swiss singer <a href="/wiki/Tina_Turner" title="Tina Turner">Tina Turner</a> were more effective than animal noises for scaring birds from its runways.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Tactile_repellents">Tactile repellents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bird_strike&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Tactile repellents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Sharpened spikes to deter perching and loafing are commonly used. Generally, large birds require different applications than small birds do (DeVault et al. 2013<sup id="cite_ref-L._DeVault,_B._F_2013_25-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-L._DeVault,_B._F_2013-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>).\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical_repellents">Chemical repellents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bird_strike&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Chemical repellents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>There are only two chemical bird repellents registered for use in the United States. They are <a href="/wiki/Methyl_anthranilate" title="Methyl anthranilate">methyl anthranilate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Anthraquinone" title="Anthraquinone">anthraquinone</a>. Methyl anthranilate is a primary repellent that produces an immediate unpleasant sensation that is reflexive and does not have to be learned. As such it is most effective for transient populations of birds (DeVault et al. 2013<sup id="cite_ref-L._DeVault,_B._F_2013_25-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-L._DeVault,_B._F_2013-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>). Methyl anthranilate has been used with great success at rapidly dispersing birds from flight lines at Homestead Air Reserve Station (Engeman et al. 2002<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>). Anthraquinone is a secondary repellent that has a laxative effect that is not instantaneous. Because of this it is most effective on resident populations of wildlife that will have time to learn an aversive response (Izhaki 2002,<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> DeVault et al. 2013<sup id="cite_ref-L._DeVault,_B._F_2013_25-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-L._DeVault,_B._F_2013-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>).\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Relocation">Relocation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bird_strike&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Relocation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Relocation of raptors from airports is often considered preferable to lethal control methods by both biologists and the public. There are complex legal issues surrounding the capture and relocation of species protected by the Migratory Bird Treaty Act of 1918 and the Bald and Golden Eagle Protection Act of 1940. Prior to capture, proper permits must be obtained and the high mortality rates as well as the risk of disease transmission associated with relocation must be weighed. Between 2008 and 2010, U.S. Department of Agriculture Wildlife Services personnel relocated 606 red-tailed hawks from airports in the United States after the failure of multiple harassment attempts. The return rate of these hawks was 6%; however the relocation mortality rate for these hawks was never determined (DeVault et al. 2013<sup id="cite_ref-L._DeVault,_B._F_2013_25-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-L._DeVault,_B._F_2013-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>).\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Lethal">Lethal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bird_strike&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Lethal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Lethal wildlife control on airports falls into two categories: reinforcement of other non-lethal methods and population control.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Reinforcement">Reinforcement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bird_strike&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Reinforcement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>The premise of effigies, pyrotechnics, and propane exploders is that there be a perceived immediate danger to the species to be dispersed. Initially, the sight of an unnaturally positioned effigy or the sound of pyrotechnics or exploders is enough to elicit a danger response from wildlife. As wildlife become habituated to non-lethal methods the culling of small numbers of wildlife in the presence of conspecifics can restore the danger response (Baxter and Allan 2008, Cook et al. 2008, Commander, Naval Installations Command 2010,<sup id="cite_ref-Commander,_Naval_Installations_Command_2010_26-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Commander,_Naval_Installations_Command_2010-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> DeVault et al. 2013<sup id="cite_ref-L._DeVault,_B._F_2013_25-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-L._DeVault,_B._F_2013-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>).\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Population_control">Population control</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bird_strike&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Population control">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Under certain circumstances, lethal wildlife control is needed to control the population of a species. This control can be localized or regional. Localized population control is often used to control species that are residents of the airfield such as deer that have bypassed the perimeter fence. In this instance sharpshooting would be highly effective, such as is seen at Chicago O\'Hare International Airport (DeVault et al. 2013<sup id="cite_ref-L._DeVault,_B._F_2013_25-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-L._DeVault,_B._F_2013-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>).\n</p><p>Regional population control has been used on species that cannot be excluded from the airport environment. A nesting colony of laughing gulls at Jamaica Bay Wildlife Refuge contributed to 98\xe2\x80\x93315 bird strikes per year, in 1979\xe2\x80\x931992, at adjacent John F. Kennedy International Airport (JFK). Though JFK had an active bird management program that precluded birds from feeding and loafing on the airport, it did not stop them from overflying the airport to other feeding sites. U.S. Department of Agriculture Wildlife Services personnel began shooting all gulls that flew over the airport, hypothesizing that eventually, the gulls would alter their flight patterns. They shot 28,352 gulls in two years (approximately half of the population at Jamaica Bay and 5\xe2\x80\x936% of the nationwide population per year). Strikes with laughing gulls decreased by 89% by 1992. However this was more a function of the population reduction than the gulls altering their flight pattern (<a href="/wiki/Richard_Dolbeer" title="Richard Dolbeer">Dolbeer</a> et al. 1993,<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> Dolbeer et al. 2003,<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> DeVault et al. 2013<sup id="cite_ref-L._DeVault,_B._F_2013_25-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-L._DeVault,_B._F_2013-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>).\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Flight_path">Flight path</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bird_strike&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Flight path">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Pilots should not take off or land in the presence of wildlife and should avoid migratory routes,<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> wildlife reserves, estuaries and other sites where birds may congregate. When operating in the presence of bird flocks, pilots should seek to climb above 3,000 feet (910&#160;m) as rapidly as possible as most bird strikes occur below 3,000 feet (910&#160;m). Additionally, pilots should slow down their aircraft when confronted with birds. The energy that must be dissipated in the collision is approximately the relative <a href="/wiki/Kinetic_energy" title="Kinetic energy">kinetic energy</a> (<span class="mwe-math-element"><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"  alttext="{\\displaystyle E_{k}}">\n  <semantics>\n    <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n      <mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">\n        <msub>\n          <mi>E</mi>\n          <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n            <mi>k</mi>\n          </mrow>\n        </msub>\n      </mstyle>\n    </mrow>\n    <annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\\displaystyle E_{k}}</annotation>\n  </semantics>\n</math></span><img src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7587849b44d775263271e89499f4327eeac5dc81" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" aria-hidden="true" style="vertical-align: -0.671ex; width:1.128ex; height:1.676ex;" alt="v"/></span> is the relative velocity (the difference of the velocities of the bird and the plane, resulting in a lower absolute value if they are flying in the same direction and higher absolute value if they are flying in opposite directions). Therefore, the speed of the aircraft is much more important than the size of the bird when it comes to reducing energy transfer in a collision. The same can be said for jet engines: the slower the rotation of the engine, the less energy which will be imparted onto the engine at collision.\n</p><p>The body density of the bird is also a parameter that influences the amount of damage caused.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The US Military Avian Hazard Advisory System (AHAS) uses near real-time data from the 148 CONUS based National Weather Service Next Generation Weather Radar (NEXRAD or WSR 88-D) system to provide current bird hazard conditions for published military low-level routes, ranges, and military operating areas (MOAs). Additionally, AHAS incorporates weather forecast data with the Bird Avoidance Model (BAM) to predict soaring bird activity within the next 24 hours and then defaults to the BAM for planning purposes when activity is scheduled outside the 24-hour window. The BAM is a static historical hazard model based on many years of bird distribution data from Christmas Bird Counts (CBC), Breeding Bird Surveys (BBS), and National Wildlife Refuge Data. The BAM also incorporates potentially hazardous bird attractions such as landfills and golf courses. AHAS is now an integral part of military low-level mission planning, aircrew being able to access the current bird hazard conditions at <a href="/w/index.php?title=Www.usahas.com&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Www.usahas.com (page does not exist)">www.usahas.com</a>. AHAS will provide relative risk assessments for the planned mission and give aircrew the opportunity to select a less hazardous route should the planned route be rated severe or moderate. Prior to 2003, the US Air Force BASH Team bird strike database indicated that approximately 25% of all strikes were associated with low-level routes and bombing ranges. More importantly, these strikes accounted for more than 50% of all of the reported damage costs. After a decade of using AHAS for avoiding routes with severe ratings, the strike percentage associated with low-level flight operations has been reduced to 12% and associated costs cut in half.\n</p><p>Avian radar<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> is an important tool for aiding in bird strike mitigation as part of overall safety management systems at civilian and military airfields. Properly designed and equipped avian radars can track thousands of birds simultaneously in real-time, night and day, through 360\xc2\xb0 of coverage, out to ranges of 10&#160;km and beyond for flocks, updating every target\'s position (longitude, latitude, altitude), speed, heading, and size every 2\xe2\x80\x933 seconds. Data from these systems can be used to generate information products ranging from real-time threat alerts to historical analyses of bird activity patterns in both time and space. The United States Federal Aviation Administration (FAA) and the United States Department of Defense (DOD) have conducted extensive science-based field testing and validation of commercial avian radar systems for civil and military applications, respectively. The FAA used evaluations of commercial 3D avian radar systems developed and marketed by Accipiter Radar<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> as the basis for FAA Advisory Circular 150/5220-25<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> and a guidance letter<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> on using Airport Improvement Program funds to acquire avian radar systems at Part 139 airports.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> Similarly, the DOD-sponsored Integration and Validation of Avian Radars (IVAR)<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> project evaluated the functional and performance characteristics of Accipiter\xc2\xae avian radars under operational conditions at Navy, Marine Corps, and Air Force airfields. Accipiter avian radar systems operating at Seattle-Tacoma International Airport,<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup> Chicago O\'Hare International Airport, and Marine Corps Air Station Cherry Point made significant contributions to the evaluations carried out in the aforementioned FAA and DoD initiatives. Additional scientific and technical papers on avian radar systems are listed below,<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> and on the Accipiter Radar web site.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>A US company, DeTect, in 2003, developed the only production model bird radar in operational use for real-time, tactical bird-aircraft strike avoidance by air traffic controllers. These systems are operational at both commercial airports and military airfields. The system has widely used technology available for bird\xe2\x80\x93aircraft strike hazard (BASH) management and for real-time detection, tracking and alerting of hazardous bird activity at commercial airports, military airfields, and military training and bombing ranges. After extensive evaluation and on-site testing, MERLIN technology was chosen by NASA and was ultimately used for detecting and tracking dangerous vulture activity during the 22 space shuttle launches from 2006 to the conclusion of the program in 2011. The US Air Force has contracted DeTect since 2003 to provide the Avian Hazard Advisory System (AHAS)previously mentioned.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Netherlands_Organisation_for_Applied_Scientific_Research" title="Netherlands Organisation for Applied Scientific Research">TNO</a>, a Dutch R&amp;D Institute, has developed the successful ROBIN (Radar Observation of Bird Intensity) for the Royal Netherlands Airforce. ROBIN is a near real-time monitoring system for flight movements of birds. ROBIN identifies flocks of birds within the signals of large radar systems. This information is used to give Air Force pilots warning during landing and take-off. Years of observation of bird migration with ROBIN have also provided a better insight into bird migration behavior, which has had an influence on averting collisions with birds, and therefore on flight safety. Since the implementation of the ROBIN system at the Royal Netherlands Airforce the number of collisions between birds and aircraft in the vicinity of military airbases has decreased by more than 50%.\n</p><p>There are no civil aviation counterparts to the above military strategies. Some experimentation with small portable radar units has taken place at some airports. However, no standard has been adopted for radar warning nor has any governmental policy regarding warnings been implemented.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_incidents">History of There were 4,300 bird strikes listed by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Air_Force" title="United States Air Force">United States Air Force</a> and 5,900 by US civil aircraft in 2003.\n</p><p>The first reported bird strike was by <a href="/wiki/Wright_brothers" title="Wright brothers">Orville Wright</a> in 1905. According to the Wright Brothers\' diaries, "Orville [...] flew 4,751 meters in 4 minutes 45 seconds, four complete circles. Twice passed over the fence into Beard\'s cornfield. Chased flock of birds for two rounds and killed one which fell on top of the upper surface and after a time fell off when swinging a sharp curve."<sup id="cite_ref-thorpe_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-thorpe-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>During the <a href="/wiki/1911_Paris_to_Madrid_air_race" title="1911 Paris to Madrid air race">1911 Paris to Madrid air race</a>, French pilot <a href="/wiki/Eugene_Gilbert" class="mw-redirect" title="Eugene Gilbert">Eugene Gilbert</a> encountered an angry mother eagle over the <a href="/wiki/Pyrenees" title="Pyrenees">Pyrenees</a>. Gilbert, flying an open-cockpit <a href="/wiki/Bleriot_XI" class="mw-redirect" title="Bleriot XI">Bleriot XI</a>, was able to ward off the large bird by firing pistol shots at it but did not kill it.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-domenica_59-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-domenica-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The first recorded bird strike fatality was reported in 1912 when aero-pioneer <a href="/wiki/Cal_Rodgers" class="mw-redirect" title="Cal Rodgers">Cal Rodgers</a> collided with a gull which became jammed in his aircraft control cables. He crashed at <a href="/wiki/Long_Beach,_California" title="Long Beach, California">Long Beach</a>, California, was pinned under the wreckage, and drowned.<sup id="cite_ref-sodhi_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sodhi-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>During the 1952 edition of the <a href="/wiki/Carrera_Panamericana" title="Carrera Panamericana">Carrera Panamericana</a>, eventual race winners <a href="/wiki/Karl_Kling" title="Karl Kling">Karl Kling</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hans_Klenk" title="Hans Klenk">Hans Klenk</a> suffered a bird strike incident when the <a href="/wiki/Mercedes-Benz_W194" title="Mercedes-Benz W194">Mercedes-Benz W194</a> was struck by a <a href="/wiki/Vulture" title="Vulture">vulture</a> in the windscreen. During a long right-hand bend in the opening stage taken at almost 200&#160;km/h (120&#160;mph), Kling failed to spot vultures sitting by the side of the road. When the vultures were scattered after hearing the virtually unsilenced W194 coming towards them, one vulture impacted through the windscreen on the passenger side. The impact was enough to briefly knock Klenk unconscious. Despite bleeding badly from facial injuries caused by the shattered windscreen, Klenk ordered Kling to maintain speed, and held on until a tire change almost 70&#160;km (43&#160;mi) later to clean himself and the car up. For extra protection, eight vertical steel bars were bolted over the new windscreen.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup> Kling and Klenk also discussed the species and size of the dead bird, agreeing that had had a minimum 115-centimetre (45&#160;in) wingspan and weighed as much as five fattened geese.<sup id="cite_ref-worldcarfans2_62-0" The aircraft crashed into <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston</a> harbor shortly after takeoff, with 62 fatalities out of 72 passengers.<sup id="cite_ref-Telegraph_64-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Telegraph-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup> Subsequently, minimum bird ingestion standards for jet engines were developed by the FAA.\n</p><p>NASA astronaut <a href="/wiki/Theodore_Freeman" title="Theodore Freeman">Theodore Freeman</a> was killed in 1964 when a goose shattered the plexiglass cockpit canopy of his <a href="/wiki/Northrop_T-38_Talon" title="Northrop T-38 Talon">Northrop T-38 Talon</a>. Shards were ingested by the engines, leading to a fatal crash.<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1988, <a href="/wiki/Ethiopian_Airlines_Flight_604" title="Ethiopian Airlines Flight 604">Ethiopian Airlines Flight 604</a> sucked <a href="/wiki/Columbidae" title="Columbidae">pigeons</a> into both engines during takeoff and then crashed, killing 35 passengers.\n</p><p>In 1995, a <a href="/wiki/Dassault_Falcon_20" title="Dassault Falcon 20">Dassault Falcon 20</a> crashed at a <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a> airport during an emergency landing attempt after sucking <a href="/wiki/Lapwing" title="Lapwing">lapwings</a> into an engine, which caused an engine failure and a fire in the airplane\'s <a href="/wiki/Fuselage" title="Fuselage">fuselage</a>; all 10 people on board were killed.<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>On September 22, 1995, a U.S. Air Force <a href="/wiki/Boeing_E-3_Sentry" title="Boeing E-3 Sentry">Boeing E-3 Sentry</a> AWACS aircraft (Callsign Yukla 27, serial number 77-0354), <a href="/wiki/1995_Alaska_Boeing_E-3_Sentry_accident" title="1995 Alaska Boeing E-3 Sentry accident">crashed</a> shortly after takeoff from <a href="/wiki/Elmendorf_AFB" class="mw-redirect" title="Elmendorf AFB">Elmendorf AFB</a>. The aircraft lost power in both port side engines after these engines ingested several <a href="/wiki/Canada_geese" class="mw-redirect" title="Canada geese">Canada geese</a> during takeoff. The roller coaster has a height of over 200 feet and reaches speeds over 70 miles per hour.<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>On November 28, 2004, the nose landing gear of KLM Flight 1673, a <a href="/wiki/Boeing_737_Classic" title="Boeing 737 Classic">Boeing 737-400</a>, struck a bird during takeoff at <a href="/wiki/Amsterdam_Airport_Schiphol" title="Amsterdam Airport Schiphol">Amsterdam Airport Schiphol</a>. The incident was reported to air traffic control, the landing gear was raised normally, and the flight continued normally to its destination. Upon touching down at <a href="/wiki/Barcelona_International_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Barcelona International Airport">Barcelona International Airport</a>, the aircraft started deviating to the left of the runway centreline. The crew applied right rudder, braking, and the nose wheel steering tiller but could not keep the aircraft on the runway. After it veered off the paved surface of the runway at about 100 knots, the jet went through an area of soft sand. The nose landing gear leg collapsed and the left main landing gear leg detached from its fittings shortly before the aircraft came to a stop perched over the edge of a drainage canal. All 140 passengers and six crew evacuated safely, but the aircraft itself had to be written off. The cause was discovered to be a broken cable in the nose wheel steering system caused by the bird collision. Contributing to the snapped cable was the improper application of grease during routine maintenance which led to severe wear of the cable.<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In April 2007, a Thomsonfly <a href="/wiki/Boeing_757" title="Boeing 757">Boeing 757</a> from <a href="/wiki/Manchester_Airport" title="Manchester Airport">Manchester Airport</a> to <a href="/wiki/Lanzarote_Airport" title="Lanzarote Airport">Lanzarote Airport</a> suffered a bird strike when at least one bird, supposedly a crow, was ingested by the starboard engine. The plane landed safely back at Manchester Airport a while later. The incident was captured by two <a href="/wiki/Plane_spotter" class="mw-redirect" title="Plane spotter">plane spotters</a> on opposite sides of the airport, as well as the emergency calls picked up by a plane spotter\'s radio.<sup id="cite_ref-Telegraph_64-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Telegraph-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle_Discovery" title="Space Shuttle Discovery">Space Shuttle Discovery</a> also hit a bird (a vulture) during the launch of <a href="/wiki/STS-114" title="STS-114">STS-114</a> on July 26, 2005, although the collision occurred soon after lift-off and at low speed, with no obvious damage to the shuttle.<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>On November 10, 2008, <a href="/wiki/Ryanair_Flight_4102" title="Ryanair Flight 4102">Ryanair Flight 4102</a> from Frankfurt to Rome made an <a href="/wiki/Emergency_landing" title="Emergency landing">emergency landing</a> at <a href="/wiki/Ciampino_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Ciampino Airport">Ciampino Airport</a> after multiple bird strikes caused both engines to fail. After touchdown, the left main <a href="/wiki/Landing_gear" title="Landing gear">landing gear</a> collapsed, and the aircraft briefly veered off the runway. Passengers and crew were evacuated through the starboard emergency exits.<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">&#91;71&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>On January 4, 2009, a <a href="/wiki/Sikorsky_S-76" title="Sikorsky S-76">Sikorsky S-76</a> helicopter hit a <a href="/wiki/Red-tailed_hawk" title="Red-tailed hawk">red-tailed hawk</a> in Louisiana. The hawk hit the helicopter just above the windscreen. The impact forced the activation of the engine fire suppression control handles, retarding the throttles and causing the engines to lose power. Eight of the nine people on board died in the subsequent crash; the survivor, a passenger, was seriously injured.<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">&#91;72&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>On January 15, 2009, <a href="/wiki/US_Airways_Flight_1549" title="US Airways Flight 1549">US Airways Flight 1549</a> from <a href="/wiki/LaGuardia_Airport" title="LaGuardia Airport">LaGuardia Airport</a> to <a href="/wiki/Charlotte/Douglas_International_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Charlotte/Douglas International Airport">Charlotte/Douglas International Airport</a> ditched into the <a href="/wiki/Hudson_River" title="Hudson River">Hudson River</a> after experiencing a loss of both turbines. Future United States Air Force general <a href="/wiki/Henry_H._Arnold" title="Henry H. Arnold">Henry H. Arnold</a>, as a young officer, nearly lost control of his <a href="/wiki/Wright_Model_B" title="Wright Model B">Wright Model B</a> in 1911 after a bug flew into his eye while he was not wearing goggles, distracting him.\n</p><p>In 1986, a <a href="/wiki/Boeing_B-52_Stratofortress" title="Boeing B-52 Stratofortress">Boeing B-52 Stratofortress</a> on a low-level training mission entered a swarm of <a href="/wiki/Locust" title="Locust">locusts</a>. The insects\' impacts on the aircraft\'s windscreens rendered the crew unable to see, forcing them to abort the mission and fly using the aircraft\'s instruments alone. The aircraft eventually landed safely.<sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75">&#91;75&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 2010, the Australian <a href="/wiki/Civil_Aviation_Safety_Authority" title="Civil Aviation Safety Authority">Civil Aviation Safety Authority</a> (CASA) issued a warning to pilots about the potential dangers of flying through a locust swarm. CASA warned that the insects could cause loss of engine power and loss of visibility, and blocking of an aircraft\'s <a href="/wiki/Pitot_tube" title="Pitot tube">pitot tubes</a>, causing inaccurate <a href="/wiki/Airspeed" title="Airspeed">airspeed</a> readings.<sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76">&#91;76&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77">&#91;77&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Bug strikes can also affect the operation of machinery on the ground, especially <a href="/wiki/Motorcycles" class="mw-redirect" title="Motorcycles">motorcycles</a>.
