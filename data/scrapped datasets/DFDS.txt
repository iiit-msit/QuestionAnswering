It is the busiest shipping company of its kind in <a href="/wiki/Northern_Europe" title="Northern Europe">Northern Europe</a> and one of the busiest in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>. The company\'s name is an abbreviation of <b>Det Forenede Dampskibs-Selskab</b> (literally <i>The United Steamship Company</i>). DFDS was founded in 1866, when  <a href="/wiki/Carl_Frederik_Tietgen" title="Carl Frederik Tietgen">C.F. Tietgen</a> merged the three biggest Danish steamship companies of that day.\n</p><p>Although DFDS has generally concentrated on freight and passenger traffic on the <a href="/wiki/North_Sea" title="North Sea">North Sea</a> and to the <a href="/wiki/Baltic_Sea" title="Baltic Sea">Baltic Sea</a>, it has also operated freight services to the US, South America and the Mediterranean in the past. Since the 1980s, DFDS\' focus for shipping has been northern Europe. Today, DFDS operates a network of 25 routes with 50 freight and passenger ships in the North Sea, Baltic Sea and the English Channel under the name <a href="/wiki/DFDS_Seaways" title="DFDS Seaways">DFDS Seaways</a>. The rail and land based haulage and container activities are operated by DFDS Logistics.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc" role="navigation" aria-labelledby="mw-toc-heading"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" style="display:none" /><div class="toctitle" lang="en" dir="ltr"><h2 id="mw-toc-heading">Contents</h2><span class="toctogglespan"><label class="toctogglelabel" for="toctogglecheckbox"></label></span></div>\n<ul>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-1"><a href="#History"><span class="tocnumber">1</span> <span class="toctext">History</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-2"><a href="#Current_operations"><span class="tocnumber">2</span> <span class="toctext">Current operations</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-3"><a href="#Acquired_activities_now_integrated_in_DFDS"><span class="tocnumber">3</span> <span class="toctext">Acquired activities now integrated in DFDS</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-4"><a href="#See_also"><span class="tocnumber">4</span> <span class="toctext">See also</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-5"><a href="#References"><span class="tocnumber">5</span> <span class="toctext">References</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-6"><a href="#External_links"><span class="tocnumber">6</span> <span class="toctext">External links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=DFDS&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><b>The Beginnings</b>\n</p><p>Det Forenede Dampskibs-Selskab was formed on 11 December 1866 as a merger of the three biggest Danish steamship companies under the leadership of Danish financier <a href="/wiki/Carl_Frederik_Tietgen" title="Carl Frederik Tietgen">Carl Frederik Tietgen</a>. Operations began on 1 January 1867 with 19 ships with Copenhagen as the main starting point. The company\'s routes at the time were from Denmark to Norway, the Baltic, Belgium, the United Kingdom, Iceland and The Faeroe Islands, with ships carrying both freight and passengers. As the company grew, new connections were opened to Sweden, France, the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean" class="mw-redirect" title="Mediterranean">Mediterranean</a> and <a href="/wiki/Black_Sea" title="Black Sea">Black Sea</a>, as well as North America and South America. In addition, DFDS operated various domestic services in Denmark. After continued expansion of the fleet in the 1880s, DFDS became one of the world\'s ten largest ship-owning companies. After the takeover of <a href="/wiki/Thingvalla_Line" title="Thingvalla Line">Dampskibsselskabet Thingvalla</a> in 1898, the <a href="/wiki/Scandinavian_America_Line" title="Scandinavian America Line">Scandinavian-American Line</a> was established.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The Scandinavian-American Line continued trading to the United States until 1935.<sup id="cite_ref-Cowsill_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cowsill-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><b>The Two World Wars</b>\n</p><p>The First World War took a heavy toll on DFDS\' fleet, with 26 ships lost. During the post-war depression, a further 30 ships were laid up. The company revived with the establishment of new routes, and by the mid-1920s, DFDS\'s fleet consisted of 124 ships with a combined tonnage of 233,364&#160;<a href="/wiki/Gross_register_tonnage" title="Gross register tonnage">GRT</a>. The Second World War saw further losses to the company, with nine ships lost before the German invasion of Denmark in April 1940. A large number of DFDS ships fell into British hands after the German invasion, and they were used as troopships. German forces commandeered a total of 21 DFDS ships during the war. One DFDS ship, the <i>Kronprins Frederik</i>, was under construction when the war began. To prevent her usage by the Germans, vital engine parts were "lost", only to be discovered after the end of the war. In total, DFDS lost 31 ships during World War II, with a further three ships lost due to hitting mines after the end of the war.<sup id="cite_ref-Cowsill_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cowsill-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> In 1948, 48 people drowned when <i>Kj\xc3\xb8benhavn</i> hit a mine. Five people lost their lives in the mine explosion of <i>Ivar</i> in 1949 and, as recently as 1950, <i>Frigga</i> sank, without loss of life, after having hit a mine. To replace some of the lost ships, a number of almost-completed motor ships, which had been laid up awaiting the end of the war, were made ready. Gradually, the routes that had been discontinued since the beginning of the war, were reopened.\n</p><p><b>Between the Wars</b>\n</p><p>DFDS created a sensation when they launched the <b>World\'s first motor-driven short-sea passenger ship</b> in 1925, from the Elsinore Shipbuilding  &amp; Engineering Co.  The first of four sister vessels built between 1925 and 1932, the M.V. <i>Parkeston</i> ( 2,762&#160;<a href="/wiki/Gross_register_tonnage" title="Gross register tonnage">GRT</a> ) made her maiden voyage from Esbjerg to Harwich on 8 October 1925 at an average speed of 16.5 knots, burning 18 tons of oil per day compared with 55 tons of coal burnt by a similar predecessor on the route.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><b>The Fifties &amp; Sixties</b>\n</p><p>In 1950, DFDS was one of the first to introduce a door-to-door solution. Two ships were specially designed to transport small wooden containers. DFDS commenced a new service, linking Danish mainland to Greenland. This was discontinued in 1959. In 1957, Nordana Line - cargo service Gulf of Mexico-Mediterranean - began. For the first time in DFDS\'s history, the company played the role of cross-trader. 1964 saw the introduction of the first <a href="/wiki/Ro-ro" class="mw-redirect" title="Ro-ro">ro-ro</a> passenger ferry, when M.S. England entered service on a route connecting <a href="/wiki/Esbjerg" title="Esbjerg">Esbjerg</a> to <a href="/wiki/Harwich" title="Harwich">Harwich</a>. In 1965, the Transport Rationalization Department, which later became DFDS Transport, began its activities. M.S. <i>Akershus</i>, the first real passenger-and-car ship which could also take trucks and trailers, was entered into service on the <a href="/wiki/Frederikshavn" title="Frederikshavn">Frederikshavn</a>-<a href="/wiki/Oslo" title="Oslo">Oslo</a> route.\n</p><p>In 1966, a hundred years after its start, the DFDS fleet consisted of 13 passenger ships, 53 cargo vessels, 4 tugboats and 39 barges. A comprehensive new ship programme commenced, with 25 ships on order. The passenger ships served on routes connecting Denmark to Norway, the UK, Faroe Islands, Iceland and Finland (though the Finland service was discontinued in 1966) alongside domestic services. The freight services continued, linking Denmark to the Americas and various European and Mediterranean ports. <i>Botnia</i>, the last steamship, was sold after more than 50 years of service. DFDS was no longer a steamship company. Between 1967 and 1970, four identical car-passenger ferries, originally named m.s. <i>Kong Olav</i> V, m.s. <i>Prinsesse Margarethe</i>, m.s. <i>Aalborghus</i> and m.s. <i>Trekroner</i> entered service on the Copenhagen\xe2\x80\x94Oslo and Copenhagen\xe2\x80\x94<a href="/wiki/Aalborg" title="Aalborg">Aalborg</a> routes. However, the Copenhagen\xe2\x80\x94Aalborg service was closed that same year, in 1970.\n</p><p><b>The Seventies &amp; Eighties</b>\n</p><p>Subsequently, the <i>Aalborghus</i> and <i>Trekroner</i> were rebuilt and renamed <i>Dana Sirena</i> and <i>Dana Corona</i>, respectively, for ferry services on the Mediterranean. Confusingly, the ships\' names were later reversed, with <i>Dana Sirena</i> becoming <i>Dana Corona</i> and vice versa. For the Denmark\xe2\x80\x94UK service, new ships arrived in 1974 and 1978 in the form of <a href="/wiki/MS_Dana_Regina" class="mw-redirect" title="MS Dana Regina">MS&#160;<i>Dana Regina</i></a> and <a href="/wiki/MS_Dana_Anglia" class="mw-redirect" title="MS Dana Anglia">MS&#160;<i>Dana Anglia</i></a>, respectively.<sup id="cite_ref-Cowsill_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cowsill-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Domestic passenger traffic was discontinued in 1970 and domestic freight service in 1971. A great chapter in the history of DFDS had come to an end. The requirement for transport of cars was sharply on the rise at the beginning of the 1970s. Tonnage was designed to accommodate this, and all DFDS passenger routes were served by passenger ships with roll on/roll off facilities.\n</p><p>The 1980s saw period of growth for DFDS, with the acquisition of <a href="/wiki/Tor_Line" title="Tor Line">Tor Line</a> and Prinzenlinien. In addition to the ferries <i>Tor Britannia</i>, <i>Tor Scandinavia</i> and MS <i>Prinz Hamlet</i> that were acquired with these two deals, DFDS also purchased the <a href="/wiki/Effoa" class="mw-redirect" title="Effoa">Effoa</a> ferry <i>Wellamo</i> in 1981, renaming her <i>Dana Gloria</i>. Following this brief expansion, in 1982, the Mediterranean ferry services were discontinued and in 1984 DFDS gave up its deep-sea cargo routes completely, now concentrating solely on the European market. 1982 also saw the beginning of an ambitious project of operating a large <a href="/wiki/Cruiseferry" title="Cruiseferry">ferry/cruise ship</a>, <a href="/wiki/MS_Island_Escape" class="mw-redirect" title="MS Island Escape">MS <i>Scandinavia</i></a>, on the US East Coast under the brand of Scandinavian World Cruises. However, in 1983, the <i>Scandinavia</i> was moved to the Copenhagen\xe2\x80\x94Oslo service and sold two years later.<sup id="cite_ref-Cowsill_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cowsill-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>From 1982 to 1983, DFDS\'s passenger operations were branded as DFDS Danish Seaways (<a href="/wiki/Esbjerg" title="Esbjerg">Esbjerg</a>\xe2\x80\x94<a href="/wiki/Harwich" title="Harwich">Harwich</a>/<a href="/wiki/Newcastle_upon_Tyne" title="Newcastle upon Tyne">Newcastle upon Tyne</a>/<a href="/wiki/T%C3%B3rshavn" title="T\xc3\xb3rshavn">T\xc3\xb3rshavn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Copenhagen" title="Copenhagen">Copenhagen</a>\xe2\x80\x94<a href="/wiki/Oslo" title="Oslo">Oslo</a>, Newcastle upon Tyne\xe2\x80\x94Oslo), <a href="/wiki/DFDS_Tor_Line" class="mw-redirect" title="DFDS Tor Line">DFDS Tor Line</a> (<a href="/wiki/Gothenburg" title="Gothenburg">Gothenburg</a>\xe2\x80\x94Harwich/Newcastle upon Tyne/<a href="/wiki/Amsterdam" title="Amsterdam">Amsterdam</a>) and DFDS Prins Ferries (Harwich\xe2\x80\x94<a href="/wiki/Hamburg" title="Hamburg">Hamburg</a>/<a href="/wiki/Bremerhaven" title="Bremerhaven">Bremerhaven</a>).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> DFDS also operated the Fred. Olsen Bergen Line routes Newcastle upon Tyne\xe2\x80\x94<a href="/wiki/Stavanger" title="Stavanger">Stavanger</a>/<a href="/wiki/Bergen" title="Bergen">Bergen</a> and Stavanger\xe2\x80\x94Amsterdam.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>. In 1987, the Transport Division and the Liner Division merged into DFDS Transport. The new division soon expanded, first in Sweden and subsequently acquired several forwarding companies, e.g. in the UK and the Netherlands. In 1988, it was decided to use Scandinavian Seaways for the DFDS passenger operations as a whole.\n</p><p><b>The Nineties</b>\n</p><p>In 1989-1990, the tonnage on the Oslo\xe2\x80\x94Copenhagen route was renewed with the entering into service in June 1990 of <a href="/wiki/MS_Queen_of_Scandinavia" class="mw-redirect" title="MS Queen of Scandinavia"><i>Queen Of Scandinavia</i></a>, a ship with a capacity of 2,000 passengers and 400 cars. Queen was followed, in 1994, by <a href="/wiki/MS_Crown_of_Scandinavia" class="mw-redirect" title="MS Crown of Scandinavia"><i>Crown Of Scandinavia</i></a>, with a similar capacity. In 1999, DFDS took over Dan Transport Holding, including the Dan Transport Travel Bureau and <a href="/wiki/Canal_Tours" title="Canal Tours">Canal Tours</a> Copenhagen. However, 17 months later, the whole transport division, named DFDS Dan Transport Group, was sold to transport group DSV, and the focus was once again on the former core business activities: ro-ro liner traffic and passenger shipping on overnight routes.\n</p><p><b>As of 2000</b>\n</p><p>In June 2001, the deal to acquire a 76.4 per cent shareholding in the Lithuanian shipping company LISCO was finalised. Lys-Line Rederi AS and Lys-Line AS were taken over 100 per cent by DFDS in 2005.\nIn August 2005 DFDS purchased 66 per cent of the trailer-forwarding company Hall\xc3\xa9ns in Belgium. In 2006, DFDS acquired the container shipping company Norfolk Line Containers. In 2010, DFDS purchased <a href="/wiki/Norfolkline" title="Norfolkline">Norfolkline</a> from <a href="/wiki/Maersk" title="Maersk">Maersk</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> In 2010, DFDS sold DFDS Canal Tours because it was not regarded as a core activity.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Current_operations">Current operations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=DFDS&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Current operations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">For more information, see <a href="/wiki/DFDS_Seaways" title="DFDS Seaways">DFDS Seaways</a>.</div>\n<p>DFDS Seaways is the shipping division of DFDS <a href="/wiki/Aktieselskab" title="Aktieselskab">A/S</a> operating a network of 25 shipping routes with 50 freight and passenger ships on the North Sea, Baltic Sea and the English Channel. DFDS Logistics operates land transport and logistics activities including the former DFDS Lys Line and DFDS Container Line. Below is an overview of the shipping activities that are integrated in the DFDS Group.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Acquired_activities_now_integrated_in_DFDS">Acquired activities now integrated in DFDS</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=DFDS&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Acquired activities now integrated in DFDS">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><b>DFDS Tor Line</b>\n</p>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">For more information, see <a href="/wiki/DFDS_Tor_Line" class="mw-redirect" title="DFDS Tor Line">DFDS Tor Line</a>.</div>\n<p>DFDS Tor Line was the main freight-carrying division of DFDS. It operated a large number of <a href="/wiki/RO-RO" class="mw-redirect" title="RO-RO">RO-RO</a> freighters on the <a href="/wiki/North_Sea" title="North Sea">North Sea</a> as well as the <a href="/wiki/Baltic_Sea" title="Baltic Sea">Baltic Sea</a>. It was formed as merger of DFDS\' and <a href="/wiki/Tor_Line" title="Tor Line">Tor Line</a>\'s freight operations after DFDS bought the latter in 1982 (until 1988 the passenger service between Sweden and the United Kingdom was also marketed as DFDS Tor Line). The operation of DFDS Tor Line were integrated into DFDS following the acquisition of <a href="/wiki/Norfolkline" title="Norfolkline">Norfolkline</a> in 2010.\n</p><p><b>DFDS Lisco</b>\nDFDS Lisco was a <a href="/wiki/Lithuania" title="Lithuania">Lithuanian</a> subsidiary acquired by DFDS in 2001. It operated five lines with seven ferries for passengers and freight connecting Germany to Lithuania, Latvia and Russia as well as Germany to Sweden. DFDS Lisco had subsidiaries in Germany (DFDS Lisco GmbH), Sweden (DFDS Tor Line AB), Latvia (DFDS Tor Line SIA) and Russia (OOO DFDS Lisco). The operation of DFDS Lisco were integrated into DFDS Seaways following the acquisition of <a href="/wiki/Norfolkline" title="Norfolkline">Norfolkline</a> in 2010.\n</p><p><b>DFDS Lys Line</b>\nDFDS Lys Line transported freight from Norway, Sweden and Denmark to Germany, the Netherlands, Belgium, the United Kingdom, Ireland, Spain, Portugal and Italy, offering door-to-door transportation. The company was founded in 1970. The operation of DFDS Lys Line were integrated into DFDS following the acquisition of <a href="/wiki/Norfolkline" title="Norfolkline">Norfolkline</a> in 2010.\n</p><p><b>DFDS Container Line</b>\nDFDS Container Line transports containers between <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a>. On October 2, 2006 the acquisition of Norfolkline Containers by DFDS A/S was completed<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>.  Established in August 1997 Norfolkline Containers was set up to offer Direct Port-to-Port services between Ireland and mainland Europe<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>. The operation of DFDS Container Line were integrated into DFDS following the acquisition of <a href="/wiki/Norfolkline" title="Norfolkline">Norfolkline</a> in 2010. As of now all of the things from 2010 were made by Raj Patel of Hanover Park\n</p><p><b>Norfolkline</b>\nNorfolkline was a European ferry operator and logistics company owned by Maersk. It provided freight ferry services on the English channel, Irish Sea, and the North Sea; and passenger ferry services on the English channel and Irish Sea; and logistics services across Europe. Norfolkline employed more than 2,200 employees in 13 countries across Europe, operating out of 35 different locations. It was acquired by DFDS in July 2010. Maersk received a 31.3 percent stake in DFDS as part payment. After a two-year lock-up period of the shares they were sold in September 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><b>U.N. Ro-Ro</b> U.N. Ro-Ro operates five freight ferry routes connecting Turkey with Trieste in Italy and Toulon in France with 12 freight ferries and its own port terminals in Istanbul and Trieste. In Europe, the company also offers intermodal solutions to and from other European markets. The company has an annual revenue of EUR 240 million and 500 employees. In April 2018, European shipping and logistics group DFDS signed an agreement with the owners of the Turkish shipping company U.N. Ro-Ro to acquire the shares in the company.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span
