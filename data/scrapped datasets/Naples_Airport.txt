<b>NAP</b>, <a href="/wiki/ICAO_airport_code" title="ICAO airport code">ICAO</a>: <b>LIRN</b>) (<a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">Italian</a>: <i lang="it">Aeroporto Internazionale di Napoli</i>) is the <a href="/wiki/International_airport" title="International airport">international airport</a> serving <a href="/wiki/Naples" title="Naples">Naples</a> and the Southern Italian region of <a href="/wiki/Campania" title="Campania">Campania</a>. According to 2019 data,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> the airport is the <a href="/wiki/List_of_the_busiest_airports_in_Italy" title="List of the busiest airports in Italy">fifth-busiest airport</a> in <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a> and the first one in <a href="/wiki/Southern_Italy" title="Southern Italy">Southern Italy</a>. The airport serves as a base for <a href="/wiki/EasyJet" title="EasyJet">easyJet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ryanair" title="Ryanair">Ryanair</a> and <a href="/wiki/Volotea" title="Volotea">Volotea</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Located 3.2&#160;<a href="/wiki/Nautical_mile" title="Nautical mile">NM</a> (5.9&#160;km; 3.7&#160;mi) north-northeast<sup id="cite_ref-AIP_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AIP-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> of the city in the <a href="/wiki/San_Pietro_a_Patierno" title="San Pietro a Patierno">San Pietro a Patierno</a> quarter of Naples, the airport is officially named <i>Aeroporto di Napoli-Capodichino Ugo Niutta</i>,  after decorated <a href="/wiki/WWI" class="mw-redirect" title="WWI">WWI</a> pilot Ugo Niutta.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc" role="navigation" aria-labelledby="mw-toc-heading"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Naples_International_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The district of <a href="/wiki/Capodichino" class="mw-redirect" title="Capodichino">Capodichino</a> \xe2\x80\x93 in the area known as "Campo di Marte" \xe2\x80\x93 hosted the first flight exhibitions in Naples in 1910. During the <a href="/wiki/First_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="First World War">First World War</a>, "Campo di Marte" became a military airport in order to defend the town against <a href="/wiki/Austro-Hungarian" class="mw-redirect" title="Austro-Hungarian">Austro-Hungarian</a> and German air attacks.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>During <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, it was used as a combat airfield by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army_Air_Forces" title="United States Army Air Forces">United States Army Air Forces</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Air_Force" title="Royal Air Force">Royal Air Force</a> extensively during the <a href="/wiki/Italian_Campaign_(World_War_II)" class="mw-redirect" title="Italian Campaign (World War II)">Italian Campaign</a>. The airfield was first used by RAF <a href="/w/index.php?title=No._324_Wing_RAF&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="No. 324 Wing RAF (page does not exist)">No. 324 Wing</a> with its five squadrons of <a href="/wiki/Supermarine_Spitfire" title="Supermarine Spitfire">Supermarine Spitfires</a> in 1943. It was then used by the US <a href="/wiki/Twelfth_Air_Force" title="Twelfth Air Force">Twelfth Air Force</a> which stationed the following units at the airport: <a href="/wiki/79th_Fighter_Group" class="mw-redirect" title="79th Fighter Group">79th Fighter Group</a> (January\xe2\x80\x93May 1944, <a href="/wiki/Curtiss_P-40_Warhawk" title="Curtiss P-40 Warhawk">P-40 Warhawk/P-47 Thunderbolt</a>); <a href="/wiki/47th_Bombardment_Group" class="mw-redirect" title="47th Bombardment Group">47th Bombardment Group</a> (March\xe2\x80\x93April 1944, <a href="/wiki/A-20_Havoc" class="mw-redirect" title="A-20 Havoc">A-20 Havoc</a>); <a href="/wiki/33d_Fighter_Group" class="mw-redirect" title="33d Fighter Group">33d Fighter Group</a> (April\xe2\x80\x93May 1944, <a href="/wiki/P-40_Warhawk" class="mw-redirect" title="P-40 Warhawk">P-40 Warhawk</a>). When the combat units moved out, <a href="/wiki/Air_Transport_Command_(United_States_Air_Force)" class="mw-redirect" title="Air Transport Command (United States Air Force)">Air Transport Command</a> used the airport as a major transshipment hub for cargo, transiting aircraft and personnel for the remainder of the war.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Commercial traffic started in 1950. In 1980 GE.S.A.C. ("Gestione Servizi Aeroporto Capodichino") was established to administer the airport; in 1982 it became "Gestione Servizi Aeroporti Campani" and participated in by the City Council, the province of Naples and Alitalia. In 1995 GE.S.A.C. drew up \xe2\x80\x93 with BAA assistance \xe2\x80\x93 a new master plan, which marked the beginning of a twenty-year development plan. After two years (1997) GE.S.A.C. was the first airport management company in Italy to be privatised: BAA acquires 70% of the share package from the City Council and Province of Naples.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> In 1998 the "Galleria Napoli" opened, a shopping arcade open 365 days a year inside Terminal 1. In 2002 <a href="/wiki/Prince_Charles" class="mw-redirect" title="Prince Charles">H.R.H. Prince Charles</a> inaugurated the new departure lounge.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Facilities">Facilities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span Following a report 30 minutes after departure when the crew reported en route at 6500 feet and switching to the Rome ATC, nothing further was heard from the flight, which never contacted Rome,<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> nor arrived in Greece. Dense fog over the <a href="/wiki/Ionian_Sea" title="Ionian Sea">Ionian Sea</a> and mountainous southern Italy on 17 February greatly impeded search efforts for the missing aircraft. "U.S. authorities did not exclude the possibility the plane might have been forced down in Communist <a href="/wiki/Albania" title="Albania">Albania</a>."<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>On 19 February 1958, the burned and scattered wreckage was found high on the rugged slope of <a href="/wiki/Mount_Vesuvius" title="Mount Vesuvius">Mount Vesuvius</a> at the 3,800-foot level, about 200 feet below the top of the cone of the <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">volcano</a>. A search plane first spotted the wreckage following "four days of fruitless ground, sea and air search impeded by fog, rain and snow." Patrols of U.S. servicemen, Italian soldiers and <a href="/wiki/Carabinieri" title="Carabinieri">carabinieri</a> reached the crash site four hours after it was found, battling though heavy snow, but reported no survivors amongst the 16 on board. They stated that all had been identified. According to a 1958 Associated Press report, "a surgeon said death apparently was instantaneous." There were 15 Air Force officers and men from Ramstein-Landstuhl Air Base, and one seaman of the <a href="/wiki/USS_Tripoli_(CVE-64)" title="USS Tripoli (CVE-64)">USS <i>Tripoli</i></a> on board. The report stated that "officials declined to venture a theory on the cause of the crash except that the weather was bad and the pilot, Capt. Martin S. Schwartz of <a href="/wiki/Ashland,_Kentucky" title="Ashland, Kentucky">Ashland</a>, Kentucky, had not previously flown from Capodichino field."<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Use_by_U.S._military_forces">Use by U.S. military forces</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Naples_International_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Use by U.S. military forces">edit</a><span
