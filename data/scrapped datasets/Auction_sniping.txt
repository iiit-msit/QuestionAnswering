href="#eSnipe"><span class="tocnumber">5</span> <span class="toctext">eSnipe</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-13"><a href="#See_also"><span class="tocnumber">6</span> <span class="toctext">See also</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-14"><a href="#References"><span class="tocnumber">7</span> <span class="toctext">References</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-15"><a href="#External_links"><span class="tocnumber">8</span> <span class="toctext">External links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Usage">Usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Auction_sniping&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Online services claim that their use decreases the failure rate of the snipe, because they have more reliable servers and a faster Internet connection with less variation in latency, allowing the bid to more reliably be placed close to the deadline.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Strategy">Strategy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Auction_sniping&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Strategy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Experienced bidders on online auctions with fixed ending-times often prefer entering bids late in the auction<sup id="cite_ref-best_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-best-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> to avoid <b>bidding wars</b> (multiple rounds of bidders each increasing their maximum bid to temporarily regain "current highest bid" status)<sup id="cite_ref-pays_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pays-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> or <b>bid chasing</b> (where the presence of an existing bid encourages others to bid on the same item).\n</p><p>One economic analysis of sniping suggests that sniping is a rational gain-maximizing (in other words, price-minimizing) strategy for bidders in auctions that fulfill two criteria:\n</p>\n<ol><li>the end time is rigidly fixed</li>\n<li>it is possible to gain additional information about the "true" value of the item by inspecting previous bids<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup></li></ol>\n<p>For example, a novice antiques buyer may prefer to bid in auctions which already have bids placed by more experienced antiques buyers, on the grounds that the items which the experienced buyers are interested in are more likely to be valuable. In this case, more-informed buyers may delay bidding until the last minutes of the auction to avoid creating competition for their bids, leading to a lower winning bid.\n</p><p>An analysis of actual winning bids on eBay suggests that winning bidders are more likely to have placed a single bid late in the auction, rather than to have placed multiple incremental bids as the auction progresses.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Avoidance_of_maximum_bid_fishing">Avoidance of maximum bid fishing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Auction_sniping&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Avoidance of maximum bid fishing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Many online auctions use <a href="/wiki/Proxy_bid" title="Proxy bid">proxy bidding</a>, an iterative sealed bid auction where winners pay a fixed increment over the second highest bid. The auctioneer does not disclose the current maximum bid, but the second highest bid is always public.\n</p><p>In proxy bidding, the wise bidder must know in advance the "true" value of an item as a basis for their secret bidding limit.<sup id="cite_ref-pays_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pays-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The fact that the maximum bid is revealed when it is outbid introduces the possibility of maximum bid fishing. Bidders unsure of the value of an item may incrementally increase their bid until they narrowly exceed the previously hidden maximum, thus placing themselves in a winning position without placing a very high bid.\n</p><p>Sniping eliminates this possibility and effectively converts the auction to a <a href="/wiki/Vickrey_auction" title="Vickrey auction">Vickrey auction</a>, the same as a proxy bidding auction <i>except</i> that all bids are kept secret until the close of bidding.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Shill_avoidance">Shill avoidance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Auction_sniping&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Shill avoidance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Sniping closes a loophole to fraudulent practice by a <a href="/wiki/Shill" title="Shill">shill</a> (an agent for the seller, which may be another account of the seller) to raise the bid to the maximum. They then hope the original bidder will increase their maximum bid even by a small amount to win the auction. The danger to the seller in this case is that the original bidder may not choose to increase their bid, leaving the seller with a futile transaction (selling the item to themselves) which will often still incur a fee from the auction service. Bidding at the last moment prevents a shill bidder from pushing the auction higher, as they have no time to make the incremental bids required.\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Simultaneous_bidding.2C_without_potentially_winning_multiple_bids"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Simultaneous_bidding,_without_potentially_winning_multiple_bids">Simultaneous bidding, without potentially winning multiple bids</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Auction_sniping&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Simultaneous bidding, without potentially winning multiple bids">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Bid sniping can be used when many similar items are available at auction simultaneously, with various ending times, and a bidder only wishes to win one item. Automated bid sniping tools allow for an efficient way to bid on multiple items, up to the maximum price the bidder wishes to pay, without bidding on the actual auction platform itself and potentially winning more than one of the auctions.\n</p><p>Once the bidder wins the desired item, they can cancel the other scheduled snipe bids before they are actually placed in the auction system itself. This is not a factor if the auction platform allows bidders to instantly withdraw their bids without reason, but many do not.\n</p><p>Increasing the number of similar or identical items bid on, reduces the attachment to winning any one of them. The ability to batch a queue of snipe bids for auctions long in advance and waiting for notification of the win without further management allows for a more efficient use of time.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Objections">Objections</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Auction_sniping&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Objections">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Non-sniping bidders may object to sniping, claiming that it is unfair to place bids at a point when it is impossible or unfeasible for other bidders to evaluate and possibly counter the bid, causing them to lose auctions even though they would have been willing to meet the winning bid amount. Bidders sometimes object to sniping when multiple identical items are listed as a separate lot, or on <b>breakup auctions</b>, where items that constitute a set are broken down and sold separately, as they must wait to find whether their maximum bid on one lot has been exceeded before being in a position to decide whether to bid on another.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="for everything in this para (May 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>However, online auction sites, unlike live auctions, usually have an automatic bidding system which allows a bidder to enter their maximum acceptable bid. This is a hidden or <a href="/wiki/Proxy_bid" title="Proxy bid">proxy bid</a>, known to the system, but not any other bidders; during the auction the actual bid is incremented only enough to beat the existing highest bid. For example, if an item\'s current maximum high bid is 57 and someone is prepared to pay 100 and bids accordingly, the displayed bid will be 58, with the hidden maximum of 100.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The failure of a maximum acceptable bid beaten by a sniper prepared to pay more is not due to the act of sniping, unless the original bidder would have bid higher on seeing their bid exceeded.  For this reason, opposition to sniping can be analyzed as more of a subjective reaction to losing an auction for the usual reason of not bidding enough, than a reaction to a "dirty trick".<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The beaten bidder would have beaten the sniper if their maximum bid had been more than the sniper was willing to offer.\n</p><p>However, if the minimum bid increment is very low, the <a href="/wiki/Sorites_paradox" title="Sorites paradox">sorites paradox</a> can come into play, and make it difficult for a person to establish a single maximum bid.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> For example, if the minimum bid increment on an auction is 10 cents, it can be difficult or impossible for a person to identify a price which they would be willing to pay to win the item but which they would not be willing to exceed by ten cents.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:No_original_research" title="Wikipedia:No original research"><span title="The material near this tag possibly contains original research. (July 2018)">original research?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Banning_by_auction_sites">Banning by auction sites</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Auction_sniping&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Banning by auction sites">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>While some people disapprove of auction sniping, it is not forbidden by the rules of many auction sites. For example, it is permitted by <a href="/wiki/EBay" title="EBay">eBay</a>.  eBay Germany banned <i>automated</i> sniping services in 2002,<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> but the ban was declared illegal by <a href="/wiki/Berlin" title="Berlin">Berlin</a>\'s County Court, and revoked.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Deterrents">Deterrents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Auction_sniping&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Deterrents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<table class="box-Multiple_issues plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-multiple_issues compact-ambox" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/b/b4/Ambox_important.svg/40px-Ambox_important.svg.png" decoding="async" width="40" height="40" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/b/b4/Ambox_important.svg/60px-Ambox_important.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/b/b4/Ambox_important.svg/80px-Ambox_important.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="40" data-file-height="40" /></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span"><div class="mw-collapsible" style="width:95%; This ensures that all bids are entered manually. Some software can read some CAPTCHAs, potentially neutralising the protection.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Final_extension">Final extension</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Auction_sniping&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Final extension">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Also known simply as "anti-snipe". Some online auction systems attempt to discourage sniping (manual or automated) by automatically extending the auction time if a last-minute bid is placed. This approach leaves all <a href="/wiki/English_auction" title="English auction">bidding open</a>, and allows any bidders who are watching during the final few minutes to raise the bid. It can also lead to last-minute automated out-of-control bidding wars between bidders, which can extend the bidding time long beyond what the seller desired, greatly raising the final selling price. Any site which implements a limit to the number of time extensions allowed simply causes a <b>final extension snipe</b>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Buy_It_Now">Buy It Now</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Auction_sniping&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Buy It Now">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Some auction systems allow buyers to end an auction early by paying a predetermined final price for the item (generally substantially more than the minimum opening bid). This may discourage some sniping because another bidder can simply purchase the item outright while the sniper is waiting for the auction end time, even if a successful snipe bid could win it for substantially less than the Buy It Now price. On eBay the <a href="/wiki/Buy_It_Now" class="mw-redirect" title="Buy It Now">Buy It Now</a> (BIN) option is not intended to deter sniping, and is removed as soon as a bid (exceeding a reserve price, if set) is made. A bidder intending to snipe can eliminate the Buy It Now option by making the lowest acceptable bid early in the auction.\n</p><p>The practice of buying newly listed Buy It Now items offered at an attractively low price is sometimes called "BIN sniping",<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> though unrelated to last-minute sniping. BIN sniping cannot be automated; the sniper must check items as soon they are listed, and buy before anyone else.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="eSnipe">eSnipe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Auction_sniping&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: eSnipe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><b>eSnipe</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Website" title="Website">website</a> designed to allow <a href="/wiki/EBay" title="EBay">eBay</a> buyers automatical auction sniping. The company eSnipe, Inc. was founded in 1999 and is based in <a href="/wiki/Bellevue,_Washington" title="Bellevue, Washington">Bellevue</a>, <a href="/wiki/Washington_(state)" title="Washington (state)">Washington</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> As of December 2010, eSnipe charges a fee if an <a href="/wiki/EBay" title="EBay">eBay</a> buyer makes a winning bid, and does not charge anything if the buyer does not make a winning bid.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
