class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span id="Early_years_under_Joseph_Segel_.281986.E2.80.931992.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_years_under_Joseph_Segel_(1986\xe2\x80\x931992)">Early years under Joseph Segel (1986\xe2\x80\x931992)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=QVC&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early years under Joseph Segel (1986\xe2\x80\x931992)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<h4><span id="QVC.27s_founding_and_television_launch"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="QVC\'s_founding_and_television_launch">QVC\'s founding and television launch</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=QVC&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: QVC&#039;s founding and television launch">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>QVC was founded on June 13, 1986, by <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Segel" title="Joseph Segel">Joseph Segel</a>. One of the first brands to sign a two-year deal with QVC for its products was <a href="/wiki/Sears" title="Sears">Sears</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The corporation later set a new record for first full-year fiscal sales for a new public company of $112 million.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The channel was launched on November 24, 1986 with program hosts Kathy Levine, John Eastman, Bob Bowersox, and Cindy Briggs-Moore.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Each November 24, QVC celebrated their birthday annually through 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Initially broadcasting live from 7:30&#160;p.m. until midnight ET each weekday and 24 hours a day each weekend, the channel extended its live programming to 24/7/364 in January 1987.  Former <a href="/wiki/QUBE" title="QUBE">QUBE</a> host and producer, <a href="/wiki/Ron_Giles_(television_executive)" title="Ron Giles (television executive)">Ron Giles</a>, was named an executive vice president and executive producer at QVC in late 1987.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> In October 1988, the board of directors elected Michael C. Boyd to the position of senior executive vice president and chief operating officer.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> In early 1990, Boyd would take the title of president, reportedly to relieve some of Segel\'s load.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="QVC_seeks_to_buy_out_its_competition">QVC seeks to buy out its competition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=QVC&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: QVC seeks to buy out its competition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>In 1989, QVC acquired its top competitor, the <a href="/wiki/Cable_Value_Network" class="mw-redirect" title="Cable Value Network">Cable Value Network</a> (CVN), founded by <a href="/wiki/Irwin_L._Jacobs" title="Irwin L. Jacobs">Irwin L. Jacobs</a>. The $380 million deal contributed to a loss of $17 million during the next fiscal quarter, and then led to difficulties in the couple of years that followed.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>QVC first offered to buy out competitor <a href="/wiki/J._C._Penney" class="mw-redirect" title="J. C. Penney">J. C. Penney</a> Television Shopping Channel on March 16, 1991,<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> a bid rejected by its producers and the Los Angeles Superior Court. On May 21, 1991, QVC picked up a further 4 million cable subscribers when it took over the <a href="/wiki/Burbank,_California" title="Burbank, California">Burbank</a>-produced JC Penney Shopping Channel, which owed $2 million to its producers.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>A QVC offer to buy rival <a href="/wiki/Home_Shopping_Network" class="mw-redirect" title="Home Shopping Network">Home Shopping Network</a> in March 1992 was sidetracked by legal problems.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> On July 12, 1993, QVC offered to acquire Home Shopping in a stock swap valued at about $1.1 billion, but talks faltered when QVC pursued a bid for <a href="/wiki/Paramount_Communications_Inc." class="mw-redirect" title="Paramount Communications Inc.">Paramount</a> in fall 1993.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Liberty Media Corp. held a controlling interest in the St. Petersberg, Florida-based Home Shopping Network along with their share of QVC.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Barry_Diller.2C_Celebrity_Executive_.281993.E2.80.931995.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Barry_Diller,_Celebrity_Executive_(1993\xe2\x80\x931995)">Barry Diller, Celebrity Executive (1993\xe2\x80\x931995)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=QVC&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Barry Diller, Celebrity Executive (1993\xe2\x80\x931995)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Plans_for_a_fifth_broadcast_network">Plans for a fifth broadcast network</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=QVC&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Plans for a fifth broadcast network">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Introduced to televised shopping by designer <a href="/wiki/Diane_von_F%C3%BCrstenberg" title="Diane von F\xc3\xbcrstenberg">Diane von F\xc3\xbcrstenberg</a> a decade before their marriage, <a href="/wiki/Fox_Broadcasting_Company" title="Fox Broadcasting Company">Fox</a> founder <a href="/wiki/Barry_Diller" title="Barry Diller">Barry Diller</a> pursued slick "infotainment"-style programming as his next television venture.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> After resigning as chairman of <a href="/wiki/Fox_Corporation" title="Fox Corporation">Fox Inc.</a> in early 1992, Diller\'s Arrow Investments purchased a $25 million stake in QVC, or just under 3 percent of the company, in December 1992 and Diller succeeded Segel as chairman and chief executive on January 18, 1993.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Diller, known for building Fox as a fourth national television network in just five years, replaced QVC\'s second channel, The Fashion Channel, with his prototype for a fifth broadcast television network, Q2. Debuting in spring 1994, Q2 was aimed at younger, slightly less educated and slightly less affluent consumers and broadcast from New York City. The spin-off network was shelved in 1996, costing QVC $55 million.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Failed_Paramount_takeover_bid">Failed Paramount takeover bid</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=QVC&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Failed Paramount takeover bid">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>QVC, under Diller, first placed a hostile $9.6 billion bid for Paramount in September 1993, when talks for a friendly merger between Paramount and <a href="/wiki/Viacom_(1952%E2%80%932006)" title="Viacom (1952\xe2\x80\x932006)">Viacom</a>, worth $7.2 billion at the time, were already under way.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> QVC\'s more attractive bid was forced on Paramount in the February 4, 1994 decision of <a href="/wiki/Paramount_Communications,_Inc._v._QVC_Network,_Inc." title="Paramount Communications, Inc. v. QVC Network, Inc.">Paramount Communications, Inc. v. QVC Network, Inc.</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Delaware_Supreme_Court" title="Delaware Supreme Court">Delaware Supreme Court</a>. Following Viacom\'s merger with <a href="/wiki/Blockbuster_LLC" title="Blockbuster LLC">Blockbuster</a> that gave Viacom the financial lead, Diller proposed that QVC financial backer <a href="/wiki/BellSouth" title="BellSouth">BellSouth</a> could buy QVC shares after the merger to boost the value of QVC\'s stock to Paramount shareholders.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Diller dropped the proposal when reminded of its legal challenges and on February 14, 1994, QVC lost its bid for Paramount to a $9.85 million bid from Viacom.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> Diller\'s reported five-word response to the end of what <i><a href="/wiki/The_Los_Angeles_Times" class="mw-redirect" title="The Los Angeles Times">The Los Angeles Times</a></i> called "the biggest takeover war of the 1990s" was: "They won. We lost. Next."<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Becoming_a_worldwide_multimedia_company">Becoming a worldwide multimedia company</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=QVC&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Becoming a worldwide multimedia company">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Diller changed the name of QVC Network to QVC, Inc. in 1994, while creating a holding company to allow the firm to diversify and build assets and divisions separately. Among the changes were the creation of two new divisions, Q Direct, to produce infomercials and 60- and 120-second direct response TV commercials, and QVC Interactive, an online-shopping service.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> QVC launched their internet shopping site, iQVC, on September 15, 1996.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>QVC\'s shopping channel based in Mexico, airing in non-primetime programming hours on Canal 4, launched November 1, 1993 in a partnership with local media company Televisa.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> The shopping channel, called CVC, which stood for \xe2\x80\x9cQuality, Value &amp; Convenience\xe2\x80\x9d in Spanish, closed August 4, 1995 after a devaluation of the peso and drop in purchasing power during Mexico\'s recession.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span id="New_frontiers_with_Douglas_Briggs_.281995.E2.80.932005.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="New_frontiers_with_Douglas_Briggs_(1995\xe2\x80\x932005)">New frontiers with Douglas Briggs (1995\xe2\x80\x932005)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=QVC&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: New frontiers with Douglas Briggs (1995\xe2\x80\x932005)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<h4><span id="Developing_QVC.27s_brand_power"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Developing_QVC\'s_brand_power">Developing QVC\'s brand power</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=QVC&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Developing QVC&#039;s brand power">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>On September 29, 1994, QVC Vice President Douglas Briggs unveiled the QVC Local, a customized, $1.7 million state-of-the-art television studio in a bus, in Washington, D.C.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> In January 1995, QVC kicked off the "Quest for America\'s Best: 50 in 50 Tour," a 50-week nationwide product search to promote local and regional products with live broadcasts from every State. The QVC Local traveled 88,796 miles of American road during the 50 in 50 Tour in 1995.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Comcast" title="Comcast">Comcast</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tele-Communications_Inc." title="Tele-Communications Inc.">TCI</a> spin-off company <a href="/wiki/Liberty_Media" title="Liberty Media">Liberty Media</a> completed their acquisition of the company on February 2, 1995 and Diller resigned. Douglas S. Briggs was announced as QVC Inc. CEO on March 6, 1995, after overseeing the daily business of the company as president of QVC electronic retailing and executive vice president of QVC Inc. since February 1994.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> Briggs was tasked with boosting Diller\'s many start-up ventures, including QVC UK and Q2.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>On September 24, 1997 at 7pm ET, QVC signed off their live broadcast from their previous studio and celebrated the opening of their new broadcast center and corporate offices, Studio Park, a nearly 17-acre property with more than 58,000 square feet of filming studios in West Chester, Pennsylvania.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>QVC tested a retail concept in 2000 at <a href="/wiki/The_Mall_of_America" class="mw-redirect" title="The Mall of America">The Mall of America</a> in <a href="/wiki/Bloomington,_Minnesota" title="Bloomington, Minnesota">Bloomington, Minnesota</a> with a limited-term lease on a 500 square-foot store.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> The next year, QVC signed a ten-year lease on a 2,500 square-foot store with broadcasting capabilities and opened QVC @ The Mall on August 8, 2001.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> The Mall of America store remained the only location for this format and the store closed at the conclusion of the ten-year lease on March 22, 2011.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span id="Legal_hurdles_and_Comcast.27s_exit"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Legal_hurdles_and_Comcast\'s_exit">Legal hurdles and Comcast\'s exit</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=QVC&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Legal hurdles and Comcast&#039;s exit">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>In 1998, two former hosts filed a class-action lawsuit against QVC, claiming that they were discriminated against by the shopping channel based on their race. The lawsuit went on to state that QVC refused to allow non-white hosts any permanent daytime/primetime spots, which relegated them to the overnight hours, otherwise known as the "<a href="/wiki/Graveyard_slot" title="Graveyard slot">graveyard shift</a>." Because of this, the non-white hosts were paid considerably less than the white hosts.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>On July 3, 2003, Comcast sold its majority share to <a href="/wiki/Liberty_Media" title="Liberty Media">Liberty Media</a>, which purchased the remaining 56.5% of QVC it didn\'t already own for $7.9 billion. Comcast, for which QVC was a financial asset, not a strategic one, continued to carry QVC for its 21 million cable subscribers.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>On Wednesday, March 24, 2004, the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Trade_Commission" title="Federal Trade Commission">FTC</a> sued QVC over violating a June 2000 order barring the company from making misleading claims about dietary supplements.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> A March 2009 settlement with the FTC charged QVC with paying $6 million for consumer redress and a $1.5 million civil penalty and for QVC to discontinue the dietary supplements products.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 2006, the U.S. District Court in Philadelphia settled a dispute between QVC and HSN over the use of the phrase "<a href="/wiki/Christmas_in_July" title="Christmas in July">Christmas in July</a>," QVC maintaining their use of it since 1987, and HSN claiming copyright on it in 2000.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span id="The_Mike_George_Years_.282006.E2.80.932021.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Mike_George_Years_(2006\xe2\x80\x932021)">The Mike George Years (2006\xe2\x80\x932021)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=QVC&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: The Mike George Years (2006\xe2\x80\x932021)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<h4><span id="New_CEO.2C_new_brand_identity"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="New_CEO,_new_brand_identity">New CEO, new brand identity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=QVC&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: New CEO, new brand identity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>CEO Douglas Briggs announced his retirement in April 2005 and on November 1, 2005, Michael A. George, who previously served as chief marketing officer and general manager of the U.S. consumer business at <a href="/wiki/Dell_Inc" class="mw-redirect" title="Dell Inc">Dell Inc</a>, was named successor. George was named QVC CEO on April 15, 2006.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>On September 23, 2007, QVC U.S. rebranded itself, changing its logo on-air and online. The rebranding was accompanied by an advertising campaign with the tagline "iQdoU?" ("I shop QVC, do you?") that had preceded the rebrand with billboards in major U.S. cities. The iQdoU? campaign also included a "teaser" website.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Advancements_in_broadcasting_technology_and_entering_new_markets">Advancements in broadcasting technology and entering new markets</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=QVC&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Advancements in broadcasting technology and entering new markets">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>QVC was the first shopping network to offer a native <a href="/wiki/High-definition_television" title="High-definition television">high-definition</a> simulcast channel in May 2009. Formerly, the <a href="/wiki/4:3" class="mw-redirect" title="4:3">4:3</a> cut for its <a href="/wiki/Standard-definition_television" title="Standard-definition television">standard definition</a> feed in a <a href="/wiki/16:9" class="mw-redirect" title="16:9">16:9</a> presentation was made to the right of the screen rather than on both sides of the 4:3 frame, allowing the network to place its graphics fully to the left and lower portions of the screen to maximize camera presentation space. Eventually, the standard definition feed was converted to a downscaled letterboxing of the 16:9 HD channel at the provider\'s headend level.\n</p><p>On September 30, 2010, at 11 p.m., QVC began broadcasting in Italy, both on <a href="/wiki/Satellite_television" title="Satellite television">satellite</a> and through <a href="/wiki/Digital_terrestrial_television" title="Digital terrestrial television">digital terrestrial television</a>. In 2012, QVC partnered with <a href="/wiki/China_National_Radio" title="China National Radio">China National Radio</a> to take over operations of its home shopping network and associated internet e-commerce site, <a href="/w/index.php?title=CNR_Mall&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="CNR Mall (page does not exist)">CNR Mall</a>. In 2014 the joint venture reached 89 million households.<sup id="cite_ref-QVC&#39;s_International_Ventures_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-QVC&#39;s_International_Ventures-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 2013, QVC partnered with <a href="/wiki/Ion_Media_Networks" class="mw-redirect" title="Ion Media Networks">Ion Media Networks</a> to bring its programming to <a href="/wiki/Terrestrial_television" title="Terrestrial television">broadcast television</a>, through <a href="/wiki/Ion_Television" class="mw-redirect" title="Ion Television">Ion Television</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-QVC_Celebrates_30_Years_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-QVC_Celebrates_30_Years-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> QVC began to be carried as the fifth <a href="/wiki/Digital_subchannel" title="Digital subchannel">digital subchannel</a> on most Ion Television <a href="/wiki/Owned-and-operated_station" title="Owned-and-operated station">owned-and-operated stations</a> beginning on August 5, 2013; due to technical limitations caused by the number of subchannels Ion requires its stations to carry, QVC is carried in a squeezed full-screen <a href="/wiki/4:3" class="mw-redirect" title="4:3">4:3</a> format and is transmitted in standard definition. It is organized with the Fashion Footwear Association of New York, which runs a coinciding Shoes on Sale initiative along with an awards gala.<sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77">&#91;77&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78">&#91;78&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79">&#91;79&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-80" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-80">&#91;80&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="QVC_UK">QVC UK</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=QVC&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: QVC UK">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/QVC_(UK)" class="mw-redirect" title="QVC (UK)">QVC (UK)</a></div>\n<p>QVC UK was launched on October 1, 1993. QVC UK\'s headquarters and broadcasting facilities are in Chiswick Park, West London. Call centre and distribution warehouse are situated in <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_Borough_of_Knowsley" title="Metropolitan Borough of Knowsley">Knowsley</a> in <a href="/wiki/Merseyside" title="Merseyside">Merseyside</a>. QVC UK also runs an outlet store in <a href="/wiki/Warrington" title="Warrington">Warrington</a>; another was in <a href="/wiki/Shrewsbury" title="Shrewsbury">Shrewsbury</a>, but this closed in June 2020. QVC UK also operates three channels made up mostly of rerun segments from the live channel, <a href="/wiki/QVC_Beauty" title="QVC Beauty">QVC Beauty</a>, QVC Extra and QVC Style. The company\'s UK sales in 2013 were worth $660 million, now reaching to 27 million households in Britain and <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-QVC&#39;s_International_Ventures_48-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-QVC&#39;s_International_Ventures-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-qvc.fr_74-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-qvc.fr-74">&#91;74&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>QVC UK\'s main channel broadcasts live 364 days a year from 09:00 to 01:00. For the 8 \'non-live\' hours a day and on one day a year, <a href="/wiki/Christmas_Day" class="mw-redirect" title="Christmas Day">Christmas Day</a>, the main channel shows rerun segments from the live channel.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="QVC_Germany">QVC Germany</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=QVC&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: QVC Germany">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/QVC_Germany" title="QVC Germany">QVC Germany</a></div>\n<p>QVC Germany, incorporated in <a href="/wiki/D%C3%BCsseldorf" title="D\xc3\xbcsseldorf">D\xc3\xbcsseldorf</a>, runs call centre operations from sites in <a href="/wiki/Bochum" title="Bochum">Bochum</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kassel" title="Kassel">Kassel</a>, whilst distribution is handled from a dedicated warehouse in <a href="/wiki/H%C3%BCckelhoven" title="H\xc3\xbcckelhoven">H\xc3\xbcckelhoven</a>. The company\'s 2013 sales were worth $970 million.<sup id="cite_ref-qvc.fr_74-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-qvc.fr-74">&#91;74&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>QVC Germany first broadcast December 1, 1996 and reaches 41 million households in Germany and Austria.<sup id="cite_ref-QVC&#39;s_International_Ventures_48-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-QVC&#39;s_International_Ventures-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> QVC Germany broadcasts live 17 hours a day, 363.5 days a year (the channel goes off-air on Christmas Eve (with no programming after noon) and Christmas Day). QVC has two additional channels in Germany, QVC 2 and QVC Style.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="QVC_Japan">QVC Japan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=QVC&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: QVC Japan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>QVC Japan, a joint venture with <a href="/wiki/Mitsui_%26_Co." title="Mitsui &amp; Co.">Mitsui &amp; Co.</a>, is based in <a href="/wiki/Chiba,_Chiba" class="mw-redirect" title="Chiba, Chiba">Makuhari</a>, where its broadcast studio, corporate headquarters, and call center facility are located. Distribution facilities are in <a href="/wiki/Sakura,_Chiba" title="Sakura, Chiba">Sakura City</a>. The company\'s 2013 sales were worth $1.02 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-qvc.fr_74-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-qvc.fr-74">&#91;74&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>QVC Japan first broadcast on April 1, 2001 and reaches 27 million households. The channel once broadcast live programming 24 hours a day and now airs 19 live programming hours daily.<sup id="cite_ref-QVC&#39;s_International_Ventures_48-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-QVC&#39;s_International_Ventures-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="QVC_Italy">QVC Italy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=QVC&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: QVC Italy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>QVC Italy first broadcast on October 1, 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-81" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-81">&#91;81&#93;</a></sup> QVC Italy\'s headquarters and broadcasting facilities are located at <a href="/wiki/Brugherio" title="Brugherio">Brugherio</a>, near <a href="/wiki/Milan" title="Milan">Milan</a> and the distribution center is located in <a href="/wiki/Castel_San_Giovanni" title="Castel San Giovanni">Castel San Giovanni</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-QVC&#39;s_International_Ventures_48-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-QVC&#39;s_International_Ventures-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> The company\'s 2013 sales were worth $130 million.<sup id="cite_ref-qvc.fr_74-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-qvc.fr-74">&#91;74&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>QVC Italy broadcasts live 17 hours a day (although the channel runs 24 hours a day), 364 days a year to 25 million households. The primary distribution platforms for QVC Italy are digital terrestrial television and satellite.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="QVC_France">QVC France</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=QVC&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: QVC France">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>On August 1, 2015, QVC reached its seventh international market with France.<sup id="cite_ref-QVC&#39;s_International_Ventures_48-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-QVC&#39;s_International_Ventures-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-82" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-82">&#91;82&#93;</a></sup> Before the launch, the company said it expected to create about 200 jobs in its first two years in the country. QVC France broadcast from their studio and administration facility in <a href="/wiki/Seine-Saint-Denis" title="Seine-Saint-Denis">Seine-Saint-Denis</a> live on weekdays from 15:00 to 23:00 and weekends from 11:00 to 23:00, online, on mobile devices and on major satellite TV, cable TV and <a href="/wiki/IPTV" class="mw-redirect" title="IPTV">internet TV</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-83" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-83">&#91;83&#93;</a></sup> The channel\'s corporate website said QVC stood for: <i>Qualit\xc3\xa9, Valeur, <b>Confiance</b>,</i> replacing <i>convenience</i> with (the French for) <i>trust</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-qvc.fr_74-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-qvc.fr-74">&#91;74&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Qurate Retail Group ceased operations of QVC France on March 13, 2019, stating that "QVC France had underperformed against financial and operational expectations, in large part due to unique in-market structural challenges and market dynamics that evolved in the years following the launch of the operation."<sup id="cite_ref-84" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-84">&#91;84&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span id="QVC.2FCNR_.28China.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="QVC/CNR_(China)">QVC/CNR (China)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=QVC&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: QVC/CNR (China)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>QVC/CNR (China) is based in Beijing and operates both a television broadcast and associated e-commerce website cnrmall.com. The China operation is a 51/49 <a href="/wiki/Joint_venture" title="Joint venture">joint venture</a> between state-owned <a href="/wiki/China_National_Radio" title="China National Radio">China National Radio</a> and QVC, based on the pre-existing CNR channel reaching 35 million households, with plans to grow to 195 million households that have <a href="/wiki/Digital_cable" title="Digital cable">digital cable</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-85" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-85">&#91;85&#93;</a></sup> Chinese law prohibits private control of television stations, so this is the maximum position QVC can hold in its Chinese operations. QVC/CNR broadcasts live 17 hours a day.<sup id="cite_ref-QVC&#39;s_International_Ventures_48-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-QVC&#39;s_International_Ventures-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> The company\'s 2013 sales were worth $110 million.<sup id="cite_ref-qvc.fr_74-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-qvc.fr-74">&#91;74&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span id="Today.27s_Special_Value"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Today\'s_Special_Value">Today\'s Special Value</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=QVC&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Today&#039;s Special Value">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Today%27s_Special_Value_logo.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/b/be/Today%27s_Special_Value_logo.jpg/150px-Today%27s_Special_Value_logo.jpg" decoding="async" width="150" height="108" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/b/be/Today%27s_Special_Value_logo.jpg/225px-Today%27s_Special_Value_logo.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/b/be/Today%27s_Special_Value_logo.jpg/300px-Today%27s_Special_Value_logo.jpg 2x" data-file-width="360" data-file-height="260" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Today%27s_Special_Value_logo.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Today\'s Special Value logo, in use from 2000 through January 16, 2006.</div></div></div>\n<p>Since August 11, 1987, QVC has branded their daily featured product as <i>Today\'s Special Value</i>.
