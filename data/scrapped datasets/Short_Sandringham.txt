links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Design_and_development">Design and development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Short_Sandringham&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Design and development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>From late 1942, six RAF Sunderland IIIs were stripped of their armament and fitted with bench-type seats, gaining civil markings and registrations and joining the airline <a href="/wiki/BOAC" class="mw-redirect" title="BOAC">BOAC</a>. Six more Sunderlands were converted for transport use in 1943, with a further 12 following in 1944.<sup id="cite_ref-barnesp398-9_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-barnesp398-9-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-londonp203-4_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-londonp203-4-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Following the end of the Second World War, BOAC converted its Sunderlands to a less-austere standard, more suitable for peace-time operations, and known as the "<a href="/wiki/Short_Hythe" class="mw-redirect" title="Short Hythe">Hythe</a>". The primitive bench seats were removed, with seats fitted for sixteen passengers on one deck in the initial H.1 configuration, with the addition of a promenade deck giving the H.2 configuration, while the H.3 layout had an additional eight seats. 6,500 pounds (2,900&#160;kg) of mail could be carried. Engines were standardised as the <a href="/wiki/Bristol_Pegasus" title="Bristol Pegasus">Bristol Pegasus</a> 38.<sup id="cite_ref-Jackson_88v3_p152_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jackson_88v3_p152-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-londonp215_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-londonp215-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In November 1945, Shorts flew a further refined conversion of one of BOAC\'s Sunderlands from their <a href="/wiki/Rochester,_Kent" title="Rochester, Kent">Rochester</a> works, with revised low-drag fairings on the aircraft\'s nose and tail, and a refurbished interior, which was known as the Sandringham. All the Sandringhams were civil conversions of former Royal Air Force <a href="/wiki/Coastal_Command" class="mw-redirect" title="Coastal Command">Coastal Command</a> Short Sunderlands. The first prototype retained the Pegasus engines of the Sunderland III and Hythe, and was designated the Sandringham 1 by Shorts, but later Sandringhams, converted by Short and Harland Ltd at <a href="/wiki/Belfast" title="Belfast">Belfast</a> Harbour, were based on the Sunderland V, and were powered by <a href="/wiki/Pratt_%26_Whitney_R-1830" class="mw-redirect" title="Pratt &amp; Whitney R-1830">Pratt &amp; Whitney "Twin Wasp"</a> engines.<sup id="cite_ref-Jackson_88v3_p152-3_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jackson_88v3_p152-3-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1963 an additional conversion of a former <a href="/wiki/Royal_New_Zealand_Air_Force" title="Royal New Zealand Air Force">Royal New Zealand Air Force</a> Sunderland V was carried out by <a href="/wiki/Ansett_Australia" title="Ansett Australia">Ansett</a> to a similar standard to the Sandringhams. The aircraft, named <i>Islander</i>, was fitted with a 43-seat interior.<sup id="cite_ref-londonp226_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-londonp226-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="702" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Short_S.25_Sandr_VH-BRC_Ansett_Rose_Bay_03.10.70_edited-3.jpg" class="internal" commenced operations with BOAC on the  <a href="/wiki/Poole_Harbour" title="Poole Harbour">Poole Harbour</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dorset" title="Dorset">Dorset</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lagos" title="Lagos">Lagos</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nigeria" title="Nigeria">Nigeria</a> in March 1943.<sup id="cite_ref-Jackson_88v3_p152_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jackson_88v3_p152-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-barnesp398_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-barnesp398-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Following a proving flight to <a href="/wiki/British_India" class="mw-redirect" title="British India">British India</a>, the Sunderlands were transferred in October 1943 to flights between Poole and <a href="/wiki/Karachi" title="Karachi">Karachi</a>, via <a href="/wiki/Gibraltar" title="Gibraltar">Gibraltar</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cairo" title="Cairo">Cairo</a>. As Egypt was under military control, the aircraft were given military serial numbers and operated as part of RAF Transport Command.<sup id="cite_ref-barnesp398-9_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-barnesp398-9-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-londonp204_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-londonp204-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The service was extended to <a href="/wiki/Calcutta" class="mw-redirect" title="Calcutta">Calcutta</a> in May 1944, while <a href="/wiki/VE-Day" class="mw-redirect" title="VE-Day">VE-Day</a>, the end of the war in Europe, allowed the aircraft reverted to BOAC control. They continued on the India route, which was extended again to <a href="/wiki/Rangoon" class="mw-redirect" title="Rangoon">Rangoon</a> in <a href="/wiki/Burma" class="mw-redirect" title="Burma">Burma</a> following <a href="/wiki/VJ-Day" class="mw-redirect" title="VJ-Day">VJ-Day</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-barnesp399_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-barnesp399-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>BOAC\'s Hythes took on flights to Australia in 1946, starting flying the Poole\xe2\x80\x93<a href="/wiki/Sydney" title="Sydney">Sydney</a> route in conjunction with <a href="/wiki/Qantas" title="Qantas">Qantas</a> on 12 May 1946. In August that year, BOAC\'s Hythes were also deployed on services to <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong" title="Hong Kong">Hong Kong</a> on what was known as the Dragon route.<sup id="cite_ref-londonp215_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-londonp215-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-barnesp400_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-barnesp400-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Sandringham 5 was operated by BOAC from 1947 as the "Plymouth class" on the Far East routes from Southampton via Alexandria to Hong Kong and Tokyo.  These were replaced by <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_Constellation" title="Lockheed Constellation">Lockheed Constellation</a> land planes during 1949. <a href="/wiki/TEAL" title="TEAL">TEAL</a> used Sandringhams on the <a href="/wiki/Auckland" title="Auckland">Auckland</a> to <a href="/wiki/Sydney" title="Sydney">Sydney</a> route and flights to Pacific Islands. In 1950, <a href="/wiki/Qantas" title="Qantas">Qantas</a> introduced the first of five aircraft which flew from the <a href="/wiki/Rose_Bay,_New_South_Wales" title="Rose Bay, New South Wales">Rose Bay</a> flying boat base on <a href="/wiki/Sydney_Harbour" class="mw-redirect" title="Sydney Harbour">Sydney Harbour</a> to destinations in <a href="/wiki/New_Caledonia" title="New Caledonia">New Caledonia</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Hebrides" title="New Hebrides">New Hebrides</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fiji" title="Fiji">Fiji</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Guinea" title="New Guinea">New Guinea</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lord_Howe_Island" title="Lord Howe Island">Lord Howe Island</a>. Two of these were purchased from <a href="/wiki/TEAL" title="TEAL">TEAL</a> and the other three were purchased from <a href="/wiki/BOAC" class="mw-redirect" title="BOAC">BOAC</a>. These were in service through to 1955.<sup id="cite_ref-barnesp405_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-barnesp405-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The type was used by <a href="/wiki/Ansett_Australia" title="Ansett Australia">Ansett</a> Flying Boat Services on the Sydney (Rose Bay) to <a href="/wiki/Lord_Howe_Island" title="Lord Howe Island">Lord Howe Island</a> scheduled service until 1974. One of Ansett\'s Sandringhams was converted from a S-25 Sunderland previously owned by the <a href="/wiki/Royal_New_Zealand_Air_Force" title="Royal New Zealand Air Force">Royal New Zealand Air Force</a>. It was also used in Norway by <a href="/wiki/Scandinavian_Airlines" title="Scandinavian Airlines">DNL \xe2\x80\x93 Norwegian Airlines</a> 1946\xe2\x80\x931952 on the domestic service from <a href="/wiki/Oslo" title="Oslo">Oslo</a> to <a href="/wiki/Troms%C3%B8" title="Troms\xc3\xb8">Troms\xc3\xb8</a>, and in Uruguay by <a href="/wiki/Compa%C3%B1%C3%ADa_Aeron%C3%A1utica_Uruguaya" title="Compa\xc3\xb1\xc3\xada Aeron\xc3\xa1utica Uruguaya">Compa\xc3\xb1\xc3\xada Aeron\xc3\xa1utica Uruguaya</a> S.A. (CAUSA) on the passenger services between <a href="/wiki/Montevideo" title="Montevideo">Montevideo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Buenos_Aires" title="Buenos Aires">Buenos Aires</a> (1950\xe2\x80\x931962).\n</p><p>In October 1954, <a href="/wiki/Gordon_Taylor_(aviator)" title="Gordon Taylor (aviator)">Captain Sir Gordon Taylor</a> flew his newly acquired Sandringham 7 from the UK to Australia to begin a series of flying boat cruises of the south Pacific. The aircraft later passed to R\xc3\xa9seau A\xc3\xa9rien Interinsulaire in Tahiti and is now stored at the <a href="/wiki/Mus%C3%A9e_de_l%27Air_et_de_l%27Espace" class="mw-redirect" title="Mus\xc3\xa9e de l&#39;Air et de l&#39;Espace">Mus\xc3\xa9e de l\'Air et de l\'Espace</a> at <a href="/wiki/Le_Bourget_aerodrome" class="mw-redirect" title="Le Bourget aerodrome">Paris Le Bourget</a>.\n</p><p>One of the last operators of the Sandringham was Antilles Air Boats in the <a href="/wiki/Virgin_Islands" title="Virgin Islands">Virgin Islands</a> of the <a In May 1950, ZK-AMH, was sold to <a href="/w/index.php?title=Barrier_Reef_Airlines&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Barrier Reef Airlines (page does not exist)">Barrier Reef Airlines</a> of Australia where it was renamed <i>Beachcomber</i> and registered VH-BRC. Barrier Reef Airlines were subsequently taken over by the major Australian airline <a href="/wiki/Ansett_Australia" title="Ansett Australia">Ansett</a> and became Ansett Flying Boat Services and operated out of <a href="/wiki/Rose_Bay,_New_South_Wales" title="Rose Bay, New South Wales">Rose Bay</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sydney" title="Sydney">Sydney</a>, <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> until 1974. The vast amounts of money required to keep the aircraft in flying condition were not available and in 1981, the aircraft was purchased for the <a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Aeronautical_Collection&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="National Aeronautical Collection (page does not exist)">National Aeronautical Collection</a> by the Science Museum and is now on display at <a href="/wiki/Solent_Sky" title="Solent Sky">Solent Sky</a> museum in <a href="/wiki/Southampton" title="Southampton">Southampton</a>, Hampshire, UK.</li></ul>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:R%C3%A9serves_mus%C3%A9e_de_l%27Air_Dugny_-_2.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/R%C3%A9serves_mus%C3%A9e_de_l%27Air_Dugny_-_2.jpg/220px-R%C3%A9serves_mus%C3%A9e_de_l%27Air_Dugny_-_2.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="148" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/R%C3%A9serves_mus%C3%A9e_de_l%27Air_Dugny_-_2.jpg/330px-R%C3%A9serves_mus%C3%A9e_de_l%27Air_Dugny_-_2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/R%C3%A9serves_mus%C3%A9e_de_l%27Air_Dugny_-_2.jpg/440px-R%C3%A9serves_mus%C3%A9e_de_l%27Air_Dugny_-_2.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3896" data-file-height="2616" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:R%C3%A9serves_mus%C3%A9e_de_l%27Air_Dugny_-_2.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Short S.25 Sandringham Mk7 Bermuda <i>F-OBIP</i> at the Mus\xc3\xa9e de l\'Air et de l\'Espace (2015)</div></div></div>\n<ul><li>Short S-25 Sandringham 7 Bermuda Class. c/n SH-57C.
