737 Next Generation</a>\n</td></tr>\n</tbody></table>\n<p>The <b>Boeing 737 Classic</b> are <a href="/wiki/Narrow-body_aircraft" title="Narrow-body aircraft">narrow-body airliners</a> produced by <a href="/wiki/Boeing_Commercial_Airplanes" title="Boeing Commercial Airplanes">Boeing Commercial Airplanes</a>, the second generation of the original <a href="/wiki/Boeing_737" title="Boeing 737">Boeing 737</a>-100/-200.\nDevelopment began in 1979 and the first variant, the 737-300, first flew in February 1984 and entered service in December of that year.\nThe stretched 737-400 first flew in February 1988 and entered service later that year. The shortest variant, the 737-500, first flew in June 1989 and entered service in 1990.\n</p><p>It is <a href="/wiki/Re-engine" class="mw-redirect" title="Re-engine">re-engined</a> with higher bypass ratio <a href="/wiki/CFM56" class="mw-redirect" title="CFM56">CFM56</a> turbofans for a better <a href="/wiki/Fuel_economy_in_aircraft" title="Fuel economy in aircraft">fuel economy</a> and has upgraded avionics.\nWith a 133,500\xe2\x80\x93150,000&#160;lb (60.6\xe2\x80\x9368.0&#160;t) <a href="/wiki/Maximum_Takeoff_Weight" class="mw-redirect" title="Maximum Takeoff Weight">MTOW</a>, it has a range of 2,060 to 2,375&#160;nmi (3,815 to 4,398&#160;km).\nAt 102&#160;ft (31 m), the -500 is similar in length to the original 737-200 and can fly 110 to 132 passengers.\nThe 110&#160;ft (33.4 m) long -300 can seat 126 to 149 passengers while the 120&#160;ft (36.4 m) long -400 accommodates 147 to 168 seats.\n</p><p>It competed with the McDonnell Douglas <a href="/wiki/McDonnell_Douglas_MD-80" title="McDonnell Douglas MD-80">MD-80</a> series, then with the <a href="/wiki/Airbus_A320" class="mw-redirect" title="Airbus A320">Airbus A320</a> family which prompted Boeing to update its offer with the <a href="/wiki/737_Next_Generation" class="mw-redirect" title="737 Next Generation">737 Next Generation</a>, thus designating the -300/400/500 variants as the 737 Classic.\nIn total, 1,988 aircraft were delivered from 1984 until production end in the year 2000: 1,113 -300s, 486 -400s and 389 -500s.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc" role="navigation" initial 737-300 (foreground) is 9&#160;ft 5&#160;in (2.87&#160;m) longer than the 737-200 (background) and is <a href="/wiki/Re-engine" class="mw-redirect" title="Re-engine">re-engined</a> with wider and more efficient <a href="/wiki/CFM_International_CFM56" title="CFM International CFM56">CFM56</a> turbofans</div></div></div>\n<p>Following the success of the <a href="/wiki/737-200" class="mw-redirect" title="737-200">Boeing 737-200 Advanced</a>, Boeing wanted to increase capacity and <a href="/wiki/Range_(aircraft)" class="mw-redirect" title="Range (aircraft)">range</a>, incorporating improvements to upgrade the plane to modern specifications, while also retaining commonality with previous 737 variants. Development began in 1979, and in 1980, preliminary aircraft specifications were released at the <a href="/wiki/Farnborough_Airshow" title="Farnborough Airshow">Farnborough Airshow</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Endres_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Endres-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The new series featured <a href="/wiki/CFM_International_CFM56" title="CFM International CFM56">CFM56</a> <a href="/wiki/Turbofan" title="Turbofan">turbofan</a> engines, yielding significant gains in <a href="/wiki/Fuel_efficiency" title="Fuel efficiency">fuel economy</a> and a reduction in noise, but also posing an engineering challenge given the low ground clearance of the 737 - a trait of its <a href="/wiki/Boeing_707" title="Boeing 707">707</a>-derived fuselage. Boeing and engine supplier CFM International solved the problem by placing the engine ahead of (rather than below) the wing, and by moving engine accessories to the sides (rather than the bottom) of the engine pod, giving the 737 a distinctive noncircular air intake.<sup id="cite_ref-Endres_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Endres-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The wing incorporated a number of changes for improved <a href="/wiki/Aerodynamics" title="Aerodynamics">aerodynamics</a>. The wing tip was extended 9 inches (23&#160;cm). The leading-edge <a href="/wiki/Leading_edge_slats" class="mw-redirect" title="Leading edge slats">slats</a> and trailing-edge <a href="/wiki/Flap_(aircraft)" class="mw-redirect" title="Flap (aircraft)">flaps</a> were adjusted.<sup id="cite_ref-Endres_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Endres-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The flight deck was improved with the optional electronic flight instrumentation system, and the passenger cabin incorporated improvements similar to those on the <a href="/wiki/Boeing_757" title="Boeing 757">Boeing 757</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Model_developments">Model developments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boeing_737_Classic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Model developments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In March 1981, <a href="/wiki/US_Airways" title="US Airways">USAir</a> and <a href="/wiki/Southwest_Airlines" title="Southwest Airlines">Southwest Airlines</a> each ordered 10 aircraft of the 737-300 series, with an option for 20 more. That aircraft, the initial model of the 737 Classic series, first flew in February 1984 and entered service in December of that year with Southwest Airlines. A further stretched model, the 737-400, was launched with an order for 25 aircraft with 30 options from <a href="/wiki/Piedmont_Airlines_(1948%E2%80%931989)" title="Piedmont Airlines (1948\xe2\x80\x931989)">Piedmont Airlines</a> in June 1986. That aircraft first flew in February 1988 and entered service later that year with Piedmont Airlines. The final model of the series, the 737-500, was launched with an order for 30 aircraft from Southwest Airlines in May 1987. That aircraft, which was designed as a replacement for the 737-200 and had similar passenger capacity and dimensions, as well as the longest range of any member of the 737 Classic family, first flew in June 1989 and entered service with Southwest Airlines in 1990.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Engines">Engines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boeing_737_Classic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Engines">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Boeing_737-400_Engine.JPG" class="image"><img alt="A zoomed-in view of the front of an engine nacelle: The fan blades of the engine are in the middle of the image. They are surrounded by the engine nacelle, which is seemingly circular on the top half, and flattened on the bottom half." src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Boeing_737-400_Engine.JPG/220px-Boeing_737-400_Engine.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Boeing_737-400_Engine.JPG/330px-Boeing_737-400_Engine.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Boeing_737-400_Engine.JPG/440px-Boeing_737-400_Engine.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2288" data-file-height="1712" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Boeing_737-400_Engine.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Engine inlet of a CFM56-3 engine on a Boeing&#160;737-400 series showing the noncircular design</div></div></div>\n<p>Boeing selected the <a href="/wiki/CFM_International_CFM56#Boeing_737" title="CFM International CFM56">CFM56-3</a> exclusively to power the 737-300 variant. The 737 wings were closer to the ground than previous applications for the CFM56, necessitating several modifications to the engine. The fan diameter was reduced, which reduced the bypass ratio, and the engine accessory gearbox was moved from the bottom of the engine (the 6&#160;o\'clock position) to the 9&#160;o\'clock position, giving the engine nacelle its distinctive flat-bottomed shape, which is often nicknamed the "hamster <a href="/wiki/Cheek_pouch" title="Cheek pouch">pouch</a>". The overall thrust was also reduced, from 24,000 to 20,000&#160;lbf (107 to 89&#160;kN), mostly due to the reduction in <a href="/wiki/Bypass_ratio" title="Bypass ratio">bypass ratio</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-CFM56-3_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CFM56-3-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Speed_Trim_System">Speed Trim System</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boeing_737_Classic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Speed Trim System">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The 737 Classic saw introduction of Speed Trim System (STS), a flight augmentation system that adjusts the stabilizer automatically at low speed, low weight, aft center of gravity and high thrust with autopilot disengaged. Most frequently it can be observed during takeoffs and go-arounds. The system relies on most of the same hardware and software used in autopilot mode.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> STS is not fail-safe in that it uses only one of each sensor types required for its functionality and a single computer. Such a single-channel design is not common for augmentation systems that have full control of the stabilizer. This design was considered acceptable because of the ability for the aft and forward column cutout switches as well as center console cutout switches to constrain its malfunction.<sup id="cite_ref-path-to-disaster_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-path-to-disaster-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The limited flight envelope protections on the 737 Next Generation series, as well as MCAS on the 737 MAX, are later extensions of this system.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Further_developments">Further developments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boeing_737_Classic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Further developments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Boeing_737_Next_Generation" title="Boeing 737 Next Generation">Boeing 737 Next Generation</a></div>\n<p>Throughout the 1980s, the 737 Classic series attracted large orders from airlines in the United States and Europe, with its order totals exceeding those of preceding 737 models. By far, the most successful model was the 737-300, with deliveries totaling 1,113 aircraft (the 737-400 and -500 reached 486 and 389 deliveries, respectively). Major operators included US carriers, small national airlines, and charter carriers. \nBy the 1990s, when regular Boeing customer <a href="/wiki/United_Airlines" title="United Airlines">United Airlines</a> bought the <a href="/wiki/Airbus_A320" class="mw-redirect" title="Airbus A320">Airbus A320</a>, this prompted Boeing to update the slower, shorter-range 737 Classic -400 into the rewinged, updated, more efficient, longer <a href="/wiki/737NG" class="mw-redirect" title="737NG">737NG</a>-800.<sup id="cite_ref-Leeham28nov2017_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Leeham28nov2017-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\nProduction of the 737 Classic continued alongside that of the Next Generation for a period of time; the first 737-700 was completed in December 1996; the last 737 Classic was completed in February 2000.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modifications">Modifications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boeing_737_Classic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Modifications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Six former <a href="/wiki/Southwest_Airlines" title="Southwest Airlines">Southwest</a> 737-300s are modified and operated for <a href="/wiki/Aerial_firefighting" title="Aerial firefighting">aerial firefighting</a> by <a href="/wiki/British_Columbia" title="British Columbia">British Columbia</a>-based Coulson Group, supported by a C$3.4 million ($2.6 million) loan from the Canadian government.\nThe converted 737 FireLiner can carry 4,000&#160;US&#160;gal (15&#160;m<sup>3</sup>) with a <a href="/wiki/Volumetric_flow_rate" title="Volumetric flow rate">flow rate</a> of 3,000&#160;US&#160;gal (11&#160;m<sup>3</sup>)/s, and retains 66 seats.\nThe first was completed in 2018 and deployed to Australia.<sup id="cite_ref-AvWeek19oct2019_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AvWeek19oct2019-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Variants">Variants</span><span Classics, were delivered to <a href="/wiki/CSA_Czech_Airlines" class="mw-redirect" title="CSA Czech Airlines">CSA Czech Airlines</a> on February 28, 2000.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\nThe 737-400 was replaced by the <a href="/wiki/B737-800" class="mw-redirect" title="B737-800">737-800</a> in the Boeing 737 Next Generation The -500 was offered, due to customer demand, as a modern and direct replacement of the 737-200, incorporating the improvements of the 737 Classic series in a model that allowed longer routes with fewer passengers to be more economical than with the 737-300. Though smaller than the -300 and -400, the fuselage length of the -500 is 1&#160;ft 7&#160;in (48&#160;cm) longer than the 737-200, accommodating up to 140 passengers.<sup id="cite_ref-A16WE_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-A16WE-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Both glass and older style mechanical cockpits arrangements were available. However, unlike the 737-500, the 737-600 was a slow seller for Boeing, with only 69 aircraft delivered since its introduction.\n</p><p>As the retirement of all 737 Classic models progressed, with retirement of 300s and -500s reaching 40% in 2012, the 737-500 has faced accelerated retirement because of its smaller size.
