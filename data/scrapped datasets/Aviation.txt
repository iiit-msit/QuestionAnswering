href="/wiki/Aircraft" title="Aircraft">aircraft</a> industry. <i>Aircraft</i> includes <a href="/wiki/Airplane" title="Airplane">fixed-wing</a> and <a href="/wiki/Helicopter" title="Helicopter">rotary-wing</a> types, morphable wings, wing-less lifting bodies, as well as <a href="/wiki/Aerostat" title="Aerostat">lighter-than-air craft</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Balloon_(aeronautics)" title="Balloon (aeronautics)">hot air balloons</a> and <a href="/wiki/Airship" title="Airship">airships</a>.\n</p><p>Aviation began in the 18th century with the development of the <a href="/wiki/Hot_air_balloon" class="mw-redirect" title="Hot air balloon">hot air balloon</a>, an apparatus capable of atmospheric displacement through <a href="/wiki/Buoyancy" title="Buoyancy">buoyancy</a>. Some of the most significant advancements in aviation technology came with the controlled gliding flying of <a href="/wiki/Otto_Lilienthal" title="Otto Lilienthal">Otto Lilienthal</a> in 1896; then a large step in significance came with the construction of the first powered airplane by the <a href="/wiki/Wright_brothers" title="Wright brothers">Wright brothers</a> in the early 1900s. Since that time, aviation has been technologically revolutionized by the introduction of the <a href="/wiki/Jet_aircraft" title="Jet aircraft">jet</a> which permitted a major form of transport class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aviation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The word <i>aviation</i> was coined by the French writer and former naval officer Gabriel La Landelle in 1863.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> He derived the term from the verb <i>avier</i> (an unsuccessful <a href="/wiki/Neologism" title="Neologism">neologism</a> for "to fly"), itself derived from the Latin word <i>avis</i> ("bird") and the suffix <i>-ation</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECassard200877_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECassard200877-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aviation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_aviation" title="History of aviation">History of aviation</a></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_beginnings">Early beginnings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aviation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Early beginnings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>There are early legends of human flight such as the stories of <a href="/wiki/Icarus" title="Icarus">Icarus</a> in Greek myth, <a href="/wiki/Jamshid" title="Jamshid">Jamshid</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shah" title="Shah">Shah</a> <a href="/wiki/Kay_K%C4%81vus" title="Kay K\xc4\x81vus">Kay K\xc4\x81vus</a> in Persian myth,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> and the flying automaton of <a href="/wiki/Archytas" title="Archytas">Archytas</a> of <a href="/wiki/Taranto" title="Taranto">Tarentum</a> (428\xe2\x80\x93347 BC).<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBerliner199628_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBerliner199628-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Later, somewhat more credible claims of short-distance human flights appear, such as the winged flights of <a href="/wiki/Abbas_ibn_Firnas" title="Abbas ibn Firnas">Abbas ibn Firnas</a> (810\xe2\x80\x93887), <a href="/wiki/Eilmer_of_Malmesbury" title="Eilmer of Malmesbury">Eilmer of Malmesbury</a> (11th century), and the hot-air Passarola of <a href="/wiki/Bartholomeu_Louren%C3%A7o_de_Gusm%C3%A3o" class="mw-redirect" title="Bartholomeu Louren\xc3\xa7o de Gusm\xc3\xa3o">Bartholomeu Louren\xc3\xa7o de Gusm\xc3\xa3o</a> (1685\xe2\x80\x931724).\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lighter_than_air">Lighter than air</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aviation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Lighter than air">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Hindenburg_at_lakehurst.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/Hindenburg_at_lakehurst.jpg/220px-Hindenburg_at_lakehurst.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="172" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/Hindenburg_at_lakehurst.jpg/330px-Hindenburg_at_lakehurst.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/Hindenburg_at_lakehurst.jpg/440px-Hindenburg_at_lakehurst.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2931" data-file-height="2298" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Hindenburg_at_lakehurst.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/LZ_129_Hindenburg" title="LZ 129 Hindenburg">LZ 129 Hindenburg</a> at <a href="/wiki/Lakehurst_Naval_Air_Station" class="mw-redirect" title="Lakehurst Naval Air Station">Lakehurst Naval Air Station</a>, 1936</div></div></div>\n<p>The modern age of aviation began with the first untethered human lighter-than-air flight on November 21, 1783, of a <a href="/wiki/Hot_air_balloon" class="mw-redirect" title="Hot air balloon">hot air balloon</a> designed by the <a href="/wiki/Montgolfier_brothers" title="Montgolfier brothers">Montgolfier brothers</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Ballon_Flight_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ballon_Flight-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The practicality of balloons was limited because they could only travel downwind. It was immediately recognized that a steerable, or <a href="/wiki/Airship" title="Airship">dirigible</a>, balloon was required. <a href="/wiki/Jean-Pierre_Blanchard" title="Jean-Pierre Blanchard">Jean-Pierre Blanchard</a> flew the first human-powered dirigible in 1784 and crossed the English Channel in one in 1785.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Rigid_airship" title="Rigid airship">Rigid airships</a> became the first aircraft to transport passengers and cargo over great distances. The best known aircraft of this type were manufactured by the German <a href="/wiki/Zeppelin" title="Zeppelin">Zeppelin</a> company.\n</p><p>The most successful Zeppelin was the <a href="/wiki/LZ_127_Graf_Zeppelin" title="LZ 127 Graf Zeppelin">Graf Zeppelin</a>. It flew over one million miles, including an around-the-world flight in August 1929. However, the dominance of the Zeppelins over the airplanes of that period, which had a range of only a few hundred miles, was diminishing as airplane design advanced. The "Golden Age" of the airships ended on May 6, 1937, when the <a href="/wiki/LZ_129_Hindenburg" title="LZ 129 Hindenburg">Hindenburg</a> caught fire, killing 36 people. The cause of the Hindenburg accident was initially blamed on the use of hydrogen instead of helium as the lift gas. An internal investigation by the manufacturer revealed that the coating used in the material covering the frame was highly flammable and allowed static electricity to build up in the airship.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDe_Angelis199787\xe2\x80\x93101_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDe_Angelis199787\xe2\x80\x93101-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Changes to the coating formulation reduced the risk of further Hindenburg type accidents. Although there have been periodic initiatives to revive their use, airships have seen only niche application since that time.<sup id="cite_ref-Civil_Transport_Aircraft_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Civil_Transport_Aircraft-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Heavier_than_air">Heavier than air</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aviation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Heavier than air">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In 1799, Sir <a href="/wiki/George_Cayley" title="George Cayley">George Cayley</a> set forth the concept of the modern airplane as a fixed-wing flying machine with separate systems for lift, propulsion, and control.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Early dirigible developments included machine-powered propulsion (<a href="/wiki/Henri_Giffard" title="Henri Giffard">Henri Giffard</a>, 1852), rigid frames (<a href="/wiki/David_Schwarz_(aviation_inventor)" title="David Schwarz (aviation inventor)">David Schwarz</a>, 1896) and improved speed and maneuverability (<a href="/wiki/Alberto_Santos-Dumont" title="Alberto Santos-Dumont">Alberto Santos-Dumont</a>, 1901)\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:First_flight2.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/First_flight2.jpg/220px-First_flight2.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="143" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/First_flight2.jpg/330px-First_flight2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/First_flight2.jpg/440px-First_flight2.jpg 2x" data-file-width="6603" data-file-height="4280" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:First_flight2.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>First powered and controlled flight by the <a href="/wiki/Wright_brothers" title="Wright brothers">Wright brothers</a>, December 17, 1903</div></div></div>\n<p>There are <a href="/wiki/Early_flying_machines" title="Early flying machines">many competing claims</a> for the earliest powered, heavier-than-air flight. The first recorded powered flight was carried out by <a href="/wiki/Cl%C3%A9ment_Ader" title="Cl\xc3\xa9ment Ader">Cl\xc3\xa9ment Ader</a> on October 9, 1890, in his bat-winged, fully self-propelled <a href="/wiki/Fixed-wing_aircraft" title="Fixed-wing aircraft">fixed-wing aircraft</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Ader_%C3%89ole" title="Ader \xc3\x89ole">Ader \xc3\x89ole</a>. It was reportedly the first manned, powered, heavier-than-air flight of a significant distance (50&#160;m (160&#160;ft)) but insignificant altitude from level ground.<sup id="cite_ref-b_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-b-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Seven years later, on 14 October 1897, Ader\'s <a href="/wiki/Ader_Avion_III" title="Ader Avion III">Avion III</a> was tested without success in front of two officials from the French War ministry. The report on the trials was not publicized until 1910, as they had been a military secret. In November 1906, Ader claimed to have made a successful flight on 14 October 1897, achieving an "uninterrupted flight" of around 300 metres (980 feet). Although widely believed at the time, these claims were later discredited.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Wright_brothers" title="Wright brothers">Wright brothers</a> made the first successful powered, controlled and sustained airplane flight on December 17, 1903, a feat made possible by their invention of <a href="/wiki/Flight_dynamics_(fixed-wing_aircraft)" title="Flight dynamics (fixed-wing aircraft)">three-axis</a> control. Only a decade later, at the start of <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>, heavier-than-air powered aircraft had become practical for reconnaissance, artillery spotting, and even attacks against ground positions.\n</p><p>Aircraft began to transport people and cargo as designs grew larger and more reliable. The Wright brothers took aloft the first passenger, Charles Furnas, one of their mechanics, on May 14, 1908.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>During the 1920s and 1930s great progress was made in the field of aviation, including the first <a href="/wiki/Transatlantic_flight_of_Alcock_and_Brown" title="Transatlantic flight of Alcock and Brown">transatlantic flight of Alcock and Brown</a> in 1919, <a href="/wiki/Charles_Lindbergh" title="Charles Lindbergh">Charles Lindbergh</a>\'s solo transatlantic flight in 1927, and <a href="/wiki/Charles_Kingsford_Smith" title="Charles Kingsford Smith">Charles Kingsford Smith</a>\'s transpacific flight the following year. One of the most successful designs of this period was the <a href="/wiki/Douglas_DC-3" title="Douglas DC-3">Douglas DC-3</a>, which became the first <a href="/wiki/Airliner" title="Airliner">airliner</a> to be profitable carrying passengers exclusively, starting the modern era of passenger airline service. By the beginning of World War II, many towns and cities had built airports, and there were numerous qualified pilots available. The war brought many innovations to aviation, including the first <a href="/wiki/Turbojet" title="Turbojet">jet</a> aircraft and the first liquid-fueled <a href="/wiki/Rocket" title="Rocket">rockets</a>.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Cessna_172S_Skyhawk_SP,_Private_JP6817606.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Cessna_172S_Skyhawk_SP%2C_Private_JP6817606.jpg/220px-Cessna_172S_Skyhawk_SP%2C_Private_JP6817606.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Cessna_172S_Skyhawk_SP%2C_Private_JP6817606.jpg/330px-Cessna_172S_Skyhawk_SP%2C_Private_JP6817606.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Cessna_172S_Skyhawk_SP%2C_Private_JP6817606.jpg/440px-Cessna_172S_Skyhawk_SP%2C_Private_JP6817606.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1200" data-file-height="800" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Cessna_172S_Skyhawk_SP,_Private_JP6817606.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Cessna_172" title="Cessna 172">Cessna 172</a> is the most produced aircraft in history<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div>\n<p>After World War II, especially in North America, there was a boom in <a href="/wiki/General_aviation" title="General aviation">general aviation</a>, both private and commercial, as thousands of pilots were released from military service and many inexpensive war-surplus transport and training aircraft became available. Manufacturers such as <a href="/wiki/Cessna" title="Cessna">Cessna</a>, <a href="/wiki/Piper_Aircraft" title="Piper Aircraft">Piper</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Beechcraft" title="Beechcraft">Beechcraft</a> expanded production to provide light aircraft for the new middle-class market.\n</p><p>By the 1950s, the development of civil jets grew, beginning with the <a href="/wiki/De_Havilland_Comet" title="De Havilland Comet">de Havilland Comet</a>, though the first widely used passenger jet was the <a href="/wiki/Boeing_707" title="Boeing 707">Boeing 707</a>, because it was much more economical than other aircraft at that time. At the same time, <a href="/wiki/Gas_turbine" title="Gas turbine">turboprop</a> propulsion began to appear for smaller commuter planes, making it possible to serve small-volume routes in a much wider range of weather conditions.\n</p><p>Since the 1960s <a href="/wiki/Composite_material" title="Composite material">composite material</a> airframes and quieter, more efficient engines have become available, and <a href="/wiki/Concorde" title="Concorde">Concorde</a> provided <a href="/wiki/Supersonic_transport" title="Supersonic transport">supersonic passenger service</a> for more than two decades, but the most important lasting innovations have taken place in instrumentation and control. The arrival of <a href="/wiki/Transistor" title="Transistor">solid-state</a> electronics, the <a href="/wiki/Global_Positioning_System" title="Global Positioning System">Global Positioning System</a>, <a href="/wiki/Communications_satellite" title="Communications satellite">satellite communications</a>, and increasingly small and powerful computers and <a href="/wiki/Light-emitting_diode" title="Light-emitting diode">LED</a> displays, have dramatically changed the cockpits of <a href="/wiki/Airliner" title="Airliner">airliners</a> and, increasingly, of smaller aircraft as well. Pilots can navigate much more accurately and view terrain, obstructions, and other nearby aircraft on a map or through <a href="/wiki/Synthetic_vision" class="mw-redirect" title="Synthetic vision">synthetic vision</a>, even at night or in low visibility.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Helios_cthomas.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fc/Helios_cthomas.jpg/220px-Helios_cthomas.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="158" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fc/Helios_cthomas.jpg/330px-Helios_cthomas.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fc/Helios_cthomas.jpg/440px-Helios_cthomas.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3000" data-file-height="2160" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Helios_cthomas.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/Helios_Prototype" class="mw-redirect" title="Helios Prototype">Helios</a> researches <a href="/wiki/Solar_power" title="Solar power">solar powered</a> flight.</div></div></div>\n<p>On June 21, 2004, <a href="/wiki/SpaceShipOne" title="SpaceShipOne">SpaceShipOne</a> became the first privately funded aircraft to make a <a href="/wiki/Spaceflight" title="Spaceflight">spaceflight</a>, opening the possibility of an aviation market capable of leaving the Earth\'s atmosphere. Meanwhile, flying prototypes of aircraft powered by alternative fuels, such as <a href="/wiki/Ethanol" title="Ethanol">ethanol</a>, <a href="/wiki/Electricity" title="Electricity">electricity</a>, and even <a href="/wiki/Solar_energy" title="Solar energy">solar energy</a>, are becoming more common.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Operations_of_aircraft">Operations of aircraft</span><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Civil_aviation">Civil aviation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/wiki/General_aviation" title="General aviation">general aviation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Scheduled_air_transport" class="mw-redirect" title="Scheduled air transport">scheduled air href="/wiki/Ilyushin" title="Ilyushin">Ilyushin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tupolev" title="Tupolev">Tupolev</a> concentrate on wide-body and narrow-body jet <a href="/wiki/Airliner" Large networks of specialized parts suppliers from around the world support these manufacturers, who sometimes provide only the initial design and final assembly in their own plants. The Chinese <a href="/wiki/ACAC_consortium" title="ACAC consortium">ACAC consortium</a> will also soon enter the civil transport market with its <a href="/wiki/Comac_ARJ21" title="Comac ARJ21">Comac ARJ21</a> regional jet.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Until the 1970s, most major airlines were <a href="/wiki/Flag_carrier" title="Flag carrier">flag carriers</a>, sponsored by their governments and heavily protected from competition. Since then, <a href="/wiki/Open_skies" class="mw-redirect" title="Open skies">open skies</a> agreements have resulted in increased competition and choice for consumers, coupled with falling prices for airlines. The combination of high fuel prices, low fares, high salaries, and crises such as the <a href="/wiki/September_11_attacks" title="September 11 attacks">September 11 attacks</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Severe_acute_respiratory_syndrome" title="Severe acute respiratory syndrome">SARS epidemic</a> have driven many older airlines to government-bailouts, bankruptcy or mergers. usually falls under different regulations depending on whether it is private or commercial and on the type of equipment involved.\n</p><p>Many small aircraft manufacturers serve the general aviation market, with a focus on private aviation and flight training.\n</p><p>The most important recent developments for small aircraft (which form the bulk of the GA fleet) have been the introduction of advanced <a href="/wiki/Avionics" title="Avionics">avionics</a> (including <a href="/wiki/Global_Positioning_System" title="Global Positioning System">GPS</a>) that were formerly found only in large <a href="/wiki/Airliner" title="Airliner">airliners</a>, and the introduction of <a href="/wiki/Composite_material" title="Composite material">composite materials</a> to make small aircraft lighter and faster. <a href="/wiki/Ultralight_aviation" title="Ultralight aviation">Ultralight</a> and <a href="/wiki/Homebuilt_aircraft" title="Homebuilt aircraft">homebuilt aircraft</a> have also become increasingly popular for recreational use, since in most countries that allow private aviation, they are much less expensive and less heavily regulated than certified aircraft.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Military_aviation">Military aviation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aviation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Military aviation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Military_aviation" title="Military aviation">Military aviation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aerial_warfare" title="Aerial warfare">Aerial warfare</a></div>\n<p>Simple <a href="/wiki/Balloon_(aeronautics)" title="Balloon (aeronautics)">balloons</a> were used as surveillance aircraft as early as the 18th century. Over the years, <a href="/wiki/Military_aircraft" title="Military aircraft">military aircraft</a> have been built to meet ever increasing capability requirements. Manufacturers of military aircraft compete for contracts to supply their government\'s arsenal. Aircraft are selected based on factors like cost, performance, and the speed of production.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Lockheed_SR-71_Blackbird.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/Lockheed_SR-71_Blackbird.jpg/220px-Lockheed_SR-71_Blackbird.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="172" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/Lockheed_SR-71_Blackbird.jpg/330px-Lockheed_SR-71_Blackbird.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/Lockheed_SR-71_Blackbird.jpg/440px-Lockheed_SR-71_Blackbird.jpg 2x" data-file-width="5100" data-file-height="3996" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Lockheed_SR-71_Blackbird.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_SR-71" class="mw-redirect" title="Lockheed SR-71">Lockheed SR-71</a> remains unsurpassed in many areas of performance.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Types_of_military_aviation">Types of military aviation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aviation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Types of military aviation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Fighter_aircraft" title="Fighter aircraft">Fighter aircraft</a>\'s primary function is to destroy other aircraft. (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Sopwith_Camel" title="Sopwith Camel">Sopwith Camel</a>, <a href="/wiki/A6M_Zero" class="mw-redirect" title="A6M Zero">A6M Zero</a>, <a href="/wiki/McDonnell_Douglas_F-15_Eagle" title="McDonnell Douglas F-15 Eagle">F-15</a>, <a href="/wiki/MiG-29" class="mw-redirect" title="MiG-29">MiG-29</a>, <a href="/wiki/Su-27" class="mw-redirect" title="Su-27">Su-27</a>, and <a href="/wiki/F-22" class="mw-redirect" title="F-22">F-22</a>).</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Ground_attack" class="mw-redirect" title="Ground attack">Ground attack aircraft</a> are used against <a href="/wiki/Tactical_bombing" title="Tactical bombing">tactical</a> earth-bound targets. (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Junkers_Ju_87" title="Junkers Ju 87">Junkers Stuka</a>, <a href="/wiki/A-10_Thunderbolt_II" class="mw-redirect" title="A-10 Thunderbolt II">A-10</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ilyushin_Il-2" title="Ilyushin Il-2">Il-2</a>, <a href="/wiki/J-22_Orao" class="mw-redirect" title="J-22 Orao">J-22 Orao</a>, <a href="/wiki/AH-64" class="mw-redirect" title="AH-64">AH-64</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sukhoi_Su-25" title="Sukhoi Su-25">Su-25</a>).</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Bomber" title="Bomber">Bombers</a> are generally used against more <a href="/wiki/Strategic_bombing" title="Strategic bombing">strategic</a> targets, such as factories and oil fields. (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Zeppelin" title="Zeppelin">Zeppelin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tu-95" class="mw-redirect" title="Tu-95">Tu-95</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dassault_Mirage_IV" title="Dassault Mirage IV">Mirage IV</a>, and <a href="/wiki/B-52_Stratofortress" class="mw-redirect" title="B-52 Stratofortress">B-52</a>).</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Military_transport_aircraft" title="Military transport aircraft">Transport aircraft</a> are used to transport hardware and personnel. (e.g. <a href="/wiki/C-17_Globemaster_III" class="mw-redirect" title="C-17 Globemaster III">C-17 Globemaster III</a>, <a href="/wiki/C-130_Hercules" class="mw-redirect" title="C-130 Hercules">C-130 Hercules</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mil_Mi-26" title="Mil Mi-26">Mil Mi-26</a>).</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Surveillance_aircraft" title="Surveillance aircraft">Surveillance</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aerial_reconnaissance" title="Aerial reconnaissance">reconnaissance</a> aircraft obtain information about enemy forces. (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Rumpler_Taube" class="mw-redirect" title="Rumpler Taube">Rumpler Taube</a>, <a href="/wiki/De_Havilland_Mosquito" title="De Havilland Mosquito">Mosquito</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_U-2" title="Lockheed U-2">U-2</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bell_OH-58_Kiowa" title="Bell OH-58 Kiowa">OH-58</a> and <a href="/wiki/MiG-25R" class="mw-redirect" title="MiG-25R">MiG-25R</a>).</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Unmanned_aerial_vehicle" title="Unmanned aerial vehicle">Unmanned aerial vehicles (UAVs)</a> are used primarily as reconnaissance fixed-wing aircraft, though many also carry payloads. Cargo aircraft are in development.  (e.g. <a href="/wiki/RQ-7" class="mw-redirect" title="RQ-7">RQ-7B Shadow</a>, <a href="/wiki/MQ-8" class="mw-redirect" title="MQ-8">MQ-8 Fire Scout</a>, and <a href="/wiki/MQ-1C" class="mw-redirect" title="MQ-1C">MQ-1C Gray Eagle</a>).</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Missile" title="Missile">Missiles</a> deliver warheads, normally explosives.</li></ul>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Air_safety">Air safety</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aviation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Air safety">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Aviation_safety" title="Aviation safety">Aviation safety</a></div>\n<p>Aviation safety means the state of an aviation system or organization in which risks associated with aviation activities, related to, or in direct support of the operation of aircraft, are reduced and controlled to an acceptable level. It encompasses the theory, practice, investigation, and categorization of flight failures, and the prevention of such failures through regulation, education, and training. It can also be applied in the context of campaigns that inform the public as to the safety of air travel. The worst aviation accident in history was the <a href="/wiki/Tenerife_airport_disaster" title="Tenerife airport disaster">Tenerife airport disaster</a> on March 27, 1977, when 583 people died when two Boeing 747 jumbo jets, operated by Pan Am and KLM collided on a runway in Los Rodeos airport, now known as Tenerife North.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aviation_accidents_and_incidents">Aviation accidents and incidents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Aviation_accidents_and_incidents" title="Aviation accidents and incidents">Aviation accidents and incidents</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Crash.arp.600pix.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" href="/wiki/Air_show" title="Air show">air show</a> in 2003</div></div></div>\n<p>An <i>aviation accident</i> is defined by the <a href="/wiki/Convention_on_International_Civil_Aviation" class="mw-redirect" title="Convention on International Civil Aviation">Convention on International Civil Aviation</a> Annex 13 as an occurrence associated with the operation of an aircraft which takes place between the time any person boards the aircraft with the intention of flight until such time as all such persons have disembarked, in which a person is fatally or seriously injured, the aircraft sustains damage or structural failure or the aircraft is missing or is completely inaccessible.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The first fatal aviation accident occurred in a <a href="/wiki/Wright_Model_A" title="Wright Model A">Wright Model A</a> aircraft at <a href="/wiki/Fort_Myer" title="Fort Myer">Fort Myer, Virginia, USA</a>, on September 17, 1908, resulting in injury to the pilot, <a href="/wiki/Orville_Wright" class="mw-redirect" title="Orville Wright">Orville Wright</a>, and death of the passenger, Signal Corps Lieutenant <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Selfridge" title="Thomas Selfridge">Thomas Selfridge</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>An <i>aviation incident</i> is defined as an occurrence, other than an accident, associated with the operation of an aircraft that affects or could affect the safety of operations.<sup id="cite_ref-AirSafe.com_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AirSafe.com-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>An accident in which the damage to the aircraft is such that it must be written off, or in which the plane is destroyed, is called a <i>hull loss accident</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-AirSafe.com_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AirSafe.com-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Air_traffic_control">Air traffic control</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aviation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Air traffic control">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Air_traffic_control" title="Air traffic control">Air traffic control</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Towers_Schiphol_small.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Towers_Schiphol_small.jpg/170px-Towers_Schiphol_small.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="250" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Towers_Schiphol_small.jpg/255px-Towers_Schiphol_small.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/e/ee/Towers_Schiphol_small.jpg 2x" data-file-width="261" data-file-height="384" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Towers_Schiphol_small.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Air_traffic_control_tower" class="mw-redirect" title="Air traffic control tower">Air traffic control towers</a> at <a href="/wiki/Amsterdam_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Amsterdam Airport">Amsterdam Airport</a></div></div></div>\n<p>Air traffic control (ATC) involves communication with aircraft to help maintain separation&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 that is, they ensure that aircraft are sufficiently far enough apart horizontally or vertically for no risk of collision. Controllers may co-ordinate position reports provided by pilots, or in high traffic areas (such as the United States) they may use <a href="/wiki/Radar" title="Radar">radar</a> to see aircraft positions.\n</p><p>There are generally four different types of ATC:\n</p>\n<ul><li>center controllers, who control aircraft en route between airports</li>\n<li>control towers (including tower, ground control, clearance delivery, and other services), which control aircraft within a small distance (typically 10\xe2\x80\x9315&#160;km horizontal, and 1,000 m vertical) of an airport.</li>\n<li>oceanic controllers, who control aircraft over international waters between continents, generally without radar service.</li>\n<li>terminal controllers, who control aircraft in a wider area (typically 50\xe2\x80\x9380&#160;km) around busy airports.</li></ul>\n<p>ATC is especially important for aircraft flying under <a href="/wiki/Instrument_flight_rules" title="Instrument flight rules">instrument flight rules</a> (IFR), when they may be in weather conditions that do not allow the pilots to see other aircraft. However, in very high-traffic areas, especially near major airports, aircraft flying under <a href="/wiki/Visual_flight_rules" title="Visual flight rules">visual flight rules</a> (VFR) are also required to follow instructions from ATC.\n</p><p>In addition to separation from other aircraft, ATC may provide weather advisories, terrain separation, navigation assistance, and other services to pilots, depending on their workload.\n</p><p>ATC do not control all flights. The majority of VFR (Visual Flight Rules) flights in North America are not required to contact ATC (unless they are passing through a busy terminal area or using a major airport), and in many areas, such as northern Canada and low altitude in northern Scotland, Air traffic control services are not available even for IFR flights at lower altitudes.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_impact">Environmental impact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aviation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Environmental impact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Environmental_impact_of_aviation" title="Environmental impact of aviation">Environmental impact of aviation</a></div><p>\nLike all activities involving <a href="/wiki/Combustion" title="Combustion">combustion</a>, operating powered aircraft (from airliners to hot air balloons) releases <a href="/wiki/Soot" title="Soot">soot</a> and other pollutants into the atmosphere. <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">Greenhouse gases</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> (CO<sub>2</sub>) are also produced. These may contribute to <a href="/wiki/Cirrus_cloud" title="Cirrus cloud">cirrus cloud</a> formation.</div></div></div>\n<ul><li>Aircraft operating at high altitudes near the <a href="/wiki/Tropopause" title="Tropopause">tropopause</a> (mainly large <a href="/wiki/Jet_airliner" title="Jet airliner">jet airliners</a>) emit aerosols and leave <a href="/wiki/Contrail" title="Contrail">contrails</a>, both of which can increase <a href="/wiki/Cirrus_cloud" title="Cirrus cloud">cirrus cloud</a> formation&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 cloud cover may have increased by up to 0.2% since the birth of aviation.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> Clouds can have both a cooling and warming effect. They reflect some of the sun\'s rays back into space, but also block some of the heat radiated by Earth\'s surface. On average, both thin natural cirrus clouds and contrails have a net warming effect.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>Aircraft operating at high altitudes near the tropopause can also release chemicals that interact with greenhouse gases at those altitudes, particularly <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_oxide" title="Nitrogen oxide">nitrogen compounds</a>, which interact with ozone, increasing ozone concentrations.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>Most light piston aircraft burn <a href="/wiki/Avgas" title="Avgas">avgas</a>, which contains <a href="/wiki/Tetraethyllead" title="Tetraethyllead">tetraethyllead</a> (TEL). Some lower-compression piston engines can operate on unleaded <a href="/wiki/Gasoline" title="Gasoline">mogas</a>, and turbine engines and diesel engines&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 neither of which require lead&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 are appearing on some newer <a href="/wiki/Light_aircraft" title="Light aircraft">light aircraft</a>.</li></ul>\n<p>Another environmental impact of aviation is <a href="/wiki/Noise_pollution" title="Noise pollution">noise pollution</a>, mainly caused by aircraft taking off and landing.
