and use of <a href="/wiki/Computing" title="Computing">computer technology</a>, focused on the <a href="/wiki/Interface_(computing)" title="Interface (computing)">interfaces</a> between people (<a href="/wiki/User_(computing)" title="User (computing)">users</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Computer" title="Computer">computers</a>. Researchers in the field of HCI observe the ways in which humans interact with computers and design technologies that let humans interact with computers in novel ways. \n</p><p>As a field of research, human-computer interaction is situated at the intersection of <a href="/wiki/Computer_science" title="Computer science">computer science</a>, <a href="/wiki/Behavioural_sciences" title="Behavioural sciences">behavioural sciences</a>, <a href="/wiki/Design" title="Design">design</a>, <a href="/wiki/Media_studies" title="Media studies">media studies</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Outline_of_human%E2%80%93computer_interaction#Related_fields" title="Outline of human\xe2\x80\x93computer interaction">several other fields of study</a>. The term was popularized by <a href="/wiki/Stuart_K._Card" class="mw-redirect" title="Stuart K. Card">Stuart K. Card</a>, <a href="/wiki/Allen_Newell" title="Allen Newell">Allen Newell</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Thomas_P._Moran" title="Thomas P. Moran">Thomas P. Moran</a> in their seminal 1983 book, <i>The Psychology of Human\xe2\x80\x93Computer Interaction</i>, although the authors first used the term in 1980<sup id="cite_ref-The_keystroke-level_model_for_user_performance_time_with_interactive_systems_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_keystroke-level_model_for_user_performance_time_with_interactive_systems-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> and the first known use was in 1975.<sup id="cite_ref-Evaluating_the_impact_of_office_automation_on_top_management_communication_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Evaluating_the_impact_of_office_automation_on_top_management_communication-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The term connotes that, unlike other tools with only limited uses (such as a wooden mallet, useful for hitting things, but not much else), a computer has many uses and this takes place as an open-ended dialog between the user and the computer. The notion of dialog likens human\xe2\x80\x93computer interaction to human-to-human interaction, an analogy which is crucial to theoretical considerations in the field.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc" class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human%E2%80%93computer_interaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Introduction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Humans interact with computers in many ways; the interface between humans and computers is crucial to facilitate this <a href="/wiki/Interaction" title="Interaction">interaction</a>. HCI is also sometimes termed <i>human\xe2\x80\x93machine interaction</i> (HMI), <i>man-machine interaction</i> (MMI) or <i>computer-human interaction</i> (CHI). Desktop applications, internet browsers, handheld computers, ERP, and computer kiosks make use of the prevalent <a href="/wiki/Graphical_user_interface" title="Graphical user interface">graphical user interfaces</a> (GUI) of today.<sup id="cite_ref-ACM_SIGCHI_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ACM_SIGCHI-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Voice_user_interface" title="Voice user interface">Voice user interfaces</a> (VUI) are used for <a href="/wiki/Speech_recognition" title="Speech recognition">speech recognition</a> and synthesizing systems, and the emerging <a href="/wiki/Multimodal_interaction" title="Multimodal interaction">multi-modal</a> and Graphical user interfaces (GUI) allow humans to engage with <a href="/wiki/Embodied_agent" title="Embodied agent">embodied character agents</a> in a way that cannot be achieved with other interface paradigms. The growth in human\xe2\x80\x93computer interaction field has been in quality of interaction, and in different branching in its history. Instead of designing regular interfaces, the different research branches have had a different focus on the concepts of <a href="/wiki/Multimodality" title="Multimodality">multimodality</a><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> rather than unimodality, intelligent adaptive interfaces rather than command/action based ones, and finally active rather than passive interfaces.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Association_for_Computing_Machinery" title="Association for Computing Machinery">Association for Computing Machinery</a> (ACM) defines human-computer interaction as "a discipline that is concerned with the design, evaluation and implementation of interactive computing systems for human use and with the study of major phenomena surrounding them".<sup id="cite_ref-ACM_SIGCHI_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ACM_SIGCHI-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> An important facet of HCI is user satisfaction (or simply End User Computing Satisfaction).\n"Because human\xe2\x80\x93computer interaction studies a human and a machine in communication, it draws from supporting knowledge on both the machine and the human side. On the machine side, techniques in <a href="/wiki/Computer_graphics" title="Computer graphics">computer graphics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Operating_system" title="Operating system">operating systems</a>, <a href="/wiki/Programming_language" title="Programming language">programming languages</a>, and development environments are relevant. On the human side, <a href="/wiki/Communication_theory" title="Communication theory">communication theory</a>, <a href="/wiki/Graphic_design" title="Graphic design">graphic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Industrial_design" title="Industrial design">industrial design</a> disciplines, <a href="/wiki/Linguistics" title="Linguistics">linguistics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Social_science" title="Social science">social sciences</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cognitive_psychology" title="Cognitive psychology">cognitive psychology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Social_psychology" title="Social psychology">social psychology</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Human_factors" class="mw-redirect" title="Human factors">human factors</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Computer_user_satisfaction" title="Computer user satisfaction">computer user satisfaction</a> are relevant. And, of course, engineering and design methods are relevant."<sup id="cite_ref-ACM_SIGCHI_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ACM_SIGCHI-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Due to the multidisciplinary nature of HCI, people with different backgrounds contribute to its success. \n</p><p>Poorly designed <a href="/wiki/Human-machine_interface" class="mw-redirect" title="Human-machine interface">human-machine interfaces</a> can lead to many unexpected problems. A classic example is the <a href="/wiki/Three_Mile_Island_accident" title="Three Mile Island accident">Three Mile Island accident</a>, a nuclear meltdown accident, where investigations concluded that the design of the human-machine interface was at least partly responsible for the disaster.<sup id="cite_ref-What_is_Cognitive_Ergonomics?_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-What_is_Cognitive_Ergonomics?-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-NRC:_Backgrounder_on_the_Three_Mile_Island_Accident_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NRC:_Backgrounder_on_the_Three_Mile_Island_Accident-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-threemileisland_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-threemileisland-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Similarly, accidents in aviation have resulted from manufacturers\' decisions to use non-standard <a href="/wiki/Flight_instruments#Layout" title="Flight instruments">flight instruments</a> or throttle quadrant layouts: even though the new designs were proposed to be superior in basic human-machine interaction, pilots had already ingrained the "standard" layout and thus the conceptually good idea actually had undesirable results.\n</p>\n<h2><span id="Human.E2.80.93computer_interface"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Human\xe2\x80\x93computer_interface">Human\xe2\x80\x93computer interface</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human%E2%80%93computer_interaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Human\xe2\x80\x93computer interface">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/User_interface" title="User interface">User interface</a></div>\n<p>The human\xe2\x80\x93computer interface can be described as the point of communication between the human user and the computer. The flow of information between the human and computer is defined as the <i>loop of interaction</i>. The loop of interaction has several aspects to it, including:\n</p>\n<ul><li>Visual Based :The visual based human computer interaction is probably the most widespread area in Human Computer Interaction (HCI) research.</li>\n<li>Audio Based&#160;: The audio based interaction between a computer and a human is another important area of in HCI systems. This area deals with information acquired by different audio signals.</li>\n<li><i>Task environment</i>: The conditions and goals set upon the user.</li>\n<li><i>Machine environment</i>: The environment that the computer is connected to, e.g. a laptop in a college student\'s dorm room.</li>\n<li><i>Areas of the interface</i>: Non-overlapping areas involve processes of the human and computer not pertaining to their interaction. Meanwhile, the overlapping areas only concern themselves with the processes pertaining to their interaction.</li>\n<li><i>Input flow</i>: The flow of information that begins in the task environment, when the user has some task that requires using their computer.</li>\n<li><i>Output</i>: The flow of information that originates in the machine environment.</li>\n<li><i>Feedback</i>: Loops through the interface that evaluate, moderate, and confirm processes as they pass from the human through the interface to the computer and back.</li>\n<li><i>Fit</i>: This is the match between the computer design, the user and the task to optimize the human resources needed to accomplish the task.</li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Goals_for_computers">Goals for computers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human%E2%80%93computer_interaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Goals for computers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Human\xe2\x80\x93computer interaction studies the ways in which humans make\xe2\x80\x94or do not make\xe2\x80\x94use of computational artifacts, systems and infrastructures. Much of the research in the field seeks to <i>improve</i> human\xe2\x80\x93computer interaction by improving the <i>usability</i> of computer interfaces.<sup id="cite_ref-Grudin1992usability_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Grudin1992usability-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> How usability is to be precisely understood, how it relates to other social and cultural values and when it is, and when it may not be a desirable property of computer interfaces is increasingly debated.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Much of the research in the field of human\xe2\x80\x93computer interaction takes an interest in:\n</p>\n<ul><li>Methods for Present day models, in general, center around a steady input and discussion between clients, creators, and specialists and push for specialized frameworks to be folded with the sorts of encounters clients need to have, as opposed to wrapping <a href="/wiki/User_experience" title="User experience">user experience</a> around a finished framework.\n</p>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Activity_theory" title="Activity theory">Activity theory</a>: utilized in HCI to characterize and consider the setting where human cooperations with PCs occur. Before a display is designed, the task that the display is intended to support must be defined (e.g. navigating, controlling, decision making, learning, entertaining, etc.). A user or operator must be able to process whatever information that a system generates and displays; therefore, the information must be displayed according to principles in a manner that will support perception, situation awareness, and understanding.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Thirteen_principles_of_display_design">Thirteen principles of display design</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human%E2%80%93computer_interaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Thirteen principles of display design">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Christopher Wickens et al. defined 13 principles of display design in their book <i>An Introduction to Human Factors Engineering</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-introduction_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-introduction-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>These principles of human perception and information processing can be utilized to create an effective display design. A reduction in errors, a reduction in required training time, an increase in efficiency, and an increase in user satisfaction are a few of the many potential benefits that can be achieved through utilization of these principles.\n</p><p>Certain principles may not be applicable to different displays or situations. Some principles may seem to be conflicting, and there is no simple solution to say that one principle is more important than another. The principles may be tailored to a specific design or situation. Striking a functional balance among the principles is critical for an effective design.<sup id="cite_ref-guidelines_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-guidelines-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Perceptual_principles">Perceptual principles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human%E2%80%93computer_interaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Perceptual principles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p><i>1.\tMake displays legible (or audible)</i>. A display\'s legibility is critical and necessary for designing a usable display. If the characters or objects being displayed cannot be discernible, then the operator cannot effectively make use of them.\n</p><p><i>2.\tAvoid absolute judgment limits</i>. Do not ask the user to determine the level of a variable on the basis of a single sensory variable (e.g. color, size, loudness). These sensory variables can contain many possible levels.\n</p><p><i>3.\tTop-down processing</i>. Signals are likely perceived and interpreted in accordance with what is expected based on a user\'s experience. If a signal is presented contrary to the user\'s expectation, more physical evidence of that signal may need to be presented to assure that it is understood correctly.\n</p><p><i>4.\tRedundancy gain</i>. If a signal is presented more than once, it is more likely that it will be understood correctly. This can be done by presenting the signal in alternative physical forms (e.g. color and shape, voice and print, etc.), as redundancy does not imply repetition. A traffic light is a good example of redundancy, as color and position are redundant.\n</p><p><i>5.\tSimilarity causes confusion: Use distinguishable elements</i>. Signals that appear to be similar will likely be confused. The ratio of similar features to different features causes signals to be similar. For example, A423B9 is more similar to A423B8 than 92 is to 93. Unnecessarily similar features should be removed and dissimilar features should be highlighted.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Mental_model_principles">Mental model principles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human%E2%80%93computer_interaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Mental model principles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p><i>6.\tPrinciple of pictorial realism</i>. A display should look like the variable that it represents (e.g. high temperature on a thermometer shown as a higher vertical level). If there are multiple elements, they can be configured in a manner that looks like it would in the represented environment.\n</p><p><i>7.\tPrinciple of the moving part</i>. Moving elements should move in a pattern and direction compatible with the user\'s mental model of how it actually moves in the system. For example, the moving element on an altimeter should move upward with increasing altitude.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Principles_based_on_attention">Principles based on attention</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human%E2%80%93computer_interaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Principles based on attention">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p><i>8.\tMinimizing <a href="/wiki/Information_access" title="Information access">information access</a> cost</i> or <a href="/wiki/Interaction_cost" title="Interaction cost">interaction cost</a>. When the user\'s attention is diverted from one location to another to access necessary information, there is an associated cost in time or effort. A display design should minimize this cost by allowing for frequently accessed sources to be located at the nearest possible position. However, adequate legibility should not be sacrificed to reduce this cost.\n</p><p><i>9.\tProximity compatibility principle</i>. Divided attention between two information sources may be necessary for the completion of one task. These sources must be mentally integrated and are defined to have close mental proximity. Information access costs should be low, which can be achieved in many ways (e.g. proximity, linkage by common colours, patterns, shapes, etc.). However, close display proximity can be harmful by causing too much clutter.\n</p><p><i>10.\tPrinciple of multiple resources</i>. A user can more easily process information across different resources. For example, visual and auditory information can be presented simultaneously rather than presenting all visual or all auditory information.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Memory_principles">Memory principles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human%E2%80%93computer_interaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Memory principles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p><i>11.\tReplace memory with visual information: knowledge in the world</i>. A user should not need to retain important information solely in working memory or retrieve it from long-term memory. A menu, checklist, or another display can aid the user by easing the use of their memory. However, the use of memory may sometimes benefit the user by eliminating the need to reference some type of knowledge in the world (e.g., an expert computer operator would rather use direct commands from memory than refer to a manual). The use of knowledge in a user\'s head and knowledge in the world must be balanced for an effective design.\n</p><p><i>12.\tPrinciple of predictive aiding</i>. Proactive actions are usually more effective than reactive actions. A display should attempt to eliminate resource-demanding cognitive tasks and replace them with simpler perceptual tasks to reduce the use of the user\'s mental resources. This will allow the user to focus on current conditions, and to consider possible future conditions. An example of a predictive aid is a road sign displaying the distance to a certain destination.\n</p><p><i>13.\tPrinciple of consistency</i>. Old habits from other displays will easily transfer to support processing of new displays if they are designed consistently. A user\'s long-term memory will trigger actions that are expected to be appropriate. A design must accept this fact and utilize consistency among different displays.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Current_research">Current research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human%E2%80%93computer_interaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Current research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<table class="box-Unreferenced_section plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Unreferenced" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><a href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" decoding="async" width="50" height="39" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="512" data-file-height="399" /></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>does not <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">cite</a> any <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">sources</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Human%E2%80%93computer_interaction&amp;action=edit">improve this section</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Referencing_for_beginners" title="Help:Referencing for beginners">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. For example, one study found out that people expected a computer with a man\'s name to cost more than a machine with a woman\'s name.<sup id="cite_ref-posard2014_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-posard2014-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Other research finds that individuals perceive their interactions with computers more negatively than humans, despite behaving the same way towards these machines.<sup id="cite_ref-posardrinderknecht2015_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-posardrinderknecht2015-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Knowledge-driven_human.E2.80.93computer_interaction"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Knowledge-driven_human\xe2\x80\x93computer_interaction">Knowledge-driven human\xe2\x80\x93computer interaction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human%E2%80%93computer_interaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Knowledge-driven human\xe2\x80\x93computer interaction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In human and computer interactions, a semantic gap usually exists between human and computer\'s understandings towards mutual behaviors. <a href="/wiki/Ontology_(information_science)" title="Ontology (information science)">Ontology</a>, as a formal representation of domain-specific knowledge, can be used to address this problem, through solving the semantic ambiguities between the two parties.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Emotions_and_human-computer_interaction">Emotions and human-computer interaction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human%E2%80%93computer_interaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Emotions and human-computer interaction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Affective_computing" title="Affective computing">Affective computing</a> and <a href="/wiki/Emotion_recognition" title="Emotion recognition">Emotion recognition</a></div>\n<p>In the interaction of humans and computers, research has studied how computers can detect, process and react to human emotions to develop emotionally intelligent information systems. Researchers have suggested several \'affect-detection channels\'.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> The potential of telling human emotions in an automated and digital fashion lies in improvements to the effectiveness of human-computer interaction.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> The influence of emotions in human-computer interaction has been studied in fields such as financial decision making using <a href="/wiki/Ecg" class="mw-redirect" title="Ecg">ECG</a><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> and organisational knowledge sharing using <a href="/wiki/Eye_tracking" title="Eye tracking">eye tracking</a> and face readers as affect-detection channels.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> In these fields it has been shown that affect-detection channels have the potential to <a href="/wiki/Emotion_detection" class="mw-redirect" title="Emotion detection">detect human emotions</a> and that information systems can incorporate the data obtained from affect-detection channels to improve decision models.\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Brain.E2.80.93computer_interfaces"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Brain\xe2\x80\x93computer_interfaces">Brain\xe2\x80\x93computer interfaces</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human%E2%80%93computer_interaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Brain\xe2\x80\x93computer interfaces">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Brain%E2%80%93computer_interface" title="Brain\xe2\x80\x93computer interface">Brain\xe2\x80\x93computer interface</a></div>\n<p>A <a href="/wiki/Brain%E2%80%93computer_interface" title="Brain\xe2\x80\x93computer interface">brain\xe2\x80\x93computer interface</a> (BCI), is a direct communication pathway between an enhanced or wired <a href="/wiki/Brain" title="Brain">brain</a> and an external device. BCI differs from <a href="/wiki/Neuromodulation_(medicine)" title="Neuromodulation (medicine)">neuromodulation</a> in that it allows for bidirectional information flow. BCIs are often directed at researching, mapping, assisting, augmenting, or repairing human cognitive or sensory-motor functions.<sup id="cite_ref-Krucoff_584_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Krucoff_584-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Factors_of_change">Factors of change</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human%E2%80%93computer_interaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Factors of change">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Traditionally, computer use was modeled as a human\xe2\x80\x93computer dyad in which the two were connected by a narrow explicit communication channel, such as text-based terminals. Much work has been done to make the interaction between a computing system and a human more reflective of the multidimensional nature of everyday communication. Because of potential issues, human\xe2\x80\x93computer interaction shifted focus beyond the interface to respond to observations as articulated by D. Engelbart: "If ease of use was the only valid criterion, people would stick to tricycles and never try bicycles."<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The means by which humans interact with computers continues to evolve rapidly. Human\xe2\x80\x93computer interaction is affected by developments in computing. These forces include:\n</p>\n<ul><li>Decreasing hardware costs leading to larger memory and faster systems</li>\n<li>Miniaturization of hardware leading to portability</li>\n<li>Reduction in power requirements leading to portability</li>\n<li>New display technologies leading to the packaging of computational devices in new forms</li>\n<li>Specialized hardware leading to new functions</li>\n<li>Increased development of network communication and distributed computing</li>\n<li>Increasingly widespread use of computers, especially by people who are outside of the computing profession</li>\n<li>Increasing innovation in input techniques (e.g., voice, <a href="/wiki/Gesture_recognition" title="Gesture recognition">gesture</a>, pen), combined with lowering cost, leading to rapid computerization by people formerly left out of the <i>computer revolution</i>.</li>\n<li>Wider social concerns leading to improved access to computers by currently disadvantaged groups</li></ul>\n<p>As of 2010<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Human%E2%80%93computer_interaction&amp;action=edit">&#91;update&#93;</a></sup> the future for HCI is expected<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> to include the following characteristics:\n</p>\n<ul><li><i><a href="/wiki/Ubiquitous_computing" title="Ubiquitous computing">Ubiquitous computing</a> and communication</i>. Computers are expected to communicate through high speed local networks, nationally over wide-area networks, and portably via infrared, ultrasonic, cellular, and other technologies. Data and computational services will be portably accessible from many if not most locations to which a user travels.</li>\n<li><i>High-functionality systems</i>. Systems can have large numbers of functions associated with them. There are so many systems that most users, technical or non-technical, do not have time to learn about in the traditional way (e.g., through thick user manuals).</li>\n<li><i>Mass availability of computer graphics</i>. Computer graphics capabilities such as image processing, graphics transformations, rendering, and interactive animation are becoming widespread as inexpensive chips become available for inclusion in general workstations and mobile devices.</li>\n<li><i>Mixed media</i>. Commercial systems can handle images, voice, sounds, video, text, formatted data. These are exchangeable over communication links among users. The separate fields of consumer electronics (e.g., stereo sets, DVD players, televisions) and computers are beginning to merge. Computer and print fields are expected to cross-assimilate.</li>\n<li><i>High-<a href="/wiki/Bandwidth_(signal_processing)" title="Bandwidth (signal processing)">bandwidth</a> interaction</i>. The rate at which humans and machines interact is expected to increase substantially due to the changes in speed, computer graphics, new media, and new input/output devices. This can lead to some qualitatively different interfaces, such as <a href="/wiki/Virtual_reality" title="Virtual reality">virtual reality</a> or computational video.</li>\n<li><i>Large and thin <a href="/wiki/Displays" class="mw-redirect" title="Displays">displays</a></i>. New display technologies are maturing, enabling very large displays and displays that are thin, lightweight, and low in power use. This is having large effects on portability and will likely enable developing paper-like, pen-based computer interaction systems very different in feel from present desktop workstations.</li>\n<li><i>Information utilities</i>. Public information utilities (such as home banking and shopping) and specialized industry services (e.g., weather for pilots) are expected to proliferate. The rate of proliferation can accelerate with the introduction of high-bandwidth interaction and the improvement in quality of interfaces.</li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Scientific_conferences">Scientific conferences</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human%E2%80%93computer_interaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Scientific conferences">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>One of the main conferences for new research in human\xe2\x80\x93computer interaction is the annually held <a href="/wiki/Association_for_Computing_Machinery" title="Association for Computing Machinery">Association for Computing Machinery</a>\'s (ACM) <i><a href="/wiki/Conference_on_Human_Factors_in_Computing_Systems" title="Conference on Human Factors in Computing Systems">Conference on Human Factors in Computing Systems</a></i>, usually referred to by its short name CHI (pronounced <i>kai</i>, or <i>khai</i>). CHI is organized by ACM Special Interest Group on Computer\xe2\x80\x93Human Interaction (<a href="/wiki/SIGCHI" title="SIGCHI">SIGCHI</a>).
