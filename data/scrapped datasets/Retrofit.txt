class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Retrofitting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Benefits">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<ul><li>Saving on <a href="/wiki/Capital_expenditure" title="Capital expenditure">capital expenditure</a> while benefiting from new technologies</li>\n<li>Optimization of existing plant components</li>\n<li>Adaptation of the plant for new or changed products</li>\n<li>Increase in piece number and cycle time</li>\n<li>Guaranteed spare parts availability<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>Reduced maintenance costs and increased reliability<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Manufacturing">Manufacturing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Retrofitting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Manufacturing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Principally retrofitting describes the measures taken in the manufacturing industry to allow new or updated parts to be fitted to old or outdated assemblies (like blades to wind turbines).<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Retrofitting parts are necessary for manufacture when the design of a large assembly is changed or revised. If, after the changes have been implemented, a customer (with an old version of the product) wishes to purchase a replacement part, then retrofit parts and assembling techniques will have to be used so that the revised parts will fit suitably onto the older assembly.\n</p><p>Retrofitting is an important process used for valves and actuators to ensure optimal operation of an industrial plant. One example is retrofitting a 3-way valve into a 2-way valve, which results in closing one of the three openings to continue using the valve for certain industrial systems.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Retrofitting can improve a machine or system\'s overall functionality by using advanced and updated equipment and technology\xe2\x80\x94such as integrating Human Machine Interfaces into older factories.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Another example of this is <a href="/wiki/Custom_car" title="Custom car">car customizing</a>, where older vehicles are fitted with new technologies: <a href="/wiki/Power_window" title="Power window">power windows</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cruise_control" title="Cruise control">cruise control</a>, <a href="/wiki/Remote_keyless_system" title="Remote keyless system">remote keyless systems</a>, electric <a href="/wiki/Fuel_pump_(engine)" class="mw-redirect" title="Fuel pump (engine)">fuel pumps</a>, <a href="/wiki/Self-driving_car" title="Self-driving car">driverless systems</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> etc.\n</p><p>Trucks<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Driverless_tractor#Tractor_upgrade_kits" title="Driverless tractor">agricultural machines</a> can also be given retrofits to make them driverless.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_management">Environmental management</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Retrofitting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Environmental management">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The term is also used in the field of <a href="/wiki/Environmental_engineering" title="Environmental engineering">environmental engineering</a>, particularly to describe <a href="/wiki/Construction" title="Construction">construction</a> or renovation projects on previously built sites, to improve <a href="/wiki/Water_quality" title="Water quality">water quality</a> in nearby <a href="/wiki/Stream" title="Stream">streams</a>, <a href="/wiki/River" title="River">rivers</a> or <a href="/wiki/Lake" title="Lake">lakes</a>. The concept has also been applied to changing the output mix of <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a> from <a href="/wiki/Power_plant" class="mw-redirect" title="Power plant">power plants</a> to <a href="/wiki/Cogeneration" title="Cogeneration">cogeneration</a> in <a href="/wiki/Urban_area" title="Urban area">urban areas</a> with a potential for <a href="/wiki/District_heating" title="District heating">district heating</a>.\n</p><p>Sites with extensive <a href="/wiki/Impervious_surface" title="Impervious surface">impervious surfaces</a> (such as parking lots and rooftops) can generate high levels of <a href="/wiki/Stormwater" title="Stormwater">stormwater</a> <a href="/wiki/Surface_runoff" title="Surface runoff">runoff</a> during rainstorms, and this can damage nearby water bodies.  These problems can often be addressed by installing new stormwater management features on the site, a process that practitioners refer to as stormwater retrofitting. Stormwater management practices used in retrofit projects include <a href="/wiki/Rain_garden" title="Rain garden">rain gardens</a>, <a href="/wiki/Permeable_paving" title="Permeable paving">permeable paving</a> and <a href="/wiki/Green_roof" title="Green roof">green roofs</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> <i>(See also <a href="/wiki/Stream_restoration" title="Stream restoration">stream restoration</a>.)</i>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Naval_vessels">Naval vessels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Retrofitting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Naval vessels">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Many naval vessels have undergone retrofitting and refitting, sometimes entire classes at once. For instance, the <a href="/wiki/New_Threat_Upgrade" title="New Threat Upgrade">New Threat Upgrade</a> program of the US Navy saw many vessels retrofitted for improved anti-air capability. Naval vessels are often retrofit for one of three reasons: to incorporate new technology, to compensate for performance gaps or weaknesses in design, or to change the ship\'s <a href="/wiki/Hull_classification_symbol#The_modern_hull_classification_system" title="Hull classification symbol">classification</a>.\n</p><p>Militaries of the world are often ardent adopters of the latest technology, and many technological advances have been spurred by warfare, especially in fields such as radar and radio communications. Because of this, and the significant investment that a ship hull represents, it is common for retrofitting to be performed whenever new systems are developed. This may be as small as replacing one type of radio with another, or replacing out-dated cryptography equipment with more secure methods of communication, or as major as replacing entire guns and turrets, adding armor plate, or new propulsion systems.\n</p><p>Other ships are retrofit to compensate for weaknesses perceived in their operational capabilities. This was the secondary purpose of the US Navy\'s New Threat Upgrade program, for instance. Major changes in doctrine or the art of warfare also necessitate changes, such as the anti-aircraft upgrades performed on many World War Two-era vessels as air power became a dominant part of naval strategy and tactics.\n</p><p>Additionally, because of the investment a hull represents, few navies scrap front-line warships. Many times smaller ships are retrofitted for patrol, coast guard, or specialized roles when they are no longer fit for duty as part of a warfleet. The Japanese <a href="/wiki/Momi-class_destroyer" title="Momi-class destroyer">Momi class</a> from the interwar period, for example, was converted from destroyers to patrol boats in 1939, as they were no longer capable enough to serve in the role of destroyer. Other times classes are retrofit because they are no longer needed in warfare, due to changes in tactics. For instance, the <a href="/wiki/USS_Langley_(CV-1)" title="USS Langley (CV-1)">USS&#160;<i>Langley</i></a> was an aircraft carrier converted from a collier (coal-carrying ship to supply coal-fired steamships with fuel) of the Jupiter-class.\n</p><p>Because of the heavy use of retrofitting and refitting, fictional navies also include the concept. As an example, in the Star Trek <a href="/wiki/MMORPG" class="mw-redirect" title="MMORPG">MMORPG</a> Star Trek Online players can purchase retrofitted ships of famous Star Trek ship classes, such as those crewed by the protagonists of the Star Trek TV series. This is done to allow players to pilot iconic ships from old series of the show, that wouldn\'t naturally be latest-and-greatest ships due to their obsolescence or size, but are retrofitted to be suitable for a maximum-level player-character admiral.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
