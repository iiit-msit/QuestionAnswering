template">e</abbr></a></li></ul></div></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p><b>Strike action</b>, also called <b>labor strike</b>, <b>labour strike</b>, or simply <b>strike</b>, is a work stoppage, caused by the mass refusal of <a href="/wiki/Employee" class="mw-redirect" title="Employee">employees</a> to <a href="/wiki/Labor_(economics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Labor (economics)">work</a>. A strike usually takes place in response to <a href="/wiki/Grievance_(labour)" title="Grievance (labour)">employee grievances</a>. Strikes became common during the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a>, when <a href="/wiki/Labour_economics" title="Labour economics">mass labor</a> became important in factories and mines. In most countries, strike actions were quickly made illegal,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> as factory owners had far more <a href="/wiki/Power_(social_and_political)" title="Power (social and political)">power</a> than workers. Most Western countries partially legalized striking in the late 19th or early 20th centuries.\n</p><p>Strikes are sometimes used to pressure governments to change policies. Occasionally, strikes destabilize the rule of a particular political party or ruler; in such cases, strikes are often part of a broader social movement taking the form of a campaign of <a href="/wiki/Civil_resistance" title="Civil resistance">civil resistance</a>. Notable examples are the 1980 <a href="/wiki/Gda%C5%84sk_Shipyard" title="Gda\xc5\x84sk Shipyard">Gda\xc5\x84sk Shipyard</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/1981_warning_strike_in_Poland" title="1981 warning strike in Poland">1981 Warning Strike</a>, led by <a href="/wiki/Lech_Wa%C5%82%C4%99sa" title="Lech Wa\xc5\x82\xc4\x99sa">Lech Wa\xc5\x82\xc4\x99sa</a>. These strikes were significant in the long campaign of civil resistance for political change in <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a>, and were an important mobilizing effort that contributed to <a href="/wiki/Revolutions_of_1989" title="Revolutions of 1989">the fall of the Iron Curtain</a> and the end of communist party rule in eastern Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc" role="navigation" aria-labelledby="mw-toc-heading"><input type="checkbox" role="button" title="List of strikes">List of strikes</a></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Strike_action&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The use of the English word "strike" to describe a work protest was first seen in 1768, when sailors, in support of demonstrations in <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>, "struck" or removed the <a href="/wiki/Topgallant_sail" title="Topgallant sail">topgallant sails</a> of merchant ships at port, thus crippling the ships.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Official publications have typically used the more neutral words "work stoppage" or "industrial dispute".\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pre-industrial_strikes">Pre-industrial strikes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Strike_action&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Pre-industrial strikes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The first historically certain account of strike action was towards the end of the <a href="/wiki/Twentieth_dynasty_of_Egypt" class="mw-redirect" title="Twentieth dynasty of Egypt">20th dynasty</a>, under Pharaoh <a href="/wiki/Ramesses_III" title="Ramesses III">Ramses III</a> in <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egypt" title="Ancient Egypt">ancient Egypt</a> on 14 November in 1152 BC. The artisans of the Royal Necropolis at <a href="/wiki/Deir_el-Medina" title="Deir el-Medina">Deir el-Medina</a> walked off their jobs because they had not been paid.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The Egyptian authorities raised the wages.\n</p><p>The first Jewish source for the idea of a labor strike appears in the <a href="/wiki/Talmud" title="Talmud">Talmud</a>, which describes that the bakers who prepared <a href="/wiki/Showbread" title="Showbread">showbread</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Altar" title="Altar">altar</a> went on strike.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>An early predecessor of the <a href="/wiki/General_strike" title="General strike">general strike</a> may have been the <i><a href="/wiki/Secessio_plebis" title="Secessio plebis">secessio plebis</a></i> in ancient Rome. In <i><a href="/wiki/The_Outline_of_History" title="The Outline of History">The Outline of History</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/H._G._Wells" title="H. G. Wells">H. G. Wells</a> characterized this event as "the general strike of the <a href="/wiki/Plebeian" class="mw-redirect" title="Plebeian">plebeians</a>; the plebeians seem to have invented the strike, which now makes its first appearance in history."<sup id="cite_ref-H.G._Wells_1920,_page_225_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-H.G._Wells_1920,_page_225-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Their first strike occurred because they "saw with indignation their friends, who had often served the state bravely in the legions, thrown into chains and <a href="/wiki/Debt_bondage" title="Debt bondage">reduced to slavery</a> at the demand of <a href="/wiki/Patrician_(ancient_Rome)" title="Patrician (ancient Rome)">patrician</a> creditors".<sup id="cite_ref-H.G._Wells_1920,_page_225_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-H.G._Wells_1920,_page_225-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="During_and_after_the_Industrial_Revolution">During and after the Industrial Revolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Strike_action&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: During and after the Industrial Revolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:%22Der_Streik%22_von_Robert_Koehler.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/%22Der_Streik%22_von_Robert_Koehler.jpg/220px-%22Der_Streik%22_von_Robert_Koehler.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="136" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/%22Der_Streik%22_von_Robert_Koehler.jpg/330px-%22Der_Streik%22_von_Robert_Koehler.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/%22Der_Streik%22_von_Robert_Koehler.jpg/440px-%22Der_Streik%22_von_Robert_Koehler.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4411" data-file-height="2731" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:%22Der_Streik%22_von_Robert_Koehler.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Agitated workers face the factory owner in <i>The Strike</i>, painted by <a href="/wiki/Robert_Koehler" title="Robert Koehler">Robert Koehler</a> in 1886</div></div></div>\n<p>The strike action only became a feature of the political landscape with the onset of the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a>. For the first time in history, large numbers of people were members of the industrial working class; they lived in cities and exchanged their labor for payment. By the 1830s, when the <a href="/wiki/Chartism" title="Chartism">Chartist movement</a> was at its peak in Britain, a true and widespread \'workers consciousness\' was awakening. In 1838, a Statistical Society of London committee "used the first written questionnaire... The committee prepared and printed a list of questions \'designed to elicit the complete and impartial history of strikes.\'" <sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1842 the demands for fairer wages and conditions across many different industries finally exploded into the first modern <a href="/wiki/1842_General_Strike" class="mw-redirect" title="1842 General Strike">general strike</a>. After the second <a href="/wiki/Chartism#1842" title="Chartism">Chartist Petition</a> was presented to Parliament in April 1842 and rejected, the strike began in the coal mines of <a href="/wiki/Staffordshire" title="Staffordshire">Staffordshire</a>, <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>, and soon spread through Britain affecting <a href="/wiki/Factory" title="Factory">factories</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mill_(grinding)" title="Mill (grinding)">mills</a> in Lancashire and <a href="/wiki/Coal_mine" class="mw-redirect" title="Coal mine">coal mines</a> from <a href="/wiki/Dundee" title="Dundee">Dundee</a> to South <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">Cornwall</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Plug_Plot_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Plug_Plot-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Instead of being a spontaneous uprising of the mutinous masses, the strike was politically motivated and was driven by an agenda to win concessions. Probably as much as half of the then industrial work force were on strike at its peak&#160;&#8211;&#32;over 500,000 men.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> The local leadership marshalled a growing working class tradition to politically organize their followers to mount an articulate challenge to the capitalist, political establishment. <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Engels" title="Friedrich Engels">Friedrich Engels</a>, an observer in <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a> at the time, wrote:\n</p>\n<blockquote><p><i>by its numbers, this class has become the most powerful in England, and woe betide the wealthy Englishmen when it becomes conscious of this fact ... The English proletarian is only just becoming aware of his power, and the fruits of this awareness were the disturbances of last summer.</i><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Suurlakko_Tampereella.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/Suurlakko_Tampereella.jpg/220px-Suurlakko_Tampereella.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="161" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/Suurlakko_Tampereella.jpg/330px-Suurlakko_Tampereella.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/Suurlakko_Tampereella.jpg/440px-Suurlakko_Tampereella.jpg 2x" data-file-width="580" data-file-height="425" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Suurlakko_Tampereella.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A <a href="/wiki/General_strike" title="General strike">general strike</a> on November 5, 1905 in <a href="/wiki/Tampere" title="Tampere">Tampere</a>, <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finland</a></div></div></div>\n<p>As the 19th century progressed, strikes became a fixture of industrial relations across the industrialized world, as workers organized themselves to <a href="/wiki/Collective_bargaining" title="Collective bargaining">collectively bargain</a> for better wages and standards with their employers. <a href="/wiki/Karl_Marx" title="Karl Marx">Karl Marx</a> has condemned the theory of <a href="/wiki/Pierre-Joseph_Proudhon" title="Pierre-Joseph Proudhon">Pierre-Joseph Proudhon</a> criminalizing strike action in his work <i><a href="/wiki/The_Poverty_of_Philosophy" title="The Poverty of Philosophy">The Poverty of Philosophy</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Recognition_strikes">Recognition strikes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Strike_action&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Recognition strikes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><span class="anchor" id="Recognition_strike"></span>\nA recognition strike is an industrial strike implemented in order to force a particular employer or industry to recognize a <a href="/wiki/Trade_union" title="Trade union">trade union</a> as the legitimate <a href="/wiki/Collective_bargaining" title="Collective bargaining">collective bargaining</a> agent for a company\'s workers.<sup id="cite_ref-uslegal_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-uslegal-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>\n<sup id="cite_ref-incessant_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-incessant-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-manager_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-manager-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>  In 1949, their use in the United States was described as "a weapon used with varying results by labor for the last forty years or more".  One example cited was the successful formation of the <a href="/wiki/United_Auto_Workers" title="United Auto Workers">United Auto Workers</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-hein_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hein-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>  They were more common prior to the advent of modern American <a href="/wiki/Labor_law" class="mw-redirect" title="Labor law">labor law</a> (including the <a href="/wiki/National_Labor_Relations_Act" class="mw-redirect" title="National Labor Relations Act">National Labor Relations Act</a>), which introduced processes legally compelling an employer to recognize the legitimacy of properly certified unions.<sup id="cite_ref-hein_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hein-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-uslegal_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-uslegal-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>  Two examples include the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Steel_recognition_strike_of_1901" title="U.S. Steel recognition strike of 1901">U.S. Steel recognition strike of 1901</a>, and the subsequent <a href="/wiki/Coal_strike_of_1902" title="Coal strike of 1902">coal strike of 1902</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-anthracite_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-anthracite-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>  A 1936 study of strikes in the United States indicated that about one third of the total number of strikes between 1927 and 1928, and over 40 percent in 1929, were due to "demands for union recognition, closed shop, and protest against union discrimination and violation of union agreements".<sup id="cite_ref-peterson_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-peterson-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>  A 1988 study of strike activity and unionization in non-union municipal <a href="/wiki/Police_departments" class="mw-redirect" title="Police departments">police departments</a> between 1972 and 1978 found that recognition strikes were carried out "primarily where bargaining laws [provided] little or no protection of bargaining rights."<sup id="cite_ref-police_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-police-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1937 there were 4,740 strikes in the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> This was the greatest strike wave in <a href="/wiki/Labor_history_of_the_United_States" title="Labor history of the United States">American labor history</a>. The number of major strikes and lockouts in the U.S. fell by 97% from 381 in 1970 to 187 in 1980 to only 11 in 2010. Companies countered the threat of a strike by threatening to close or move a plant.<sup id="cite_ref-census_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-census-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/International_Covenant_on_Economic,_Social_and_Cultural_Rights" title="International Covenant on Economic, Social and Cultural Rights">International Covenant on Economic, Social and Cultural Rights</a>, adopted in 1967, ensures the right to strike in Article 8. The <a href="/wiki/European_Social_Charter" title="European Social Charter">European Social Charter</a>, adopted in 1961, also ensures the right to strike in Article 6.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Farah_strike" title="Farah strike">The Farah Strike</a>, 1972\xe2\x80\x931974, labeled the "strike of the century," was organized and led by Mexican American women predominantly in El Paso, Texas.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Frequency_and_duration">Frequency and duration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Strike_action&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Frequency and duration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ladies_tailors_strikers.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Ladies_tailors_strikers.jpg/220px-Ladies_tailors_strikers.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="143" class="thumbimage" class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ladies_tailors_strikers.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Female tailors on strike, New York City, February 1910.</div></div></div>\n<p>Strikes are rare, in part because many workers are not covered by a <a href="/wiki/Collective_bargaining_agreement" class="mw-redirect" title="Collective bargaining agreement">collective bargaining agreement</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Kennan_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kennan-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> Strikes that do occur are generally fairly short in duration.<sup id="cite_ref-Kennan_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kennan-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> Labor economist John Kennan notes:\n</p>\n<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r996844942">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>In Britain in 1926 (the year of <a href="/wiki/1926_United_Kingdom_general_strike" title="1926 United Kingdom general strike">the general strike</a>) about 9 workdays per worker were lost due to strikes. In 1979, the loss due to strikes was a little more than one day per worker. These are the extreme cases. In the 79 years following 1926, the number of workdays lost in Britain was less than 2 hours per year per worker. In the U.S., idleness due to strikes never exceeded one half of one percent of total working days in any year during the period 1948-2005; the average loss was 0.1% per year. Similarly, in Canada over the period 1980-2005, the annual number of work days lost due to strikes never exceeded one day per worker; on average over this period lost worktime due to strikes was about one-third of a day per worker. Although the data are not readily available for a broad sample of developed countries, the pattern described above seems quite general: days lost due to strikes amount to only a fraction of a day per worker per annum, on average, exceeding one day only in a few exceptional years.<sup id="cite_ref-Kennan_24-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kennan-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote>\n<p>Since the 1990s, strike actions have generally further declined, a phenomenon that might be attributable to lower information costs (and thus more readily available access to information on <a href="/wiki/Economic_rent" title="Economic rent">economic rents</a>) made possible by computerization.<sup id="cite_ref-Kennan_24-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kennan-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> In the United States, the number of workers involved in major work stoppages (including strikes and, less commonly, lockouts) that involved at least a thousand works for at least one full shift generally declined from 1973 to 2017 (coinciding with a general decrease in overall union membership), before substantially increasing in 2018 and 2019.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Variations">Variations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Strike_action&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Variations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Unison_strike_rally_Oxford_20060328.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/Unison_strike_rally_Oxford_20060328.jpg/250px-Unison_strike_rally_Oxford_20060328.jpg" decoding="async" width="250" <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Unison_strike_rally_Oxford_20060328.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A rally of the trade union <a href="/wiki/UNISON" (Estonia)">Ministry of Education</a> building on 7 March 2012.</div></div></div>\n<p>Most strikes are undertaken by <a href="/wiki/Trade_union" title="Trade union">labor unions</a> during <a href="/wiki/Collective_bargaining" title="Collective bargaining">collective bargaining</a> as a last resort. The object of collective bargaining is for the employer and the union to come to an agreement over wages, benefits, and working conditions. A <a href="/wiki/Collective_bargaining_agreement" class="mw-redirect" title="Collective bargaining agreement">collective bargaining agreement</a> may include a clause (a contractual "no-strike clause") which prohibits the union from striking during the term of the agreement.<sup id="cite_ref-FeldackerHayes_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FeldackerHayes-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> Under U.S. labor law, a strike in violation of a no-strike clause is not a <a href="/wiki/Protected_concerted_activity" title="Protected concerted activity">protected concerted activity</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FeldackerHayes_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FeldackerHayes-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> The scope of a no-strike clause varies; generally, the U.S. courts and <a href="/wiki/National_Labor_Relations_Board" title="National Labor Relations Board">National Labor Relations Board</a> have determined that a collective bargaining agreement\'s no-strike clause has the same scope as the agreement\'s arbitration clauses, such that "the union cannot strike over an arbitrable issue."<sup id="cite_ref-FeldackerHayes_26-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FeldackerHayes-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> The U.S. Supreme Court held in <i>Jacksonville Bulk Terminals Inc. v. International Longshoremen\'s Association</i> (1982), a case involving the <a href="/wiki/International_Longshoremen%27s_Association" title="International Longshoremen&#39;s Association">International Longshoremen\'s Association</a> refusing to work with goods for export to the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> in protest against its <a href="/wiki/Soviet%E2%80%93Afghan_War" title="Soviet\xe2\x80\x93Afghan War">invasion of Afghanistan</a>, that a no-strike clause does not bar unions from refusing to work as a political protest (since that is not an "arbitrable" issue), although such activity may lead to damages for a <a href="/wiki/Secondary_boycott" class="mw-redirect" title="Secondary boycott">secondary boycott</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FeldackerHayes_26-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FeldackerHayes-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> Whether a no-strike clause applies to <a href="/wiki/Sympathy_strikes" class="mw-redirect" title="Sympathy strikes">sympathy strikes</a> depends on the context.<sup id="cite_ref-FeldackerHayes_26-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FeldackerHayes-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> Some in the labor movement consider no-strike clauses to be an unnecessary detriment to unions in the collective bargaining process.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Occasionally, workers decide to strike without the sanction of a labor union, either because the union refuses to endorse such a tactic, or because the workers concerned are non-unionized. Such strikes are often described as <i>unofficial</i>. Strikes without formal union authorization are also known as <a href="/wiki/Wildcat_strike_action" title="Wildcat strike action">wildcat strikes</a>.\n</p><p>In many countries, wildcat strikes do not enjoy the same legal protections as recognized union strikes, and may result in penalties for the union members who participate or their union. The same often applies in the case of strikes conducted without an official ballot of the union membership, as is required in some countries such as the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>.\n</p><p>A strike may consist of workers refusing to attend work or <a href="/wiki/Picketing_(protest)" class="mw-redirect" title="Picketing (protest)">picketing</a> outside the workplace to prevent or dissuade people from working in their place or conducting business with their employer. Less frequently workers may occupy the workplace, but refuse either to do their jobs or to leave. This is known as a <a href="/wiki/Sit-down_strike" class="mw-redirect" title="Sit-down strike">sit-down strike</a>. A similar tactic is the <a href="/wiki/Work-in" title="Work-in">work-in</a>, where employees occupy the workplace but still continue work, often without pay, which attempts to show they are still useful, or that <a href="/wiki/Worker_self-management" class="mw-redirect" title="Worker self-management">worker self-management</a> can be successful. For instance, this occurred with factory occupations in the <i><a href="/wiki/Biennio_Rosso" title="Biennio Rosso">Biennio Rosso</a></i> strikes&#160;&#8211;&#32;the "two red years" of Italy from 1919 to 1920.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>Another unconventional tactic is <a href="/wiki/Work-to-rule" title="Work-to-rule">work-to-rule</a> (also known as an <i>Italian strike</i>, in <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">Italian</a>: <i lang="it">Sciopero bianco</i>), in which workers perform their tasks exactly as they are required to but no better. For example, workers might follow all safety regulations in such a way that it impedes their productivity or they might refuse to work <a href="/wiki/Overtime" title="Overtime">overtime</a>. Such strikes may in some cases be a form of "partial strike" or "slowdown".\n</p><p>During the development boom of the 1970s in Australia, the <a href="/wiki/Green_ban" title="Green ban">Green ban</a> was developed by certain unions described by some as more socially conscious. This is a form of strike action taken by a trade union or other organized labor group for <a href="/wiki/Environmentalism" title="Environmentalism">environmentalist</a> or conservationist purposes. This developed from the black ban, strike action taken against a particular job or employer in order to protect the economic interests of the strikers.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/United_States_labor_law" title="United States labor law">United States labor law</a> also draws a distinction, in the case of private sector employers covered by the <a href="/wiki/National_Labor_Relations_Act" class="mw-redirect" title="National Labor Relations Act">National Labor Relations Act</a>, between "economic" and "unfair labor practice" strikes. An employer may not fire, but may permanently replace, workers who engage in a strike over economic issues. On the other hand, employers who commit <a href="/wiki/Unfair_labor_practice" title="Unfair labor practice">unfair labor practices</a> (ULPs) may not replace employees who strike over them, and must fire any strikebreakers they have hired as replacements in order to reinstate the striking workers.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Battle_strike_1934.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Battle_strike_1934.jpg/300px-Battle_strike_1934.jpg" decoding="async" width="300" height="239" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Battle_strike_1934.jpg/450px-Battle_strike_1934.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Battle_strike_1934.jpg/600px-Battle_strike_1934.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1386" data-file-height="1106" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Battle_strike_1934.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Teamsters" class="mw-redirect" title="Teamsters">Teamsters</a>, wielding pipes, clash with <a href="/wiki/Riot_police" title="Riot police">armed police</a> in the streets of <a href="/wiki/Minneapolis" title="Minneapolis">Minneapolis</a> during a <a href="/wiki/Minneapolis_Teamsters_Strike_of_1934" class="mw-redirect" title="Minneapolis Teamsters Strike of 1934">1934 strike</a>.</div></div></div>\n<p>Strikes may be specific to a particular workplace, employer, or unit within a workplace, or they may encompass an entire industry, or every worker within a city or country. Strikes that involve all workers, or a number of large and important groups of workers, in a particular community or region are known as <a href="/wiki/General_strike" title="General strike">general strikes</a>. Under some circumstances, strikes may take place in order to put pressure on the State or other authorities or may be a response to unsafe conditions in the workplace.\n</p><p>A <a href="/wiki/Sympathy_strike" class="mw-redirect" title="Sympathy strike">sympathy strike</a> is, in a way, a small scale version of a general strike in which one group of workers refuses to cross a picket line established by another as a means of supporting the striking workers. Sympathy strikes, once the norm in the construction industry in the United States, have been made much more difficult to conduct due to decisions of the <a href="/wiki/National_Labor_Relations_Board" title="National Labor Relations Board">National Labor Relations Board</a> permitting employers to establish separate or "reserved" gates for particular trades, making it an unlawful <a href="/wiki/Secondary_boycott" class="mw-redirect" title="Secondary boycott">secondary boycott</a> for a union to establish a picket line at any gate other than the one reserved for the employer it is picketing. Sympathy strikes may be undertaken by a union as an orgition or by individual union members choosing not to cross a picket line.\n</p><p>A <a href="/wiki/Jurisdictional_strike" title="Jurisdictional strike">jurisdictional strike</a> in <a href="/wiki/United_States_labor_law" title="United States labor law">United States labor law</a> refers to a concerted refusal to work undertaken by a union to assert its members\xe2\x80\x99 right to particular job assignments and to protest the assignment of disputed work to members of another union or to unorganized workers.\n</p><p>A <a href="/wiki/Student_strike" class="mw-redirect" title="Student strike">student strike</a> has the students (sometimes supported by faculty) not attending schools. In some cases, the strike is intended to draw media attention to the institution so that the grievances that are causing the students to "strike" can be aired before the public; this usually damages the institution\'s (or government\'s) public image. In other cases, especially in government-supported institutions, the student strike can cause a budgetary imbalance and have actual economic repercussions for the institution.\n</p><p>A <a href="/wiki/Hunger_strike" title="Hunger strike">hunger strike</a> is a deliberate refusal to eat. Hunger strikes are often used in prisons as a form of political protest. Like student strikes, a hunger strike aims to worsen the public image of the target.\n</p><p>A "sickout", or (especially by uniformed police officers) "blue flu", is a type of strike action in which the strikers <a href="/wiki/Malingering" title="Malingering">call in sick</a>. This is used in cases where laws prohibit certain employees from declaring a strike. Police, firefighters, air traffic controllers, and teachers in some <a href="/wiki/U.S._states" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. states">U.S. states</a> are among the groups commonly barred from striking usually by state and federal laws meant to ensure the safety or security of the general public.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Newspaper" title="Newspaper">Newspaper</a> writers may withhold their names from their stories as a way to protest actions of their employer.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Activists may form "<span id="flying_squad">flying squad</span>" groups for strikes or other actions to disrupt the workplace or another aspect of capitalism: supporting other strikers or unemployed workers, participating in protests against globalization, or opposing abusive landlords.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legal_prohibitions">Legal prohibitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Strike_action&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Legal prohibitions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Canada">Canada</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Strike_action&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Canada">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>On 30 January 2015, the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_Canada" title="Supreme Court of Canada">Supreme Court of Canada</a> ruled that there is a constitutional right to strike.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> In this 5\xe2\x80\x932 majority decision, <a href="/wiki/Rosalie_Abella" title="Rosalie Abella">Justice Rosalie Abella</a> ruled that "[a]long with their right to associate, speak through a bargaining representative of their choice, and bargain collectively with their employer through that representative, the right of employees to strike is vital to protecting the meaningful process of collective bargaining..." [paragraph 24]. This decision adopted the dissent by <a href="/wiki/Brian_Dickson" title="Brian Dickson">Chief Justice Brian Dickson</a> in a 1987 Supreme Court ruling on a <a href="/wiki/Reference_case" class="mw-redirect" title="Reference case">reference case</a> brought by the province of <a href="/wiki/Alberta" title="Alberta">Alberta</a>. The exact scope of this right to strike remains unclear.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> Prior to this Supreme Court decision, the federal and provincial governments had the ability to introduce "back to work legislation", a <a href="/wiki/Special_law" title="Special law">special law</a> that blocks the strike action (or a lockout) from happening or continuing. Canadian governments could also have imposed <a href="/wiki/Binding_arbitration" class="mw-redirect" title="Binding arbitration">binding arbitration</a> or a new contract on the disputing parties. Back to work legislation was first used in 1950 during a railway strike, and as of 2012 had been used 33 times by the federal government for those parts of the economy that are regulated federally (grain handling, rail and air travel, and the postal service), and in more cases provincially. In addition, certain parts of the economy can be proclaimed "<a href="/wiki/Essential_services" title="Essential services">essential services</a>" in which case all strikes are illegal.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Examples include when the government of Canada passed back to work legislation during the 2011 <a href="/wiki/Canada_Post" title="Canada Post">Canada Post</a> lockout and the 2012 <a href="/wiki/CP_Rail" class="mw-redirect" title="CP Rail">CP Rail</a> strike, thus effectively ending the strikes. In 2016, the government\'s use of back to work legislation during the 2011 Canada Post lockout was ruled unconstitutional, with the judge specifically referencing the Supreme Court of Canada\'s 2015 decision <i>Saskatchewan Federation of Labour v Saskatchewan</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="618" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Strajk_sierpniowy_w_Stoczni_Gda%C5%84skiej_im._Lenina_09.jpg" class="internal" href="/wiki/Marxism%E2%80%93Leninism" title="Marxism\xe2\x80\x93Leninism">Marxist\xe2\x80\x93Leninist</a> states, such as the <a href="/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China" class="mw-redirect" title="People&#39;s Republic of China">People\'s Republic of China</a>, striking was illegal and viewed as <a href="/wiki/Counter-revolutionary" title="Counter-revolutionary">counter-revolutionary</a>. In 1976, China signed the <a href="/wiki/International_Covenant_on_Economic,_Social_and_Cultural_Rights" title="International Covenant on Economic, Social and Cultural Rights">International Covenant on Economic, Social and Cultural Rights</a>, which guaranteed the right to unions and striking, but Chinese officials declared that they had no interest in allowing these liberties.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> (In June 2008, however, the municipal government in <a href="/wiki/Shenzhen" title="Shenzhen">Shenzhen</a> in southern China introduced draft labor regulations, which labor rights advocacy groups say would, if implemented, virtually restore Chinese workers\' right to strike.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>)\n</p><p>In the Soviet Union, strikes occurred throughout the existence of the USSR, most notably in the 1930s. A law "on social dialogue and continuity of public service in regular terrestrial transports of passengers" was adopted on 12 August 2007, and it took effect on 1 January 2008.\n</p><p>This law, among other measures, forces certain categories of public transport workers (such as train and bus drivers) to declare to their employer 48 hours in advance if they intend to go on strike. Should they go on strike without having declared their intention to do so beforehand, they leave themselves open to sanctions.\n</p><p>Unions oppose this law, arguing these 48 hours are used not only to pressure the workers but also to keep files on the more militant workers, who will more easily be undermined in their careers by the employers. They also argue this law prevents the more hesitant workers from making the decision to join the strike the day before, once they have been convinced to do so by their colleagues and more particularly the union militants, who maximize their efforts in building the strike (by handing out leaflets, organizing meetings, discussing the demands with their colleagues) in the last few days preceding the strike. This law makes it also more difficult for the strike to spread rapidly to other workers, as they are required to wait at least 48 hours before joining the strike.\n</p><p>This law also makes it easier for the employers to organize the production as it may use its human resources more effectively, knowing beforehand who is going to be at work and not, thus undermining, albeit not very much, the effects of the strike.\n</p><p>However, this law has not had much effect as strikes in public transport still occur in France, and at times the workers refuse to comply by the rules of this law. The public transport industry&#160;&#8211;&#32;public or privately owned&#160;&#8211;&#32;remains very militant in France and keen on taking strike action when their interests are threatened by the employers or the government.\n</p><p>The public transport workers in France, in particular the "Cheminots" (employees of the national French railway company) are often seen as the most radical "vanguard" of the French working class. href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Jurisprudence_and_philosophy">Jurisprudence and philosophy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Strike_action&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Jurisprudence and philosophy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Strike actions have also been discussed from the perspective of <a href="/wiki/Jurisprudence" title="Jurisprudence">jurisprudence</a> and philosophy, with issues being raised such as whether people have a right to strike, the interaction of strikes with other rights, civil order, coercion, justice and the interplay between striking and contracts.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Strikebreakers">Strikebreakers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Strike_action&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Strikebreakers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Strikebreaker" title="Strikebreaker">Strikebreaker</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:New_York_garbage_cart_being_stoned.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/New_York_garbage_cart_being_stoned.jpg/250px-New_York_garbage_cart_being_stoned.jpg" decoding="async" width="250" height="171" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/New_York_garbage_cart_being_stoned.jpg/375px-New_York_garbage_cart_being_stoned.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/New_York_garbage_cart_being_stoned.jpg/500px-New_York_garbage_cart_being_stoned.jpg 2x" data-file-width="591" data-file-height="404" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:New_York_garbage_cart_being_stoned.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Strikebreaking driver and cart being stoned during sanitation worker strike. <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>, 1911.</div></div></div>\n<p>A <i>strikebreaker</i> (sometimes derogatorily called a <i>scab</i>, <i>blackleg</i>, or <i>knobstick</i>) is a person who works despite an ongoing strike. Strikebreakers are usually individuals who are not employed by the company prior to the <a href="/wiki/Trade_union" title="Trade union">trade union</a> dispute, but rather hired after or during the strike to keep the organization running. "Strikebreakers" may also refer to workers (union members or not) who cross <a href="/wiki/Picketing_(protest)" class="mw-redirect" title="Picketing (protest)">picket lines</a> to work.\n</p><p>Irwin, Jones, McGovern (2008)<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources#What_information_to_include" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="A complete citation is needed. (March 2021)">full citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> believe that the term "scab" is part of a larger metaphor involving strikes. They argue that the picket line is symbolic of a wound and those who break its borders to return to work are the scabs who bond that wound. Others have argued that the word is not a part of a larger metaphor but, rather, was an old-fashioned English insult whose meaning narrowed over time.\n</p><p>"Blackleg" is an older word and is found in the 19th-century folk song "<a href="/wiki/Blackleg_Miner" title="Blackleg Miner">Blackleg Miner</a>" which originated in <a href="/wiki/Northumberland" title="Northumberland">Northumberland</a>. The term does not necessarily owe its origins to this tune of unknown origin.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:177px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Chicago_Tribune_1986_strikebreakers.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/Chicago_Tribune_1986_strikebreakers.jpg/175px-Chicago_Tribune_1986_strikebreakers.jpg" decoding="async" width="175" height="264" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/Chicago_Tribune_1986_strikebreakers.jpg/263px-Chicago_Tribune_1986_strikebreakers.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/Chicago_Tribune_1986_strikebreakers.jpg/350px-Chicago_Tribune_1986_strikebreakers.jpg 2x" data-file-width="452" data-file-height="682" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Chicago_Tribune_1986_strikebreakers.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Strike breakers, <i><a href="/wiki/Chicago_Tribune" title="Chicago Tribune">Chicago Tribune</a></i> strike, 1986, <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago</a>, Illinois</div></div></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Union_strikebreaking">Union strikebreaking</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Strike_action&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Union strikebreaking">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The concept of <i>union strikebreaking</i> or <i>union scabbing</i> refers to any circumstance in which union workers themselves cross picket lines to work.\n</p><p>Unionized workers are sometimes required to cross the picket lines established by other unions due to their organizations having signed contracts which include no-strike clauses. The no-strike clause typically requires that members of the union not conduct any strike action for the duration of the contract; such actions are called <i>sympathy</i> or <i>secondary strikes</i>. Members who honor the picket line in spite of the contract frequently face discipline, for their action may be viewed as a violation of provisions of the contract. Therefore, any union conducting a strike action typically seeks to include a provision of amnesty for all who honored the picket line in the agreement that settles the strike.\n</p><p>No-strike clauses may also prevent unionized workers from engaging in solidarity actions for other workers even when no picket line is crossed. For example, striking workers in manufacturing or mining produce a product which must be transported. In a situation where the factory or mine owners have replaced the strikers, unionized transport workers may feel inclined to refuse to haul any product that is produced by strikebreakers, yet their own contract obligates them to do so.\n</p><p>Historically the practice of union strikebreaking has been a contentious issue in the union movement, and a point of contention between adherents of different union philosophies. For example, supporters of <a href="/wiki/Industrial_unionism" title="Industrial unionism">industrial unions</a>, which have sought to organize entire workplaces without regard to individual skills, have criticized <a href="/wiki/Craft_unionism" title="Craft unionism">craft unions</a> for organizing workplaces into separate unions according to skill, a circumstance that makes union strikebreaking more common. Union strikebreaking is not, however, unique to craft unions.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Anti-strike_action">Anti-strike action</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Strike_action&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Anti-strike action">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Most strikes called by unions are somewhat predictable; they typically occur after the contract has expired. However, not all strikes are called by union organizations&#160;&#8211;&#32;some strikes have been called in an effort to pressure employers to recognize unions. Other strikes may be spontaneous actions by working people. Spontaneous strikes are sometimes called "<a href="/wiki/Wildcat_strike" class="mw-redirect" title="Wildcat strike">wildcat strikes</a>"; they were the key fighting point in <a href="/wiki/May_1968_in_France" class="mw-redirect" title="May 1968 in France">May 1968 in France</a>; most commonly, they are responses to serious (often life-threatening) safety hazards in the workplace rather than wage or hour disputes, etc.\n</p><p>Whatever the cause of the strike, employers are generally motivated to take measures to prevent them, mitigate the impact, or to undermine strikes when they do occur.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Giant_inflatable_rat.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Giant_inflatable_rat.jpg/200px-Giant_inflatable_rat.jpg" decoding="async" width="200" height="237" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Giant_inflatable_rat.jpg/300px-Giant_inflatable_rat.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Giant_inflatable_rat.jpg/400px-Giant_inflatable_rat.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1545" data-file-height="1832" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Giant_inflatable_rat.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>To bring public attention, a giant inflatable rat (named \'Scabby\') is used in the U.S. at the site of a labor dispute. The rat represents strike-breaking replacement workers, otherwise known as \'scabs\'.</div></div></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Strike_preparation">Strike preparation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Strike_action&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Strike preparation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Companies which produce products for sale will frequently increase inventories prior to a strike. Salaried employees may be called upon to take the place of strikers, which may entail advance training. If the company has multiple locations, personnel may be redeployed to meet the needs of reduced staff.\n</p><p>Companies may also take out <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Strike_insurance&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Strike insurance (page does not exist)">strike insurance</a></i>, to help offset the losses which a strike would cause.\n</p><p>When established unions commence strike action, some companies may decline entirely to negotiate with the union, and respond to the strike by hiring replacement workers. For strikers, this may be concerning for multiple reasons. For example, they may fear that the strike will be lost. The length of time that the strike may last could cause many workers to cease striking, which would likely cause it to fail. They may also be concerned that they will lose their jobs entirely. Companies that hire strikebreakers typically use these concerns to attempt to convince union members to abandon the strike and cross the union\'s <a href="/wiki/Picketing_(protest)" class="mw-redirect" title="Picketing (protest)">picket line</a>.\n</p><p>Unions faced with a strikebreaking situation may try to inhibit the use of strikebreakers by a variety of methods&#160;&#8211;&#32;establishing picket lines where the strikebreakers enter the workplace; discouraging strike breakers from taking, or from keeping, strikebreaking jobs; raising the cost of hiring strikebreakers for the company; or employing public relations tactics. Companies may respond by increasing security forces and seeking court injunctions.\n</p><p>Examining conditions in the late 1990s, John Logan observed that union busting agencies helped to "transform economic strikes into a virtually suicidal tactic for US unions". Logan further observed, "as strike rates in the United States have plummeted to historic low levels, the demand for strike management firms has also declined."<sup id="cite_ref-Logan_651-675_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Logan_651-675-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In the US, as established in the <a href="/wiki/National_Labor_Relations_Act" class="mw-redirect" title="National Labor Relations Act">National Labor Relations Act</a> there is a legally protected right for private sector employees to strike to gain better wages, benefits, or working conditions and they cannot be fired. Striking for economic reasons (like protesting workplace conditions or supporting a union\'s bargaining demands) allows an employer to hire permanent replacements. The replacement worker can continue in the job and then the striking worker must wait for a vacancy. But if the strike is due to unfair labor practices, the strikers replaced can demand immediate reinstatement when the strike ends. If a collective bargaining agreement is in effect, and it contains a "no-strike clause", a strike during the life of the contract could result in the firing of all striking employees which could result in dissolution of that union. Although this is legal it could be viewed as union busting.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Strike_breaking">Strike breaking</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Strike_action&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Strike breaking">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Some companies negotiate with the union during a strike; other companies may see a strike as an opportunity to eliminate the union. This is sometimes accomplished by the importation of replacement workers, <a href="/wiki/Strikebreaker" title="Strikebreaker">strikebreakers</a> or "scabs". Historically, strike breaking has often coincided with union busting. It was also called \'<a href="/wiki/Blackleg_labour" class="mw-redirect" title="Blackleg labour">black legging</a>\' in the early twentieth century, during the Russian socialist movement.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Union_busting">Union busting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Strike_action&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Union busting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Stanis%C5%82aw_Lentz,_Strajk.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Stanis%C5%82aw_Lentz%2C_Strajk.jpg/200px-Stanis%C5%82aw_Lentz%2C_Strajk.jpg" decoding="async" width="200" height="322" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Stanis%C5%82aw_Lentz%2C_Strajk.jpg/300px-Stanis%C5%82aw_Lentz%2C_Strajk.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Stanis%C5%82aw_Lentz%2C_Strajk.jpg/400px-Stanis%C5%82aw_Lentz%2C_Strajk.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3024" data-file-height="4872" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Stanis%C5%82aw_Lentz,_Strajk.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>Strike</i>, painting by Stanis\xc5\x82aw Lentz</div></div></div>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Union_busting" title="Union busting">Union busting</a></div>\n<p>One method of inhibiting or ending a strike is firing union members who are striking which can result in elimination of the union. Although this has happened, it is rare due to laws regarding firing and "right to strike" having a wide range of differences in the US depending on whether union members are public or private sector. Laws also vary country to country. In the UK, "It is important to understand that there is no right to strike in UK law."<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup> Employees who strike risk dismissal, unless it is an official strike (one called or endorsed by their union) in which case they are protected from unlawful dismissal, and cannot be fired for at least 12 weeks. UK laws regarding work stoppages and strikes are defined within the <a href="/wiki/Employment_Relations_Act_1999" title="Employment Relations Act 1999">Employment Relations Act 1999</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Trade_Union_and_Labour_Relations_(Consolidation)_Act_1992" title="Trade Union and Labour Relations (Consolidation) Act 1992">Trade Union and Labour Relations (Consolidation) Act 1992</a>.\n</p><p>A significant case of mass-dismissals in the UK in 2005 involved the sacking of over 600 Gate Gourmet employees at Heathrow Airport.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup> The sacking prompted a walkout by British Airways ground staff leading to cancelled flights and thousands of delayed passengers. The walkout was illegal under UK law and the T&amp;GWU quickly brought it to an end. A subsequent court case ruled that demonstrations on a grass verge approaching the Gate Gourmet premises were not illegal, but limited the number and made the T&amp;G responsible for their action.<sup id="cite_ref-BBC_GG_54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC_GG-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1962 US President <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy" title="John F. Kennedy">John F. Kennedy</a> issued Executive Order #10988<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> which permitted federal employees to form trade unions but prohibited strikes (codified in 1966 at 5 U.S.C. 7311&#160;&#8211;&#32;Loyalty and Striking). In 1981, after public sector union PATCO (<a href="/wiki/Professional_Air_Traffic_Controllers_Organization_(1968)" title="Professional Air Traffic Controllers Organization (1968)">Professional Air Traffic Controllers Organization</a>) went on strike illegally, President <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Reagan" title="Ronald Reagan">Ronald Reagan</a> fired all of the controllers. His action resulted in the dissolution of the union. PATCO reformed to become the <a href="/wiki/National_Air_Traffic_Controllers_Association" title="National Air Traffic Controllers Association">National Air Traffic Controllers Association</a>.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ob%C4%9Bti_sr%C3%A1%C5%BEky_st%C3%A1vkuj%C3%ADc%C3%ADch_s_vojskem_v_dubnu_1917.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Ob%C4%9Bti_sr%C3%A1%C5%BEky_st%C3%A1vkuj%C3%ADc%C3%ADch_s_vojskem_v_dubnu_1917.jpg/220px-Ob%C4%9Bti_sr%C3%A1%C5%BEky_st%C3%A1vkuj%C3%ADc%C3%ADch_s_vojskem_v_dubnu_1917.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="157" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Ob%C4%9Bti_sr%C3%A1%C5%BEky_st%C3%A1vkuj%C3%ADc%C3%ADch_s_vojskem_v_dubnu_1917.jpg/330px-Ob%C4%9Bti_sr%C3%A1%C5%BEky_st%C3%A1vkuj%C3%ADc%C3%ADch_s_vojskem_v_dubnu_1917.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Ob%C4%9Bti_sr%C3%A1%C5%BEky_st%C3%A1vkuj%C3%ADc%C3%ADch_s_vojskem_v_dubnu_1917.jpg/440px-Ob%C4%9Bti_sr%C3%A1%C5%BEky_st%C3%A1vkuj%C3%ADc%C3%ADch_s_vojskem_v_dubnu_1917.jpg 2x" data-file-width="600" data-file-height="429" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ob%C4%9Bti_sr%C3%A1%C5%BEky_st%C3%A1vkuj%C3%ADc%C3%ADch_s_vojskem_v_dubnu_1917.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Victims of a clash between striking workers and the army in <a href="/wiki/Prost%C4%9Bjov" title="Prost\xc4\x9bjov">Prost\xc4\x9bjov</a>, Austria-Hungary, April 1917</div></div></div>\n<p>In the U.S., as established in the <a href="/wiki/National_Labor_Relations_Act" class="mw-redirect" title="National Labor Relations Act">National Labor Relations Act</a> there is a legally protected right for private sector employees to strike to gain better wages, benefits, or working conditions and they cannot be fired. Striking for economic reasons (i.e., protesting workplace conditions or supporting a union\'s bargaining demands) allows an employer to hire permanent replacements. The replacement worker can continue in the job and then the striking worker must wait for a vacancy. But if the strike is due to <a href="/wiki/Unfair_labor_practice" title="Unfair labor practice">unfair labor practices</a> (ULP), the strikers replaced can demand immediate reinstatement when the strike ends. If a collective bargaining agreement is in effect, and it contains a "no-strike clause", a strike during the life of the contract could result in the firing of all striking employees which could result in dissolution of that union.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lockout">Lockout</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Strike_action&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Lockout">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Another counter to a strike is a <a href="/wiki/Lockout_(industry)" title="Lockout (industry)">lockout</a>, a form of work stoppage in which an employer refuses to allow employees to work. Two of the three employers involved in the Caravan park grocery workers strike of 2003\xe2\x80\x932004 locked out their employees in response to a strike against the third member of the employer bargaining group. Lockouts are, with certain exceptions, lawful under <a href="/wiki/United_States_labor_law" title="United States labor law">United States labor law</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Violence">Violence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Strike_action&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Violence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ramon_Casas_Charge.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/Ramon_Casas_Charge.jpg/220px-Ramon_Casas_Charge.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="140" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/Ramon_Casas_Charge.jpg/330px-Ramon_Casas_Charge.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/Ramon_Casas_Charge.jpg/440px-Ramon_Casas_Charge.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1280" data-file-height="812" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ramon_Casas_Charge.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>The charge</i> by <a href="/wiki/Ramon_Casas" title="Ramon Casas">Ramon Casas</a> (1899)</div></div></div>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Anti-union_violence" title="Anti-union violence">Anti-union violence</a></div>\n<p>Historically, some employers have attempted to break union strikes by force. One of the most famous examples of this occurred during the <a href="/wiki/Homestead_Strike" class="mw-redirect" title="Homestead Strike">Homestead Strike</a> of 1892. Industrialist <a href="/wiki/Henry_Clay_Frick" title="Henry Clay Frick">Henry Clay Frick</a> sent private security agents from the <a href="/wiki/Pinkerton_National_Detective_Agency" class="mw-redirect" title="Pinkerton National Detective Agency">Pinkerton National Detective Agency</a> to break the <a href="/wiki/Amalgamated_Association_of_Iron_and_Steel_Workers" title="Amalgamated Association of Iron and Steel Workers">Amalgamated Association of Iron and Steel Workers</a> strike at a <a href="/wiki/Homestead,_Pennsylvania" title="Homestead, Pennsylvania">Homestead</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania</a> steel mill. Two strikers were killed, twelve wounded, along with two Pinkertons killed and eleven wounded. In the aftermath, Frick was shot in the neck and then stabbed by <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Berkman" title="Alexander Berkman">Alexander Berkman</a>, surviving the attack, while Berkman was sentenced to 22 years in prison.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Films">Films</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
