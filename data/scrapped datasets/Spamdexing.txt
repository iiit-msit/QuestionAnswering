message</a>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p><b>Spamdexing</b> (also known as <b>search engine spam</b>, <b>search engine poisoning</b>, <b><a href="/wiki/Black_hat_hacker" class="mw-redirect" title="Black hat hacker">black-hat</a> <a href="/wiki/Search_engine_optimization" title="Search engine optimization">search engine optimization</a></b> (<b>SEO</b>), <b>search spam</b> or <b>web spam</b>)<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> is the deliberate manipulation of <a href="/wiki/Web_search_engine" class="mw-redirect" title="Web search engine">search engine</a> <a href="/wiki/Index_(search_engine)" class="mw-redirect" title="Index (search engine)">indexes</a>. It involves a number of methods, such as <a href="/wiki/Link_building" title="Link building">link building</a> and repeating unrelated phrases, to manipulate the relevance or prominence of resources indexed, in a manner inconsistent with the purpose of the indexing system.<sup id="cite_ref-wspy_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wspy-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Gyongyi_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gyongyi-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Spamdexing could be considered to be a part of <a href="/wiki/Search_engine_optimization" title="Search engine optimization">search engine optimization</a>, although there are many search engine optimization methods that improve the quality and appearance of the content of web sites and serve content useful to many users.<sup id="cite_ref-Ntoulas_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ntoulas-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> \n</p><p>Search engines use a variety of <a href="/wiki/Algorithm" title="Algorithm">algorithms</a> to determine relevancy ranking. Some of these include determining whether the search term appears in the <a href="/wiki/Body_text" title="Body text">body text</a> or <a href="/wiki/Uniform_Resource_Locator" class="mw-redirect" title="Uniform Resource Locator">URL</a> of a <a href="/wiki/Web_page" title="Web page">web page</a>. Many search engines check for instances of spamdexing and will remove suspect pages from their indexes. Also, search-engine operators can quickly block the results listing from entire websites that use spamdexing, perhaps in response to user complaints of false matches. The rise of spamdexing in the mid-1990s made the leading search engines of the time less useful. Using unethical methods to make websites rank higher in search engine results than they otherwise would is commonly referred to in the SEO (search engine optimization) industry as "black-hat SEO". These methods are more focused on breaking the search-engine-promotion rules and guidelines. In addition to this, the perpetrators run the risk of their websites being severely penalized by the <a href="/wiki/Google_Panda" title="Google Panda">Google Panda</a> and <a href="/wiki/Google_Penguin" title="Google Penguin">Google Penguin</a> search-results ranking algorithms.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Common spamdexing techniques can be classified into two broad classes: <i>content spam</i><sup id="cite_ref-Ntoulas_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ntoulas-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> (or <i>term spam</i>) and <i>link spam</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Gyongyi_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gyongyi-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc" role="navigation" aria-labelledby="mw-toc-heading"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spamdexing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The earliest known reference<sup id="cite_ref-wspy_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wspy-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> to the term <i>spamdexing</i> is by Eric Convey in his article "Porn sneaks way back on Web," <a href="/wiki/The_Boston_Herald" class="mw-redirect" title="The Boston Herald">The Boston Herald</a>, May 22, 1996, where he said:\n</p>\n<blockquote><p>The problem arises when site operators load their Web pages with hundreds of extraneous terms so search engines will list them among legitimate addresses.\nThe process is called "spamdexing," a combination of <a href="/wiki/Spamming" title="Spamming">spamming</a> \xe2\x80\x94 the Internet term for sending users unsolicited information \xe2\x80\x94 and "<a href="/wiki/Web_indexing" title="Web indexing">indexing</a>."<sup id="cite_ref-wspy_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wspy-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Content_spam">Content spam</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spamdexing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Content spam">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>These techniques involve altering the logical view that a search engine has over the page\'s contents. They all aim at variants of the <a href="/wiki/Vector_space_model" title="Vector space model">vector space model</a> for information retrieval on text collections.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Keyword_stuffing">Keyword stuffing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spamdexing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Keyword stuffing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Keyword stuffing involves the calculated placement of keywords within a page to raise the keyword count, variety, and density of the page. This is useful to make a page appear to be relevant for a <a href="/wiki/Web_crawler" title="Web crawler">web crawler</a> in a way that makes it more likely to be found. Example: A promoter of a <a href="/wiki/Ponzi_scheme" title="Ponzi scheme">Ponzi scheme</a> wants to attract web surfers to a site where he advertises his scam. He places hidden text appropriate for a fan page of a popular music group on his page, hoping that the page will be listed as a fan site and receive many visits from music lovers. Older versions of indexing programs simply counted how often a keyword appeared, and used that to determine relevance levels. Most modern search engines have the ability to analyze a page for keyword stuffing and determine whether the frequency is consistent with other sites created specifically to attract search engine traffic. Also, large webpages are truncated, so that massive dictionary lists cannot be indexed on a single webpage.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> (However, spammers can circumvent this webpage-size limitation merely by setting up multiple webpages, either independently or linked to each other.)\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hidden_or_invisible_text">Hidden or invisible text</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spamdexing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Hidden or invisible text">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Unrelated <a href="/wiki/Hidden_text" title="Hidden text">hidden text</a> is disguised by making it the same color as the background, using a tiny font size, or hiding it within <a href="/wiki/HTML" title="HTML">HTML</a> code such as "no frame" sections, <a href="/wiki/Alt_attribute" title="Alt attribute">alt attributes</a>, zero-sized <a href="/wiki/Span_and_div" title="Span and div">DIVs</a>, and "no script" sections. People manually screening red-flagged websites for a search-engine company might temporarily or permanently block an entire website for having invisible text on some of its pages. However, hidden text is not always spamdexing: it can also be used to enhance <a href="/wiki/Web_accessibility" title="Web accessibility">accessibility</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2021)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Meta-tag_stuffing">Meta-tag stuffing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spamdexing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Meta-tag stuffing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>This involves repeating keywords in the <a href="/wiki/Meta_tag" class="mw-redirect" title="Meta tag">meta tags</a>, and using meta keywords that are unrelated to the site\'s content. This tactic has been ineffective since 2005.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Doorway_pages">Doorway pages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spamdexing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Doorway pages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>"Gateway" or <a href="/wiki/Doorway_page" title="Doorway page">doorway pages</a> are low-quality web pages created with very little content, which are instead stuffed with very similar keywords and phrases. They are designed to rank highly within the search results, but serve no purpose to visitors looking for information. A doorway page will generally have "click here to enter" on the page; autoforwarding can also be used for this purpose. In 2006, Google ousted vehicle manufacturer <a href="/wiki/BMW" title="BMW">BMW</a> for using "doorway pages" to the company\'s German site, BMW.de.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Scraper_sites">Scraper sites</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spamdexing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Scraper sites">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Scraper_sites" class="mw-redirect" title="Scraper sites">Scraper sites</a> are created using various programs designed to "scrape" search-engine results pages or other sources of content and create "content" for a website.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> The specific presentation of content on these sites is unique, but is merely an amalgamation of content taken from other sources, often without permission. Such websites are generally full of advertising (such as <a href="/wiki/Pay_per_click" class="mw-redirect" title="Pay per click">pay-per-click</a> ads), or they redirect the user to other sites. It is even feasible for scraper sites to outrank original websites for their own information and organization names.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Article_spinning">Article spinning</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spamdexing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Article spinning">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Article_spinning" title="Article spinning">Article spinning</a> involves rewriting existing articles, as opposed to merely scraping content from other sites, to avoid penalties imposed by search engines for <a href="/wiki/Duplicate_content" title="Duplicate content">duplicate content</a>. This process is undertaken by hired writers<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2021)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> or automated using a <a href="/wiki/Thesaurus" title="Thesaurus">thesaurus</a> database or a <a href="/wiki/Neural_network" title="Neural network">neural network</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Machine_translation">Machine translation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spamdexing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Machine translation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Similarly to <a href="/wiki/Article_spinning" title="Article spinning">article spinning</a>, some sites use <a href="/wiki/Machine_translation" title="Machine translation">machine translation</a> to render their content in several languages, with no human editing, resulting in unintelligible texts that nonetheless continue to be indexed by search engines, thereby attracting traffic.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Link_spam">Link spam</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spamdexing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Link spam">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Link spam\' is defined as links between pages that are present for reasons\nother than merit.<sup id="cite_ref-Davison_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Davison-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Link spam takes advantage of link-based ranking algorithms, which gives <a href="/wiki/Websites" class="mw-redirect" title="Websites">websites</a> higher rankings the more other highly ranked websites link to it. These techniques also aim at influencing other link-based ranking techniques such as the <a href="/wiki/HITS_algorithm" title="HITS algorithm">HITS algorithm</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Link_farms">Link farms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spamdexing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Link farms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Link_farm" title="Link farm">Link farm</a></div>\n<p>Link farms are tightly-knit networks of websites that link to each other for the sole purpose of gaming the search engine ranking algorithms. These are also known facetiously as <i>mutual admiration societies</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Use of links farms has been greatly reduced after Google launched the first Panda Update in February 2011, which introduced significant improvements in its spam-detection algorithm.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Private_blog_networks">Private blog networks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spamdexing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Private blog networks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Blog networks (PBNs) are a group of authoritative websites used as a source of contextual links that point to the owner\'s main website to achieve higher search engine ranking. Owners of PBN websites use expired domains or <a href="/wiki/Domain_name_auction" title="Domain name auction">auction domains</a> that have <a href="/wiki/Backlink" title="Backlink">backlinks</a> from high-authority websites. Google targeted and penalized PBN users on several occasions with several massive deindexing campaigns since 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hidden_links">Hidden links</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spamdexing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Hidden links">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Putting <a href="/wiki/Hyperlink" title="Hyperlink">hyperlinks</a> where visitors will not see them is used to increase <a href="/wiki/Link_popularity" class="mw-redirect" title="Link popularity">link popularity</a>. Highlighted link text can help rank a webpage higher for matching that phrase.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sybil_attack">Sybil attack</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spamdexing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Sybil attack">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>A <a href="/wiki/Sybil_attack" title="Sybil attack">Sybil attack</a> is the forging of multiple identities for malicious intent, named after the famous multiple personality disorder patient "<a href="/wiki/Sybil_(Schreiber_book)" title="Sybil (Schreiber book)">Sybil</a>"<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2021)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>. A spammer may create multiple web sites at different <a href="/wiki/Domain_name" title="Domain name">domain names</a> that all link to each other, such as fake blogs (known as <a href="/wiki/Spam_blogs" class="mw-redirect" title="Spam blogs">spam blogs</a>).\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Spam_blogs">Spam blogs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spamdexing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Spam blogs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Spam_blog" title="Spam blog">Spam blog</a></div>\n<p>Spam blogs are blogs created solely for commercial promotion and the passage of link authority to target sites. Often these "splogs" are designed in a misleading manner that will give the effect of a legitimate website but upon close inspection will often be written using spinning software or be very poorly written with barely readable content. They are similar in nature to link farms.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2021)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Guest_blog_spam">Guest blog spam</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spamdexing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Guest blog spam">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Guest blog spam is the process of placing guest blogs on websites for the sole purpose of gaining a link to another website or websites. Unfortunately, these are often confused with legitimate forms of guest blogging with other motives than placing links. This technique was made famous by <a href="/wiki/Matt_Cutts" title="Matt Cutts">Matt Cutts</a>, who publicly declared "war" against this form of link spam.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Buying_expired_domains">Buying expired domains</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spamdexing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Buying expired domains">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Domaining" class="mw-redirect" title="Domaining">Domaining</a></div>\n<p>Some link spammers utilize expired domain crawler software or monitor DNS records for domains that will expire soon, then buy them when they expire and replace the pages with links to their pages. However, it is possible but not confirmed that Google resets the link data on expired domains.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> To maintain all previous Google ranking data for the domain, it is advisable that a buyer grab the domain before it is "dropped". \n</p><p>Some of these techniques may be applied for creating a <a href="/wiki/Google_bomb" class="mw-redirect" title="Google bomb">Google bomb</a> \xe2\x80\x94 that is, to cooperate with other users to boost the ranking of a particular page for a particular query.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cookie_stuffing">Cookie stuffing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spamdexing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Cookie stuffing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Cookie_stuffing" title="Cookie stuffing">Cookie stuffing</a> involves placing an <a href="/wiki/Affiliate_marketing" title="Affiliate marketing">affiliate</a> tracking cookie on a website visitor\'s computer without their knowledge, which will then generate revenue for the person doing the cookie stuffing. This not only generates fraudulent affiliate sales, but also has the potential to overwrite other affiliates\' cookies, essentially stealing their legitimately earned commissions.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Using_world-writable_pages">Using world-writable pages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spamdexing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Using world-writable pages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Forum_spam" title="Forum spam">Forum spam</a></div>\n<p>Web sites that can be edited by users can be used by spamdexers to insert links to spam sites if the appropriate anti-spam measures are not taken.\n</p><p>Automated <a href="/wiki/Spambot" title="Spambot">spambots</a> can rapidly make the user-editable portion of a site unusable.\nProgrammers have developed a variety of automated <a href="/wiki/Forum_spam#Spam_prevention" title="Forum spam">spam prevention techniques</a> to block or at least slow down spambots.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Spam_in_blogs">Spam in blogs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spamdexing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Spam in blogs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Spam_in_blogs" title="Spam in blogs">Spam in blogs</a></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Spam_in_blogs" title="Spam in blogs">Spam in blogs</a> is the placing or solicitation of links randomly on other sites, placing a desired keyword into the hyperlinked text of the inbound link. Guest books, forums, blogs, and any site that accepts visitors\' comments are particular targets and are often victims of drive-by spamming where automated software creates nonsense posts with links that are usually irrelevant and unwanted.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Comment_spam">Comment spam</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spamdexing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Comment spam">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Comment spam is a form of link spam that has arisen in web pages that allow dynamic user editing such as <a href="/wiki/Wikis" class="mw-redirect" title="Wikis">wikis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Blogs" class="mw-redirect" title="Blogs">blogs</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Guestbooks" class="mw-redirect" title="Guestbooks">guestbooks</a>. It can be problematic because <a href="/wiki/Software_agent" title="Software agent">agents</a> can be written that automatically randomly select a user edited web page, such as a Wikipedia article, and add spamming links.<sup id="cite_ref-Mishne_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mishne-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Wiki_spam">Wiki spam</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spamdexing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Wiki spam">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Wiki spam is a form of link spam on wiki pages. The spammer uses the open editability of wiki systems to place links from the wiki site to the spam site.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Referrer_log_spamming">Referrer log spamming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spamdexing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Referrer log spamming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Referrer_spam" title="Referrer spam">Referrer spam</a> takes place when a spam perpetrator or facilitator accesses a <a href="/wiki/Web_page" title="Web page">web page</a> (the <i>referee</i>), by following a link from another web page (the <i><a href="/wiki/Referrer" class="mw-redirect" title="Referrer">referrer</a></i>), so that the referee is given the address of the referrer by the person\'s Internet browser. Some <a href="/wiki/Website" title="Website">websites</a> have a referrer log which shows which pages link to that site. By having a <a href="/wiki/Internet_bot" title="Internet bot">robot</a> randomly access many sites enough times, with a message or specific address given as the referrer, that message or Internet address then appears in the referrer log of those sites that have referrer logs. Since some <a href="/wiki/Web_search_engine" class="mw-redirect" title="Web search engine">Web search engines</a> base the importance of sites on the number of different sites linking to them, referrer-log spam may increase the search engine rankings of the spammer\'s sites.  Also, site administrators who notice the referrer log entries in their logs may follow the link back to the spammer\'s referrer page.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Countermeasures">Countermeasures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spamdexing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Countermeasures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Because of the large amount of spam posted to user-editable webpages, Google proposed a nofollow tag that could be embedded with links. A link-based search engine, such as Google\'s <a href="/wiki/PageRank" title="PageRank">PageRank</a> system, will not use the link to increase the score of the linked website if the link carries a nofollow tag. This ensures that spamming links to user-editable websites will not raise the sites ranking with search engines. Nofollow is used by several major websites, including <a href="/wiki/WordPress" title="WordPress">Wordpress</a>, <a href="/wiki/Blogger_(service)" title="Blogger (service)">Blogger</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia" title="Wikipedia">Wikipedia</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_types">Other types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spamdexing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Other types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mirror_websites">Mirror websites</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spamdexing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Mirror websites">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>A <a href="/wiki/Mirror_website" class="mw-redirect" title="Mirror website">mirror site</a> is the hosting of multiple websites with conceptually similar content but using different <a href="/wiki/Uniform_Resource_Locator" class="mw-redirect" title="Uniform Resource Locator">URLs</a>. Some search engines give a higher rank to results where the keyword searched for appears in the URL.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="URL_redirection">URL redirection</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spamdexing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: URL redirection">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><a href="/wiki/URL_redirection" title="URL redirection">URL redirection</a> is the taking of the user to another page without his or her intervention, <i>e.g.</i>, using <a href="/wiki/META_refresh" class="mw-redirect" title="META refresh">META refresh</a> tags, <a href="/wiki/Adobe_Flash" title="Adobe Flash">Flash</a>,  <a href="/wiki/JavaScript" title="JavaScript">JavaScript</a>, <a href="/wiki/Java_(programming_language)" title="Java (programming language)">Java</a> or <a href="/wiki/Server_side_redirect" class="mw-redirect" title="Server side redirect">Server side redirects</a>. However, <a href="/wiki/HTTP_301" title="HTTP 301">301 Redirect</a>, or permanent redirect, is not considered as a malicious behavior.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cloaking">Cloaking</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spamdexing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Cloaking">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Cloaking" title="Cloaking">Cloaking</a> refers to any of several means to serve a page to the search-engine <a href="/wiki/Web_crawler" title="Web crawler">spider</a> that is different from that seen by human users. It can be an attempt to mislead search engines regarding the content on a particular web site. Cloaking, however, can also be used to ethically increase accessibility of a site to users with disabilities or provide human users with content that search engines aren\'t able to process or parse. It is also used to deliver content based on a user\'s location; Google itself uses <a href="/wiki/IP_delivery" class="mw-redirect" title="IP delivery">IP delivery</a>, a form of cloaking, to deliver results. Another form of cloaking is <i>code swapping</i>, <i>i.e.</i>, optimizing a page for top ranking and then swapping another page in its place once a top ranking is achieved. Google refers to these type of redirects as <i>Sneaky Redirects</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Spamdexed pages are sometimes eliminated from search results by the search engine.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Page_omission_by_user">Page omission by user</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spamdexing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Page omission by user">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Users can employ search operators for filtering. For Google, a keyword preceded by "-" (minus) will omit sites that contains the keyword in their pages or in the URL of the pages from search result. As an example, the search "-&lt;unwanted site&gt;" will eliminate sites that contains word "&lt;unwanted site&gt;" in their pages and the pages whose URL contains "&lt;unwanted site&gt;".\n</p><p>Users could also use the <a href="/wiki/Google_Chrome" title="Google Chrome">Google Chrome</a> extension "Personal Blocklist (by Google)", launched by Google in 2011 as part of countermeasures against <a href="/wiki/Content_farm" title="Content farm">content farming</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Via the extension, users could block a specific page, or set of pages from appearing in their search results.
