de la Navigation a\xc3\xa9rienne</b></i> (French; <abbr title="literal translation">lit.</abbr><span style="white-space:nowrap">&#8201;</span>\'Belgian Limited Company for the Exploitation of Aerial Navigation\'), better known by the acronym <b>Sabena</b> or <b>SABENA</b>, was the national airline of Belgium from 1923 to 2001, with its base at <a href="/wiki/Brussels_Airport" title="Brussels Airport">Brussels National Airport</a>. After its <a href="/wiki/Bankruptcy" title="Bankruptcy">bankruptcy</a> in 2001, the newly formed <a href="/wiki/SN_Brussels_Airlines" title="SN Brussels Airlines">SN Brussels Airlines</a> took over part of Sabena\'s assets in February 2002, which became <a href="/wiki/Brussels_Airlines" title="Brussels Airlines">Brussels Airlines</a> after a merger with <a href="/wiki/Virgin_Express" title="Virgin Express">Virgin Express</a> in March 2007. The airline\'s corporate headquarters were located in the Sabena House on the grounds of <a href="/wiki/Brussels_Airport" title="Brussels Airport">Brussels Airport</a> in <a href="/wiki/Zaventem" title="Zaventem">Zaventem</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc" role="navigation" History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span id="1923.E2.80.931939"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1923\xe2\x80\x931939">1923\xe2\x80\x931939</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sabena&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: 1923\xe2\x80\x931939">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Sabena began operations on 23 May 1923 as the national carrier of Belgium. The airline was created by the Belgian government after its predecessor <a href="/wiki/SNETA" title="SNETA">SNETA</a> (<i>Syndicat national pour l\'\xc3\xa9tude des transports a\xc3\xa9riens</i>, National Syndicate for the Study of Aerial Transports) - formed in 1919 to pioneer commercial aviation in Belgium - ceased operations. Sabena operated its first commercial flight from <a href="/wiki/Haren_Airport" title="Haren Airport">Brussels</a> to London (UK) on 1 July 1923, via Ostend. Services to <a href="/wiki/Rotterdam" title="Rotterdam">Rotterdam</a> (Netherlands) and <a href="/wiki/Strasbourg" title="Strasbourg">Strasbourg</a> (France) were launched on 1 April 1924. The Strasbourg service was extended to <a href="/wiki/Basle" class="mw-redirect" title="Basle">Basle</a> (Switzerland) on 10 June 1924. Amsterdam (Netherlands) was added on 1 September 1924, and Hamburg (Germany) followed on 1 May 1929 via Antwerp, D\xc3\xbcsseldorf, and Essen.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Belgian_Congo">Belgian Congo</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sabena&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Belgian Congo">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<table class="box-Unreferenced_section plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Unreferenced" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><a href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" decoding="async" width="50" height="39" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="512" data-file-height="399" /></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>does not <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">cite</a> any <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">sources</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Sabena&amp;action=edit">improve this section</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Referencing_for_beginners" title="Help:Referencing for beginners">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. data-file-height="1000" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:AL79-123_Refueling_at_Zinder_Niger_(14284829346).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A <a href="/wiki/Fokker_F.VII" title="Fokker F.VII">Fokker F.VII</a> in Sabena colours</div></div></div>\n<p>When Sabena was created, the airline was partly funded by Belgians in the <a href="/wiki/Belgian_Congo" title="Belgian Congo">Belgian Congo</a> <a href="/wiki/Colony" title="Colony">colony</a> who had lost their air service, an experimental passenger and cargo company (<a href="/wiki/Ligne_Aerienne_du_Roi_Albert" class="mw-redirect" title="Ligne Aerienne du Roi Albert">LARA</a>) between <a href="/wiki/L%C3%A9opoldville" class="mw-redirect" title="L\xc3\xa9opoldville">L\xc3\xa9opoldville</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lisala" title="Lisala">Lisala</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Kisangani" title="Kisangani">Stanleyville</a> a year earlier. They expected the new Belgian national airline to fill this gap. On 12 February 1925, Sabena pioneered a long haul across Africa to <a href="/wiki/Kinshasa" title="Kinshasa">Leopoldville</a>, capital of the Belgian Congo. Throughout its history, Sabena had a long tradition of service to African destinations and for a long time, these were the only profitable routes served by the airline.\n</p><p>Sabena used land planes for its Congo operations and a program of aerodrome construction was initiated in the Congo. This was finished in 1926 and Sabena immediately began flights within the Belgian colony, the main route being <a href="/wiki/Boma,_Kongo_Central" class="mw-redirect" title="Boma, Kongo Central">Boma</a>-<a href="/wiki/L%C3%A9opoldville" class="mw-redirect" title="L\xc3\xa9opoldville">L\xc3\xa9opoldville</a>-<a href="/wiki/%C3%89lisabethville" class="mw-redirect" title="\xc3\x89lisabethville">\xc3\x89lisabethville</a>, a 2,288&#160;km (1,422&#160;mi) route over dense <a href="/wiki/Jungle" title="Jungle">jungle</a>. First, flights were operated with <a href="/wiki/De_Havilland_DH.50" title="De Havilland DH.50">De Havilland DH.50s</a>, although these were quickly replaced by the larger <a href="/wiki/Handley_Page_Type_W" title="Handley Page Type W">Handley Page W.8f</a> which had three engines and offered ten seats.\n</p><p>By 1931 Sabena\'s fleet, including the aircraft used on the Congo network, totalled 43 aircraft. Its mainstay type was the <a href="/wiki/Fokker_F.VII" title="Fokker F.VII">Fokker F.VII</a>B with a lesser number of smaller Fokker VIIA and 14 Handley-Page types. It also flew British <a href="/wiki/Westland_IV" title="Westland IV">Westland Wessex</a> aircraft.\n</p><p>Sabena occasionally flew to tropical Africa, Belgium\'s Congo colony, but mostly these aircraft were shipped out. There was no direct flight yet between Belgium and the colony. As the 1930s progressed, Sabena cooperated with <a href="/wiki/Air_France" title="Air France">Air France</a> and <a href="/wiki/Deutsche_Luft_Hansa" title="Deutsche Luft Hansa">Deutsche Luft Hansa</a>, which also had interests in routes to destinations across Africa.\n</p><p>Sabena\'s first long-haul flight to the Congo occurred on 12 February 1935 and took five and a half days, for which Sabena used a Fokker F-VII/3m aircraft. The following year, Sabena purchased the <a href="/wiki/Savoia-Marchetti_SM.73" class="mw-redirect" title="Savoia-Marchetti SM.73">Savoia-Marchetti SM.73</a> airliner. With a speed of 300&#160;km/h (200&#160;mph), it reduced the journey time to only four days, and the Sabena service ran on alternate weeks to an <a href="/wiki/Air_Afrique" title="Air Afrique">Air Afrique</a> service.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Savoia_Marchetti_S.73.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Savoia_Marchetti_S.73.jpg/220px-Savoia_Marchetti_S.73.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="128" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Savoia_Marchetti_S.73.jpg/330px-Savoia_Marchetti_S.73.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Savoia_Marchetti_S.73.jpg/440px-Savoia_Marchetti_S.73.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1006" data-file-height="585" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Savoia_Marchetti_S.73.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A <a href="/wiki/Savoia-Marchetti_SM.73" class="mw-redirect" title="Savoia-Marchetti SM.73">Savoia-Marchetti SM.73</a> from Sabena, similar to the one that crashed in the <a href="/wiki/1935_SABENA_Savoia-Marchetti_S.73_crash" title="1935 SABENA Savoia-Marchetti S.73 crash">1935 crash</a>.</div></div></div>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Expansion_in_Europe">Expansion in Europe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sabena&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Expansion in Europe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>In Europe, Sabena opened services to <a href="/wiki/Copenhagen" title="Copenhagen">Copenhagen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Malm%C3%B6" title="Malm\xc3\xb6">Malm\xc3\xb6</a> in 1931 and a route to Berlin was initiated in 1932. The mainstay pre-war airliner that Sabena used in Europe was the successful <a href="/wiki/Junkers_Ju_52" title="Junkers Ju 52">Junkers Ju 52</a>/3m airliner. The airline\'s pre-war routes covered almost 6,000&#160;km within Europe. While the Brussels <a href="/wiki/Haren_Airport" title="Haren Airport">Haren Airport</a> was Sabena\'s main base, the company also operated services from other Belgian airports and had a domestic network that was mainly used by businessmen who wanted to be in their coastal villas for the weekend.\n</p><p>In 1938, the airline purchased the new <a href="/wiki/Savoia-Marchetti_SM.83" title="Savoia-Marchetti SM.83">Savoia-Marchetti SM.83</a>, a development of the S.M. 73 with a speed of 435&#160;km/h (270&#160;mph), although it flew services at a cruising speed of about 400&#160;km/h (250&#160;mph).\n</p>\n<h3><span id="1939.E2.80.931946"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1939\xe2\x80\x931946">1939\xe2\x80\x931946</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sabena&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 1939\xe2\x80\x931946">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>At the outbreak of <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War&#160;II</a> in 1939, Sabena\'s fleet totalled 18 aircraft. Its mainstay fleet type was the <a href="/wiki/Savoia-Marchetti_SM.73" class="mw-redirect" title="Savoia-Marchetti SM.73">Savoia-Marchetti SM.73</a> airliner (it had 11 of the type) and the Junkers Ju 52/3m airliner (it had five). Sabena also had just taken delivery of two <a href="/wiki/Douglas_DC-3" title="Douglas DC-3">Douglas DC-3s</a>.\n</p><p>During the war, the airline managed to maintain its Belgian Congo routes, but all European services ceased.\n</p>\n<h3><span id="1946.E2.80.931960"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1946\xe2\x80\x931960">1946\xe2\x80\x931960</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span Sabena resumed operating a network of intra-European scheduled services. The fleet initially consisted mainly of Douglas DC-3s. There were thousands of surplus <a href="/wiki/Douglas_C-47_Skytrain" title="Douglas C-47 Skytrain">Douglas C-47 Skytrains</a> (the military variant of the DC-3) available to help airlines restart operations after the war. The airline now flew under the name of <b>SABENA - Belgian World Airlines</b>.\n</p><p>Sabena started its first transatlantic route to New York City on 4 June 1946, initially using unpressurised <a href="/wiki/Douglas_DC-4" title="Douglas DC-4">Douglas DC-4</a> airliners which were augmented and later replaced by <a href="/wiki/Douglas_DC-6" title="Douglas DC-6">Douglas DC-6</a>Bs. The DC-4s also restarted the airline\'s traditional route to the Belgian Congo. Sabena was the first airline to introduce transatlantic schedules from the north of England, when one of its DC-6Bs inaugurated the Brussels-Manchester-New York route on 28 October 1953.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Convair_240" class="mw-redirect" title="Convair 240">Convair 240</a> was introduced in 1949 to partially replace the DC-3s that until then had flown most European services. As of 1956, improved <a href="/wiki/Convair_440" class="mw-redirect" title="Convair 440">Convair&#160;440 "Metropolitan"</a> twins began replacing the Convair 240 twins and were used successfully well into the 1960s between European regional destinations.\n</p><p>In 1957, the long-range <a href="/wiki/Douglas_DC-7" title="Douglas DC-7">Douglas DC-7</a>C was introduced for long-haul routes but this plane would begin to be supplanted after only three years by the <a href="/wiki/Jet_age" class="mw-redirect" title="Jet age">jet age</a>. It remained in service on the transatlantic route until 1962.\n</p><p>On 3 June 1954, a Soviet Air Force <a href="/wiki/Mikoyan-Gurevich_MiG-15" title="Mikoyan-Gurevich MiG-15">Mikoyan-Gurevich MiG-15</a> (<a href="/wiki/NATO_reporting_name" title="NATO reporting name">NATO reporting name</a> "Fagot") attacked a Sabena-operated <a href="/wiki/Douglas_DC-3" title="Douglas DC-3">Douglas DC-3</a> on a cargo flight from the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> to <a href="/wiki/Socialist_Federal_Republic_of_Yugoslavia" title="Socialist Federal Republic of Yugoslavia">Yugoslavia</a>, killing the radio operator and wounding both the captain and engineer. Co-pilot Douglas Wilson managed to land in <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austria</a> but the plane suffered significant damage.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>For the <a href="/wiki/Expo_58" title="Expo 58">1958 world exposition</a> in Brussels, Sabena leased two <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_L-1049_Super_Constellation" title="Lockheed L-1049 Super Constellation">Lockheed Super Constellations</a> from <a href="/wiki/Seaboard_World_Airlines" title="Seaboard World Airlines">Seaboard World Airlines</a>, using them mainly on transatlantic routes. In the same period, there were experiments with helicopter passenger service using <a href="/wiki/Sikorsky_S-58" class="mw-redirect" title="Sikorsky S-58">Sikorsky S-58</a> aircraft from Brussels to Antwerp, Rotterdam, Eindhoven and the Paris heliport at <a href="/wiki/Issy-les-Moulineaux" title="Issy-les-Moulineaux">Issy-les-Moulineaux</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span id="1960.E2.80.931990"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1960\xe2\x80\x931990">1960\xe2\x80\x931990</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sabena&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: 1960\xe2\x80\x931990">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<table class="box-Unreferenced_section plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Unreferenced" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><a href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" decoding="async" width="50" height="39" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="512" data-file-height="399" /></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>does not <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">cite</a> any <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">sources</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Sabena&amp;action=edit">improve this section</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Referencing_for_beginners" title="Help:Referencing for beginners">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. 707">Boeing 707</a> near its end of service, in 1981.</div></div></div>\n<p>1960 saw the introduction of the <a href="/wiki/Boeing_707" title="Boeing 707">Boeing 707-320</a> intercontinental jet for trans-Atlantic flights to New York. SABENA was mainland Europe\'s first airline to operate a jet across the Atlantic (<a href="/wiki/BOAC" class="mw-redirect" title="BOAC">BOAC</a> had been flying jet transatlantic services using the <a href="/wiki/De_Havilland_Comet" title="De Havilland Comet">de Havilland Comet 4</a> since 4 October 1958). One of Sabena\'s aircraft became the first Boeing 707 to crash while in commercial service when <a href="/wiki/Sabena_Flight_548" title="Sabena Flight 548">Flight 548</a> crashed while preparing to land at Brussels on 15 February 1961. The United States Figure Skating Team was aboard the aircraft, en route from New York to Prague via Brussels to compete in a figure skating championship.\n</p><p>Six <a href="/wiki/Sud_Aviation_Caravelle" title="Sud Aviation Caravelle">Caravelle</a> jetliners were introduced on all medium-haul routes in Europe from February 1961, being flown on most routes alongside the <a href="/wiki/Convair_440" class="mw-redirect" title="Convair 440">Convair 440s</a>, until the early 1970s.\n</p><p>The beginning of the 1960s saw a major upheaval for Sabena in the <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Republic_of_the_Congo" title="Democratic Republic of the Congo">Congo</a>. Widespread rioting against Belgian colonials in the months leading up to, and after the independence of the <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Republic_of_the_Congo" title="Democratic Republic of the Congo">Democratic Republic of the Congo</a>, caused thousands of Belgians to flee the country. The Belgian government commandeered Sabena\'s entire long haul fleet to get the refugees back to Europe. Independence also meant the end of the impressive regional network of routes that the airline had built up in the Congo since 1924. When the new republic began its own airline, <a href="/wiki/Air_Congo" class="mw-redirect" title="Air Congo">Air Congo</a>, in June 1961, Sabena held 30 percent of that airline\'s shares.\n</p><p>The Douglas DC-6Bs remained in service with Sabena in the mid-1960s although they were no longer used on the airline\'s main routes. The Boeing&#160;707s and Caravelles became the mainstay types during this decade. <a href="/wiki/Boeing_727" title="Boeing 727">Boeing 727-100s</a> were introduced on important European routes and also some African services from 1967 in a unique colour scheme; the fin markings incorporated bare-metal rudder and white engine colours. The only other aircraft to have its own special markings was the <a href="/wiki/McDonnell_Douglas_DC-10" title="McDonnell Douglas DC-10">McDonnell Douglas DC-10</a>. At this time <a href="/wiki/Fokker_F27_Friendship" title="Fokker F27 Friendship">Fokker F27 Friendships</a> entered service between regional Belgian airports and European destinations such as <a href="/wiki/London_Heathrow" class="mw-redirect" title="London Heathrow">London Heathrow</a>.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Boeing_747" title="Boeing 747">Boeing&#160;747-100</a> was introduced in 1971, on transatlantic routes flying alongside the Boeing&#160;707-320Cs. Sabena, like many other trans-Atlantic airlines, was satisfied with the Boeing&#160;707s. For commercial reasons it was recognised that it had to buy jumbo jets for its prestige services, notably <a href="/wiki/New_York_JFK" class="mw-redirect" title="New York JFK">New York JFK</a> and as of the mid-seventies, <a href="/wiki/Chicago_O%27Hare" class="mw-redirect" title="Chicago O&#39;Hare">Chicago O\'Hare</a>. Sabena purchased only two first generation jumbo jets, one named <i>Tante Agathe</i> (which means Aunt Agathe in French), and it continued to fly the 707 into the late&#160;1970, as the Boeing 747-100\'s last flight occurred in 1993.\n</p><p>In 1973, the Boeing 727s on the European network were replaced by <a href="/wiki/Boeing_737" title="Boeing 737">Boeing 737-200s</a>. The <a href="/wiki/McDonnell_Douglas_DC-10" title="McDonnell Douglas DC-10">McDonnell Douglas DC-10-30</a> entered service in 1974. In total, Sabena purchased five of these convertible (Passengers and/or freight) <a href="/wiki/Combi_aircraft" title="Combi aircraft">combi aircraft</a> wide-body jets.\n</p><p>In 1984 <a href="/wiki/Airbus_A310" title="Airbus A310">Airbus A310s</a> were introduced on routes that had high passenger density. This aircraft type also introduced a modernisation of the 1973 Sabena livery, in which a lighter blue was used and the titles on the fuselage were in a more modern style.\n</p><p>In June 1986 the first of two <a href="/wiki/Boeing_747" title="Boeing 747">Boeing 747-300</a> aircraft joined the fleet, eventually replacing the older 747-100s.\n</p><p>In an advertisement in <i><a href="/wiki/National_Geographic_(magazine)" class="mw-redirect" title="National Geographic (magazine)">National Geographic</a></i> June&#160;1982 (volume 161, no. 6) Sabena prided itself with a superior inflight service. "And unlike most European airlines Sabena still offers <a href="/wiki/First_class_(aviation)" title="First class (aviation)">First Class</a> service within Europe and all the way through." This advertisement also stated that "Sabena flies to 76 destinations on 4 continents" and used the slogan "belgian world airlines".\n</p><p>In 1989 Sabena invited Belgian fashion designer <a href="/wiki/Olivier_Strelli" title="Olivier Strelli">Olivier Strelli</a> to create a new range of uniforms for its cabin crews.\n</p>\n<h3><span id="1990.E2.80.931995"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1990\xe2\x80\x931995">1990\xe2\x80\x931995</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sabena&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: 1990\xe2\x80\x931995">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Sabena,_Dublin,_May_1995.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Sabena%2C_Dublin%2C_May_1995.jpg/220px-Sabena%2C_Dublin%2C_May_1995.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Sabena%2C_Dublin%2C_May_1995.jpg/330px-Sabena%2C_Dublin%2C_May_1995.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Sabena%2C_Dublin%2C_May_1995.jpg/440px-Sabena%2C_Dublin%2C_May_1995.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3419" data-file-height="2279" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Sabena,_Dublin,_May_1995.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A Sabena Boeing 737 at <a href="/wiki/Dublin_Airport" title="Dublin Airport">Dublin Airport</a> in 1985.</div></div></div>\n<p>A new name, <b>Sabena World Airlines</b>, and colours were introduced for the 1990s. The new livery had an overall white colour and the white circle tail logo in blue on the fin. A large "Sabena" title covered the <a href="/wiki/Fuselage" title="Fuselage">fuselage</a> in light blue and the name "Belgian World Airlines" was at times just visible, though the title was also painted on the fuselage in small, clear letters. The 1990s saw further fleet type renewal: the DC-10-30s were replaced with twin-engined <a href="/wiki/Airbus_A330" title="Airbus A330">Airbus&#160;A330</a> and the Boeing&#160;747s with four-engined <a href="/wiki/Airbus_A340" title="Airbus A340">Airbus A340</a>.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:38be_-_Sabena_Airbus_A340-211;_OO-SCW@ZRH;23.08.1998_(5126928987).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/38be_-_Sabena_Airbus_A340-211%3B_OO-SCW%40ZRH%3B23.08.1998_%285126928987%29.jpg/220px-38be_-_Sabena_Airbus_A340-211%3B_OO-SCW%40ZRH%3B23.08.1998_%285126928987%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="149" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/38be_-_Sabena_Airbus_A340-211%3B_OO-SCW%40ZRH%3B23.08.1998_%285126928987%29.jpg/330px-38be_-_Sabena_Airbus_A340-211%3B_OO-SCW%40ZRH%3B23.08.1998_%285126928987%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/38be_-_Sabena_Airbus_A340-211%3B_OO-SCW%40ZRH%3B23.08.1998_%285126928987%29.jpg/440px-38be_-_Sabena_Airbus_A340-211%3B_OO-SCW%40ZRH%3B23.08.1998_%285126928987%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4198" data-file-height="2838" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:38be_-_Sabena_Airbus_A340-211;_OO-SCW@ZRH;23.08.1998_(5126928987).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>To replace their old <a href="/wiki/Boeing_747" title="Boeing 747">Boeing 747s</a>, Sabena purchased many <a href="/wiki/Airbus_A340" title="Airbus A340">Airbus A340s</a>. The first Sabena Airbus A340 first flew in 1993.</div></div></div>\n<p>After the liberalisation of the airline industry throughout Europe and the economic consequences of the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_War" title="Gulf War">Gulf War</a>, the Belgian government, the main shareholder of the company, realized that Sabena had little chance of surviving on its own in this very competitive market, and began searching for a suitable partner.\n</p><p>Sabena remained in a poor financial state, and year after year the Belgian government had to cover losses, however it was prevented from providing new funds due to EU State Aid rules. For help in business due to their financial problems, Sabena leased a couple of <a href="/wiki/Boeing_747" title="Boeing 747">Boeing 747s</a> from <a href="/wiki/Air_France" title="Air France">Air France</a>. Many more aircraft were leased for longer periods, but had to keep their distinctive French <a href="/wiki/Aircraft_registration" title="Aircraft registration">registration numbers</a>. \n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Boeing_747-128,_Sabena_(Air_France)_AN0157599.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Boeing_747-128%2C_Sabena_%28Air_France%29_AN0157599.jpg/220px-Boeing_747-128%2C_Sabena_%28Air_France%29_AN0157599.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="138" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Boeing_747-128%2C_Sabena_%28Air_France%29_AN0157599.jpg/330px-Boeing_747-128%2C_Sabena_%28Air_France%29_AN0157599.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Boeing_747-128%2C_Sabena_%28Air_France%29_AN0157599.jpg/440px-Boeing_747-128%2C_Sabena_%28Air_France%29_AN0157599.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1000" data-file-height="628" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Boeing_747-128,_Sabena_(Air_France)_AN0157599.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A <a href="/wiki/Boeing_747" title="Boeing 747"> Boeing 747-128</a> leased from <a href="/wiki/Air_France" title="Air France">Air France</a> in 1992.</div></div></div>\n<p>Around 1987, <a href="/wiki/Scandinavian_Airlines" title="Scandinavian Airlines">SAS</a> tried to merge with the carrier, but the merger was blocked by the Belgian authorities. In 1989, <a href="/wiki/British_Airways" title="British Airways">British Airways</a> and <a href="/wiki/KLM" title="KLM">KLM</a> purchased stakes in Sabena, which were later sold back to the Belgian government. In 1993, <a href="/wiki/Air_France" title="Air France">Air France</a> purchased a large minority stake in Sabena, which it sold soon after. Finally, in 1995, <a href="/wiki/Swissair" title="Swissair">Swissair</a> purchased a 49 percent stake in Sabena and took over management.\n</p><p>In 1993 Sabena adopted its third and final logo, and its head office was in the Air Terminus building on Rue Cardinal Mercier in <a href="/wiki/Brussels" title="Brussels">Brussels</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1994, <a href="/wiki/Paul_Rusesabagina" title="Paul Rusesabagina">Paul Rusesabagina</a>, a manager for Sabena-owned hotels in the former Belgian territory of <a href="/wiki/Rwanda" title="Rwanda">Rwanda</a>, sheltered over 1200 Tutsis and moderate Hutus at the <a href="/wiki/H%C3%B4tel_des_Mille_Collines" title="H\xc3\xb4tel des Mille Collines">H\xc3\xb4tel des Mille Collines</a> of <a href="/wiki/Kigali" title="Kigali">Kigali</a>, saving them from being slaughtered by the <a href="/wiki/Interahamwe" title="Interahamwe">Interahamwe</a> militia during the <a href="/wiki/Rwandan_genocide" title="Rwandan genocide">Rwandan genocide</a> (this is depicted in the 2004 motion picture <i><a href="/wiki/Hotel_Rwanda" title="Hotel Rwanda">Hotel Rwanda</a></i>).<sup id="cite_ref-NYTimes_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYTimes-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span airline, few modifications were made to the aircraft\'s liveries, including a sticker, saying: <i>Flying together with Swissair</i>. \nIn March and April 1998 two <a href="/wiki/McDonnell-Douglas_MD-11" class="mw-redirect" title="McDonnell-Douglas MD-11">McDonnell-Douglas MD-11</a> aircraft, both leased from <a href="/wiki/CityBird" title="CityBird">CityBird</a>, joined the fleet and such long-haul destinations as <a href="/wiki/Newark,_New_Jersey" title="Newark, New Jersey">Newark</a>, <a href="/wiki/Montreal" title="Montreal">Montreal</a> and <a href="/wiki/S%C3%A3o_Paulo" title="S\xc3\xa3o Paulo">S\xc3\xa3o Paulo</a> were (re)introduced. Also, that year saw the delivery of the last passenger version of the MD-11 from Boeing, which merged with <a href="/wiki/McDonnell_Douglas" title="McDonnell Douglas">McDonnell Douglas</a> a year before.\n</p><p>1999 saw new colours introduced to the fleet, beginning with an <a href="/wiki/Airbus_A340" title="Airbus A340">Airbus&#160;A340</a>. One of the latest fleet types that Sabena introduced, right after the <a href="/wiki/Airbus_A321" title="Airbus A321">A321</a> and <a href="/wiki/Airbus_A320_family" title="Airbus A320 family">A320</a> was the <a href="/wiki/Airbus_A319" title="Airbus A319">A319</a> which saw service in 2000. These new planes were part of a record-order of 34 <a href="/wiki/Airbus_A320_family" title="Airbus A320 family">Airbus A320 family</a> aircraft, imposed on Sabena when under Swissair management.\n</p><p>After an airline recession and the effects on the airline industry of the <a href="/wiki/September_11_attacks" title="September 11 attacks">September&#160;11 attacks</a> in 2001, all airlines that flew across the Atlantic suffered. Swissair had pledged to invest millions in Sabena but failed to do so, partly because the airline had financial problems itself, having filed for bankruptcy protection one month prior. Sabena filed for legal protection against its creditors on 3 October, and went into liquidation on 6 November 2001. <a href="/wiki/Fred_Chaffart" title="Fred Chaffart">Fred Chaffart</a>, Chairman of the Board of Directors of Sabena, read a declaration on this day to explain the decision.\n</p><p>7 November 2001 was the final day of operations for Sabena. Flight&#160;690 (operated by an <a href="/wiki/Airbus_A340-311" class="mw-redirect" title="Airbus A340-311">Airbus A340-311</a> registered as OO-SCZ) from <a href="/wiki/Abidjan" title="Abidjan">Abidjan</a>, Ivory Coast and <a href="/wiki/Cotonou" title="Cotonou">Cotonou</a>, Benin, was the last Sabena flight to land in Brussels.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The flight had  266 passengers and eleven crewmembers.\n</p><p>A group of investors managed to take over <a href="/wiki/Delta_Air_Transport" title="Delta Air Transport">Delta Air Transport</a>, one of Sabena\'s subsidiaries, and transformed it into <a href="/wiki/SN_Brussels_Airlines" title="SN Brussels Airlines">SN Brussels Airlines</a>. That airline merged with <a href="/wiki/Virgin_Express" title="Virgin Express">Virgin Express</a> in 2006 to form a new company, <a href="/wiki/Brussels_Airlines" title="Brussels Airlines">Brussels Airlines</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span id="2001.E2.80.93present"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="2001\xe2\x80\x93present">2001\xe2\x80\x93present</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sabena&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: 2001\xe2\x80\x93present">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Bae146.avrorj85.arp.750pix.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/Bae146.avrorj85.arp.750pix.jpg/220px-Bae146.avrorj85.arp.750pix.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/Bae146.avrorj85.arp.750pix.jpg/330px-Bae146.avrorj85.arp.750pix.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/Bae146.avrorj85.arp.750pix.jpg/440px-Bae146.avrorj85.arp.750pix.jpg 2x" data-file-width="750" data-file-height="562" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Bae146.avrorj85.arp.750pix.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>SN Brussels Airlines <a href="/wiki/BAe_146" class="mw-redirect" title="BAe 146">BAe 146</a> in the former Sabena livery at <a href="/wiki/London_Heathrow_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="London Heathrow Airport">London Heathrow Airport</a> in 2002.</div></div></div>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Belgian_Parliament" class="mw-redirect" title="Belgian Parliament">Belgian Parliament</a> formed a committee to investigate the reasons behind the bankruptcy of Sabena and the involvement of Switzerland\'s flag carrier. At the same time, the company\'s administrator investigated possible legal steps against Swissair, and its successors in interest <a href="/wiki/Swiss_International_Air_Lines" title="Swiss International Air Lines">Swiss International Air Lines</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lufthansa" title="Lufthansa">Lufthansa</a>.\n</p><p>In 2006, the Belgian government, a former major shareholder, filed criminal charges against the former Swissair management. The former Swissair management was condemned by the judges.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>On 16 January 2007 the Belgian - Flemish news program <i>Terzake</i> reported that during the 1990s, several members of the board were paid large sums illegally through a Sabena affiliate in <a href="/wiki/Bermuda" title="Bermuda">Bermuda</a>. When Paul Reutlinger became the CEO of the company, he stopped the illegal payments. <i>Terzake</i> went on to state that this might explain why the Belgian board members remained quiet when it became apparent Swissair was exploiting Sabena and eventually drove the company into bankruptcy.\n</p><p>On December 14, 2007, Georges Jaspis, a former World War&#160;II pilot in <a href="/wiki/No._609_Squadron_RAF" title="No. 609 Squadron RAF">No.&#160;609 Squadron RAF</a> and the Sabena pilot with the most flying hours (27,000) died.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Captain Jaspis was the pilot who inaugurated the Manchester to New York service in October 1953 and who collected the first Sabena Boeing&#160;707 and 747. He had escaped Belgium during the war and made his way to England where he joined the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Air_Force" title="Royal Air Force">Royal Air Force</a>. He was awarded the <a href="/wiki/Distinguished_Flying_Cross_(United_States)" title="Distinguished Flying Cross (United States)">Distinguished Flying Cross</a>. He was buried in Opprebais, a village south-east of Brussels on December 19, 2007. The Belgian military attended and a flypast of four F-16&#160;jets in <a href="/wiki/Missing_man_formation" title="Missing man formation">missing man formation</a> was made.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reasons_for_bankruptcy">Reasons for bankruptcy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sabena&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Reasons for bankruptcy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The reasons for SABENA\'s bankruptcy are numerous.  One of the direct causes was <a href="/wiki/Swissair" title="Swissair">Swissair</a> not living up to their contractual obligations and failing to inject necessary funds into the company. This was because at the time Swissair was having its own financial problems. During the so-called "Hotel agreement", signed on July 17, 2001, Belgian prime minister <a href="/wiki/Guy_Verhofstadt" title="Guy Verhofstadt">Guy Verhofstadt</a> met with Swissair boss <a href="/wiki/Mario_Corti_(manager)" title="Mario Corti (manager)">Mario Corti</a>, who agreed to inject \xe2\x82\xac258&#160;million into SABENA. Mr Corti had made a terrible mistake as the sum was never paid, due to Swissair\'s own financial problems. The purchase of 34 new <a href="/wiki/Airbus_A320_family" title="Airbus A320 family">Airbus A320 family</a> planes, imposed under Swissair\'s management, was a burden SABENA could not cope with.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>After the bankruptcy, a parliamentary commission in Belgium was established to investigate the demise of the airline. The Belgian politicians got a part of the blame; <a href="/wiki/Rik_Daems" title="Rik Daems">Rik Daems</a>, who, at the time, was Minister of Public Enterprises and Participations, Telecommunication and Middle Classes, received most criticism due to his lack of effort. Swissair itself went bankrupt in October 2001 and was liquidated in March 2002.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Destinations">Destinations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span Engine power was lost upon takeoff from <a href="/wiki/Frankfurt_Airport" title="Frankfurt Airport">Frankfurt Airport</a>, making the aircraft impossible to control.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>One passenger on board a flight from Brussels to <a href="/wiki/Zurich" class="mw-redirect" title="Zurich">Zurich</a> was killed on 19 December of the same year, when the aircraft (a CV-240 registered OO-AWO) hit the ground 2.5&#160;km short of the runway threshold of <a href="/wiki/Zurich_Airport" title="Zurich Airport">Kloten Airport</a> at 18:55 local time. In low visibility conditions, the pilot descended below the <a href="/wiki/Instrument_landing_system" title="Instrument landing system">glidepath</a>. The other 39 passengers and three crew members survived the accident.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>On 3 June 1954, a DC-3 (registered OO-CBY) was attacked by a <a href="/wiki/Mikoyan-Gurevich_MiG-15" title="Mikoyan-Gurevich MiG-15">Mikoyan-Gurevich MiG-15</a> fighter aircraft near <a href="/wiki/Maribor" title="Maribor">Maribor</a>, Yugoslavia. The aircraft, on a cargo flight from <a href="/wiki/Blackbushe_Airport" title="Blackbushe Airport">Blackbushe Airport</a> to <a href="/wiki/Belgrade" title="Belgrade">Belgrade</a>, remained airborne, and a forced landing at <a href="/wiki/Graz_Airport" title="Graz Airport">Graz Airport</a> was carried out, during which it ran off the runway. In the incident, one out of the four people on board was killed.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>On 13 February 1955, the pilots of <b><a href="/wiki/Sabena_Flight_503" title="Sabena Flight 503">Flight 503</a></b> from Brussels to Rome lost orientation when approaching <a href="/wiki/Rome_Ciampino_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Rome Ciampino Airport">Ciampino Airport</a>, resulting in the Douglas DC-6 registered OO-SDB crashing into the slope of <a href="/wiki/Monte_Terminillo" title="Monte Terminillo">Monte Terminillo</a> at 18:53 local time, killing the 21 passengers and eight crew on board.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>The disaster of <b><a href="/wiki/Sabena_Flight_548" title="Sabena Flight 548">Flight 548</a></b> with its 73 casualties marked the worst accident in the history of SABENA. It happened on 15 February 1961 at 09:05 UTC, when the aircraft (a <a href="/wiki/Boeing_707" title="Boeing 707">Boeing 707</a> registered OO-SJB) crashed at <a href="/wiki/Brussels_Airport" title="Brussels Airport">Brussels Airport</a> following a flight from New York City.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> Among the dead were the entire American delegation to the 1961 <a href="/wiki/World_Figure_Skating_Championships" title="World Figure Skating Championships">World Figure Skating Championships</a>, slated to be held in <a href="/wiki/Prague" title="Prague">Prague</a>; the competition was canceled in the aftermath.</li>\n<li>On 13 July 1968, a cargo-configured 707 (registered OO-SJK) crashed upon approach to <a href="/wiki/Murtala_Muhammed_International_Airport" title="Murtala Muhammed International Airport">Lagos Airport</a> in Nigeria on a flight from Brussels, killing the seven occupants. It was determined that the aircraft descended too low and struck trees.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>A DC-3 registered OO-AUX was damaged beyond repair in a ground accident at <a href="/wiki/Amsterdam_Airport_Schiphol" title="Amsterdam Airport Schiphol">Amsterdam Airport Schiphol</a> on 9 May 1970. The pilots began to taxi the aircraft even though they had not been cleared to do so, which resulted in the right propeller hitting an obstacle on the ground, and debris destroying the airliner beyond economic repair. The aircraft had been leased from Delta Air Transport.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>On 8 May 1972, <b><a href="/wiki/Sabena_Flight_571_hijacking" class="mw-redirect" title="Sabena Flight 571 hijacking">Flight 571</a></b> from Vienna to Tel Aviv with 101 people on board (a Boeing 707 registered OO-SJG) was hijacked by four members of the terrorist organization <a href="/wiki/Black_September_(group)" class="mw-redirect" title="Black September (group)">Black September</a>, in order to secure the release of 315 detainees from Israeli prisons. At <a href="/wiki/Ben-Gurion_International_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Ben-Gurion International Airport">Ben-Gurion International Airport</a>, two hijackers were shot and killed by the Israeli <i><a href="/wiki/Sayeret_Matkal" title="Sayeret Matkal">Sayeret Matkal</a></i> special forces. One passenger died later of the wounds she had suffered in the shootout.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>On 15 February 1978, a Boeing 707 (registered OO-SJE) undershot the runway at <a href="/wiki/Tenerife_North_Airport" title="Tenerife North Airport">Los Rodeos Airport</a>, causing the nosegear to collapse, following a chartered holiday flight from Brussels with 189 passengers and seven crew on board. After all passengers and crew were evacuated spilled fuel caught fire, destroying the aircraft.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>On 4 April of the same year at 18:07 local time, a Boeing&#160;737-200 (registered OO-SDH) on a crew training flight suffered a <a href="/wiki/Bird_strike" title="Bird strike">bird strike</a> during landing practice at <a href="/wiki/Brussels_South_Charleroi_Airport" title="Brussels South Charleroi Airport">Charleroi Airport</a>. The pilot instructor attempted to get the aircraft airborne again, but failed because of insufficient remaining runway. The aircraft was damaged beyond repair.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>On 29 August 1998, <b>Flight 542</b> from New York to Brussels with 248 passengers and 11 crew members on board, an <a href="/wiki/Airbus_A340" title="Airbus A340">Airbus A340-200</a> (registered OO-SCW), suffered a broken right landing gear upon landing at Brussels Airport. The aircraft veered off the runway. There were no notable injuries in the ensuing evacuation, and the aircraft was repaired.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>On 13 October 2000, <b>Flight 689</b> from Brussels to <a href="/wiki/Abidjan" title="Abidjan">Abidjan</a> was hijacked by a Nigerian national who was due to be deported. The <a href="/wiki/Airbus_A330" title="Airbus A330">Airbus A330-200</a> with 146 other passengers and 11 crew members on board was forced to land at <a href="/wiki/M%C3%A1laga_Airport" title="M\xc3\xa1laga Airport">M\xc3\xa1laga Airport</a> in Spain, where the perpetrator was overpowered by local police forces.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>On 4 December 2000, a flight from Brussels to <a href="/wiki/Nairobi" title="Nairobi">Nairobi</a>, Kenya, via <a href="/wiki/Bujumbura" title="Bujumbura">Bujumbura</a>, Burundi, was struck by machine gun fire as it approached Bujumbura, injuring a passenger and crew member. The Airbus&#160;A330, carrying 170&#160;passengers and crew, was struck in an attack blamed by the government on Hutu rebels, who denied responsibility. The aircraft landed safely but did not continue the flight.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Flights_in_the_Belgian_colonies">Flights in the Belgian colonies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sabena&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Flights in the Belgian colonies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<ul><li>On 26 January 1937, a SABCA S.73 (OO-AGR) crashed south of Oran-es Senia Airport, Algeria during a round trip from Belgium to Belgian Congo, killing all 12 on board. During the flight back to Belgium the aircraft was approaching Oran for an en route stop. Witnesses noticed the aircraft flying at 1,000&#160;m (3,300&#160;ft) when it went into a sudden nosedive until it crashed. The cause was not determined, but bad weather and engine problems were ruled out. Several recommendations were issued in the wake of the accident: the center of gravity and baggage requirements are checked to ensure proper load distribution, ensure adequate maintenance and inspection personnel are available in the Congo and ports of call, review maximum hours of piloting to avoid fatigue as well as provide use of the autopilot, require seat belts for pilots, ensure all security measures are taken for crossing the Mediterranean, and to install more cockpit instruments.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>On 1 January 1943, a <a href="/wiki/Junkers_Ju_52" title="Junkers Ju 52">Junkers Ju 52</a> (registered OO-AUG) crashed 80&#160;mi from <a href="/wiki/Bangui" title="Bangui">Bangui</a> in then <a href="/wiki/French_Equatorial_Africa" title="French Equatorial Africa">French Equatorial Africa</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>On 25 March 1944, a Junkers Ju 52/3mge (OO-AGU) crashed at Costermansville (now <a href="/wiki/Bukavu" title="Bukavu">Bukavu</a>), <a href="/wiki/Belgian_Congo" title="Belgian Congo">Belgian Congo</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>Only some days later, on 3 April, another aircraft of the same type (OO-AUF) crashed nearby at Mongena.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>On 14 December 1945, a <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_Model_18_Lodestar" title="Lockheed Model 18 href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>On 7 January 1947, a Douglas DC-3 (registered OO-CBO) crashed near Costermansville.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="/wiki/1948_Sabena_DC-4_Crash" title="1948 Sabena DC-4 Crash">crashed</a> near Magazini after flying into a tornado at low altitude during a scheduled passenger flight from L\xc3\xa9opoldville (now <a href="/wiki/Kinshasa" title="Kinshasa">Kinshasa</a>) to <a href="/wiki/Libenge" title="Libenge">Libenge</a>, then Belgian Congo, killing the 24 passengers and seven crew members; only one passenger survived.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>Another 13 people (ten passengers, three crew) were killed on 31&#160;August of that href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>On 27 August 1949, a Douglas DC-3 (registered OO-CBK) experienced a loss of engine power shortly after take-off from <a href="/wiki/N%27Dolo_Airport" title="N&#39;Dolo Airport">Leopoldville Airport</a> for a flight to Elizabethville (now <a href="/wiki/Lubumbashi" title="Lubumbashi">Lubumbashi</a>), with 17&#160;passengers and three crew on board. The three crew members and two out of the seventeen passengers on board died in the ensuing crash.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>On 24 July 1951, the right engine of a cargo-configured DC-3 (registered OO-CBA) failed on takeoff from <a href="/wiki/Gao_International_Airport" title="Gao International Airport">Gao Airfield</a> and crashed, resulting in the loss of lives of the three persons on board. The engine failed due to hydraulic failure; hydraulic fluid squirted on the windshield, temporarily blinding the pilots who then lost control of the aircraft.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>On 4 February 1952, <b>Flight 425</b>, a C-47A (registered OO-CBA), crashed in the Dibata forest some 20&#160;km (12&#160;mi) from <a href="/wiki/Kikwit" title="Kikwit">Kikwit</a> en route from Costermansville to L\xc3\xa9opoldville after a blade broke off the right side propeller and cut through the fuselage, severing control cables and resulting in a loss of control, killing all 16 on board. The propeller blade broke off following a shaft failure when the number two engine stopped suddenly after the piston lug on the No. 6 cylinder failed due to fatigue.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>On 18 May 1958, a <a href="/wiki/Douglas_DC-7" title="Douglas DC-7">Douglas DC-7</a> (registered OO-SFA) suffered a problem with its leftmost engine, whilst on a flight from <a href="/wiki/Lisbon" title="Lisbon">Lisbon</a> to L\xc3\xa9opoldville with 56&#160;passengers and nine crew members. The pilots prepared for an emergency landing at <a href="/wiki/Casablanca%E2%80%93Anfa_Airport" title="Casablanca\xe2\x80\x93Anfa Airport">Casablanca\xe2\x80\x93Anfa Airport</a>, but shortly before touchdown, a <a href="/wiki/Go-around" title="Go-around">go-around</a> was attempted, which resulted in a <a href="/wiki/Stall_(flight)" class="mw-redirect" title="Stall (flight)">stall</a> because of the lesser available engine power.
