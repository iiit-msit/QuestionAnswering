class="infobox-label">Children</th><td class="infobox-data">3</td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p><b>John Leahy</b> (born August 1950) is an American businessman. He is a former <a href="/wiki/Chief_operating_officer" title="Chief operating officer">chief operating officer</a>\xe2\x80\x93Customers at <a href="/wiki/Airbus" title="Airbus">Airbus</a> (2005\xe2\x80\x932017), as well as the company\'s <a href="/wiki/Chief_commercial_officer" title="Chief commercial officer">chief commercial officer</a> between August 1994 and 2017, and a former member of the Airbus main board of management. Well known as one of the commercial aviation industry\'s most prolific aircraft salesmen, he made a significant contribution to the increase in Airbus\'s market share, from 18% in 1993 to 57% a decade later.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-sellingsky_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sellingsky-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> He is responsible for over $1 trillion in sales.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Airbus SAS was a government-owned company between 1993 and 2003.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc" role="navigation" aria-labelledby="mw-toc-heading"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" style="display:none" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Leahy_(executive)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Leahy has an MBA from <a href="/wiki/Syracuse_University" title="Syracuse University">Syracuse University</a> and a BA from <a href="/wiki/Fordham_University" title="Fordham University">Fordham University</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> He is a licensed multi-engine commercial pilot and is a former flight instructor with nearly 4,000 hours of flying time. He flew cargo across the Great Lakes. From 1977 to 1984, Leahy worked in marketing at <a href="/wiki/Piper_Aircraft" title="Piper Aircraft">Piper Aircraft</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Leahy_(executive)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Leahy joined Airbus North America in January 1985, becoming head of sales soon after. He was appointed president of Airbus North America in 1988 with responsibility for the penetration of the strategic North American market, where most major U.S. airlines were <a href="/wiki/Boeing" title="Boeing">Boeing</a> customers.\n</p><p>His first deal was selling A320s to <a href="/wiki/Northwest_Airlines" title="Northwest Airlines">Northwest Airlines</a>, which was a Boeing customer which was then the <a href="/wiki/Risk_averse" class="mw-redirect" title="Risk averse">risk averse</a> choice.\nWhen regular Boeing customer <a href="/wiki/United_Airlines" title="United Airlines">United Airlines</a> bought the A320, this prompted Boeing to update the slower, shorter-range <a href="/wiki/737_Classic" class="mw-redirect" title="737 Classic">737 Classic</a>-400 into the more efficient, longer <a href="/wiki/737NG" class="mw-redirect" title="737NG">737NG</a>-800.\nSimilar to loaning four A300B2 to <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Airlines" class="mw-redirect" title="Eastern Airlines">Eastern Airlines</a> for a test run before Leahy, selling 25 <a href="/wiki/Airbus_A300" title="Airbus A300">A300</a>-600s to American required walk-away leases with little or no penalty at the return, and Boeing had to offer the same to place its <a href="/wiki/767-300ER" class="mw-redirect" title="767-300ER">767-300ER</a>.\nTo feed its New York and Miami hubs, <a href="/wiki/Pan_Am" title="Pan Am">Pan Am</a> replaced its <a href="/wiki/Boeing_727" title="Boeing 727">Boeing 727s</a> by A320s but when it collapsed, <a href="/wiki/Aircraft_leasing" class="mw-redirect" title="Aircraft leasing">lessor</a> GPA placed them to <a href="/wiki/Braniff_(1991%E2%80%931992)" title="Braniff (1991\xe2\x80\x931992)">Braniff</a>, which then collapsed, <a href="/wiki/America_West_Airlines" title="America West Airlines">America West Airlines</a> took them, went through bankruptcy, and became its largest operator with <a href="/wiki/US_Airways" title="US Airways">US Airways</a> and later acquired <a href="/wiki/American_Airlines" title="American Airlines">American Airlines</a> which ordered 400 A320s.\nIn 1993\xe2\x80\x931994, Boeing struck exclusive supplier contracts for 20 years to eliminate competition and bind American, Continental, Delta, United and Southwest: the deal was ruled illegal when Boeing and <a href="/wiki/McDonnell_Douglas" title="McDonnell Douglas">McDonnell Douglas</a> merged three years later, but was honored nonetheless.<sup id="cite_ref-Leeham28nov2017_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Leeham28nov2017-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1994, Leahy was appointed chief commercial officer at the main office of Airbus, the position of the sales chief which was previously allocated to a Briton. In this capacity, he has outlasted five Airbus CEOs, and seven Boeing sales chiefs.<sup id="cite_ref-sellingsky_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sellingsky-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Leahy set the goal of 50% market share by 2000 from low-double digits then in a little over five years.\nHe presented this goal to the board in January 1995, for which this was out of reach, targeting 25% to 30%.\nHe achieved 18% in 1995 and then <a href="/wiki/Boeing_Commercial_Airplanes" title="Boeing Commercial Airplanes">Boeing Commercial Airplanes</a> president <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ron_Woodard&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Ron Woodard (page does not exist)">Ron Woodard</a> was determined to crush Airbus by cutting prices and offering early delivery slots: the 737 production rose from 21 a month to 27, unprecedented at the time, but the supply chain couldn\'t keep up and the line was shut down for 30 days to catch up, causing Boeing\'s first loss in 40 years as Woodard lost his job.\nDouglas failed in its strategy of being a small player taking small, profitable deals which lasted about three years&#160;: by 1997, it was down to a 7% market share and was absorbed by Boeing.\nAirbus reached 50% in 1999.<sup id="cite_ref-Leeham14dec2017_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Leeham14dec2017-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\nIt maintained over 50% market share including deliveries until 2005.\n</p><p>Leahy is also known in the industry as a polarizing figure. Former Boeing sales chief Toby Bright said "John was the guy that at Boeing we loved to hate and we blamed him for a lot of the losses that we experienced against him".<sup id="cite_ref-sellingsky_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sellingsky-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Leahy was instrumental in the launch of the new Airbus flagship, the <a href="/wiki/A380-800" class="mw-redirect" title="A380-800">A380-800</a> passenger aircraft and the A380-800F freighter aircraft. With its introduction with <a href="/wiki/Singapore_Airlines" title="Singapore Airlines">Singapore Airlines</a> in October 2007, the A380 has become the largest airliner in the world, surpassing the <a href="/wiki/Boeing_747" title="Boeing 747">Boeing 747</a>. He is also leading the sales campaign for the all-new <a href="/wiki/Airbus_A350" title="Airbus A350">A350</a>, with the challenge to regain ground lost to the <a href="/wiki/Boeing_787_Dreamliner" title="Boeing 787 Dreamliner">Boeing 787 Dreamliner</a> and <a href="/wiki/Boeing_777" title="Boeing 777">Boeing 777</a>.\n</p><p>The first signs of his job taking its toll on him appeared in autumn 2006, when he had to undergo heart surgery. At the time, doctors advised him to slow his work pace.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-sellingsky_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sellingsky-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>He was supposed to retire at the end of 2017 to be replaced by his deputy <a href="/w/index.php?title=Kiran_Rao_(executive)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Kiran Rao (executive) (page does not exist)">Kiran Rao</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\nRao stepped down and Frenchmen <a href="/w/index.php?title=Eric_Schulz&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Eric Schulz (page does not exist)">Eric Schulz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aerospace_engineer" class="mw-redirect" title="Aerospace engineer">aerospace engineer</a> and president of <a href="/wiki/Rolls-Royce_plc" class="mw-redirect" title="Rolls-Royce plc">Rolls-Royce plc</a> Civil Aerospace, replaced Leahy in January 2018.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span
