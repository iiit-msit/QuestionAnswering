<b>NRN</b>, <a href="/wiki/ICAO_airport_code" title="ICAO airport code">ICAO</a>: <b>EDLV</b>), less commonly known as <i>Niederrhein Airport</i>, is a minor <a href="/wiki/International_airport" title="International airport">international airport</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Lower_Rhine_region" title="Lower Rhine region">Lower Rhine region</a> of <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>. It is used by <a href="/wiki/Ryanair" title="Ryanair">Ryanair</a>. The airport is situated 3.7&#160;km (2.3&#160;mi) southwest<sup id="cite_ref-AIP_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AIP-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> of the municipality of <a href="/wiki/Weeze" title="Weeze">Weeze</a> (<small>German pronunciation:&#32;</small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Standard_German" title="Help:IPA/Standard German">[\xcb\x88ve\xcb\x90t\xcd\xa1s\xc9\x99]</a></span>) and 7&#160;km (4.3&#160;mi) northwest<sup id="cite_ref-AIP_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AIP-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> of <a href="/wiki/Kevelaer" title="Kevelaer">Kevelaer</a>, about 33&#160;km (21&#160;mi) southeast of the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Dutch</a> city of <a href="/wiki/Nijmegen" title="Nijmegen">Nijmegen</a>, and 48&#160;km (30&#160;mi) northwest of the German city of <a href="/wiki/Duisburg" title="Duisburg">Duisburg</a>. During the 2000-15 period this was one of several German airports expanding rapidly, however the airport handled only 1,23 Mio. passengers in 2019, reflecting a decline in throughput triggered by <a href="/wiki/Ryanair" title="Ryanair">Ryanair</a> reducing its route network.<sup id="cite_ref-rponline_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rponline-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div id="toc" links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weeze_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The airport uses the facilities of the former military airbase <a href="/wiki/RAF_Laarbruch" title="RAF Laarbruch">RAF Laarbruch</a>, and began operating as a civil airport in 2003. There is also a large <a href="/wiki/Fire_department" title="Fire department">fire department</a> training facility on the airport grounds. Its <a href="/wiki/International_Air_Transport_Association" title="International Air Transport Association">IATA</a> code is <i>NRN</i> because of its official name <i>Flughafen Niederrhein</i>. The airport has had several different names in its history as a civil airport. The operators originally wanted to name it after the city of <a href="/wiki/D%C3%BCsseldorf" title="D\xc3\xbcsseldorf">D\xc3\xbcsseldorf</a>, but the significant distance of 83&#160;km (52&#160;mi) to that city, which already had two closer international airports (<a href="/wiki/D%C3%BCsseldorf_Airport" title="D\xc3\xbcsseldorf Airport">D\xc3\xbcsseldorf Airport</a> as well as <a href="/wiki/Cologne_Bonn_Airport" title="Cologne Bonn Airport">Cologne Bonn Airport</a>), resulted in the name being blocked by a court ruling that such a description would be likely to mislead passengers.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> However, Ryanair still refers to it as "<i>D\xc3\xbcsseldorf-Weeze"</i>. The airport is actually closer to the Dutch cities of <a href="/wiki/Venlo" title="Venlo">Venlo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nijmegen" title="Nijmegen">Nijmegen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Arnhem" title="Arnhem">Arnhem</a>, the German city of <a href="/wiki/Duisburg" title="Duisburg">Duisburg</a>, and the immediate Weeze area than D\xc3\xbcsseldorf.\n</p><p>Weeze was served by the short-lived, Dutch low-cost carrier <a href="/wiki/V_Bird" title="V Bird">V Bird</a>, which opened a base here and operated flights to <a href="/wiki/Berlin" title="Berlin">Berlin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Munich" title="Munich">Munich</a> and several international destinations, from its inception in 2003 until bankruptcy in 2004. During this time, passenger numbers doubled from 200,000 to 400,000 within a year.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> In February 2014, <a href="/wiki/Ryanair" title="Ryanair">Ryanair</a> announced the cancellation of 18 routes from Weeze for the 2014 summer season citing a lack of aircraft.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 2019, the airport faced severe financial difficulties due to a fall in passenger numbers by 30 percent over the previous year as a result of the cancellation of several <a href="/wiki/Ryanair" title="Ryanair">Ryanair</a> routes.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Facilities">Facilities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weeze_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Facilities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Weeze Airport has one passenger terminal building with restaurants, shops, and check-in facilities. The apron, which is to the west of the terminal building, features nine aircraft stands for mid-sized aircraft such as the <a href="/wiki/Boeing_737_Next_Generation#737-800" title="Boeing 737 Next Generation">Boeing 737-800</a>. As there are no <a href="/wiki/Jet_bridge" title="Jet bridge">jet bridges</a> due to the location of the apron to the west side of the terminal building instead in front of it \xe2\x80\x93 bus-boarding is used for six stands. Only three stands are close enough to the terminal to be accessed on foot.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Airlines_and_destinations">Airlines and destinations</span><span
