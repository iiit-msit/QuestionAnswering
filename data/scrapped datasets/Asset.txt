template">e</abbr></a></li></ul></div></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p>In <a href="/wiki/Financial_accountancy" class="mw-redirect" title="Financial accountancy">financial accounting</a>, an <b>asset</b> is any <a href="/wiki/Resource" title="Resource">resource</a> owned or controlled by a business or an economic entity. It is anything (tangible or intangible) that can be used to produce positive <a href="/wiki/Economic_value" class="mw-redirect" title="Economic value">economic value</a>. Assets represent value of <a href="/wiki/Ownership" title="Ownership">ownership</a> that can be converted into <a href="/wiki/Cash" title="Cash">cash</a> (although cash itself is also considered an asset).<sup id="cite_ref-O&#39;Sullivan_2003_272_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-O&#39;Sullivan_2003_272-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\nThe <a href="/wiki/Balance_sheet" title="Balance sheet">balance sheet</a> of a firm records the monetary<sup id="cite_ref-CPA_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CPA-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> value of the assets owned by that firm. It covers money and other valuables belonging to an individual or to a <a href="/wiki/Business" title="Business">business</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-O&#39;Sullivan_2003_272_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-O&#39;Sullivan_2003_272-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Assets can be grouped into two major classes: <a href="/wiki/Tangible_property" title="Tangible property">tangible assets</a> and <a href="/wiki/Intangible_assets" class="mw-redirect" title="Intangible assets">intangible assets</a>. Tangible assets contain various subclasses, including <a href="/wiki/Current_asset" title="Current asset">current assets</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fixed_asset" title="Fixed asset">fixed assets</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FinanceDict_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FinanceDict-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Current assets include <a href="/wiki/Inventory" title="Inventory">inventory</a>, <a href="/wiki/Accounts_receivable" title="Accounts receivable">accounts receivable</a>, while fixed assets include <a href="/wiki/Buildings" class="mw-redirect" title="Buildings">buildings</a> and <a href="/wiki/Capital_equipment" class="mw-redirect" title="Capital equipment">equipment</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-CPA_Finance_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CPA_Finance-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\nIntangible assets are non-physical resources and rights that have a value to the firm because they give the firm an advantage in the marketplace. Intangible assets include <a href="/wiki/Goodwill_(accounting)" title="Goodwill (accounting)">goodwill</a>, <a href="/wiki/Copyrights" class="mw-redirect" title="Copyrights">copyrights</a>, <a href="/wiki/Trademarks" class="mw-redirect" title="Trademarks">trademarks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Patents" class="mw-redirect" title="Patents">patents</a>, <a href="/wiki/Computer_program" title="Computer program">computer programs</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-CPA_Finance_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CPA_Finance-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> and financial assets, including financial investments, <a href="/wiki/Bond_(finance)" title="Bond (finance)">bonds</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stock" title="Stock">stocks</a>.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc" role="navigation" class="toctext">References</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Formal_definition">Formal definition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Asset&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Formal definition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>IFRS (International Financial Reporting Standards), the most widely used financial reporting system, defines: "An asset is a present economic resource controlled by the entity as a result of past events.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\nAn economic resource is a right that has the potential to produce economic benefits."<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The definition under US GAAP (Generally Accepted Accounting Principles used in the United States of America): "Assets are probable future economic benefits obtained or controlled by a particular entity as a result of past transactions or events."<sup id="cite_ref-auto_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Asset&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Con 6<sup id="cite_ref-auto_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> provides the following discussion of the nature of an asset:\n</p><p>An asset has three essential characteristics: \n<br />(a) it embodies a probable future benefit that involves a capacity, singly or in combination with other assets, to contribute directly or indirectly to future net cash inflows, \n<br />(b) a particular entity can obtain the benefit and control others\xe2\x80\x99 access to it, and \n<br />(c) the transaction or other event giving rise to the entity\xe2\x80\x99s right to or control of the benefit has already occurred.\n</p><p>This accounting definition of assets necessarily excludes employees because, while they have the capacity to generate economic benefits, an employer cannot control an employee.\n</p><p>Similarly, in <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">economics</a>, an asset is any form in which <a href="/wiki/Wealth" title="Wealth">wealth</a> can be held.\n</p><p>There is a growing analytical interest in assets and asset forms in other <a href="/wiki/Social_science" title="Social science">social sciences</a> too, especially in terms of how a variety of things (e.g., personality, personal data, ecosystems, etc.) can be turned into an asset.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Accounting">Accounting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Asset&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Accounting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Financial_accounting" title="Financial accounting">financial accounting</a> sense of the term, it is not necessary to be able to legally enforce the asset\'s benefit for qualifying a resource as being an asset, provided the entity can control its use by other means.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Accounting_equation" title="Accounting equation">accounting equation</a> is the mathematical structure of the <a href="/wiki/Balance_sheet" title="Balance sheet">balance sheet</a>. It relates assets, liabilities, and <a href="/wiki/Ownership_equity" class="mw-redirect" title="Ownership equity">owner\'s equity</a>:\n</p>\n<dl><dd>Assets = <a href="/wiki/Liability_(financial_accounting)" title="Liability (financial accounting)">Liabilities</a> + Capital (which for a corporation equals <i>owner\'s equity</i>)</dd>\n<dd>Liabilities = Assets \xe2\x88\x92 Capital</dd>\n<dd><a href="/wiki/Equity_(finance)" title="Equity (finance)">Equity</a> = Assets \xe2\x88\x92 Liabilities</dd></dl>\n<p>Assets are listed on the <a href="/wiki/Balance_sheet" title="Balance sheet">balance sheet</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> On a company\'s balance sheet certain divisions are required by <a href="/wiki/Generally_accepted_accounting_principles" class="mw-redirect" title="Generally accepted accounting principles">generally accepted accounting principles</a> (GAAP), which vary from country to country.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Assets can be divided into e.g. current assets and fixed assets, often with further subdivisions such as cash, receivables and inventory.\n</p><p>Assets are formally controlled and managed within larger organizations via the use of asset tracking tools. These monitor the purchasing, upgrading, servicing, licensing, disposal etc., of both physical and non-physical assets.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Current_assets">Current assets</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Asset&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Current assets">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Current_asset" title="Current asset">Current asset</a></div>\n<p>Current assets are cash and others that are expected to be converted to cash or consumed either in a year or in the operating cycle (whichever is longer), without disturbing the normal operations of a business. These assets are continually turned over in the course of a business during normal business activity. There are 5 major items included into current assets:\n</p>\n<ol><li><b><a href="/wiki/Cash_and_cash_equivalents" title="Cash and cash equivalents">Cash and cash equivalents</a></b> \xe2\x80\x93 it is the most <a href="/wiki/Market_liquidity" title="Market liquidity">liquid asset</a>, which includes <a href="/wiki/Currency" title="Currency">currency</a>, <a href="/wiki/Deposit_account" title="Deposit account">deposit accounts</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Negotiable_instrument" title="Negotiable instrument">negotiable instruments</a> (e.g., money orders, cheque, bank drafts).</li>\n<li><b><a href="/wiki/Investment" title="Investment">Short-term investments</a></b> \xe2\x80\x93 include securities bought and held for sale in the near future to generate income on short-term price differences (trading securities).</li>\n<li><b><a href="/wiki/Receivable" class="mw-redirect" title="Receivable">Receivables</a></b> \xe2\x80\x93 usually reported as net of allowance for non-collectable accounts.</li>\n<li><b><a href="/wiki/Inventory" title="Inventory">Inventory</a></b> \xe2\x80\x93 trading these assets is a normal business of a company. The inventory value reported on the <a href="/wiki/Balance_sheet" title="Balance sheet">balance sheet</a> is usually the historical cost or fair market value, whichever is lower. This is known as the "<a href="/wiki/Lower_of_cost_or_market" title="Lower of cost or market">lower of cost or market</a>" rule.</li>\n<li><b><a href="/wiki/Prepaid_expense" class="mw-redirect" title="Prepaid expense">Prepaid expenses</a></b> \xe2\x80\x93 these are expenses paid in cash and recorded as assets before they are used or consumed (common examples are insurance or office supplies). See also <a href="/wiki/Adjusting_entries" title="Adjusting entries">adjusting entries</a>.</li></ol>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Marketable_securities" class="mw-redirect" title="Marketable securities">Marketable securities</a>: Securities that can be converted into cash quickly at a reasonable price.\n</p><p>The phrase <i>net current assets</i> (also called <i><a href="/wiki/Working_capital" title="Working capital">working capital</a></i>) is often used and refers to the total of current assets less the total of current <a href="/wiki/Liability_(accounting)" class="mw-redirect" title="Liability (accounting)">liabilities</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Long-term_investments">Long-term investments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Asset&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Long-term investments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Often referred to simply as "investments". Long-term investments are to be held for many years and are not intended to be disposed of in the near future. This group usually consists of three types of investments&#160;:\n</p>\n<ol><li>Investments in securities such as bonds, common stock, or long-term notes.</li>\n<li>Investments in fixed assets not used in operations (e.g., land held for sale).</li>\n<li>Investments in special funds (e.g. sinking funds or pension funds).</li></ol>\n<p>Different forms of <a href="/wiki/Insurance" title="Insurance">insurance</a> may also be treated as long-term investments.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fixed_assets">Fixed assets</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Asset&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Fixed assets">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Fixed_asset" title="Fixed asset">Fixed asset</a></div>\n<p>Also referred to as PPE (property, plant, and equipment), these are purchased for continued and long-term use to earn <a href="/wiki/Profit_(economics)" title="Profit (economics)">profit</a> in a business. This group includes <a href="/wiki/Land_(economics)" title="Land (economics)">land</a>, <a href="/wiki/Building" title="Building">buildings</a>, <a href="/wiki/Machinery" class="mw-redirect" title="Machinery">machinery</a>, <a href="/wiki/Furniture" title="Furniture">furniture</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tool" title="Tool">tools</a>, <a href="/wiki/Information_technology" title="Information technology">IT</a> equipment (e.g., laptops), and certain wasting resources (e.g., timberland and <a href="/wiki/Mineral" title="Mineral">minerals</a>). They are written off against <a href="/wiki/Profit_(accounting)" title="Profit (accounting)">profits</a> over their anticipated life by charging <a href="/wiki/Depreciation" title="Depreciation">depreciation</a> expenses (with exception of land assets). Accumulated depreciation is shown in the face of the balance sheet or in the notes.\n</p><p>These are also called <a href="/wiki/Capital_asset" title="Capital asset">capital assets</a> in <a href="/wiki/Management_accounting" title="Management accounting">management accounting</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Intangible_assets">Intangible assets</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Asset&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Intangible assets">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Intangible_asset" title="Intangible asset">Intangible asset</a></div>\n<p>Intangible assets lack physical substance and usually are very hard to evaluate. They include <a href="/wiki/Patent" title="Patent">patents</a>, <a href="/wiki/Copyright" title="Copyright">copyrights</a>, <a href="/wiki/Franchising" title="Franchising">franchises</a> &amp; <a href="/wiki/License" title="License">licenses</a>, <a href="/wiki/Goodwill_(accounting)" title="Goodwill (accounting)">goodwill</a>, <a href="/wiki/Trademark" title="Trademark">trademarks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Trade_name" title="Trade name">trade names</a>, etc. These assets are (according to US GAAP) amortized to expense over 5 to 40 years with the exception of goodwill.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Website" title="Website">Websites</a> are treated differently in different countries and may fall under either tangible or intangible assets.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tangible_assets">Tangible assets</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Asset&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Tangible assets">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Tangible assets are those that have a physical substance, such as <a href="/wiki/Currency" title="Currency">currencies</a>, <a href="/wiki/Buildings" class="mw-redirect" title="Buildings">buildings</a>, <a href="/wiki/Real_estate" title="Real estate">real estate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vehicles" class="mw-redirect" title="Vehicles">vehicles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Inventory" title="Inventory">inventories</a>, <a href="/wiki/Capital_equipment" class="mw-redirect" title="Capital equipment">equipment</a>, <a href="/wiki/Private_collection" title="Private collection">art collections</a>, <a href="/wiki/Precious_metals" class="mw-redirect" title="Precious metals">precious metals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rare-earth_metals" class="mw-redirect" title="Rare-earth metals">rare-earth metals</a>, Industrial metals, and crops. The physical health of tangible assets deteriorate over time. As a result, asset managers use <a href="/wiki/Deterioration_modeling" title="Deterioration modeling">deterioration modeling</a> to predict the future conditions of assets.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Depreciation" title="Depreciation">Depreciation</a> is applied to tangible assets when those assets have an anticipated lifespan of more than one year. This process of depreciation is used instead of allocating the entire expense to one year.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Tangible assets such as art, furniture, stamps, gold, wine, toys and books are recognized as an asset class in their own right.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Many high-net-worth individuals will seek to include these tangible assets as part of their overall asset portfolio. This has created a need for tangible asset managers.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wasting_asset">Wasting asset</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Asset&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Wasting asset">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>A wasting asset is an asset that irreversibly declines in value over time. This could include vehicles and machinery, and in financial markets, options contracts which continually lose time value after purchase.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Mines and quarries in use are wasting assets.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> An asset classified as wasting may be treated differently for tax and other purposes than one that does not lose value; this may be accounted for by applying <a href="/wiki/Depreciation" title="Depreciation">depreciation</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Comparison:_current_assets.2C_liquid_assets_and_absolute_liquid_assets"></span><span class="mw-headline"
