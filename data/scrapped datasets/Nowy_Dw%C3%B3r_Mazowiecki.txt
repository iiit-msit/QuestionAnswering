style="font-size:85%">(<span class="unicode haudio"><span class="fn"><span style="white-space:nowrap;margin-right:.25em;"><a href="/wiki/File:Pl-Nowy_Dw%C3%B3r_Mazowiecki.ogg" title="About this inhabitants (2008). It is situated in the <a href="/wiki/Masovian_Voivodeship" title="Masovian Voivodeship">Masovian Voivodeship</a> (since 1999); previously, it was in <a href="/wiki/Warsaw_Voivodeship_(1975-1998)" class="mw-redirect" title="Warsaw Voivodeship (1975-1998)">Warszawa Voivodeship</a> (1975\xe2\x80\x931998).  It is the capital of <a href="/wiki/Nowy_Dw%C3%B3r_Mazowiecki_County" title="Nowy Dw\xc3\xb3r Mazowiecki County">Nowy Dw\xc3\xb3r Mazowiecki County</a>.\n</p><p>One of its districts is Modlin, created from incorporating the former village of <a href="/wiki/Modlin_(village)" class="mw-redirect" title="Modlin (village)">Modlin</a> into the growing town in 1961. <a href="/wiki/Modlin_Fortress" title="Modlin Fortress">Modlin Fortress</a> is now also part of the city. Many structures related to the fort can be seen in the Modlin district, including ruins from defensive structures designed by <a href="/wiki/Napoleon_Bonaparte" class="mw-redirect" title="Napoleon Bonaparte">Napoleon Bonaparte</a> <sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> and Tsarist Blocks built between 1899 - 1901 to house soldiers of the Russian army, and which are still in use as private flats today.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Germans occupied the town beginning in September 1939.  They immediately began to persecute the Jewish population.  Many Jews fled to Warsaw, others to Soviet occupied territory in the east. From 1941-1942, the Germans set up a <a href="/wiki/Jewish_ghettos_in_German-occupied_Poland" title="Jewish ghettos in German-occupied Poland">ghetto</a> between the four streets Na\xc5\x82\xc4\x99cza, Warszawska, Mazowiecka, and Piaskowa. Most of the 9000 or more Jewish inhabitants or the town were murdered.  Some were murdered in the environs of the town; most were sent to <a href="/wiki/Auschwitz_concentration_camp" title="Auschwitz concentration camp">Auschwitz concentration camp</a>. <a href="/wiki/Typhus" title="Typhus">Typhus</a> was rampant in the ghetto taking many lives there. Only about 400 Nowy Dwor Jews survived; most of the survivors had fled to Soviet occupied territory and later to the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> itself, where they stayed throughout the war.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Holocaust" class="mw-redirect" title="Holocaust">Holocaust</a> survivor Yehudis Pshenitse has recounted the efforts of a parish priest from Nowy Dw\xc3\xb3r to save her life after the murder of more than 2000 <a href="/wiki/Judaism" title="Judaism">Jews</a> in <a href="/wiki/Rembert%C3%B3w" title="Rembert\xc3\xb3w">Rembert\xc3\xb3w</a> <a href="/wiki/Jewish_ghettos_in_Europe" title="Jewish ghettos in Europe">ghetto</a> in August 1942. Hiding her in his cellar, he gave her false papers identifying her as a <a href="/wiki/Christians" title="Christians">Christian</a>. Betrayed to the <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">German</a> occupying forces, the priest was tortured. He was released, but mortally wounded. Pshenitse described how he blessed her before dying: "Once again, he asked [his housekeeper] that I be hidden in a safe place, and then he died." The housekeeper took her to Modlin, where she was able to survive, living "by her own wits, posing as a Christian child."<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israeli</a> city of <a href="/wiki/Holon" title="Holon">Holon</a> has a Nowy Dw\xc3\xb3r Street (\xd7\xa8\xd7\x97\xd7\x95\xd7\x91 \xd7\xa0\xd7\x95\xd7\x91\xd7\x99\xd7\x93\xd7\x91\xd7\x95\xd7\xa8). The name was given at the request of survivors of the Nowy Dw\xc3\xb3r Jewish community, who arrived in Holon after 1945.\n</p><p>Nowy Dw\xc3\xb3r Mazowiecki is also renowned for its wooden architecture, which is still faintly visible within the city limits. Some of the wooden houses and villas date back to the late 18th-century. The name <i>Nowy Dw\xc3\xb3r</i> itself, which literally means "New Manor" in English, relates to the <a href="/wiki/Manorialism" title="Manorialism">manor</a>-like architecture of the region.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc" role="navigation" aria-labelledby="mw-toc-heading"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" style="display:none"
