Art. I \xc2\xa7 8</a></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p><i><b>Feist Publications, Inc., v. Rural Telephone Service Co.</b></i>, 499 U.S. 340 (1991), was a decision by the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_the_United_States" title="Supreme Court of the United States">Supreme Court of the United States</a> establishing that information alone without a minimum of original creativity cannot be protected by copyright.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> In the case appealed, Feist had copied information from Rural\'s <a href="/wiki/Telephone_directory" title="Telephone directory">telephone listings</a> to include in its own, after Rural had refused to license the information. Rural sued for <a href="/wiki/Copyright_infringement" title="Copyright infringement">copyright infringement</a>. The Court ruled that information contained in Rural\'s phone directory was not copyrightable and that therefore no infringement existed.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc" role="navigation" class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Feist_Publications,_Inc.,_v._Rural_Telephone_Service_Co.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Telefonbog_ubt-1.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/Telefonbog_ubt-1.JPG/220px-Telefonbog_ubt-1.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="141" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/Telefonbog_ubt-1.JPG/330px-Telefonbog_ubt-1.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/Telefonbog_ubt-1.JPG/440px-Telefonbog_ubt-1.JPG 2x" data-file-width="800" data-file-height="513" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Telefonbog_ubt-1.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The "white pages" of a telephone directory</div></div></div>\n<p>Rural Telephone Service Company, Inc. is a <a href="/wiki/Telephone_cooperative" class="mw-redirect" title="Telephone cooperative">telephone cooperative</a> providing services for areas in northwest Kansas, with headquarters in the small town of <a href="/wiki/Lenora,_Kansas" title="Lenora, Kansas">Lenora</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Norton_County,_Kansas" title="Norton County, Kansas">Norton County</a>. The company was under a statutory obligation to compile and distribute a "<a href="/wiki/White_pages" class="mw-redirect" title="White pages">white pages</a>" phone directory of all its customers free of charge as a condition of its monopoly franchise.\n</p><p>Feist Publications, Inc. specialized in compiling telephone directories from larger geographic areas than Rural from other areas of Kansas. It had licensed the directory of 11 other local directories, with Rural being the only holdout in the region. Despite Rural\'s denial of a license to Feist, Feist copied 4,000 entries from Rural\'s directory. Because Rural had placed a small number of <a href="/wiki/Fictitious_entry" title="Fictitious entry">phony entries</a> to detect copying, Feist was caught.\n</p><p>Before this case, the substance of copyright in United States law followed the <a href="/wiki/Sweat_of_the_brow" title="Sweat of the brow">sweat of the brow</a> doctrine, which gave copyright to anyone who invested significant amount of time and energy into their work. At trial and appeal level the courts followed this doctrine, siding with Rural.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ruling_of_the_court">Ruling of the court</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Feist_Publications,_Inc.,_v._Rural_Telephone_Service_Co.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Ruling of the court">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The ruling of the court was written by Justice <a href="/wiki/Sandra_Day_O%27Connor" title="Sandra Day O&#39;Connor">Sandra Day O\'Connor</a>. It examined the purpose of copyright and explained the standard of copyrightability as based on <a href="/wiki/Originality" title="Originality">originality</a>.\n</p><p>The case centered on two well-established principles in United States copyright law: that facts are not copyrightable, and that compilations of facts can be.\n</p><p>"There is an undeniable tension between these two propositions", O\'Connor wrote in her opinion. "Many compilations consist of nothing but raw data\xe2\x80\x94i.e. wholly factual information not accompanied by any original expression. On what basis may one claim a copyright upon such work? Common sense tells us that 100 uncopyrightable facts do not magically change their status when gathered together in one place. \xe2\x80\xa6 The key to resolving the tension lies in understanding why facts are not copyrightable: The <i>sine qua non</i> of copyright is originality."\n</p><p>Rural claimed a collection copyright in its directory. The court clarified that the intent of copyright law was not, as claimed by Rural and some lower courts, to reward the efforts of persons collecting information\xe2\x80\x94the so-called "<a href="/wiki/Sweat_of_the_brow" title="Sweat of the brow">sweat of the brow</a>" or "industrious collection" doctrine\xe2\x80\x94but rather "to promote the Progress of Science and useful Arts" (<a href="/wiki/Copyright_Clause" title="Copyright Clause">U.S. Const. Art. I, \xc2\xa7 8, cl. 8</a>)\xe2\x80\x94that is, to encourage creative expression.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Threshold_of_originality" title="Threshold of originality">standard for creativity</a> is extremely low. It need not be novel; it need only possess a "spark" or "minimal degree" of creativity to be protected by copyright.\n</p><p>In regard to collections of facts, O\'Connor wrote that copyright can apply only to the creative aspects of collection: the creative choice of what data to include or exclude, the order and style in which the information is presented, etc.\xe2\x80\x94not to the information itself. If Feist were to take the directory and rearrange it, it would destroy the copyright owned in the data. "Notwithstanding a valid copyright, a subsequent compiler remains free to use the facts contained in another\'s publication to aid in preparing a competing work, so long as the competing work does not feature the same selection and arrangement", she wrote.\n</p><p>The court held that Rural\'s directory was nothing more than an alphabetic list of all subscribers to its service, which it was required to compile under law, and that no creative expression was involved. That Rural spent considerable time and money collecting the data was irrelevant to copyright law, and Rural\'s copyright claim was dismissed.\n</p><p>All the justices joined O\'Connor\'s opinion except <a href="/wiki/Harry_Blackmun" title="Harry Blackmun">Harry Blackmun</a>, who concurred only in judgment, but did not file a separate opinion.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Implications">Implications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Feist_Publications,_Inc.,_v._Rural_Telephone_Service_Co.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Implications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Idea-expression_divide" class="mw-redirect" title="Idea-expression divide">Idea-expression divide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Threshold_of_originality" title="Threshold of originality">Threshold of originality</a></div>\n<p>The ruling has major implications for any project that serves as a collection of knowledge. Information (<a href="/wiki/Fact" title="Fact">facts</a>, discoveries, etc.) from any source is fair game, but cannot contain any of the "expressive" content added by the source <a href="/wiki/Author" title="Author">author</a>. That includes not only the author\'s own comments, but also their choice of which facts to cover, which links to make among the bits of information, the order of presentation (unless it is something obvious like alphabetical), evaluations of the quality of various pieces of information, or anything else that might be considered the author\'s "original creative work" rather than mere facts.\n</p><p>For example, a <a href="/wiki/Recipe" title="Recipe">recipe</a> is a process, and not copyrightable, but the words used to describe it are; see <a href="/wiki/Idea-expression_divide" class="mw-redirect" title="Idea-expression divide">idea-expression divide</a> and <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Publications_International_v_Meredith_Corp.&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Publications International v Meredith Corp. (page does not exist)">Publications International v Meredith Corp.</a></i> (1996).<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Therefore, a recipe can be rewritten with different wording and be published without infringing copyright. If an individual rewrote every recipe from a particular <a href="/wiki/Cookbook" title="Cookbook">cookbook</a>, they might be found to have infringed the author\'s copyright in the choice of recipes and their "coordination" and "presentation", even if they used different words, but the West decisions below suggest that this is unlikely unless there is some significant creativity carried over from the original presentation. A sufficiently novel, useful, and unique (i.e. non-obvious) recipe can be granted protection under <a href="/wiki/Patent" title="Patent">patent</a> law.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><i>Feist</i> proved most important in the area of copyright of legal case law publications. One might assume that the text of U.S. case law is in <a href="/wiki/Public_domain" title="Public domain">public domain</a>, but <a href="/wiki/Thomson_West" class="mw-redirect" title="Thomson West">Thomson West</a> claimed a copyright as to the first-page citations and internal pinpoint page citations of its versions of court opinions (case law) found in its printed versions of the case law ("West\'s citation claims"). West also claimed a copyright in the text of its versions of the case law, which included parallel citations and typographical corrections ("West\'s text claims"). The text claim would have barred anyone from copying the text of a case from a West case law reporter, since the copied text would include West enhancements to which West claimed copyright.\n</p><p>In a 1986 pre-<i>Feist</i> case, West\'s citation <a href="/wiki/Copyright" title="Copyright">copyright</a> claim was affirmed by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Court_of_Appeals_for_the_Eighth_Circuit" title="United States Court of Appeals for the Eighth Circuit">U.S. Court of Appeals for the Eighth Circuit</a> in a preliminary injunction case brought by West against Mead Data, owner of Lexis (<i><a href="/w/index.php?title=West_Publishing_Co._v._Mead_Data_Central&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="West Publishing Co. v. Mead Data Central (page does not exist)">West v. Mead</a></i>),<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> but in a case commenced in 1994 in the <a href="/wiki/United_States_District_Court_for_the_Southern_District_of_New_York" title="United States District Court for the Southern District of New York">U.S. District Court for the Southern District of New York</a>, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Court_of_Appeals_for_the_Second_Circuit" title="United States Court of Appeals for the Second Circuit">U.S. Court of Appeals for the Second Circuit</a> found <i>Feist</i> to have undermined the reasoning in <i>West v. Mead</i>.  West\'s citation claims were challenged in 1994 by legal publisher Matthew Bender &amp; Company and by a small CD-ROM publisher HyperLaw, Inc. HyperLaw intervened, joining Matthew Bender in the citation challenge and separately challenging West\'s text copyright claims. In 1998, the Second Circuit found that West did not have a protectable copyright interest in its first-page citations or its internal pagination citations (<i>Matthew Bender v. West</i>, Citation Appeal).<sup id="cite_ref-cite-ca2_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cite-ca2-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The Second Circuit thereby rejected a Minnesota district court\'s 1996 determination (<i>Oasis Publishing Co. v. West Publishing Co.</i>, 924 F.Supp. 918, D. Minn.) that <i>Feist</i> does not change the outcome of <i>West</i>.\n</p><p>In the same case, but in separate decisions in which Matthew Bender was not involved, HyperLaw successfully challenged West\'s text claims. Judge John S. Martin ruled HyperLaw\'s favor against West in the May 1996 U.S. District Court decision <i>Matthew Bender v. West</i>, No. 94 Civ. 0589, 1997 WL 266972 (S.D.N.Y. May 19, 1997), <i>aff\'d</i>, 158 F. 3d 674 (2nd Cir. 1998), <i>cert. denied sub. nom.</i> <i>West v. Hyperlaw</i>, 526 U.S. 1154 (1999).<sup id="cite_ref-text-sdny_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-text-sdny-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> West lost to HyperLaw on appeal to the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Second Circuit and the U.S. Supreme Court denied <a href="/wiki/Certiorari" title="Certiorari">certiorari</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-text-ca2_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-text-ca2-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>After <i>West v. Mead</i>, Mead Data and Lexis were acquired by <a href="/wiki/Reed_Elsevier" class="mw-redirect" title="Reed Elsevier">Reed Elsevier</a>, a large English-Dutch based publisher. During <i>Matthew Bender v. West</i>, Reed Elsevier and Matthew Bender entered into a strategic relationship, culminating in Reed Elsevier\'s acquisition of Matthew Bender in 1998, just after the Second Circuit appeals were argued. Reed Elsevier was now on West\'s side and filed an amicus brief opposing HyperLaw and supporting West. Thus, though the name of the case might suggest that Matthew Bender challenged West on the text claim, by the middle of the case Matthew Bender was on West\'s side on the text issue. Reed Elsevier\'s support of West\'s claims to a copyright in text was consistent with the initiatives, discussed below, to sidestep <i>Feist</i> by implementing database protection, through legislation and treaties discussed below. Similarly, during the case, West was acquired by the Canadian-based international publisher the Thomson Corporation.\n</p><p>Another case covering this area is <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Assessment_Technologies_v._Wiredata&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Assessment Technologies v. Wiredata (page does not exist)">Assessment Technologies v. Wiredata</a></i> (2003),<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> in which the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Court_of_Appeals_for_the_Seventh_Circuit" title="United States Court of Appeals for the Seventh Circuit">Seventh Circuit Court of Appeals</a> ruled that a copyright holder in a compilation of public domain data cannot use that copyright to prevent others from using the underlying public domain data, but may only restrict the specific format of the compilation if that format is itself sufficiently creative. <i>Assessment Technologies</i> also held that it is a <a href="/wiki/Fair_use" title="Fair use">fair use</a> of a copyrighted work to <a href="/wiki/Reverse_engineer" class="mw-redirect" title="Reverse engineer">reverse engineer</a> that work in order to gain access to uncopyrightable facts. <i>Assessment Technologies</i> also created new law, stating that it is a <a href="/wiki/Copyright_misuse" title="Copyright misuse">copyright misuse</a> and an <a href="/wiki/Abuse_of_process" title="Abuse of process">abuse of process</a> to attempt to use a <a href="/wiki/Contract" title="Contract">contract</a> or <a href="/wiki/License_agreement" class="mw-redirect" title="License agreement">license agreement</a> based on one\'s copyright to protect uncopyrightable facts.\n</p><p>In the late 1990s, Congress attempted to pass laws to protect collections of <a href="/wiki/Data" title="Data">data</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> but the measures failed.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> By contrast, the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> has a <i><a href="/wiki/Sui_generis" title="Sui generis">sui generis</a></i> (specific to that type of work) <a href="/wiki/Database_right" title="Database right">intellectual property protection for collections of data</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_countries">Other countries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Feist_Publications,_Inc.,_v._Rural_Telephone_Service_Co.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Other countries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Sui_generis_database_rights" class="mw-redirect" title="Sui generis database rights">Sui generis database rights</a></div>\n<p>The applicability of copyright to phone directories has come up in several other countries.\n</p><p>In Canada, the appeal-level case of <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tele-Direct_(Publications)_Inc._v._American_Business_Information_Inc.&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Tele-Direct (Publications) Inc. v. American Business Information Inc. (page does not exist)">Tele-Direct (Publications) Inc. v. American Business Information Inc.</a></i> (1997) 76 C.P.R. (3d) 296 (F.C.A.) reached a similar result to <i>Feist</i>\'s. But the Supreme Court partially backed away from the originality doctrine in <i><a href="/wiki/CCH_Canadian_Ltd._v._Law_Society_of_Upper_Canada" class="mw-redirect" title="CCH Canadian Ltd. v. Law Society of Upper Canada">CCH Canadian Ltd. v. Law Society of Upper Canada</a></i>. Under <i>CCH</i> <i>Canadian</i>, someone may assert protection in a database where the facts are themselves not copied from another source. For example, a person may assert protection in a collection of their own recipes, but may not assert protection in a database of facts about persons and their ancestry compiled from census records.\n</p><p>In Australia, the Federal Court decision <a href="/wiki/Telstra_v_Desktop_Marketing_Systems" class="mw-redirect" title="Telstra v Desktop Marketing Systems"><i>Desktop Marketing Systems v Telstra</i></a><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> followed the UK approach in <i><a href="/wiki/Walter_v_Lane" title="Walter v Lane">Walter v Lane</a></i> and ruled that copyright law did, in fact, follow the "sweat of the brow" doctrine. But <i>Desktop v Telstra</i> held, like <i>CCH Canadian</i>, that collections of facts must not be copied from other sources to be eligible for protection. In 2010, the Telstra decision was overturned by Justice Gordon in <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Telstra_v_Phone_Directories&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Telstra v Phone Directories (page does not exist)">Telstra v Phone Directories</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> following the decision of the <a href="/wiki/High_Court_of_Australia" title="High Court of Australia">High Court</a> in <a href="/wiki/Australian_Digital_Alliance#IceTV_Pty_Ltd_v_Nine_Network_Australia_Pty_Ltd_(2009)" title="Australian Digital Alliance">IceTV v Nine Network</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Relation_with_treaties">Relation with treaties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Feist_Publications,_Inc.,_v._Rural_Telephone_Service_Co.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Relation with treaties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Congress has been considering whether to implement a <a href="/wiki/Treaty" title="Treaty">treaty</a> negotiated at the <a href="/wiki/World_Trade_Organization" title="World Trade Organization">World Trade Organization</a>. Part of the <a href="/wiki/Uruguay_Round" title="Uruguay Round">Uruguay Round Agreement</a> resulted in text that reads, in Part II, Section 1, Article 10:\n</p>\n<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r996844942">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>Compilations of data or other material, whether in machine readable or other form, which by reason of the selection or arrangement of their contents constitute intellectual creations shall be protected as such. Such protection, which shall not extend to the data or material itself, shall be without prejudice to any copyright subsisting in the data or material itself.</p></blockquote>\n<p>The text mirrors that of Article 2(5) of the <a href="/wiki/Berne_Convention_for_the_Protection_of_Literary_and_Artistic_Works" class="mw-redirect" title="Berne Convention for the Protection of Literary and Artistic Works">Berne Convention</a>, which applies to "collections of literary or artistic works".\n</p><p>This treaty provision is broadly in line with the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Copyright_Act" class="mw-redirect" title="United States Copyright Act">United States Copyright Act</a> and the Act\'s <a href="/wiki/Case_law" title="Case law">case law</a>, which protects compilations of data whose "selection and arrangement" is sufficiently original. <i>See</i> <a href="/wiki/Title_17_of_the_United_States_Code" title="Title 17 of the United States Code">17&#160;U.S.C.</a>&#160;<a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/17/101">\xc2\xa7&#160;101</a> ("compilation" as defined by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Copyright_Act" class="mw-redirect" title="United States Copyright Act">United States Copyright Act</a> includes compilations of data). The standard for such originality is fairly low; for example, business listings have been found to meet this standard when deciding which companies should be listed and categorizing those companies required some kind of expert judgment. <i>See Key Publ\'ns, Inc. v. Chinatown Today Pub. Enters.</i>, 945 F.2d 509 (2d Cir. 1991) (applying <i>Feist</i>). As such, implementation of this treaty would not overrule <i>Feist</i>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span
