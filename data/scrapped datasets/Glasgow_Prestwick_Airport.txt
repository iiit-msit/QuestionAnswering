Authority</a><sup id="cite_ref-stats_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stats-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup></div></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p><b>Glasgow Prestwick Airport</b> (<a href="/wiki/IATA_airport_code" title="IATA airport code">IATA</a>: <b>PIK</b>, <a href="/wiki/ICAO_airport_code" title="ICAO airport code">ICAO</a>: <b>EGPK</b>) is an <a href="/wiki/International_airport" title="International airport">international airport</a> serving the west of Scotland, situated one nautical mile (1.9&#160;km; 1.2&#160;mi) northeast of the town of <a href="/wiki/Prestwick" title="Prestwick">Prestwick</a> in <a href="/wiki/South_Ayrshire" title="South Ayrshire">South Ayrshire</a> and 32 miles (51&#160;km) from <a href="/wiki/Glasgow" title="Glasgow">Glasgow</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-aip_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aip-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> It is the less busy of the two airports serving the area, the busier one being <a href="/wiki/Glasgow_Airport" title="Glasgow Airport">Glasgow Airport</a>, which is within the Greater Glasgow conurbation itself, in the town of <a href="/wiki/Paisley,_Renfrewshire" title="Paisley, Renfrewshire">Paisley</a> in <a href="/wiki/Renfrewshire" title="Renfrewshire">Renfrewshire</a>. The airport serves the urban cluster surrounding <a href="/wiki/Ayr" title="Ayr">Ayr</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Kilmarnock" title="Kilmarnock">Kilmarnock</a>, Irvine, <a href="/wiki/Ardrossan" title="Ardrossan">Ardrossan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Troon" title="Troon">Troon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Saltcoats" title="Saltcoats">Saltcoats</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stevenston" title="Stevenston">Stevenston</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kilwinning" title="Kilwinning">Kilwinning</a> and <a href="/wiki/Prestwick" title="Prestwick">Prestwick</a> itself.\n</p><p>Glasgow Prestwick is Scotland\'s fifth-busiest airport in terms of passenger traffic, after <a href="/wiki/Edinburgh_Airport" title="Edinburgh Airport">Edinburgh</a>, <a href="/wiki/Glasgow_Airport" title="Glasgow Airport">Glasgow</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aberdeen_Airport" title="Aberdeen Airport">Aberdeen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Inverness_Airport" title="Inverness Airport">Inverness</a>, although it is the largest in terms of land area. Passenger traffic peaked at 2.4&#160;million in 2007 following a decade of rapid growth, driven in part by the boom in <a href="/wiki/Low-cost_carrier" title="Low-cost carrier">low-cost carriers</a>, particularly <a href="/wiki/Ryanair" title="Ryanair">Ryanair</a>, which uses the airport as an operating base. In recent years, passenger traffic has declined; around 670,000 passengers passed through the airport in 2016.<sup id="cite_ref-stats_2-1" class="reference"><a class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Glasgow_Prestwick_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Passenger facilities were added in 1938. These were used until further investment made Prestwick compatible with jet transportation. The October 1946 USAAF diagram shows 6,600 feet (2,000&#160;m) runway 14/32 with 4,500 feet (1,400&#160;m) runway 8/26 crossing just west of its midpoint. In 1958, runway 13/31 was 7,000 feet (2,100&#160;m) long; in May 1960, the runway\'s extension to 9,800 feet (3,000&#160;m) opened.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> \n</p><p>A parallel <a href="/wiki/Taxiway" title="Taxiway">taxiway</a>, link road and an all-new terminal building were opened by the <a href="/wiki/Queen_Elizabeth_The_Queen_Mother" title="Queen Elizabeth The Queen Mother">Queen Mother</a> in 1964. The extension of Runway 13/31 caused considerable disruption to road users, for the main road from Monkton into Prestwick now crossed the tarmac of the runway. This was controlled by a "level crossing" system until a new perimeter road was completed.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Commercial_use">Commercial use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Glasgow_Prestwick_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Commercial use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In 1945, regular <a href="/wiki/Transatlantic_flight" title="Transatlantic flight">transatlantic</a> commercial flights began between Prestwick and <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Military_use">Military use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Glasgow_Prestwick_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Military use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In the <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">Second World War</a> the <a href="/wiki/RAF" class="mw-redirect" title="RAF">RAF</a> controlled trans-Atlantic flights from Prestwick.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Until February 2016 part of the Prestwick site was occupied by the Royal Navy <a href="/wiki/Fleet_Air_Arm" title="Fleet Air Arm">Fleet Air Arm</a> with <a href="/wiki/RNAS_Prestwick" class="mw-redirect" title="RNAS Prestwick">RNAS Prestwick</a>, officially known by the Royal Navy as <a href="/wiki/HMS_Gannet_SAR_Flight" class="mw-redirect" title="HMS Gannet SAR Flight">HMS <i>Gannet</i></a>, where a detachment of three <a href="/wiki/Westland_Sea_King" title="Westland Sea King">Sea Kings</a> provided a <a href="/wiki/Search_and_rescue" title="Search and rescue">search and rescue</a> role, covering one of the largest SAR areas of the UK including Ben Nevis, the Lakes, Northern Ireland and 200 nautical miles (370&#160;km; 230&#160;mi) past the Irish coast. Additionally, Gannet SAR provided a medical evacuation service to the Scottish island communities. Personnel at the base numbered 15 officers, 11 ratings, 28 civil servants and 50 civilian staff. The crews regularly featured as part of the popular <a href="/wiki/Five_(TV_channel)" class="mw-redirect" title="Five (TV channel)">Channel 5</a> documentary series <i><a href="/wiki/Highland_Emergency" title="Highland Emergency">Highland Emergency</a></i>. 2009 saw the unit break a new record as they were tasked to 447 call-outs, 20% of the UK\'s total military SAR call outs for 2009 and making them, for the second year in succession, the busiest Search &amp; Rescue base in the UK.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>There was controversy over the airport\'s use in the CIA\'s <a href="/wiki/Extraordinary_rendition" title="Extraordinary rendition">extraordinary rendition</a> flights, as aircraft had used the airport as a stop-over point.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Since November 2013, when the Scottish government took control of the facility, service contracts have been established with the <a href="/wiki/USAF" class="mw-redirect" title="USAF">USAF</a>, <a href="/wiki/USN" class="mw-redirect" title="USN">USN</a>, <a href="/wiki/USMC" class="mw-redirect" title="USMC">USMC</a>, <a href="/wiki/Defense_Logistics_Agency" title="Defense Logistics Agency">Defense Logistics Agency</a> and <a href="/wiki/Air_National_Guard" title="Air National Guard">National Guard</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Matthew Hudson in a "dramatic rescue".<sup id="cite_ref-ayrshirepost.net_14-0" class="reference"><a past the airfield, making it the first Scottish airport with its own railway station.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> In her book about Prestwick Airport, South Ayrshire councillor Ann Galbraith writes about this tough time in the airport\'s history, saying that "if it hadn\'t been for Matthew Hudson the airport wouldn\'t be here today".<sup id="cite_ref-ayrshirepost.net_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ayrshirepost.net-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> \n</p><p>In 1994, Irish budget airline <a href="/wiki/Ryanair" title="Ryanair">Ryanair</a> opened a route to the airport from <a href="/wiki/Dublin_airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Dublin airport">Dublin</a>, followed by a second route in 1995 to <a href="/wiki/Stansted_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Stansted Airport">London Stansted</a>. In 1998, a third route to <a href="/wiki/Paris-Beauvais" class="mw-redirect" title="Paris-Beauvais">Paris-Beauvais</a> was introduced and the airport was sold by Hudson to the Scottish transport company <a href="/wiki/Stagecoach_Group" title="Stagecoach Group">Stagecoach Group</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2000s">2000s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Glasgow_Prestwick_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 2000s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In 2001, the airport was purchased by <a href="/wiki/Infratil" title="Infratil">Infratil</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> a New Zealand company and majority owner of <a href="/wiki/Wellington_International_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Wellington International Airport">Wellington International Airport</a>. Infratil also owned <a href="/wiki/Manston_Airport" title="Manston Airport">Manston Airport</a> until November 2013. Manston was sold to a shell company owned by <a href="/wiki/Ann_Gloag" title="Ann Gloag">Ann Gloag</a>, a co-founder of <a href="/wiki/Stagecoach_Group" title="Stagecoach Group">Stagecoach</a>, Prestwick\'s previous owner. In April 2005, Infratil completed a major refurbishment of the terminal building, and <a href="/wiki/Rebranded" class="mw-redirect" title="Rebranded">rebranded</a> the airport using the phrase "pure dead brilliant", taken straight from the <a href="/wiki/Glasgow_patter" title="Glasgow patter">Glasgow patter</a>. Some of the rebranding has been controversial, in particular the redecoration of the airport bar. The bar was rebranded in February 2006 with a logo depicting a man in a <a href="/wiki/Kilt" title="Kilt">kilt</a>, unconscious with an empty bottle of <a href="/wiki/Whisky" title="Whisky">whisky</a>. \n</p><p>Despite objections that it promoted the wrong image of Scotland to foreign visitors and embarrassed local travellers, the airport management insisted the logo was "fun and visually stimulating". However, it was removed a matter of weeks after installation, after the South Ayrshire Licensing Board said the logo trivialised excessive drinking.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> The "pure dead brilliant" branding was removed from the main terminal building in January 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Since 2007, the airport has occasionally been used by the <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a> TV programme <i><a href="/wiki/Top_Gear_(2002_TV_series)" title="Top Gear (2002 TV series)">Top Gear</a></i> as the location for various stunts and experiments. The best-known stunt was a scene similar to one featured in the film <i><a href="/wiki/Casino_Royale_(2006_film)" title="Casino Royale (2006 film)">Casino Royale</a></i> and featured both a <a href="/wiki/Ford_Mondeo" title="Ford Mondeo">Ford Mondeo</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Citro%C3%ABn_2CV" title="Citro\xc3\xabn 2CV">Citro\xc3\xabn 2CV</a> parked behind the engines of a <a href="/wiki/Virgin_Atlantic" title="Virgin Atlantic">Virgin Atlantic</a> <a href="/wiki/Boeing_747-400" title="Boeing 747-400">Boeing 747-400</a>, in an experiment to investigate whether the thrust from the aircraft\'s four jet engines really could lift a car off the ground.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The car park and <a href="/wiki/A79_road" class="mw-redirect" title="A79 road">A79</a> outside the terminal building have been reconstructed to comply with <a href="/wiki/UK_Government" class="mw-redirect" title="UK Government">governmental</a> movement and access restrictions mandated in the aftermath of the <a href="/wiki/2007_Glasgow_International_Airport_attack" class="mw-redirect" title="2007 Glasgow International Airport attack">Glasgow International Airport terrorist attack</a>. According to a 2008 Master Plan, the departure lounge is at capacity and congested during peak operations. The plan proposes "a central pier that provides adequate circulation and waiting space prior to boarding the aircraft" to cope with a continuing increase in passenger departures.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2010s">2010s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Glasgow_Prestwick_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: 2010s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Scottish_International_Airshow_2017_(36200707763).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/Scottish_International_Airshow_2017_%2836200707763%29.jpg/220px-Scottish_International_Airshow_2017_%2836200707763%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/Scottish_International_Airshow_2017_%2836200707763%29.jpg/330px-Scottish_International_Airshow_2017_%2836200707763%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/Scottish_International_Airshow_2017_%2836200707763%29.jpg/440px-Scottish_International_Airshow_2017_%2836200707763%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1280" data-file-height="853" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Scottish_International_Airshow_2017_(36200707763).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Glasgow Prestwick hosts the Scottish International Airshow</div></div></div> \n<p>On 8 March 2012, the airport owner Infratil announced that it planned to sell the airfield. The airport remained unsold until October 2013 when the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Government" title="Scottish Government">Scottish Government</a> announced it was in negotiations to take the airport back into <a href="/wiki/Nationalisation" class="mw-redirect" title="Nationalisation">public ownership</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-infratil.com_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-infratil.com-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> Subsequently, the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Government" title="Scottish Government">Scottish Government</a> bought the airport on 22 November 2013 for <span style="white-space: nowrap">\xc2\xa31</span>&#32;(equivalent to \xc2\xa31.15 in 2019), Infratil having incurred annual losses of \xc2\xa32,000,000. It is expected that the airport will continue to operate as normal and there will be no job losses. Then-<a href="/wiki/Deputy_First_Minister_of_Scotland" title="Deputy First Minister of Scotland">Deputy First Minister</a> <a href="/wiki/Nicola_Sturgeon" title="Nicola Sturgeon">Nicola Sturgeon</a> told <a href="/wiki/BBC_Scotland" title="BBC Scotland">BBC Scotland</a> that work would now begin for "turning Prestwick around and making it a viable enterprise".<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>On 1 April 2014, the public petition committee at <a href="/wiki/Holyrood,_Edinburgh" title="Holyrood, Edinburgh">Holyrood</a> heard that <i>The <a href="/wiki/Robert_Burns_World_Federation" title="Robert Burns World Federation">Robert Burns World Federation</a></i> wished to rename the airport to <a href="/wiki/Robert_Burns" title="Robert Burns">Robert Burns</a> International Airport.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> In June 2014, Ryanair announced the relocation of some routes from Prestwick to <a href="/wiki/Glasgow_International_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Glasgow International Airport">Glasgow International Airport</a> by October 2014; included among them were flights to <a href="/wiki/Warsaw" title="Warsaw">Warsaw</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dublin" title="Dublin">Dublin</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In November 2014 <a href="/wiki/Donald_Trump" title="Donald Trump">Donald Trump</a> signed a partnership agreement with Prestwick making it the Scottish base for all Trump Aviation Operations, in order to service his <a href="/wiki/Trump_Turnberry" class="mw-redirect" title="Trump Turnberry">Trump Turnberry</a> golf resort 20 miles away.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>As part of the privatisation of the UK\'s search and rescue service, <a href="/wiki/Bristow_Helicopters" title="Bristow Helicopters">Bristow Helicopters</a> based two <a href="/wiki/Sikorsky_S-92" title="Sikorsky S-92">Sikorsky S-92</a> helicopters in a new hangar at <a href="/wiki/HMS_Gannet_(stone_frigate)" title="HMS Gannet (stone frigate)">HMS <i>Gannet</i></a>. The handover took place in January 2016. In March 2016, the airport revealed new branding and a new look to the inside and outside of the airport building.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 2015, Glasgow Prestwick Airport was shortlisted as a potential UK <a href="/wiki/Spaceport" title="Spaceport">Spaceport</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> as part of the <a href="/wiki/British_commercial_spaceport_competition" title="British commercial spaceport competition">British commercial spaceport competition</a>.\n</p><p>In June 2019, the Scottish government announced that it was putting the airport up for sale. Bidders would be expected to commit to maintaining and developing aviation operations and employment.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2020s">2020s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Glasgow_Prestwick_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: 2020s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In February 2021, the Scottish government announced that a preferred bidder had been selected to buy the airport. The unnamed bidder is believed to be a European transport infrastructure investor.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span the only airport in Scotland with its own railway station, <a href="/wiki/Prestwick_International_Airport_railway_station" title="Prestwick International Airport railway station">Prestwick International Airport railway station</a>, built by the airport in 1994.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> The station is connected to the terminal by an enclosed walkway over the <a href="/wiki/A79_road" class="mw-redirect" title="A79 road">A79 road</a>, and platforms are accessed by stairs, escalators and lifts.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> The station building continues to be owned and operated by the Airport, and not by <a href="/wiki/Network_Rail" title="Network Rail">Network Rail</a> or <a href="/wiki/Abellio_ScotRail" title="Abellio ScotRail">ScotRail</a>. The track through the station itself remains the responsibility of Network Rail.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ayr_Airshow">Ayr Airshow</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Glasgow_Prestwick_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Ayr Airshow">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Prestwick Airport used to host a bi-annual <a href="/wiki/Airshow" class="mw-redirect" title="Airshow">airshow</a>, the first of which was held on 30 September 1967. While very small in scale compared to such shows as <a href="/wiki/RAF_Fairford" title="RAF Fairford">RAF Fairford</a> or <a href="/wiki/Farnborough_Airshow" title="Farnborough Airshow">Farnborough</a>, the Scottish air show attracted up to 100,000 spectators to Prestwick in its heyday in the 1980s.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-42">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Scottish Airshow was first held on 6 and 7 September; an air display was held at the Low green at Ayr Seafront and a static display on 7 September at the airport.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> The event included appearances by the Battle of Britain Memorial Flight, including the only two flying Avro Lancaster bombers, and the last airworthy Avro Vulcan bomber, famous for being part of the UK\'s Nuclear "V Force" bomber fleet. The second Scottish Airshow was held on 5 and 6 September 2015.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Incidents_and_accidents">Incidents and accidents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Glasgow_Prestwick_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Incidents and accidents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<ul><li>Early on 25 December 1954, at 0330 hours, a <a href="/wiki/British_Overseas_Airways_Corporation" title="British Overseas Airways Corporation">British Overseas Airways Corporation</a> <a href="/wiki/Boeing_377_Stratocruiser" title="Boeing 377 Stratocruiser">Boeing 377 Stratocruiser</a> <a href="/wiki/1954_Prestwick_air_disaster" title="1954 Prestwick air disaster">crashed</a> on landing at Prestwick, killing 28 of the 36 passengers and crew on board. The aircraft had been en route from London to New York City, when, on approach to Prestwick, it entered a steep descent before levelling out too late and too severely, hitting the ground short of the runway. The crash has been attributed to a number of factors, including: pilot fatigue (the Captain was well over his duty limit due to the aircraft being delayed); the landing lights at Prestwick being out of action due to repair; and the First Officer either not hearing a command from the Captain for landing lights (which might have helped judge the low cloud base) or mistakenly hitting the flaps, causing the aircraft to stall.</li>\n<li>On 15 June 2013, an <a href="/wiki/EgyptAir" class="mw-redirect" title="EgyptAir">EgyptAir</a> flight from Cairo bound for JFK Airport was diverted to Prestwick Airport under RAF escort due to a terror threat. Roads surrounding the airport were closed as police dealt with the incident.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>\n<h2><span
