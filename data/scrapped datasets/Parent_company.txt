<b>holding company</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Company_(law)" class="mw-redirect" title="Company (law)">company</a> that owns the <a href="/wiki/Shares_outstanding" title="Shares outstanding">outstanding stock</a> of other companies. A holding company usually does not produce goods or services itself. Its purpose is to own shares of other companies to form a <a href="/wiki/Corporate_group" title="Corporate group">corporate group</a>. \n</p><p>In many jurisdictions around the world, holding companies are usually called <b><a href="/wiki/Parent_company" class="mw-redirect" title="Parent company">parent companies</a></b>, which, besides holding stock in other companies, can conduct trade and other business activities themselves. Holding companies reduce <a href="/wiki/Risk" title="Risk">risk</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Shareholder" title="Shareholder">shareholders</a>, and can permit the ownership and control of a number of different companies. <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> also refers to the term <i>parent holding company.</i><sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Holding companies are also created to hold assets, such as <a href="/wiki/Intellectual_property" title="Intellectual property">intellectual property</a> or trade secrets, that are protected from the operation company. That creates a smaller risk when it comes to litigation.\n</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, 80% of stock, in voting and value, must be owned before <a href="/wiki/Tax" title="Tax">tax</a> consolidation benefits such as <a href="/wiki/Dividends_received_deduction" title="Dividends received deduction">tax-free</a> <a href="/wiki/Dividend" title="Dividend">dividends</a> can be claimed.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>  That is, if Company A owns 80% or more of the stock of Company B, Company A will not pay <a href="/wiki/Dividend_tax" title="Dividend tax">taxes on dividends</a> paid by Company B to its stockholders, as the payment of dividends from B to A is essentially transferring cash from one company to the other. Any other shareholders of Company B will pay the usual taxes on dividends, as they are legitimate and ordinary dividends to these shareholders.\n</p><p>Sometimes, a company intended to be a pure holding company identifies itself as such by adding "Holding" or "Holdings" to its name.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Australia">Australia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Holding_company&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Australia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The parent company\xe2\x80\x93subsidiary company relationship is defined by Part 1.2, Division 6, Section 46 of the <a href="/wiki/Corporations_Act_2001" title="Corporations Act 2001">Corporations Act 2001 (Cth)</a>, which states:<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<blockquote>\n<p>A <a href="/wiki/Body_corporate" class="mw-redirect" title="Body corporate">body corporate</a> (in this section called the first body) is a subsidiary of another body corporate if, and only if:\n</p>\n<dl><dd>(a) the other body:\n<dl><dd>(i) controls the composition of the first body\'s board; or</dd>\n<dd>(ii) is in a position to cast, or control the casting of, more than one-half of the maximum number of votes that might be cast at a <a href="/wiki/General_meeting" class="mw-redirect" title="General meeting">general meeting</a> of the first body; or</dd>\n<dd>(iii) holds more than one-half of the issued share capital of the first body (excluding any part of that issued share capital that carries no right to participate beyond a specified amount in a distribution of either profits or capital); or</dd></dl></dd>\n<dd>(b) the first body is a subsidiary of a subsidiary of the other body.</dd></dl>\n</blockquote>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Canada">Canada</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Kingdom">United Kingdom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Holding_company&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: United Kingdom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In the United Kingdom, it is generally held that an organisation holding a \'controlling stake\' in a company (a holding of over 51% of the stock) is in effect the de facto parent company of the firm, having overriding material influence over the held company\'s operations, even if no formal full takeover has been enacted. Once a full takeover or purchase is enacted, the held company is seen to have ceased to operate as an independent entity but to have become a tending subsidiary of the purchasing company, which, in turn, becomes the parent company of the subsidiary. (A holding below 50% could be sufficient to give a parent company material influence if they are the largest individual shareholder or if they are placed in control of the running of the operation by non-operational shareholders.)<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Company_law">Company law</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Holding_company&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Company law">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>In the United Kingdom, the term "Holding Company" is defined by the Companies Act 2006 at section 1159.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> It defines a Holding Company as a Company that holds a majority of the voting rights in another company, OR is a member of another company and has the right to appoint or remove a majority of its board of directors, OR is a member of another company and controls alone, pursuant to an agreement with other members, a majority of the voting rights in that company.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United company">Bank holding company</a></div>\n<p>After the <a href="/wiki/Financial_crisis_of_2007%E2%80%9308" class="mw-redirect" title="Financial crisis of 2007\xe2\x80\x9308">financial crisis of 2007\xe2\x80\x9308</a>, many U.S. investment banks converted to holding companies. According to the Federal Financial Institutions Examination Council\'s (FFIEC) website, <a href="/wiki/JPMorgan_Chase" title="JPMorgan Chase">JPMorgan Chase</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bank_of_America" title="Bank of America">Bank of America</a>, <a href="/wiki/Citigroup" title="Citigroup">Citigroup</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wells_Fargo" title="Wells Fargo">Wells Fargo</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Goldman_Sachs" title="Goldman Sachs">Goldman Sachs</a> were the five largest bank holding companies in the finance sector, as of 31 December 2013, based on total assets.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Utilities">Utilities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Holding_company&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Utilities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Public_Utility_Holding_Company_Act_of_1935" title="Public Utility Holding Company Act of 1935">Public Utility Holding Company Act of 1935</a> in the United States caused many energy companies to divest their subsidiary businesses. Between 1938 and 1958 the number of holding companies declined from 216 to 18.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Energy_Policy_Act_of_2005" title="Energy Policy Act of 2005">An energy law</a> passed in 2005 removed the 1935 requirements, and has led to mergers and holding company formation among power marketing and power brokering companies.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Broadcasting">Broadcasting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Holding_company&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Broadcasting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Media_conglomerate" title="Media conglomerate">Media conglomerate</a></div>\n<p>In US <a href="/wiki/Broadcasting" title="Broadcasting">broadcasting</a>, many major <a href="/wiki/Media_conglomerate" title="Media conglomerate">media conglomerates</a> have purchased smaller broadcasters outright, but have not changed the <a href="/wiki/Broadcast_license" title="Broadcast license">broadcast licenses</a> to reflect this, resulting in stations that are (for example) still licensed to <a href="/wiki/Jacor" title="Jacor">Jacor</a> and <a href="/wiki/Citicasters" class="mw-redirect" title="Citicasters">Citicasters</a>, effectively making them such as subsidiary companies of their owner <a href="/wiki/IHeartMedia" title="IHeartMedia">iHeartMedia</a>.  This is sometimes done on a per-<a href="/wiki/Media_market" title="Media market">market</a> basis. For example, in <a href="/wiki/Atlanta" title="Atlanta">Atlanta</a> both <a href="/wiki/WNNX_(FM)" class="mw-redirect" title="WNNX (FM)">WNNX</a> and later <a href="/wiki/WWWQ_(FM)" class="mw-redirect" title="WWWQ (FM)">WWWQ</a> are licensed to "WNNX LiCo, Inc." (LiCo meaning "license company"), both owned by <a href="/wiki/Susquehanna_Radio" class="mw-redirect" title="Susquehanna Radio">Susquehanna Radio</a> (which was later sold to <a href="/wiki/Cumulus_Media" title="Cumulus Media">Cumulus Media</a>).  In determining <a href="/wiki/Maximum" class="mw-redirect" title="Maximum">caps</a> to prevent excessive <a href="/wiki/Concentration_of_media_ownership" title="Concentration of media ownership">concentration of media ownership</a>, all of these are <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/attribute" class="extiw" title="wikt:attribute">attributed</a> to the parent company, as are <a href="/wiki/Local_marketing_agreement" title="Local marketing agreement">leased stations</a>, as a matter of <a href="/wiki/Broadcast_regulation" class="mw-redirect" title="Broadcast regulation">broadcast regulation</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_holding_company">Personal holding company</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Holding_company&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Personal holding company">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In the United States, a personal holding company is defined in section&#160;542 of the <a href="/wiki/Internal_Revenue_Code" title="Internal Revenue Code">Internal Revenue Code</a>. A corporation is a personal holding company if both of the following requirements are met:<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<ul><li>Gross income test: At least 60% of the corporation\'s adjusted ordinary gross income is from dividends, interest, rent, and royalties.</li>\n<li>Stock ownership test: More than 50% in value of the corporation\'s outstanding stock is owned by five or fewer individuals.</li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Parent_company">Parent company</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Holding_company&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Parent company">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Parent_company" class="mw-redirect" title="Parent company">Parent company</a></div>\n<p>A <b>parent company</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Company" title="Company">company</a> that owns enough <a href="/wiki/Voting_share" class="mw-redirect" title="Voting share">voting stock</a> in another firm (or <a href="/wiki/Subsidiary" title="Subsidiary">subsidiary</a>)\nto control management and operations by influencing or electing its <a href="/wiki/Board_of_directors" title="Board of directors">board of directors</a>. The second company is deemed to be a <i>subsidiary of the parent company. The definition of a parent company differs from <a href="/wiki/Jurisdiction" title="Jurisdiction">jurisdiction</a> to jurisdiction, with the definition</i> normally being defined by way of <a href="/wiki/Companies_law" class="mw-redirect" title="Companies law">laws dealing with companies</a> in that jurisdiction.\n</p><p>When an existing company establishes a new company and keeps majority shares with itself, and invites other companies to buy minority shares, it is called a parent company. A parent company could simply be a company that wholly owns another company, which is then known as a "<a href="/wiki/Wholly_owned_subsidiary" class="mw-redirect" title="Wholly owned subsidiary">wholly owned subsidiary</a>".\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
