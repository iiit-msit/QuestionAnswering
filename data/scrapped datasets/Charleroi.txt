width="170" height="214" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/Charleroi_-_Ferraris_map_%281770-1778%29.jpg/255px-Charleroi_-_Ferraris_map_%281770-1778%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/Charleroi_-_Ferraris_map_%281770-1778%29.jpg/340px-Charleroi_-_Ferraris_map_%281770-1778%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="930" data-file-height="1170" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Charleroi_-_Ferraris_map_(1770-1778).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Map of Charleroi in 1770s</div></div></div>\n<p>The Charleroi area was already settled in the <a href="/wiki/Prehistoric" class="mw-redirect" title="Prehistoric">prehistoric</a> period, with traces of metallurgical and commercial activities along the <a href="/wiki/Sambre" title="Sambre">Sambre</a>. Several public buildings, temples and villas were built in the area in the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Roman</a> period. Burial places, with jewels and weapons, have been found. The first written mention of a place called Charnoy dates from a 9th-century offering in the <a href="/wiki/Lobbes" title="Lobbes">Lobbes</a> abbey, which lists various neighboring towns and related <a href="/wiki/Tithe" title="Tithe">tithe</a> duties. During the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>, Charnoy was one of the many small hamlets in the area, with no more than about 50 inhabitants, part of the <a href="/wiki/County_of_Namur" title="County of Namur">County of Namur</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Foundation">Foundation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charleroi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Foundation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Spanish territorial losses in the 1659 <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_the_Pyrenees" title="Treaty of the Pyrenees">Treaty of the Pyrenees</a> left a gap between the key fortresses of <a href="/wiki/Mons" title="Mons">Mons</a> and <a href="/wiki/Namur" title="Namur">Namur</a>; to fill this, <a href="/wiki/Francisco_Castel_Rodrigo" class="mw-redirect" title="Francisco Castel Rodrigo">Francisco Castel Rodrigo</a>, then Governor of the Spanish Netherlands, expropriated land around Charnoy to build a fortress near the Sambre. In September 1666, it was renamed Charle-roi, or King Charles, in honour of five-year-old <a href="/wiki/Charles_II_of_Spain" title="Charles II of Spain">Charles II of Spain</a>; the <a href="/wiki/Chronogram" title="Chronogram">chronogram</a> F<b>V</b>N<b>D</b>AT<b>V</b>R <b>C</b>ARO<b>L</b>OREG<b>IVM</b> (MDCLVVVI) can be found in the register of the parish of Charnoy.<sup id="cite_ref-Belgium_&amp;_Luxembourg_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Belgium_&amp;_Luxembourg-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Construction had only just begun when the <a href="/wiki/War_of_Devolution" title="War of Devolution">War of Devolution</a> with France began in 1667, and the Spanish withdrew. France retained the town under the 1668 <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Aix-la-Chapelle_(1668)" title="Treaty of Aix-la-Chapelle (1668)">Treaty of Aix-la-Chapelle</a>, and its fortifications were completed by <a href="/wiki/Vauban" class="mw-redirect" title="Vauban">Vauban</a>. A bridge was built over the Sambre, connecting the <i>Ville Haute</i> and <i>Ville Basse</i>, with incentives offered to persuade people to settle there. The French relinquished control in 1678, and although it changed hands several times over the next 50 years, the town remained part of the Netherlands until the foundation of modern Belgium.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span id="1666.E2.80.931830"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1666\xe2\x80\x931830">1666\xe2\x80\x931830</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charleroi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 1666\xe2\x80\x931830">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Charleroi_-_plan-relief_-_copie.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Charleroi_-_plan-relief_-_copie.jpg/220px-Charleroi_-_plan-relief_-_copie.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="132" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Charleroi_-_plan-relief_-_copie.jpg/330px-Charleroi_-_plan-relief_-_copie.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Charleroi_-_plan-relief_-_copie.jpg/440px-Charleroi_-_plan-relief_-_copie.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3072" data-file-height="1837" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Charleroi_-_plan-relief_-_copie.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Copy of the <a href="/wiki/Plan-relief" title="Plan-relief">plan-relief</a> of Charleroi made in 1696. View from the southwest. On display at the town-hall.</div></div></div>\n<p>Shortly after its foundation, the new city was in turn besieged by the Dutch, ceded to the Spanish in 1678 (<a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Nijmegen" class="mw-redirect" title="Treaty of Nijmegen">Treaty of Nijmegen</a>), taken by the French in 1693, ceded again to the Spanish in 1698 (<a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Rijswijk" class="mw-redirect" title="Treaty of Rijswijk">Treaty of Rijswijk</a>), then taken by the French, the Dutch and the Austrians in 1714 (<a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Baden_(1714)" title="Treaty of Baden (1714)">Treaty of Baden</a>). The <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">French</a> <a href="/wiki/Prince_of_Conti" class="mw-redirect" title="Prince of Conti">Prince of Conti</a> took the city again in 1745, but it was ceded back to <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austria</a> in 1748, beginning a period of prosperity under <a href="/wiki/Joseph_II,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Joseph II, Holy Roman Emperor">Joseph II</a>. Glass, steel and coal industries, which had already sprung up a century earlier, could now flourish.\n</p><p>Trouble began again in 1790, the year of the civil uprising that eventually led to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_of_Belgium" class="mw-redirect" title="United States of Belgium">United States of Belgium</a>. The Austrians occupied the city, were forced out by the French after the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Jemappes" title="Battle of Jemappes">Battle of Jemappes</a> on 6 November 1792, and took it back again four months later. On 12 June 1794, the French revolutionary <a href="/wiki/Army_of_Sambre-et-Meuse" class="mw-redirect" title="Army of Sambre-et-Meuse">Army of Sambre-et-Meuse</a> under the command of <a href="/wiki/Jean-Baptiste_Jourdan" title="Jean-Baptiste Jourdan">Jean-Baptiste Jourdan</a>, invested Charleroi and won a decisive victory in the ensuing <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Fleurus_(1794)" title="Battle of Fleurus (1794)">Battle of Fleurus</a>. The city took the revolutionary name of Libre-sur-Sambre until 1800. After France\'s defeat in 1814, the whole area was annexed to the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a>, and new walls were built around the city. <a href="/wiki/Napoleon" title="Napoleon">Napoleon</a> stayed in Charleroi for a couple of days in June 1815, just before the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Waterloo" title="Battle of Waterloo">Battle of Waterloo</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1830_to_present">1830 to present</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charleroi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 1830 to present">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Charleroi_-_jour_de_march%C3%A9_-_rue_d%27Orl%C3%A9ans.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/Charleroi_-_jour_de_march%C3%A9_-_rue_d%27Orl%C3%A9ans.jpg/330px-Charleroi_-_jour_de_march%C3%A9_-_rue_d%27Orl%C3%A9ans.jpg 1.5x, data-file-width="3072" data-file-height="2304" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Charleroi_-_jour_de_march%C3%A9_-_rue_d%27Orl%C3%A9ans.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Orleans street Sunday market</div></div></div>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Belgian_Revolution" title="Belgian Revolution">Belgian Revolution</a> of 1830 gave the area its freedom from the Netherlands and ushered in a new era of prosperity, still based mostly on glass, metallurgy and coal, hence the area\xe2\x80\x99s name, <i>Pays Noir</i> ("Black Country"). After the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a>, Charleroi benefited from the increased use of coke in the metallurgical industry. People from across <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> were attracted by the economic opportunities, and the population grew rapidly.\n</p><p>Following the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_revolution" class="mw-redirect" title="Industrial revolution">Industrial revolution</a> in <a href="/wiki/Wallonia" title="Wallonia">Wallonia</a>, Charleroi from the 1850s\xe2\x80\x931860s became one of the most important places where labor strikes broke out. In 1886, 12 strikers were killed by the Belgian army in <a href="/wiki/Roux,_Belgium" title="Roux, Belgium">Roux</a>. In the 1880s, miners in <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Hainaut" class="mw-redirect" title="Province of Hainaut">Hainaut</a> were recruited by the Dominion Coal Company in <a href="/wiki/Glace_Bay,_Nova_Scotia" class="mw-redirect" title="Glace Bay, Nova Scotia">Glace Bay, Nova Scotia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> These miners were anxious to flee the repression following bloody strikes and riots in <a href="/wiki/Li%C3%A8ge" title="Li\xc3\xa8ge">Li\xc3\xa8ge</a> and Charleroi<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> during the <a href="/wiki/Walloon_Jacquerie_of_1886" class="mw-redirect" title="Walloon Jacquerie of 1886">Walloon Jacquerie of 1886</a>. Walloon miners from Charleroi also emigrated to <a href="/wiki/Alberta" title="Alberta">Alberta</a>, Canada.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> The working men of Charleroi always played an important role in <a href="/wiki/Belgian_general_strikes" class="mw-redirect" title="Belgian general strikes">Belgian general strikes</a> and particularly during the Belgian general strike of 1936, the <a href="/wiki/General_strike_against_Leopold_III_of_Belgium" class="mw-redirect" title="General strike against Leopold III of Belgium">General strike against Leopold III of Belgium</a> and the <a href="/wiki/1960-1961_Winter_General_Strike" class="mw-redirect" title="1960-1961 Winter General Strike">1960-1961 Winter General Strike</a>.\n</p><p>By 1871, the fortified walls around the city were completely torn down.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Charleroi" title="Battle of Charleroi">Heavy fighting</a> took place during <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a> due to the city\'s strategic location on the Sambre. The city was badly damaged with further destruction only being prevented by a treaty agreed with the German forces which required the payment of 10 million Belgian Francs, foodstuffs, vehicles and armaments.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Spirou_magazine" class="mw-redirect" title="Spirou magazine">Spirou magazine</a> which featured the popular cartoon characters <a href="/wiki/Lucky_Luke" title="Lucky Luke">Lucky Luke</a> and <a href="/wiki/The_Smurfs" title="The Smurfs">the Smurfs</a> was launched by the publishing company <a href="/wiki/%C3%89ditions_Dupuis" class="mw-redirect" title="\xc3\x89ditions Dupuis">\xc3\x89ditions Dupuis</a> in 1938.<sup id="cite_ref-independent1_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-independent1-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> After <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, Charleroi witnessed a general decline of its heavy industry.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Following the merger with several surrounding municipalities in 1977, the city as of 2013<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Charleroi&amp;action=edit">&#91;update&#93;</a></sup> ranks as the largest city in <a href="/wiki/Wallonia" title="Wallonia">Wallonia</a> and the 4th largest in Belgium.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Politics">Politics</span><span Charleroi is in the center of a coal basin. Even so, due to the widespread loss in industrial power in the area since the 1970s, the area suffered some of the highest unemployment and poverty rates in Europe for most of the 1980s and 1990s. However, from the early 2000s, the overall economy of the area has diversified to include health care, transportation and telecommunications. Nevertheless, the poverty rates are still significant.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Demographics">Demographics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span It was planned in the 1960s as a 48&#160;km (30&#160;mi.) light-rail network, operating on the <a href="/wiki/Heavy_rail" class="mw-redirect" title="Heavy rail">heavy rail</a> metro infrastructure, consisting of eight branch lines radiating from a central loop downtown.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> However, only one line (to Petria), part of another line (to Gilly) and three-quarters of the loop were actually built and opened to traffic, all from 1976 to 1996. Another branch line toward the suburb of Ch\xc3\xa2telet (Ch\xc3\xa2telineau) was almost fully built, to the extent of installing power cables, escalators and still-working electric signals in the first three stations<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> but was never opened as passenger numbers would be too low to economically justify the extra staff. The high costs of construction, a decline in Charleroi\'s traditional "smokestack" industries and questioning of the scope of the whole project in proportion to the actual demand for it are cited as reasons for the original plan\'s becoming unfulfilled.\n</p><p>The central loop and the Gilly branch as far as Soleilmont were completed in 2012, with funds from the <a href="/wiki/European_Investment_Bank" title="European Investment Bank">European Investment Bank</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> The Gosselies branch opened as a street-level tramline in 2013. There are no plans to open any part of the Ch\xc3\xa2telet branch.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline"
