Generally, a honeypot consists of <a href="/wiki/Data" title="Data">data</a> (for example, in a network site) that appears to be a legitimate part of the site and contain information or resources of value to attackers. It is actually isolated, monitored, and capable of blocking or analyzing the attackers. This is similar to police <a href="/wiki/Sting_operations" class="mw-redirect" title="Sting operations">sting operations</a>, colloquially known as "baiting" a suspect.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Honeypot_(computing)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Honeypots can be classified based on their deployment (use/action) and based on their level of involvement. Based on deployment, honeypots may be classified as: <sup id="cite_ref-:0_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<ul><li>production honeypots</li>\n<li>research honeypots</li></ul>\n<p><b>Production honeypots</b> are easy to use, capture only limited information, and are used primarily by corporations. Production honeypots are placed inside the production network with other production servers by an organization to improve their overall state of security. Normally, production honeypots are low-interaction honeypots, which are easier to deploy. They give less information about the attacks or attackers than research honeypots.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><b>Research honeypots</b> are run to gather information about the motives and tactics of the <a href="/wiki/Black_hat_(computer_security)" title="Black hat (computer security)">black hat</a> community targeting different networks. These honeypots do not add direct value to a specific organization; instead, they are used to research the threats that organizations face and to learn how to better protect against those threats.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Research honeypots are complex to deploy and maintain, capture extensive information, and are used primarily by research, military, or government organizations.<sup id="cite_ref-Attacks_Landscape_in_the_Dark_Side_of_the_Web_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Attacks_Landscape_in_the_Dark_Side_of_the_Web-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Based on design criteria, honeypots can be classified as: <sup id="cite_ref-:0_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<ul><li>pure honeypots</li>\n<li>high-interaction honeypots</li>\n<li>low-interaction honeypots</li></ul>\n<p><b>Pure honeypots</b> are full-fledged production systems. The activities of the attacker are monitored by using a bug tap that has been installed on the honeypot\'s link to the network. No other software needs to be installed. Even though a pure honeypot is useful, stealthiness of the defense mechanisms can be ensured by a more controlled mechanism.\n</p><p><b>High-interaction honeypots</b> imitate the activities of the production systems that host a variety of services and, therefore, an attacker may be allowed a lot of services to waste their time. By employing <a href="/wiki/Virtual_machine" title="Virtual machine">virtual machines</a>, multiple honeypots can be hosted on a single physical machine. Therefore, even if the honeypot is compromised, it can be restored more quickly. In general, high-interaction honeypots provide more security by being difficult to detect, but they are expensive to maintain. If virtual machines are not available, one physical computer must be maintained for each honeypot, which can be exorbitantly expensive. Example: <a href="/wiki/Honeynet_Project" title="Honeynet Project">Honeynet</a>.\n</p><p><b>Low-interaction honeypots</b> simulate only the services frequently requested by attackers. Since they consume relatively few resources, multiple virtual machines can easily be hosted on one physical system, the virtual systems have a short response time, and less code is required, reducing the complexity of the virtual system\'s security. Example: <a href="/wiki/Honeyd" title="Honeyd">Honeyd</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Deception_technology">Deception technology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Honeypot_(computing)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Deception technology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Recently, a new market segment called <a href="/wiki/Deception_technology" title="Deception technology">deception technology</a> has emerged using basic honeypot technology with the addition of advanced automation for scale.  Deception technology addresses the automated deployment of honeypot resources over a large commercial enterprise or government institution.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Malware_honeypots">Malware honeypots</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Honeypot_(computing)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Malware honeypots">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Malware honeypots are used to detect malware by exploiting the known replication and attack vectors of malware. Replication vectors such as <a href="/wiki/USB_flash_drives" class="mw-redirect" title="USB flash drives">USB flash drives</a> can easily be verified for evidence of modifications, either through manual means or utilizing special-purpose honeypots that emulate drives. Malware increasingly is used to search for and steal cryptocurrencies.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Spam_versions">Spam versions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Honeypot_(computing)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Spam versions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Spamming" title="Spamming">Spammers</a> abuse vulnerable resources such as <a href="/wiki/Open_mail_relay" title="Open mail relay">open mail relays</a> and <a href="/wiki/Open_proxy" title="Open proxy">open proxies</a>. These are servers which accept e-mail from anyone on the Internet\xe2\x80\x94including spammers\xe2\x80\x94and send it to its destination. Some system administrators have created honeypot programs that masquerade as these abusable resources to discover spammer activity.\n</p><p>There are several capabilities such honeypots provide to these administrators, and the existence of such fake abusable systems makes abuse more difficult or risky.  Honeypots can be a powerful countermeasure to abuse from those who rely on very high volume abuse (e.g., spammers).\n</p><p>These honeypots can reveal the abuser\'s <a href="/wiki/IP_address" title="IP address">IP address</a> and provide bulk spam capture (which enables operators to determine spammers\' <a href="/wiki/URLs" class="mw-redirect" title="URLs">URLs</a> and response mechanisms). As described by M. Edwards at ITPRo Today:\n</p>\n<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r996844942">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>Typically, spammers test a mail server for open relaying by simply sending themselves an email message. If the spammer receives the email message, the mail server obviously allows open relaying. Honeypot operators, however, can use the relay test to thwart spammers. The honeypot catches the relay test email message, returns the test email message, and subsequently blocks all other email messages from that spammer. Spammers continue to use the antispam honeypot for spamming, but the spam is never delivered. Meanwhile, the honeypot operator can notify spammers\' ISPs and have their Internet accounts canceled. If honeypot operators detect spammers who use open-proxy servers, they can also notify the proxy server operator to lock down the server to prevent further misuse.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote>\n<p>The apparent source may be another abused system. Spammers and other abusers may use a chain of such abused systems to make detection of the original starting point of the abuse traffic difficult.\n</p><p>This in itself is indicative of the power of honeypots as <a href="/wiki/Anti-spam" class="mw-redirect" title="Anti-spam">anti-spam</a> tools. In the early days of anti-spam honeypots, spammers, with little concern for hiding their location, felt safe testing for vulnerabilities and sending spam directly from their own systems. Honeypots made the abuse riskier and more difficult.\n</p><p>Spam still flows through open relays, but the volume is much smaller than in 2001-02. While most spam originates in the U.S.,<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> spammers hop through open relays across political boundaries to mask their origin. Honeypot operators may use intercepted relay tests to recognize and thwart attempts to relay spam through their honeypots. "Thwart" may mean "accept the relay spam but decline to deliver it." Honeypot operators may discover other details concerning the spam and the spammer by examining the captured spam messages.\n</p><p>Open relay honeypots include Jackpot, written in <a href="/wiki/Java_(programming_language)" title="Java (programming language)">Java</a> by Jack Cleaver; <i>smtpot.py</i>, written in <a href="/wiki/Python_(programming_language)" title="Python (programming language)">Python</a> by Karl A. Krueger;<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spamhole_(honeypot)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Spamhole (honeypot) (page does not exist)">spamhole</a>, written in <a href="/wiki/C_(programming_language)" title="C (programming language)">C</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The <i>Bubblegum Proxypot</i> is an open source honeypot (or "proxypot").<sup id="cite_ref-Ec-Council2009_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ec-Council2009-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Email_trap">Email trap</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span Compared with the term "<a href="/wiki/Spamtrap" title="Spamtrap">spamtrap</a>", the term "honeypot" might be more suitable for systems and techniques that are used to detect or counterattack probes. With a spamtrap, spam arrives at its destination "legitimately"\xe2\x80\x94exactly as non-spam email would arrive.\n</p><p>An amalgam of these techniques is <a href="/wiki/Project_Honey_Pot" title="Project Honey Pot">Project Honey Pot</a>, a distributed, open source project that uses honeypot pages installed on websites around the world. These honeypot pages disseminate uniquely tagged spamtrap email addresses and <a href="/wiki/Spammers" class="mw-redirect" title="Spammers">spammers</a> can then be tracked\xe2\x80\x94the corresponding spam mail is subsequently sent to these spamtrap e-mail addresses.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Database_honeypot">Database honeypot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Honeypot_(computing)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Database honeypot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Databases often get attacked by intruders using <a href="/wiki/SQL_injection" title="SQL injection">SQL injection</a>. As such activities are not recognized by basic firewalls, companies often use database firewalls for protection. Some of the available <a href="/wiki/SQL_database" class="mw-redirect" title="SQL database">SQL database</a> firewalls provide/support honeypot architectures so that the intruder runs against a trap database while the web application remains functional.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Honeypot_Detection">Honeypot Detection</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Honeypot_(computing)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Honeypot Detection">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Just as honeypots are weapons against spammers, honeypot detection systems are spammer-employed counter-weapons. As detection systems would likely use unique characteristics of specific honeypots to identify them, such as the property-value pairs of default honeypot configuration,<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> many honeypots in-use utilise a set of unique characteristics larger and more daunting to those seeking to detect and thereby identify them. This is an unusual circumstance in software; a situation in which <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Special:Search/versionitis" class="extiw" title="wikt:Special:Search/versionitis">"versionitis"</a> (a large number of versions of the same software, all differing slightly from each other) can be beneficial. There\'s also an advantage in having some easy-to-detect honeypots deployed. <a href="/wiki/Fred_Cohen" title="Fred Cohen">Fred Cohen</a>, the inventor of the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Deception_Toolkit&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Deception Toolkit (page does not exist)">Deception Toolkit</a>, argues that every system running his honeypot should have a deception port which adversaries can use to detect the honeypot.<sup id="cite_ref-dtk_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dtk-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Cohen believes that this might deter adversaries.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Risks">Risks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Honeypot_(computing)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Risks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The goal of honeypots is to attract and engage attackers for a sufficiently long period to obtain high-level <a href="/wiki/Indicator_of_compromise" title="Indicator of compromise">Indicators of Compromise</a> (IoC) such as attack tools and <a href="/wiki/Terrorist_Tactics,_Techniques,_and_Procedures" title="Terrorist Tactics, Techniques, and Procedures">Tactics, Techniques, and Procedures</a> (TTPs). Thus, a honeypot needs to emulate essential services in the production network and grant the attacker the freedom to perform adversarial activities to increase its attractiveness to the attacker. Although the honeypot is a controlled environment and can be monitored by using tools such as honeywall,<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> attackers may still be able to use some honeypots as pivot nodes to penetrate production systems.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> This tradeoff between the honeypot attractiveness and the penetration risk has been investigated both qualitatively<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> and quantitatively.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The second risk of honeypots is that they may attract legitimate users due to a lack of communication in large-scale enterprise networks. For example, the security team who applies and monitors the honeypot may not disclose the honeypot location to all users in time due to the lack of communication or the prevention of insider threats.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> A game-theoretical model<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> has been proposed to simultaneously incentivize adversarial users and disincentivize legitimate users for the honeypot access by exploiting the utility difference between two types of users.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Honey_nets">Honey nets</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Honeypot_(computing)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Honey nets">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="quotebox pullquote floatright" style="width:18%; ;"><style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1012376600">.mw-parser-output .quotebox{background-color:#F9F9F9;border:1px solid #aaa;box-sizing:border-box;padding:10px;font-size:88%;max-width:100%}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatleft{margin:0.5em 1.4em 0.8em 0}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatright{margin:0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.centered{margin:0.5em auto 0.8em auto}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatleft p,.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatright p{font-style:inherit}.mw-parser-output .quotebox-title{background-color:#F9F9F9;text-align:center;font-size:larger;font-weight:bold}.mw-parser-output .quotebox-quote.quoted:before{font-family:"Times New Roman",serif;font-weight:bold;font-size:large;color:gray;content:" \xe2\x80\x9c ";vertical-align:-45%;line-height:0}.mw-parser-output .quotebox-quote.quoted:after{font-family:"Times New Roman",serif;font-weight:bold;font-size:large;color:gray;content:" \xe2\x80\x9d ";line-height:0}.mw-parser-output .quotebox .left-aligned{text-align:left}.mw-parser-output .quotebox .right-aligned{text-align:right}.mw-parser-output .quotebox .center-aligned{text-align:center}.mw-parser-output .quotebox cite{display:block;font-style:normal}@media screen and (max-width:640px){.mw-parser-output .quotebox{width:100%!important;margin:0 0 0.8em!important;float:none!important}}</style>\n<div class="quotebox-quote left-aligned" style="">"A \'honey net\' is a network of high interaction honeypots that simulates a production network and configured such that all activity is monitored, recorded and in a degree, discreetly regulated."</div>\n<p><cite class="right-aligned" style="">-Lance Spitzner,&#32;<br /><a href="/wiki/Honeynet_Project" title="Honeynet Project">Honeynet Project</a></cite>\n</p>\n</div>\n<p>Two or more honeypots on a network form a <i>honey net</i>. Typically, a honey net is used for monitoring a larger and/or more diverse network in which one honeypot may not be sufficient. Honey nets and honeypots are usually implemented as parts of larger <a href="/wiki/Network_intrusion_detection_system" class="mw-redirect" title="Network intrusion detection system">network intrusion detection systems</a>. A <i>honey farm</i> is a centralized collection of honeypots and analysis tools.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The concept of the honey net first began in 1999 when Lance Spitzner, founder of the <a href="/wiki/Honeynet_Project" title="Honeynet Project">Honeynet Project</a>, published the paper "To Build a Honeypot".<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Honeypot_(computing)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The earliest honeypot techniques are described in <a href="/wiki/Clifford_Stoll" title="Clifford Stoll">Clifford Stoll</a>\'s 1989 book <i><a href="/wiki/The_Cuckoo%27s_Egg" class="mw-redirect" title="The Cuckoo&#39;s Egg">The Cuckoo\'s Egg</a></i>.\n</p><p>One of the earliest documented cases of the cybersecurity use of a honeypot began in January 1991. On January 7, 1991 while he worked at AT&amp;T Bell Laboratories Cheswick observed a criminal hacker, known as a <a href="/wiki/Security_hacker#Cracker" title="Security hacker">cracker</a>, attempting to obtain a copy of a password file. Cheswick wrote that he and colleagues constructed a "chroot "Jail" (or "roach motel")" which allowed them to observe their attacker over a period of several months. <sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 2017, <a href="/wiki/Dutch_police" class="mw-redirect" title="Dutch police">Dutch police</a> used honeypot techniques to track down users of the <a href="/wiki/Darknet_market" title="Darknet market">darknet market</a> <a href="/wiki/Hansa_(market)" title="Hansa (market)">Hansa</a>.\n</p><p>The metaphor of a bear being attracted to and stealing honey is common in many traditions, including Germanic, Celtic, and Slavic. A common Slavic word for the bear is <i>medved</i> "honey eater". The tradition of bears stealing honey has been passed down through stories and folklore, especially the well known <a href="/wiki/Winnie_the_Pooh" class="mw-redirect" title="Winnie the Pooh">Winnie the Pooh</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
