Underground">London Underground</a></div></div></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/NTT_DoCoMo" class="mw-redirect" title="NTT DoCoMo">NTT DoCoMo</a>\'s <b>i-mode</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Mobile_internet" class="mw-redirect" title="Mobile internet">mobile internet</a> (distinct from <a href="/wiki/Wireless_internet" class="mw-redirect" title="Wireless internet">wireless internet</a>) service popular in <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>. Unlike <a href="/wiki/Wireless_Application_Protocol" title="Wireless Application Protocol">Wireless Application Protocol</a>, i-mode encompasses a wider variety of internet standards, including web access, <a href="/wiki/E-mail" class="mw-redirect" title="E-mail">e-mail</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Packet-switched_network" class="mw-redirect" title="Packet-switched network">packet-switched network</a> that delivers the data. i-mode users have access to various services such as e-mail, sports results, weather forecast, <a href="/wiki/Mobile_game" title="Mobile game">games</a>, financial services, and ticket booking. Content is provided by specialized services, typically from the <a href="/wiki/Mobile_carrier" class="mw-redirect" title="Mobile carrier">mobile carrier</a>, which allows them to have tighter control over billing.\n</p><p>Like WAP, i-mode delivers only those services that are specifically converted for the service, or are converted through gateways.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc" href="/w/index.php?title=I-mode&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:372px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Mobile_Web_Standards_Evolution_Vector.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Mobile_Web_Standards_Evolution_Vector.svg/370px-Mobile_Web_Standards_Evolution_Vector.svg.png" decoding="async" width="370" height="186" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Mobile_Web_Standards_Evolution_Vector.svg/555px-Mobile_Web_Standards_Evolution_Vector.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Mobile_Web_Standards_Evolution_Vector.svg/740px-Mobile_Web_Standards_Evolution_Vector.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="1114" data-file-height="561" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Mobile_Web_Standards_Evolution_Vector.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Evolution of mobile web standards</div></div></div>\n<p>In contrast with the <a href="/wiki/Wireless_Application_Protocol" title="Wireless Application Protocol">Wireless Application Protocol</a> (WAP) standard, which used <a href="/wiki/Wireless_Markup_Language" title="Wireless Markup Language">Wireless Markup Language</a> (WML) on top of a protocol stack for wireless handheld devices, i-mode borrows from DoCoMo proprietary protocols ALP (<a href="/wiki/Hypertext_Transfer_Protocol" title="Hypertext Transfer Protocol">HTTP</a>) and TLP (<a href="/wiki/Transmission_Control_Protocol" title="Transmission Control Protocol">TCP</a>, <a href="/wiki/User_Datagram_Protocol" title="User Datagram Protocol">UDP</a>), as well as fixed Internet data formats such as C-HTML, a subset of the <a href="/wiki/HTML" title="HTML">HTML</a> language designed by DoCoMo.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> C-HTML was designed for small devices (e.g. cellular phones) with hardware restrictions such as lower memory, low-power CPUs with limited or no storage capabilities, small monochrome display screens, single-character fonts and limited input methods.<sup id="cite_ref-requirements_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-requirements-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> As a simpler form of HTML, C-HTML does not support tables, image maps, multiple fonts and styling of fonts, background colors and images, frames, or style sheets, and is limited to a monochromatic display.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>i-mode phones have a special i-mode button for the user to access the start menu. There are more than 12,000 official sites and around 100,000 or more unofficial i-mode sites, which are not linked to DoCoMo\'s i-mode portal page and DoCoMo\'s billing services. NTT DoCoMo supervises the content and operations of all official i-mode sites, most of which are commercial. These official sites are accessed through DoCoMo\'s i-mode menu but in many cases official sites can also be accessed from mobile phones by typing the URL or through the use of <a href="/wiki/QR_code" title="QR code">QR code</a> (a <a href="/wiki/Barcode" title="Barcode">barcode</a>).\n</p><p>An i-mode user pays for both sent and received data. There are services to avoid <a href="/wiki/E-mail_spam" class="mw-redirect" title="E-mail spam">unsolicited e-mails</a>. The basic monthly charge is typically on the order of <a href="/wiki/Japanese_yen" title="Japanese yen">JPY</a> \xc2\xa5200 - \xc2\xa5300 for i-mode not including the data transfer charges, with additional charges on a monthly subscription basis for premium services. A variety of discount plans exist, for example family discount and flat packet plans for unlimited transfer of data at a fixed monthly charge (on the order of \xc2\xa54,000/month).\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=I-mode&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>i-mode was launched in Japan on 22 February 1999. The content planning and service design team was led by Mari Matsunaga, while Takeshi Natsuno was responsible for the business development. Top executive Keiichi Enoki oversaw the technical and overall development. A few months after DoCoMo launched i-mode in February 1999, DoCoMo\'s competitors launched very similar mobile data services: <a href="/wiki/KDDI" title="KDDI">KDDI</a> launched <a href="/wiki/EZweb" class="mw-redirect" title="EZweb">EZweb</a>, and <a href="/wiki/J-Phone" class="mw-redirect" title="J-Phone">J-Phone</a> launched <a href="/wiki/J-Sky" title="J-Sky">J-Sky</a>. Vodafone later acquired J-Phone including J-Sky, renaming the service <a href="/wiki/Vodafone_live!" title="Vodafone live!">Vodafone live!</a>, although initially this was different from Vodafone live! in Europe and other markets. In addition, Vodafone KK was acquired by <a href="/wiki/SoftBank" class="mw-redirect" title="SoftBank">SoftBank</a>, an operator of Yahoo! Japan in October, 2006 and changed the name to <a href="/wiki/SoftBank_Mobile" class="mw-redirect" title="SoftBank Mobile">SoftBank Mobile</a>.\nAs of June 2006, the mobile data services I-Mode, EZweb, and J-Sky, had over 80 million subscribers in Japan.\n</p><p>Since 2003, i-mode center is called CiRCUS, which consists of 400 <a href="/wiki/NEC_Corporation" class="mw-redirect" title="NEC Corporation">NEC</a> NX7000 <a href="/wiki/HP-UX" title="HP-UX">HP-UX</a> servers and occupies 4,600 m<sup>2</sup> floor space in DoCoMo\'s <a href="/wiki/Kawasaki,_Kanagawa" title="Kawasaki, Kanagawa">Kawasaki</a> office.  The operation support system is called CARNiVAL, which is hosted in the <a href="/wiki/Sanno_Park_Tower" title="Sanno Park Tower">Sanno Park Tower</a>.\n</p><p>i-mode usage in Japan peaked around 2008. On October 29 2019, DoCoMo announced i-mode will end in March 2026.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Markets">Markets</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=I-mode&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Markets">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Seeing the tremendous success of i-mode in Japan, many operators in Europe, Asia and Australia sought to license the service through partnership with DoCoMo. Takeshi Natsuno was behind the expansion of i-mode to 17 countries worldwide. Kamel Maamria who was a partner with the Boston Consulting Group and who was supporting Mr. Natsuno is also thought to have had a major role in the expansion of the first Japanese service ever outside Japan.\n</p><p>i-mode showed very fast take-up in the various countries where it was launched which led to more operators seeking to launch i-mode in their markets with the footprint reaching a total of 17 markets worldwide.\n</p><p>While the i-mode service was an exceptional service which positioned DoCoMo as the global leader in value add services, another key success factor for i-mode was the Japanese smartphone makers who developed state of the art handsets to support i-mode. As i-mode was exported to the rest of the world, Nokia and other major handset vendors who controlled the markets at the time, refused at first to support i-mode by developing handsets which support the i-mode service. The operators who decided to launch i-mode had to rely on Japanese vendors who had no experience in international markets. As i-mode showed success in these markets, some vendors started customizing some of their handsets to support i-mode, however, the support was only partial and came late in time.\n</p><p>While the service was successful during the first years after launch, the lack of adequate handsets and the emergence of new handsets from new vendors which supported new Internet services on one hand, and a change of leadership of i-mode in Docomo, lead to a number of operators to migrate or integrate i-mode into new mobile Internet services. These efforts were ultimately unsuccessful, and i-mode never became popular outside of Japan.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>i-mode was
