centre</a> in an area known as Farmers Cross.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> In 2018, Cork Airport handled 2,392,821 passengers, a 3.7% annual increase.<sup id="cite_ref-DAA_Annual_Report_2018_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DAA_Annual_Report_2018-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Cork is the state\'s <a href="/wiki/List_of_the_busiest_airports_in_the_Republic_of_Ireland" title="List of the busiest airports in the Republic of Ireland">second-busiest airport</a> in terms of passenger numbers, after Dublin, and third busiest on the <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">island of Ireland</a>, after Dublin and <a href="/wiki/Belfast_International_Airport" title="Belfast International Airport">Belfast International</a>.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc" role="navigation" aria-labelledby="mw-toc-heading"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cork_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1957_to_1980">1957 to 1980</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cork_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: 1957 to 1980">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In 1957 the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Ireland" title="Government of Ireland">Government of Ireland</a> agreed in principle to the building of an airport for Cork. After considering many sites in the area, it was agreed that the airport should be built at <a href="/wiki/Ballygarvan,_County_Cork" title="Ballygarvan, County Cork">Ballygarvan</a>. Tenders were invited for the construction of the airport in 1959 at an estimated cost of \xc2\xa31 million. The airport was officially opened on 16 October 1961, following proving flights four days earlier by Aer Lingus and <a href="/wiki/Cambrian_Airways" title="Cambrian Airways">Cambrian Airways</a>. Vincent Fanning was the first manager at the airport.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> In its first year the airport handled 10,172 passengers \xe2\x80\x93 close to the average number of passengers handled each day at the airport in 2007. Throughout the 1960s the airport expanded with the arrival of more advanced aircraft and more destinations. The first jet, a <a href="/wiki/British_Overseas_Airways_Corporation" title="British Overseas Airways Corporation">British Overseas Airways Corporation</a> <a href="/wiki/De_Havilland_Comet" title="De Havilland Comet">Comet</a>, landed at Cork Airport on 29 March 1964. By 1969 Aer Lingus was operating to <a href="/wiki/London_Heathrow_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="London Heathrow Airport">London Heathrow</a>, <a href="/wiki/Manchester_Airport" title="Manchester Airport">Manchester</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bristol_Airport" title="Bristol Airport">Bristol</a>.\n</p><p>In 1975 <a href="/wiki/Dublin_Airport_Authority" class="mw-redirect" title="Dublin Airport Authority">Aer Rianta</a>, the then state airports authority, undertook a <a href="/wiki/Airport_terminal" title="Airport terminal">passenger terminal</a> study aimed at improving the terminal facilities. The findings resulted in the provision, over the next two years, of new departure and arrival halls, a new <a href="/wiki/Airport_check-in" title="Airport check-in">check-in</a> area, office complex, information desk, duty office and executive lounge. The new extensions and facilities were opened in 1978.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1980_to_2000">1980 to 2000</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cork_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 1980 to 2000">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The 1980s began with an extension of the <a href="/wiki/Airport_ramp" class="mw-redirect" title="Airport ramp">main apron</a>. New services to <a href="/wiki/London_Gatwick_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="London Gatwick Airport">London Gatwick</a> began, while Aer Lingus\' commuter division started a new domestic service to Dublin Airport. In 1985 following significant growth, Aer Rianta carried out a survey of the terminal facilities with a view to carrying out a major expansion and development programme. On 8 June 1987, Ryanair commenced services at Cork Airport. The following year, Phase I of the Terminal Expansion and Development Plan was completed. The following year the main runway extension of 1,000&#160;ft (300&#160;m) was opened.\n</p><p>The 1990s began with the completion of Phase II of the terminal expansion in 1991 and Phase III being completed in 1992 with the plan being brought to completion in 1994.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2000_to_2010">2000 to 2010</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cork_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 2000 to 2010">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>A <a href="/wiki/Great_Southern_Railways#Buses_and_hotels" title="Great Southern Railways">Great Southern Hotel</a> was opened on the airport grounds during 2001 and plans were drawn up for the construction of a new terminal building and ancillary capital investment works at an estimated cost of \xe2\x82\xac140 million. Also towards the end of 2001, new Irish regional airline <a href="/wiki/Aer_Arann" class="mw-redirect" title="Aer Arann">Aer Arann</a> opened its second base at Cork opening new routes to/from the airport.\n</p><p>Along with the construction of the terminal, roads were upgraded from <a href="/wiki/Single_carriageway" title="Single carriageway">single</a> to <a href="/wiki/Dual_carriageway" title="Dual carriageway">dual carriageway</a> and re-aligned, and a new short term <a href="/wiki/Multi-storey_car_park" class="mw-redirect" title="Multi-storey car park">multi-storey car park</a> was constructed. <a href="/wiki/Jet_bridge" title="Jet bridge">Airbridges</a> were included in the original terminal design, however only one airbridge was built because airline representatives from the <a href="/wiki/Low-cost_carrier" title="Low-cost carrier">low-cost carriers</a> who use Cork Airport made it clear that they did not want airbridges, would not use them and would not pay for them. In the circumstances, the <a href="/wiki/Dublin_Airport_Authority" class="mw-redirect" title="Dublin Airport Authority">DAA</a> removed the airbridges from the terminal design during construction of the new facility. The new terminal was completed with four fixed links to the main building, and was designed to accommodate additional airbridges in future or if needed.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>In 2005, <a href="/wiki/Ryanair" title="Ryanair">Ryanair</a> opened its 15th European base and second Irish base at Cork. The following year, the new terminal opened on 15 August 2006. Designed by <a href="/wiki/Hellmuth,_Obata_and_Kassabaum" class="mw-redirect" title="Hellmuth, Obata and Kassabaum">HOK</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jacobs_Engineering_Group" title="Jacobs Engineering Group">Jacobs Engineering Group</a>, the new terminal was the first built in Ireland in the 21st century.\n</p><p>In April 2008, the board of Cork Airport Authority agreed by one vote to accept responsibility for a debt of \xe2\x82\xac113 million incurred by the Dublin Airport Authority in the redevelopment of Cork Airport to secure independence from Dublin Airport. This was despite government commitments that the Cork Airport Authority would be established on a debt-free basis.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The Cork Airport Authority Board also stated that their strong reservations about the level of debt that Cork Airport was being levied with and the potential impact on its future sustainability. In late April 2008, Cork Airport Authority chairman, Joe Gantly, announced his resignation effective from the end of July 2008. Gerry Walsh replaced Gantly as chairman.<sup id="cite_ref-ORKhistory_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ORKhistory-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Irish_Aviation_Authority" title="Irish Aviation Authority">Irish Aviation Authority</a> completed a new control tower 1&#160;km from the old terminal to the west of the main runway.\nThe total cost was \xe2\x82\xac7.5 million and was funded entirely by the IAA with no <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Ireland" title="Government of Ireland">government</a> funding. Construction began in August 2007 and was completed in June 2008 but it took until mid October 2009 to get all the new systems tested and working. The new control tower officially opened on 20 October 2009 at 00:01.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2010_to_present">2010 to present</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cork_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 2010 to present">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>On 22 May 2011, at around 4:30pm a man entered a <a href="/wiki/Garda_S%C3%ADoch%C3%A1na" title="Garda S\xc3\xadoch\xc3\xa1na">Garda S\xc3\xadoch\xc3\xa1na</a> vehicle in <a href="/wiki/Cork_(city)" title="Cork (city)">Cork City Centre</a> and slashed the Garda in the vehicle with a knife. The Garda jumped from his vehicle and the man <a href="/wiki/Carjacking" title="Carjacking">hijacked</a> it, hitting a number of vehicles while driving to Cork Airport. At approximately 4:50pm the vehicle rammed through a perimeter airport fence, before breaking down. The man abandoned the Garda vehicle and, again producing a knife, hijacked an <a href="/wiki/Airport_Police_(Ireland)" title="Airport Police (Ireland)">Airport Fire Service</a> vehicle. By this time <a href="/wiki/Air_Traffic_Control" class="mw-redirect" title="Air Traffic Control">Air Traffic Control</a> had suspended all operations. The man drove erratically on the airport taxiway, driving underneath a stationary <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Cook_Airlines_Belgium" title="Thomas Cook Airlines Belgium">Thomas Cook</a> aircraft at high speed. The man proceeded to <a href="/wiki/Battering_ram" title="Battering ram">ram</a> several Garda and airport vehicles, before attempting to ram an <a href="/wiki/Aer_Lingus" title="Aer Lingus">Aer Lingus</a> aircraft. The hijacked vehicle <a href="/wiki/Stall_(engine)" title="Stall (engine)">stalled</a>, halting feet from the <a href="/wiki/Airbus_A320-200" class="mw-redirect" title="Airbus A320-200">Airbus A320</a>. Armed Garda\xc3\xad subdued the man with a <a href="/wiki/Taser" title="Taser">taser</a>. Flights were resumed after the man was removed and the airport perimeter secured.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>On 16 October 2011, Cork Airport celebrated its 50th anniversary.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a customer satisfaction in a global survey of passengers carried out by <a href="/wiki/Airports_Council_International_Europe" title="Airports Council International Europe">Airports Council International</a>. The survey measured customer satisfaction across eight categories in 61 regional airports worldwide, with Cork Airport scoring highest.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 2014, the Airport met its public sector obligation of a 33% reduction in energy costs, 6 years earlier than their 2020 target, and were awarded first place in the public sector category at the Sustainable Energy Awards 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 2016, Cork Airport won the Corporate Campaign of the Year Prize at the EU Public Affairs Awards (EuroPAwards) in Brussels, primarily for its efforts to secure a direct transatlantic route.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> Also in 2016, Cork Airport became the first Irish airport to launch a no drone zone within 5&#160;km of the airfield.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> The campaign was launched following a number of incidents involving drones or aircraft on approach or departure from aerodromes throughout Europe. Despite this campaign, at least two drones encroached airspace during April 2017.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In June 2017, the airport was named as \'Best Airport in Europe under 5 million passengers\' at the 27th Airports Council International (ACI) Europe General Assembly.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geographical_situation">Geographical situation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cork_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Geographical situation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>With an elevation of 153&#160;m (502&#160;ft) above sea level, Cork Airport is sometimes prone to <a href="/wiki/Fog" title="Fog">fog</a> and a low <a href="/wiki/Ceiling_(cloud)" title="Ceiling (cloud)">cloud ceiling</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Instrument_landing_system" title="Instrument landing system">Instrument landing system</a> has been upgraded to <a href="/wiki/Category_III_approach" class="mw-redirect" title="Category III approach">Category II</a>, and together with a 305&#160;m (1,001&#160;ft) extension of the main runway has significantly reduced the number of diversions. However, during times of severe inclement weather the airport can suffer from delays or diversions to airports such as Shannon, Dublin or <a href="/wiki/Kerry_Airport" title="Kerry Airport">Kerry</a>. Similarly, diversions from these airports occasionally land at Cork.\n</p><p>The length of the main runway dictates that the airport cannot handle fully laden large widebody aircraft. Large <a href="/wiki/Wide-body_aircraft" title="Wide-body aircraft">wide-bodied aircraft</a> do visit Cork Airport on an irregular basis, for example to operate ad-hoc charter services for flights to sporting events such as those of the <a href="/wiki/Munster_Rugby" title="Munster Rugby">Munster Rugby</a> team.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Operator">Operator</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cork_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Operator">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>From its opening in 1961, the airport was managed by the Department of Transport and Power, now the <a href="/wiki/Department_of_Transport,_Tourism_and_Sport" class="mw-redirect" title="Department of Transport, Tourism and Sport">Department of Transport</a>. Aer Rianta took control of Cork and Shannon Airports on 1 April 1969 and the assets of the airports were transferred to the company under the <a href="/wiki/Air_Navigation_and_Transport_Act" title="Air Navigation and Transport Act">Air Navigation and Transport (Amendment) Act, 1998</a>.  The name of Aer Rianta was changed to the Dublin Airport Authority (DAA) under the <i>State Airports Act 2004</i>, which also created the Cork Airport Authority<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> and the Shannon Airport Authority.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> These companies were charged with preparing a business plan in preparation for taking over the assets of their airports from the DAA.\n</p><p>Cork Airport is responsible for the infrastructure which enables airlines and handling agents at the airport.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> Some airlines provide their own <a href="/wiki/Aircraft_ground_handling" title="Aircraft ground handling">aircraft ground handling</a> services (such as <a href="/wiki/Aer_Lingus" title="Aer Lingus">Aer Lingus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ryanair" title="Ryanair">Ryanair</a>), while other airlines contract services to third party ground handlers (like <a href="/wiki/Swissport" title="Swissport">Swissport</a> and Weston Aviation).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Passenger_facilities">Passenger facilities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cork_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Passenger facilities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The main terminal at Cork Airport contains several shopping and eating facilities both before and after the security screening area, a bureau de change kiosk and ATM (cash machine) and a business lounge operated by Swissport.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2021)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> There are eight departure gates in the airport, two of which have an airbridge.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Wi-Fi" title="Wi-Fi">Wi-Fi</a> is available throughout the terminal.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>There are two hotels located adjacent to the passenger terminal, the <a href="/wiki/Cork_International_Hotel" title="Cork International Hotel">Cork International Hotel</a> which is located in the business park, and the Cork Airport Hotel (previously branded Park Inn by Radisson) which is located opposite the terminal on airport grounds.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Development_plans">Development plans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cork_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Development plans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In 2013 Cork Airport completed a Master Plan for the overall development of the airport until 2023.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>This plan envisages that the cargo area, currently located to the north\xe2\x80\x93east of the airport, would be moved to the south\xe2\x80\x93east of the grounds, with the <a href="/wiki/General_aviation" title="General aviation">general aviation</a> area transferred opposite the main runway at the south\xe2\x80\x93west of airport.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> Using space from the removal of the cargo area, the main terminal could be extended northwards, potentially doubling the space for aircraft. The plan also calls for the old terminal to be demolished.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Parallel plans for transport infrastructure also exist, including upgrading the <a href="/wiki/N27_road_(Ireland)" title="N27 road (Ireland)">N27</a> to two lanes in each direction and a dedicated <a href="/wiki/Bus_lane" title="Bus lane">bus lane</a> for city\xe2\x80\x93bound traffic. The roundabout at the entrance of the airport would be enhanced with an under-pass.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In February 2017 it was announced that \xe2\x82\xac4 million was to be invested at the airport for training, screening and restaurant facilities, with the existing restaurant moving from landside to airside.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span id="Passenger_numbers">Passenger numbers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cork_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Passenger numbers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Passenger numbers at Cork Airport increased every year during the ten years between 1998 and 2008 by an average of 14.8% per annum from around 1.3 million to over 3.2 million. Passenger numbers fell however during the subsequent seven years to just below 2.1 million in 2015.<sup id="cite_ref-daa15_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-daa15-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> href="#cite_note-anna-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> In 2017 this number rose to 2.30 million, increasing during 2018 by a further 4% to around 2.4 million passengers.\n</p><p><br />\n</p>\n<style serves <a href="/wiki/Business_jet" title="Business jet">business jets</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> with flying schools located to the south of the terminal building, on the east side of the main runway.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Business_park">Business park</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a acres) business park was developed at the airport from October 1998.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ORKhistory_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ORKhistory-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> As of 2017<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Cork_Airport&amp;action=edit">&#91;update&#93;</a></sup>, there were in excess of 50 tenant companies in the business park, including <a href="/wiki/Amazon.com" class="mw-redirect" title="Amazon.com">Amazon</a>, <a href="/wiki/IBM" title="IBM">IBM</a>, <a href="/wiki/BNY_Mellon" title="BNY Mellon">BNY Mellon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Logitech" title="Logitech">Logitech</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nuix" title="Nuix">Nuix</a>, <a href="/wiki/McKesson_Corporation" title="McKesson Corporation">McKesson</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aviva" title="Aviva">Aviva</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ground_transport">Ground transport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cork_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Ground transport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bus">Bus</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cork_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Bus">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Bus_%C3%89ireann" title="Bus \xc3\x89ireann">Bus \xc3\x89ireann</a> serve the airport on a number of routes from the city centre (including route numbers 226 and 226A),<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> from <a href="/wiki/Kinsale" title="Kinsale">Kinsale</a> (226) and seasonally from <a href="/wiki/Kenmare" title="Kenmare">Kenmare</a> (252). Route 225 links Cork Airport to <a href="/wiki/Cork_Kent_railway_station" title="Cork Kent railway station">Cork Kent railway station</a>, <a href="/wiki/Haulbowline" title="Haulbowline">Haulbowline</a> via <a href="/wiki/Ballygarvan" class="mw-redirect" title="Ballygarvan">Ballygarvan</a>. <a href="/wiki/Carrigaline" title="Carrigaline">Carrigaline</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ringaskiddy" title="Ringaskiddy">Ringaskiddy Ferryport</a>. Route 236 operates a daily service from <a href="/wiki/Castletownbere" title="Castletownbere">Castletownbere</a>, <a href="/wiki/Glengarriff" title="Glengarriff">Glengarriff</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bantry" title="Bantry">Bantry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dunmanway" title="Dunmanway">Dunmanway</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bandon,_County_Cork" title="Bandon, County Cork">Bandon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Innishannon" title="Innishannon">Innishannon</a>, however this only operates in the Cork City direction. <a href="/wiki/CityLink_Ireland" class="mw-redirect" title="CityLink Ireland">CityLink Ireland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bus_%C3%89ireann" title="Bus \xc3\x89ireann">Bus \xc3\x89ireann</a> operate coach services to the airport from <a href="/wiki/Galway" title="Galway">Galway</a> via <a href="/wiki/Limerick" title="Limerick">Limerick</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxi">Taxi</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cork_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Taxi">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The airport is served by <a href="/wiki/Taxicab" title="Taxicab">taxi</a>, with a taxi rank outside the arrivals entrance.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rail">Rail</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cork_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Rail">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>There is no rail link to the airport, and the nearest station is <a href="/wiki/Cork_Kent_railway_station" title="Cork Kent railway station">Cork Kent station</a> in the city. \n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Car">Car</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cork_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Car">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Cork Airport is located  7.5 kilometres (4.7&#160;mi) from <a href="/wiki/Cork_(city)" title="Cork (city)">Cork</a> city centre at the south end of the <a href="/wiki/N27_road_(Ireland)" title="N27 road (Ireland)">N27</a> when coming from the north, west, or east of Cork and off the R600 when coming from <a href="/wiki/Kinsale" title="Kinsale">Kinsale</a>. Dublin is approximately 260 kilometres (160&#160;mi) away and 107 kilometres (66&#160;mi) from Limerick. DAA operates all car parks at the airport, with both short-term and long-term parking within the campus and over 4,600 spaces in total. A number of companies offer car-hire in the arrivals hall of the terminal building.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Accidents_and_incidents">Accidents and incidents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cork_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Accidents and incidents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<ul><li>On 10 February 2011, a <a href="/wiki/Fairchild_Swearingen_Metroliner" title="Fairchild Swearingen Metroliner">Fairchild SA 227-BC Metro III</a> owned by the Spanish airline Air Lada registered EC-ITP, was operating a scheduled flight under the <a href="/wiki/Air_operator%27s_certificate" title="Air operator&#39;s certificate">AOC</a> of Flightline S.L for the ticket seller <a href="/wiki/Manx2" title="Manx2">Manx2</a>. The flight <a href="/wiki/Manx2_Flight_7100" title="Manx2 Flight 7100">NM7100</a> was operating from <a href="/wiki/George_Best_Belfast_City_Airport" title="George Best Belfast City Airport">Belfast-City</a> to Cork with ten passengers and two crew. At 09:50 hrs during the third attempt to land at Cork Airport in low visibility conditions, control was lost and the aircraft impacted the runway. The aircraft came to a rest inverted in soft ground to the right of the runway surface. Post impact fires occurred in both engines which were quickly extinguished by the Airport Fire Service (AFS). Six persons, including both pilots, were fatally injured. Four passengers were seriously injured and two received minor injuries.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a
