id="Connection-oriented_and_connectionless_communications">Connection-oriented and connectionless communications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Computer_network_programming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Connection-oriented and connectionless communications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Very generally, most of communications can be divided into <a href="/wiki/Connection-oriented_communication" title="Connection-oriented communication">connection-oriented</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Connectionless_communication" title="Connectionless communication">connectionless</a>. Whether a communication is connection-oriented or connectionless, is defined by the <a href="/wiki/Communications_protocol" class="mw-redirect" title="Communications protocol">communication protocol</a>, and not by <span class="nowrap"><a href="/wiki/Application_programming_interface" class="mw-redirect" title="Application programming interface">application programming interface</a> (API)</span>. Examples of the connection-oriented protocols include <span class="nowrap"><a href="/wiki/Transmission_Control_Protocol" title="Transmission Control Protocol">Transmission Control Protocol</a> (TCP)</span> and <span class="nowrap"><a href="/wiki/Sequenced_Packet_Exchange" title="Sequenced Packet Exchange">Sequenced Packet Exchange</a> (SPX)</span>, and examples of connectionless protocols include <span class="nowrap"><a href="/wiki/User_Datagram_Protocol" title="User Datagram Protocol">User Datagram Protocol</a> (UDP)</span>, "raw IP", and <span class="nowrap"><a href="/wiki/Internetwork_Packet_Exchange" title="Internetwork Packet Exchange">Internetwork Packet Exchange</a> (IPX)</span>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Clients_and_servers">Clients and servers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Computer_network_programming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Clients and servers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Client%E2%80%93server_model" title="Client\xe2\x80\x93server model">client\xe2\x80\x93server model</a></div>\n<p>For connection-oriented communications, communication parties usually have different roles. One party is usually waiting for incoming connections; this party is usually referred to as "<a href="/wiki/Server_(computing)" title="Server (computing)">server</a>". Another party is the one which initiates connection; this party is usually referred to as "<a href="/wiki/Client_(computing)" title="Client (computing)">client</a>".\n</p><p>For connectionless communications, one party ("server") is usually waiting for an incoming packet, and another party ("client") is usually understood as the one which sends an unsolicited <a href="/wiki/Network_packet" title="Network packet">packet</a> to "server".\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Popular_protocols_and_APIs">Popular protocols and APIs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Computer_network_programming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Popular protocols and APIs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Network programming traditionally covers different layers of <a href="/wiki/OSI_model" title="OSI model">OSI/ISO model</a> (most of application-level programming belongs to L4 and up).
