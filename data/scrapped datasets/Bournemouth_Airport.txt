href="/wiki/IATA_airport_code" title="IATA airport code">IATA</a>: <b>BOH</b>, <a href="/wiki/ICAO_airport_code" title="ICAO airport code">ICAO</a>: <b>EGHH</b>) (previously known as <b>Hurn Airport</b> and <b>Bournemouth International Airport</b>) is an airport located 3.5&#160;<a href="/wiki/Nautical_mile" title="Nautical mile">NM</a> (6.5&#160;km; 4.0&#160;mi) north-northeast of <a href="/wiki/Bournemouth" title="Bournemouth">Bournemouth</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-aip_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aip-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> in southern England. The site opened as <a href="/wiki/RAF_Hurn" title="RAF Hurn">RAF Hurn</a> in 1941, but was transferred to civil control in 1944. For a short period (between 1944 and 1946) Hurn served as London\'s international airport, until the opening of facilities at <a href="/wiki/Heathrow" class="mw-redirect" title="Heathrow">Heathrow</a>. Commercial services resumed in the late 1950s, with <a href="/wiki/Palmair" title="Palmair">Palmair</a> commencing flights to <a href="/wiki/Palma_de_Mallorca_Airport" title="Palma de Mallorca Airport">Palma, Majorca</a> in October 1958.<sup id="cite_ref-history_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Subsequently, <a href="/wiki/Ryanair" title="Ryanair">Ryanair</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thomson_Airways" class="mw-redirect" title="Thomson Airways">TUI Airways</a> based aircraft at the airport, with scheduled flights now frequently serving Western Europe and the Mediterranean area, with charter and seasonal services serving North Africa, North America, and the Caribbean.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Passenger numbers peaked in 2007 when just over one&#160;million passed through the airport. In 2016, the passenger total was around 670,000.<sup id="cite_ref-stats_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stats-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Ryanair and TUI Airways are the primary users of the airport, which was owned and operated by <a href="/wiki/Manchester_Airports_Group" class="mw-redirect" title="Manchester Airports Group">Manchester Airports Group</a> (MAG), the largest British airport operator until December 2017, when <a href="/wiki/Regional_%26_City_Airports_(RCA)" class="mw-redirect" title="Regional &amp; City Airports (RCA)">Regional &amp; City Airports (RCA)</a> acquired Bournemouth Airport for an undisclosed amount.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc" role="navigation" <span class="toctext">External links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Location">Location</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bournemouth_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Location">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Bournemouth Airport is situated on the edge of <a href="/wiki/Hurn" title="Hurn">Hurn</a> village in the <a href="/wiki/Christchurch,_Dorset" title="Christchurch, Dorset">Borough of Christchurch</a>, 4 miles (6&#160;km) north of Bournemouth, 1 mile (1.6&#160;km) west of the A338 and approximately 100 miles (160&#160;km) south west of London. The airport is accessible via the <a href="/wiki/A31_road" title="A31 road">A31</a> from the <a href="/wiki/M27_motorway" title="M27 motorway">M27</a> and <a href="/wiki/M3_motorway_(Great_Britain)" title="M3 motorway (Great Britain)">M3 motorways</a> to the east, and via the <a href="/wiki/A35_road" title="A35 road">A35</a> to the west. The nearest other airports serving the area are <a href="/wiki/Exeter_International_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Exeter International Airport">Exeter International Airport</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bristol_Airport" title="Bristol Airport">Bristol Airport</a> and <a href="/wiki/Southampton_Airport" title="Southampton Airport">Southampton Airport</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bournemouth_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" In that r\xc3\xb4le, it participated in the "First Commercial Land Plane Flight Overseas" from the United States, on 23 October. (That intercontinental flight in the <a href="/wiki/Douglas_DC-4" title="Douglas DC-4">Douglas DC-4</a> involved refueling stops at Gander, Newfoundland and Shannon in the Republic of Ireland.)<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In January 1946 <a href="/wiki/Pan_Am" title="Pan Am">Pan Am</a> opened a scheduled New York (<a href="/wiki/LaGuardia_Airport" title="LaGuardia Airport">La Guardia</a>) to London (Hurn) service, five days a week, using the new <a href="/wiki/DC-4" class="mw-redirect" title="DC-4">DC-4</a>; the journey time was 17<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>hours 40<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>minutes. It was also the starting point of the first England-Australia landplane service, which took three days in <a href="/wiki/Avro_Lancastrian" title="Avro Lancastrian">Avro Lancastrians</a> (modified <a href="/wiki/Lancaster_bomber" class="mw-redirect" title="Lancaster bomber">Lancaster bombers</a>).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="College_of_Air_Traffic_Control">College of Air Traffic Control</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bournemouth_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: College of Air Traffic Control">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Adjacent to the entrance to Bournemouth Airport was the College of Air Traffic Control, operated by <a href="/wiki/NATS_Holdings" title="NATS Holdings">NATS</a>, the now privatised provider of air traffic control services in the UK. Established by the <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_for_Transport#Minister_of_(War)_Transport_and_Minister_of_Civil_Aviation_(1941\xe2\x80\x931953)" title="Secretary of State for Transport">Ministry of Civil Aviation</a> as the School of Air Traffic Control in 1949, the establishment was retitled as a college in 1962.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Students from home and abroad were trained in all aspects of <a href="/wiki/Air_traffic_control" title="Air traffic control">ATC</a> operations and went on to work throughout the world. Electronic computer-based ATC simulators were widely employed. Usefully situated at an operational airfield, for a considerable period training in approach radar control was facilitated by the airport ATC unit. Students were able to practise live radar control exercises using temporarily detached Civil Aviation Flying Unit <a href="/wiki/De_Havilland_Dove" title="De Havilland Dove">Dove</a> aircraft as live targets. The building was also the home to the Air Traffic Control Evaluation Unit, responsible for developing technology used within the service.\n</p><p>During 2011, NATS transferred ATC training to its headquarters facility at <a href="/wiki/Whiteley" title="Whiteley">Whiteley</a> near Southampton. The Hurn facility was purchased by a <a href="/wiki/Free_school_(England)" title="Free school (England)">free school</a>, Parkfield School, serving Bournemouth and the local area.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Change_of_ownership">Change of ownership</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bournemouth_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Change of ownership">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>In 1969, the airport was purchased jointly by the Bournemouth Corporation and Dorset County Council and renamed as "Bournemouth Hurn Airport",<sup id="cite_ref-AGN_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AGN-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> later to become Bournemouth International Airport. The new owners decided to redevelop the facility as a commercial airport and, by 1980, the airport became used by charter airlines, when <a href="/wiki/European_Aviation_Air_Charter" title="European Aviation Air Charter">European Aviation</a> began services.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span id="1990s.E2.80.932000s"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1990s\xe2\x80\x932000s">1990s\xe2\x80\x932000s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bournemouth_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: 1990s\xe2\x80\x932000s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In 1993, the airport received its first regular passenger flights when Palmair <a href="/wiki/Wet_leased" class="mw-redirect" title="Wet leased">wet leased</a> its first aircraft and <a href="/wiki/European_Aviation_Air_Charter" title="European Aviation Air Charter">European Aviation Air Charter</a> (EAC) started operations.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> In 1995, the airport was sold to <a href="/wiki/National_Express" title="National Express">National Express</a> and then, in March 2001, was acquired by the <a href="/wiki/Manchester_Airports_Group" class="mw-redirect" title="Manchester Airports Group">Manchester Airports Group</a>, which was at that time the second-largest owner of UK airports.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1996, an extension to the main runway was officially opened by the arrival of <a href="/wiki/Concorde" title="Concorde">Concorde</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Travel agency Bath Travel chartered Concorde for supersonic champagne lunches across the Bay of Biscay. <a href="/wiki/Ryanair" title="Ryanair">Ryanair</a> also began services from Bournemouth to Dublin with a <a href="/wiki/Boeing_737-200" class="mw-redirect" title="Boeing 737-200">Boeing 737-200</a>.\n</p><p>Since 2001, a <a href="/wiki/Boeing_747SP" title="Boeing 747SP">Boeing 747SP</a> has been based at the airport; it is used by the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Thani" class="mw-redirect" title="House of Thani">Royal Family of Qatar</a> and other VIP government staff from the <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a> state of <a href="/wiki/Qatar" title="Qatar">Qatar</a>. The aircraft is often stored in the former BASCO building (Hangar 12) and is a regular visitor to <a href="/wiki/Z%C3%BCrich_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Z\xc3\xbcrich Airport">Z\xc3\xbcrich Airport</a> and <a href="/wiki/London_Heathrow_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="London Heathrow Airport">Heathrow</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>In late 2001, Bournemouth Flying Club took the leap into full commercial flight training and established Bournemouth Commercial Flight Training on the former SFT site adjacent to the threshold for runway 08. Bournemouth Commercial Flight Training (BCFT) has adapted and expanded throughout its history, contributing to the overall success of the airport and continuing to make Bournemouth Airport important to the overall commercial flight training in the UK.\n</p><p>Bath Travel\'s Palmair remained the prime user of the airport, with a 737-200 permanently based there. In 2005, <a href="/wiki/Thomsonfly" title="Thomsonfly">Thomsonfly</a> became the first major low-cost airline to establish a hub at Bournemouth, allocating two Boeing 737-300 aircraft for scheduled services to Europe and (in 2008) to the Caribbean.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Also in 2005, <a href="/wiki/Air_Berlin" title="Air Berlin">Air Berlin</a> and <a href="/wiki/EasyJet" title="EasyJet">EasyJet</a> began services by announcing routes to Paderborn and Geneva respectively.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Air Berlin have now ceased operations at the airport. The airport previously had a daily service to the <a href="/wiki/Channel_Islands" title="Channel Islands">Channel Islands</a> provided by the Jersey-based <a href="/wiki/Blue_Islands" title="Blue Islands">Blue Islands</a> airline, which withdrew from Bournemouth in April 2009. Hungarian-based <a href="/wiki/Wizz_Air" title="Wizz Air">Wizz Air</a> also ran routes to Gdansk, Katowice, and Krak\xc3\xb3w during 2006 and 2007.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Route_development">Route development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bournemouth_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Route development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In 2007, Ryanair began to rapidly increase the number of services from the airport, initially starting routes to <a href="/wiki/Marseille" title="Marseille">Marseille</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alicante" title="Alicante">Alicante</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Milan" title="Milan">Milan</a> which brought the total to eight.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> In 2008, <a href="/wiki/Palmair" title="Palmair">Palmair</a> introduced a new series of charter flights to <a href="/wiki/Tunisia" title="Tunisia">Tunisia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fuerteventura" title="Fuerteventura">Fuerteventura</a>, <a href="/wiki/Naples" title="Naples">Naples</a>, <a href="/wiki/Amalfi_Coast" title="Amalfi Coast">Amalfi Coast</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Rhodes" title="Rhodes">Rhodes</a>. Olympic Holidays also launched new charter flights to <a href="/wiki/Corfu" title="Corfu">Corfu</a> and <a href="/wiki/Zakynthos" title="Zakynthos">Zakynthos</a> in Greece and <a href="/wiki/Larnaca" title="Larnaca">Larnaca</a> in Cyprus.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>On 9 January 2008, Ryanair announced that they would base one of their Boeing 737-800s at Bournemouth from April 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> During the first quarter, the airline announced routes to <a href="/wiki/M%C3%A1laga" title="M\xc3\xa1laga">M\xc3\xa1laga</a>, <a href="/wiki/Murcia" title="Murcia">Murcia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Palma_de_Mallorca" title="Palma de Mallorca">Palma de Mallorca</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wroclaw" class="mw-redirect" title="Wroclaw">Wroclaw</a> and the re-introduction of the <a href="/wiki/Nantes" title="Nantes">Nantes</a> route. An additional flight each day was added to the <a href="/wiki/Glasgow_Prestwick_Airport" title="Glasgow Prestwick Airport">Glasgow Prestwick</a> route, with the addition of a twice-daily flight to <a href="/wiki/Edinburgh" title="Edinburgh">Edinburgh</a>. In May the same year, <a href="/wiki/Bergamo" title="Bergamo">Bergamo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Beauvais" title="Beauvais">Beauvais</a> routes were announced to commence in October plus a new weekly ski flight to Turin for the winter season.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>Increasing from 14 routes, after the discontinuation of the Nantes route, to 18 \xe2\x80\x93 <a href="/wiki/Carcassonne" title="Carcassonne">Carcassonne</a>, <a href="/wiki/Faro,_Portugal" title="Faro, Portugal">Faro</a>, <a href="/wiki/Limoges" title="Limoges">Limoges</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Reus" title="Reus">Reus</a> were added to the route network in February 2009.\n</p><p>In December 2009, <a href="/wiki/Bmibaby" title="Bmibaby">bmibaby</a> announced a new summer route to Jersey. It was the first time the airline had operated out of the airport; they provided three services a week, using a Boeing 737-500.\nAgain in December, Ryanair added four new routes to the network, bringing the total to 17. Another Boeing 737-800 was based at the airport and the airline projected to fly 650,000 passengers <i lang="la" title="Latin-language text">per annum</i> at Bournemouth,<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> however the actual figure was lower.\n</p><p>Following the closure of European Aviation Air Charter, Palmair chartered various aircraft types from Jet2, Viking, Blue Line, Tor Air and Astraeus, before unveiling their new Astraeus-leased Boeing 737-500 (G-PJPJ) on 13 May 2009.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> The original agreement was for the aircraft to be leased until April 2012, but the airline ceased operations in October 2010.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Flybe" title="Flybe">Flybe</a> started services in May 2010 but discontinued services in November the same year. It led to no scheduled services from the airport. After a hiatus of services, Blue Islands recommenced services to Guernsey and Jersey on 1 November 2011. Flybe announced on 10 November 2015 that all destinations would end 27 March 2016<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In September 2016 one of the UK\'s newest AOC\'s Scenic Air Tours started operations from the airport, offering pleasure/scenic flights along the south coast and aircraft rentals. The company has now expanded with a second operating base at <a href="/wiki/Durham_Tees_Valley_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Durham Tees Valley Airport">Durham Tees Valley Airport</a> in the North East. The company operates two <a href="/wiki/Piper_PA-28_Cherokee" title="Piper PA-28 Cherokee">Piper PA-28</a> and one <a href="/wiki/Grumman_American_AA-5" title="Grumman American AA-5">Grumman American AA-5</a>.\n</p><p>In November 2016, Bournemouth based Eagle European commenced operations on its A \xe2\x80\x93 B AOC, flying the <a href="/wiki/Cessna_421_Golden_Eagle" class="mw-redirect" title="Cessna 421 Golden Eagle">Cessna 421 Golden Eagle</a> on charter flights, focusing heavily on the medical role.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Expansion">Expansion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bournemouth_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Other facilities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The airport has a 200-acre (0.81&#160;km<sup>2</sup>) industrial park, including offices and <a href="/wiki/Hangar" title="Hangar">hangars</a>. In early March 2009, Manchester Airport Developments completed the construction of Cirrus Court, a development of 14 industrial units which is the first part of a number of phases to redevelop the northern aviation sector.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>When <a href="/wiki/Channel_Express" title="Channel Express">Channel Express</a> operated, its head office was in Building 470 at the airport.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Babcock_International_Group" class="mw-redirect" title="Babcock International Group">Babcock International Group</a> has its Defence and Security offices located at the airport.\n</p><p>L3 CTS (formerly CTC Aviation) has its main U.K flight training base at the airfield with students completing basic, intermediate and advanced training there. Located on the East side from its purpose built hangar, the company operates a fleet of <a href="/wiki/Diamond_DA42" class="mw-redirect" title="Diamond DA42">Diamond DA42</a>, <a href="/wiki/Slingsby_T67_Firefly" title="Slingsby T67 Firefly">Slingsby T67 Firefly</a> and recently <a href="/wiki/Diamond_DA40" class="mw-redirect" title="Diamond DA40">Diamond DA40</a> aircraft. Their Bournemouth base complements its facilities in Southampton, Coventry, Ponte de Sor (Portugal), Hamilton (New Zealand) and recently opened satellite base in Cranfield.\n</p><p>Bournemouth Commercial Flight Training and Airline Pilot Academy Operates from its main office and hangar on Aviation Park West adjacent to the <a href="/wiki/National_Police_Air_Service" title="National Police Air Service">National Police Air Service</a> (NPAS) facility. The apron provides parking for its commercial fleet including two <a href="/wiki/Beechcraft_Duchess" title="Beechcraft Duchess">BE76 Duchess</a> and four Piper PA-28 aircraft. They also have hangared aircraft belonging to the company, including a <a href="/wiki/Pilatus_PC-12" title="Pilatus PC-12">Pilatus PC-12</a>, a <a href="/wiki/SIAI-Marchetti_SF.260" title="SIAI-Marchetti SF.260">SIAI-Marchetti SF.260</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Beagle_B.121_Pup" class="mw-redirect" title="Beagle B.121 Pup">Beagle B.121 Pup</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Aviation_Bulldog" title="Scottish Aviation Bulldog">Scottish Aviation Bulldog</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Partenavia_P.68" title="Partenavia P.68">Partenavia P.68</a> and several <a href="/wiki/Robin_HR_200" class="mw-redirect" title="Robin HR 200">Robin HR 200</a> aircraft. BCFT specialises in modular flight training whilst its sister company APA is pioneering a new and dynamic approach to Integrated Flight Training with bases at Bournemouth International Airport and <a href="/wiki/Orlando_Melbourne_International_Airport" title="Orlando Melbourne International Airport">Orlando Melbourne International Airport</a>.\n</p><p>Scenic Air Tours, an A to A AOC holder, also operates from Aviation Park West with its main base of operation at the airport, its secondary base located at <a href="/wiki/Durham_Tees_Valley_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Durham Tees Valley Airport">Durham Tees Valley Airport</a> houses one of its Piper PA-28 aircraft available for Sightseeing and Aerial Photography. The Bournemouth-based fleet consists of a Piper PA-28 and Grumman American AA-5 both available for sightseeing trips and rentals.\n</p><p>Bournemouth is home of a dedicated <a href="/wiki/Pilatus_Aircraft" title="Pilatus Aircraft">Pilatus</a> maintenance facility, focusing on the <a href="/wiki/Pilatus_PC-12" title="Pilatus PC-12">Pilatus PC-12</a>, serving aircraft from across Europe. Jetfly Aviation, a fractional ownership company, uses the Bournemouth facility for its maintenance needs and has aircraft and crew based at the airport.\n</p><p>As well as the Pilatus maintenance facility the airport is also home to several others, all specialising in various areas of the industry. The largest for general aviation aircraft is Airtime Aviation, providing maintenance for aircraft up to the size of <a href="/wiki/Beechcraft_King_Air_200" class="mw-redirect" title="Beechcraft King Air 200">Beechcraft King Air</a>. Airtime also provide aircraft painting through its sister company, Airtime Paint, handling all kinds of aircraft including small jets. Fast Aviation also provides maintenance for general aviation aircraft and is based on the north side of the airfield. MCA provide specialist <a href="/wiki/King_Air_200" class="mw-redirect" title="King Air 200">King Air</a> support and maintenance, with several worldwide contracts. Jets and Technic Air provide maintenance for Jet aircraft and smaller turboprop airliners.\n</p><p>Handling for General Aviation aircraft is provided by two companies, Bliss Aviation a small flight school specialising in Helicopter training provide handling for smaller aircraft such as Piper PA-28 and larger multi engine pistons like <a href="/wiki/Piper_Chieftain" class="mw-redirect" title="Piper Chieftain">Piper Chieftain</a> and <a href="/wiki/Beechcraft_Baron" title="Beechcraft Baron">Beechcraft Baron</a>. Larger corporate aircraft are handled by Signature Flight Support, often handling aircraft up to the size of <a href="/wiki/Gulfstream_G650" title="Gulfstream G650">Gulfstream G650</a> and <a href="/wiki/Falcon_7X" class="mw-redirect" title="Falcon 7X">Falcon 7X</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Accidents_and_incidents">Accidents and incidents</span><span
