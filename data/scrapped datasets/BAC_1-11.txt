<b>BAC-111</b>/<b>BAC 1-11</b>) was an early <a href="/wiki/Jet_airliner" title="Jet airliner">jet airliner</a> produced by the <a href="/wiki/British_Aircraft_Corporation" title="British Aircraft Corporation">British Aircraft Corporation</a> (BAC).\nOriginally conceived by <a href="/wiki/Hunting_Aircraft" title="Hunting Aircraft">Hunting Aircraft</a> as a 30-seat jet, before its merger into BAC in 1960, it was launched as an 80-seat airliner with a <a href="/wiki/British_United_Airways" title="British United Airways">British United Airways</a> order on 9 May 1961.\nThe prototype conducted its <a href="/wiki/Maiden_flight" title="Maiden flight">maiden flight</a> on 20 August 1963, and it was first delivered to its launch customer on 22 January 1965.\nThe 119-seat, stretched 500 series was introduced in 1967.\nTotal production amount to 244, until 1982 in UK and between 1982 and 1989 in Romania where nine <b>Rombac One-Eleven</b> were licence-built by <a href="/wiki/Romaero" title="Romaero">Romaero</a>.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Short_haul" class="mw-redirect" title="Short haul">short haul</a>, <a href="/wiki/Narrowbody_aircraft" class="mw-redirect" title="Narrowbody aircraft">narrowbody aircraft</a> was powered by aft-mounted <a href="/wiki/Rolls-Royce_Spey" title="Rolls-Royce Spey">Rolls-Royce Spey</a> low-bypass turbofans, a configuration similar to the earlier <a href="/wiki/Sud_Aviation_Caravelle" title="Sud Aviation Caravelle">Sud Aviation Caravelle</a> and later <a href="/wiki/Douglas_DC-9" class="mw-redirect" title="Douglas DC-9">Douglas DC-9</a>.\nIt also competed with early <a href="/wiki/Boeing_737" title="Boeing 737">Boeing 737</a> variants and was used by several US carriers, as well as multiple British, overseas and European airlines, including Romanian operators. \nIt was replaced by the newer <a href="/wiki/Airbus_A320" class="mw-redirect" title="Airbus A320">Airbus A320</a> and later 737 variants, as well as by the <a href="/wiki/Bombardier_CRJ200" class="mw-redirect" title="Bombardier CRJ200">Bombardier CRJ200</a> regional jet.\n<a href="/wiki/Aircraft_noise" class="mw-redirect" title="Aircraft noise">Noise</a> restrictions accelerated its transition to African carriers in the 1990s, and the last BAC One-Eleven was retired in 2019.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc" role="navigation" class="mw-headline" id="Early_development">Early development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=BAC_One-Eleven&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Hunting_107.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/b/bd/Hunting_107.png/220px-Hunting_107.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="91" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/b/bd/Hunting_107.png/330px-Hunting_107.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/b/bd/Hunting_107.png/440px-Hunting_107.png 2x" data-file-width="500" data-file-height="207" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Hunting_107.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The initial Hunting 107 concept from <a href="/wiki/Hunting_Aircraft" title="Hunting Aircraft">Hunting Aircraft</a></div></div></div>\n<p>In the 1950s, although the pioneering <a href="/wiki/De_Havilland_Comet" title="De Havilland Comet">de Havilland Comet</a> had suffered disasters in service, strong passenger demand had been demonstrated for jet propulsion. Several manufacturers raced to release passenger jets, including those aimed at the short-haul market, such as the <a href="/wiki/Sud_Aviation_Caravelle" title="Sud Aviation Caravelle">Sud Aviation Caravelle</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> In July 1956, <a href="/wiki/British_European_Airways" title="British European Airways">British European Airways</a> published a paper calling for a "second generation" jet airliner to operate beside their existing <a href="/wiki/Turboprop" title="Turboprop">turboprop</a> designs. This led to a variety of designs from the British aerospace industry. <a href="/wiki/Hunting_Aircraft" title="Hunting Aircraft">Hunting Aircraft</a> started design studies on a jet-powered replacement for the successful <a href="/wiki/Vickers_Viscount" title="Vickers Viscount">Vickers Viscount</a>, developing the 30-seat <b>Hunting 107</b>. Around the same time, Vickers started a similar development of a 140-seat derivative of its <a href="/wiki/Vickers_VC10" title="Vickers VC10">VC10</a> project, the <b>VC11</b>. Many other aviation firms also produced designs.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1960 Hunting, under British government pressure, merged with <a href="/wiki/Vickers-Armstrongs" title="Vickers-Armstrongs">Vickers-Armstrongs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bristol_Aeroplane_Company" title="Bristol Aeroplane Company">Bristol</a>, and <a href="/wiki/English_Electric" title="English Electric">English Electric</a> to form <a href="/wiki/British_Aircraft_Corporation" title="British Aircraft Corporation">British Aircraft Corporation</a> (BAC). The new BAC decided that the Hunting project had merit, but that there would be little market for a 30-seat jet airliner.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The design was reworked into the BAC 107, a 59-seat airliner powered by two 7,000 pounds-force (31&#160;kN) Bristol Siddeley BS75 <a href="/wiki/Turbofan" title="Turbofan">turbofan</a> engines.<sup id="cite_ref-database_p68_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-database_p68-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> BAC also continued development of the larger, 140-seat VC-11 development of the <a href="/wiki/Vickers_VC10" title="Vickers VC10">Vickers VC10</a> which it had inherited.<sup id="cite_ref-Andrews_vickers_p576-7_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Andrews_vickers_p576-7-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Other competing internal projects, such as the <a href="/wiki/Bristol_Type_200" title="Bristol Type 200">Bristol Type 200</a>, were quickly abandoned following absorption of Hunting into BAC.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:RR_Spey.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/RR_Spey.jpg/220px-RR_Spey.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="177" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/RR_Spey.jpg/330px-RR_Spey.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/RR_Spey.jpg/440px-RR_Spey.jpg 2x" data-file-width="820" data-file-height="660" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:RR_Spey.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The selected <a href="/wiki/Rolls-Royce_Spey" title="Rolls-Royce Spey">Rolls-Royce Spey</a> low-bypass <a href="/wiki/Turbofan" title="Turbofan">turbofan</a></div></div></div>\n<p>Market research showed the 59-seat BAC 107 was too small, and the design was reworked in 1961, with passenger capacity growing to 80 seats, and BS75s being discarded in favour of <a href="/wiki/Rolls-Royce_Spey" title="Rolls-Royce Spey">Rolls-Royce Speys</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The revised design was redesignated the BAC 111 (later known as the One-Eleven), with BAC abandoning the VC11 project to concentrate on the more promising One-Eleven. Unlike contemporary British airliners such as the <a href="/wiki/Hawker_Siddeley_Trident" title="Hawker Siddeley Trident">Hawker Siddeley Trident</a>, the One-Eleven was not designed specifically to meet the needs of the state-owned <a href="/wiki/British_European_Airways" title="British European Airways">British European Airways</a> or <a href="/wiki/British_Overseas_Airways_Corporation" title="British Overseas Airways Corporation">British Overseas Airways Corporation</a>, but on the needs of airlines around the world, and BAC expected orders for as many as 400.<sup id="cite_ref-database_p70-1_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-database_p70-1-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>On 9 May 1961 the One-Eleven was publicly launched when <a href="/wiki/British_United_Airways" title="British United Airways">British United Airways</a> (BUA) placed the first order for ten One-Eleven 200s.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> On 20 October <a href="/wiki/Braniff_International_Airways" title="Braniff International Airways">Braniff International Airways</a> in the United States ordered six.<sup id="cite_ref-database_p71_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-database_p71-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Mohawk_Airlines" title="Mohawk Airlines">Mohawk Airlines</a> sent representatives to Europe seeking out a new aircraft to bring them into the jet era, and on 24 July 1962 concluded an agreement for four One-Elevens.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Orders followed from <a href="/wiki/Kuwait_Airways" title="Kuwait Airways">Kuwait Airways</a> for three, and <a href="/wiki/Central_African_Airways" title="Central African Airways">Central African Airways</a> for two. Braniff subsequently doubled their order to 12, while <a href="/wiki/Aer_Lingus" title="Aer Lingus">Aer Lingus</a> ordered four. <a href="/wiki/Western_Airlines" title="Western Airlines">Western Airlines</a> ordered ten but later cancelled.<sup id="cite_ref-database_p71_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-database_p71-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Bonanza_Air_Lines" title="Bonanza Air Lines">Bonanza Air Lines</a> also ordered three in 1962<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> but was stopped by the US <a href="/wiki/United_States_government_role_in_civil_aviation#Civil_Aeronautics_Authority" title="United States government role in civil aviation">Civil Aeronautics Board</a> (CAB), which claimed that subsidies would be needed to operate a jet on Bonanza\'s routes, an action claimed by some at the time to be <a href="/wiki/Protectionism" title="Protectionism">protectionism</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> The CAB also stopped <a href="/wiki/Frontier_Airlines_(1950%E2%80%931986)" title="Frontier Airlines (1950\xe2\x80\x931986)">Frontier Airlines</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ozark_Air_Lines" title="Ozark Air Lines">Ozark Air Lines</a> from ordering One-Elevens, although allowing Ozark to order the similar <a href="/wiki/Douglas_DC-9" class="mw-redirect" title="Douglas DC-9">Douglas DC-9</a> and Frontier to order <a href="/wiki/Boeing_727-100" class="mw-redirect" title="Boeing 727-100">Boeing 727-100s</a>. The CAB had also unsuccessfully tried to block Mohawk\'s orders.<sup id="cite_ref-database_p71_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-database_p71-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In May 1963, BAC announced the One-Eleven 300 and 400. The new versions used the Mk. 511 version of the Spey with increased power, allowing more fuel upload and hence longer range. The difference between the 300 and 400 lay in the equipment and <a href="/wiki/Avionics" title="Avionics">avionics</a>, the 400 intended for sales in the United States and thus equipped with US instruments.<sup id="cite_ref-database_p71_12-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-database_p71-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> On 17 July 1963, <a href="/wiki/American_Airlines" title="American Airlines">American Airlines</a> ordered 15 aircraft, bringing the total to 60, plus options for 15.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Willis_p64_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Willis_p64-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> American Airlines eventually bought 30 of the 400-series, making the airline the largest customer of One-Elevens.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Prototypes">Prototypes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a 1965</div></div></div>\n<p>The prototype (<i>G-ASHG</i>) rolled out of <a href="/wiki/Bournemouth_Airport" title="Bournemouth Airport">Hurn</a> assembly hall on 28 July 1963, at which point BAC had received orders for the type from a number of operators. On 20 August 1963, the prototype conducted its first flight, painted in BUA livery. The first flight had taken place almost a year before the <a href="/wiki/Douglas_DC-9" class="mw-redirect" title="Douglas DC-9">Douglas DC-9</a>, a rival American jetliner; BAC considered the One-Eleven to hold a technological edge.<sup id="cite_ref-Hill_1999,_p._31_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hill_1999,_p._31-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The One-Eleven\'s lead was of significant importance commercially, since, as shown by the Bonanza Air Lines case, US authorities could refuse to approve sales of foreign aircraft to domestic airlines where an American alternative existed (Bonanza ended up ordering and operating the DC-9). Test flying was conducted by Squadron Leader <a href="/wiki/Dave_Glaser" title="Dave Glaser">Dave Glaser</a>.\n</p><p>The One-Eleven prototype, flown by test pilot <a href="/wiki/Mike_Lithgow" title="Mike Lithgow">Mike Lithgow</a>, <a href="/wiki/1963_BAC_One-Eleven_test_crash" title="1963 BAC One-Eleven test crash">crashed on 22 October</a> during <a href="/wiki/Stall_(flight)" class="mw-redirect" title="Stall (flight)">stall</a> testing, with the loss of all on board. The investigation led to the discovery of what became known as <a href="/wiki/Stall_(flight)#Deep_stall" class="mw-redirect" title="Stall (flight)">deep stall</a> or superstall, a phenomenon caused by reduced airflow to the <a href="/wiki/Tailplane" title="Tailplane">tailplane</a> caused by the combined blanking effects of the wing and the aft-mounted engine <a href="/wiki/Nacelle" title="Nacelle">nacelles</a> at high <a href="/wiki/Angle_of_attack" title="Angle of attack">angles of attack</a>, which prevents recovery of normal (nose-down) flight.<sup id="cite_ref-Deepstall_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Deepstall-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> To prevent such stalls, BAC designed and added devices known as <a href="/wiki/Stick_shaker" title="Stick shaker">stick shakers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stick_pusher" title="Stick pusher">stick pushers</a> to the One-Eleven\'s control system.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> It also redesigned the wing\'s <a href="/wiki/Airfoil" title="Airfoil">leading edge</a> to smooth airflow into the engines and over the tailplane. The specially modified aircraft used for testing this problem is now preserved at <a href="/wiki/Brooklands_Museum" title="Brooklands Museum">Brooklands Museum</a>.\n</p><p>Despite the crash, testing continued and customer confidence remained high. American Airlines and Braniff took up their optional orders and placed more in February 1964. Further orders came from Mohawk, <a href="/wiki/Philippine_Airlines" title="Philippine Airlines">Philippine Airlines</a> and German businessman <a href="/wiki/Helmut_Horten" title="Helmut Horten">Helmut Horten</a>, who ordered the first executive modification of the aircraft. By the end of 1964, 13 aircraft had rolled off the production line. The One-Eleven was <a href="/wiki/Type_certificate" title="Type certificate">certified</a> and the first handover, of <i>G-ASJI</i> to BUA, was on 22 January 1965. After several weeks of route-proving flights, the first revenue service flew on 9 April from <a href="/wiki/Gatwick" class="mw-redirect" title="Gatwick">Gatwick</a> to <a href="/wiki/Genoa" title="Genoa">Genoa</a>. Braniff took delivery of its first aircraft on 11 March, while Mohawk received its first on 15 May. Deliveries continued, and by the end of 1965 airlines had received 34 aircraft. Demand remained buoyant, with a second production line set up at <a href="/wiki/Weybridge" title="Weybridge">Weybridge</a> - producing 13 1-11s between 1966 and 1970.\n</p>\n<h3><span by 13.5&#160;ft (4.1&#160;m)</div></div></div>\n<p>In 1967 a larger 119-seat version was introduced as the One-Eleven 500 (also known as Super One-Eleven). This "stretched" version was delayed for at least a year while its launch customer BEA assessed its requirements. This gave competing US aircraft (the <a href="/wiki/Douglas_DC-9" class="mw-redirect" title="Douglas DC-9">Douglas DC-9</a> and <a href="/wiki/Boeing_737" title="Boeing 737">Boeing 737</a>) the opportunity to compensate for the One-Eleven\'s early penetration of the US domestic market. The British aircraft\'s initial one-year advantage now turned into a one-year delay, and the stretched series 500 failed to sell in the US. The type saw service with <a href="/wiki/Cayman_Airways" title="Cayman Airways">Cayman Airways</a> and <a href="/wiki/Leeward_Islands_Air_Transport" class="mw-redirect" title="Leeward Islands Air Transport">Leeward Islands Air Transport</a> (LIAT) in the Caribbean with Cayman Airways operating the series 500 on scheduled services to Houston, Texas (IAH) and Miami, Florida (MIA), and LIAT flying its series 500s into San Juan, Puerto Rico (SJU). <a href="/wiki/Bahamasair" title="Bahamasair">Bahamasair</a> also operated the stretched 500 model with service between Nassau (NAS) and Miami among other routes while Guatemalan carrier <a href="/wiki/Aviateca" class="mw-redirect" title="Aviateca">Aviateca</a> operated its series 500 aircraft into both Miami and New Orleans, Louisiana (MSY).<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Costa Rican airline <a href="/wiki/LACSA" class="mw-redirect" title="LACSA">LACSA</a> operated the series 500 as well on its services to Miami.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Compared with earlier versions, the One-Eleven 500 was longer by 8&#160;ft 4in (2.54 m) ahead of the wing and 5&#160;ft 2in (1.57 m) behind it. The wing span was increased by 5&#160;ft (1.5 m), and the latest Mk. 512 version of the Spey was used. The new version sold reasonably well across the world, particularly to European charter airlines. In 1971 it received an incremental upgrade to reduce <a href="/wiki/Drag_(physics)" title="Drag (physics)">drag</a> and reduce <a href="/wiki/Runway" title="Runway">runway</a> requirements.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:BAC_111-510ED_One-Eleven,_AB_Airlines_(European_Aircharter_-_EAL)_AN0528338.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/BAC_111-510ED_One-Eleven%2C_AB_Airlines_%28European_Aircharter_-_EAL%29_AN0528338.jpg/220px-BAC_111-510ED_One-Eleven%2C_AB_Airlines_%28European_Aircharter_-_EAL%29_AN0528338.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="169" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/BAC_111-510ED_One-Eleven%2C_AB_Airlines_%28European_Aircharter_-_EAL%29_AN0528338.jpg/330px-BAC_111-510ED_One-Eleven%2C_AB_Airlines_%28European_Aircharter_-_EAL%29_AN0528338.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/BAC_111-510ED_One-Eleven%2C_AB_Airlines_%28European_Aircharter_-_EAL%29_AN0528338.jpg/440px-BAC_111-510ED_One-Eleven%2C_AB_Airlines_%28European_Aircharter_-_EAL%29_AN0528338.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="788" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:BAC_111-510ED_One-Eleven,_AB_Airlines_(European_Aircharter_-_EAL)_AN0528338.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The 510ED cockpit, similar to the <a href="/wiki/Hawker_Siddeley_Trident" title="Hawker Siddeley Trident">Hawker Siddeley Trident</a></div></div></div>\n<p>BEA/British Airways 500 series aircraft (denoted One-Eleven 510ED) varied significantly from other One-Elevens, at BEA\'s request. The One-Eleven 510ED had a modified <a href="/wiki/Cockpit" title="Cockpit">cockpit</a> which incorporated instrumentation and avionics from or similar to that of the <a href="/wiki/Hawker_Siddeley_Trident" title="Hawker Siddeley Trident">Hawker Siddeley Trident</a>, for better commonality with the type. Their additional equipment included a more sophisticated <a href="/wiki/Autopilot" title="Autopilot">Autopilot</a>, which allowed <a href="/wiki/Autoland" title="Autoland">autoland</a> in <a href="/wiki/Instrument_Landing_System#CAT_II" class="mw-redirect" title="Instrument Landing System">CAT II</a> and included an autothrottle. The modifications went as far as reversing the "on" position of most switches to match that of the Trident; indeed, the 510ED was so different from other One-Elevens and 500 series aircraft that a different <a href="/wiki/Pilot_licensing_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Pilot licensing in the United Kingdom">type rating</a> was required to fly it.\n</p><p>Having faced competition from US aircraft by 1966, by 1970 the One-Eleven also faced competition from newer, smaller aircraft such as the <a href="/wiki/Fokker_F28_Fellowship" title="Fokker F28 Fellowship">Fokker F28 Fellowship</a>. The F28 was lighter, less complex, and cheaper. The One-Eleven 475 of 1970 was launched to compete with the F28. It combined the 400 fuselage with the higher power and larger wing of the 500 and was intended for <a href="/wiki/Hot_and_high" title="Hot and high">hot and high</a> operations, however only 10 One-Eleven Mk 475 were sold. In 1977, the One-Eleven 670, a quiet and updated 475, was offered to the Japanese domestic market, also failing to sell.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Proposed_developments">Proposed developments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=BAC_One-Eleven&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Proposed developments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<table class="box-Unreferenced_section plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Unreferenced" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><a href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" decoding="async" width="50" height="39" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="512" data-file-height="399" /></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>does not <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">cite</a> any <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">sources</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=BAC_One-Eleven&amp;action=edit">improve this section</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Referencing_for_beginners" title="Help:Referencing for beginners">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. At this point orders slowed to a trickle. British production continued until 1982. There were two reasons why the production line was kept open for just 35 aircraft delivered over 11 years: first, BAC hoped that Rolls-Royce would develop a quieter and more powerful version of the Spey engine, making possible further One-Eleven developments; second, throughout the early part of the period <a href="/wiki/Romania" title="Romania">Romania</a> was negotiating to buy the entire One-Eleven programme and transfer production of the type to <a href="/wiki/Bucharest" title="Bucharest">Bucharest</a>.\n</p><p>By 1974, BAC invested significant effort into launching the One-Eleven 700. This had a longer <a href="/wiki/Fuselage" title="Fuselage">fuselage</a> with a 134-seat interior and the projected 16,900&#160;lbf (75&#160;kN) Spey 606 engine producing greater power and less noise.<sup id="cite_ref-aero212p37_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aero212p37-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-jawa75p195_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jawa75p195-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> It was approximately the same size as the latest DC-9s and 737s and would have been available in time to prevent large-scale defections by One-Eleven clients to McDonnell-Douglas and Boeing. Rolls-Royce was still recovering from bankruptcy, however, and the uprated Spey failed to materialise. An altogether less ambitious 700 made a reappearance in 1978 as a 500 with specially "hush-kitted" Speys which would be replaced by the proposed RB432 in the mid-1980s. This was offered to <a href="/wiki/British_Airways" title="British Airways">British Airways</a> in competition with Boeing 737-200s, but was ultimately rejected.\n</p><p>In 1975, BAC launched the One-Eleven 800, a further stretched aircraft to be powered by two 22,000&#160;lbf (98&#160;kN) <a href="/wiki/CFM_International_CFM56" title="CFM International CFM56">CFM International CFM56</a> high-bypass turbofans and seating 144\xe2\x80\x93161 passengers,<sup id="cite_ref-aero212p38_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aero212p38-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-jawa75p195_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jawa75p195-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> but this project was abandoned in 1976 in favour of a new derivative of the One-Eleven with a wider fuselage capable of six-abreast seating. This new airliner was unveiled later that year as the X-Eleven, which would be powered by two CFM-56s or Pratt &amp; Whitney JT10D engines and seat up 166 passengers.<sup id="cite_ref-aero212p389_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aero212p389-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> In 1977, BAC merged with <a href="/wiki/Hawker_Siddeley" title="Hawker Siddeley">Hawker Siddeley</a> to form <a href="/wiki/British_Aerospace" title="British Aerospace">British Aerospace</a> (BAe) and the new company was faced with the choice of developing the X-Eleven or joining European efforts to design an-all new aircraft. In the end, BAe became a full member of Airbus, and the X-Eleven was abandoned, with the European alternative becoming the <a href="/wiki/Airbus_A320" class="mw-redirect" title="Airbus A320">Airbus A320</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-aero212p401_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aero212p401-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/BAC_Two-Eleven_and_Three-Eleven" class="mw-redirect" title="BAC Two-Eleven and Three-Eleven">BAC Two-Eleven and Three-Eleven</a> were British airliner studies proposed by the British Aircraft Corporation in the late 1960s which never made it to production.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rombac_production">Rombac production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:ROMBAC_1-11_(YR-BRA).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The first Rombac 1-11 was delivered to <a href="/wiki/TAROM" title="TAROM">TAROM</a> on 29 December 1982</div></div></div>\n<p>On 9 June 1979, Romanian president <a href="/wiki/Nicolae_Ceau%C5%9Fescu" class="mw-redirect" title="Nicolae Ceau\xc5\x9fescu">Nicolae Ceau\xc5\x9fescu</a> signed a contract for One-Eleven licence production in Romania. This was to involve the delivery of three complete One-Elevens (two 500-series aircraft and one 475 series) plus the construction of at least 22 in Bucharest, with reducing British content. It also involved Romanian production of Spey engines and certification of the aircraft to British standards by the <a href="/wiki/Civil_Aviation_Authority_(United_Kingdom)" title="Civil Aviation Authority (United Kingdom)">Civil Aviation Authority</a>. A market for up to 80 Romanian-built aircraft was projected at the time, largely in China and other developing economies, and possibly <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Europe" title="Eastern Europe">Eastern Europe</a>. The aircraft was redesignated <b>Rombac 1-11</b>.<sup id="cite_ref-database_p80-1_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-database_p80-1-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-JAWA82_p173_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JAWA82_p173-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> The Spey 512-14 DW engines were produced under license by Turbomecanica Bucharest.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The first Rombac One-Eleven, (YR-BRA cn 401) a series 561RC, was rolled out at <a href="/wiki/Romaero" title="Romaero">Romaero</a> B\xc4\x83neasa factory on 27 August 1982 and first flew on 18 September 1982. Production continued until 1989 at a much slower pace than foreseen in the contract. Nine aircraft were delivered, with the 10th and 11th aircraft on the production line being abandoned when they were 85% and 70% complete.<sup id="cite_ref-database_p80-1_28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-database_p80-1-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-pilling_p213-4_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pilling_p213-4-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> The first aircraft was delivered to <a href="/wiki/TAROM" title="TAROM">TAROM</a> on 29 December 1982.<sup id="cite_ref-pilling_p213-4_31-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pilling_p213-4-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> The Romanian carrier took delivery of all but two of the aircraft produced, the remaining two going to <a href="/wiki/Romavia" title="Romavia">Romavia</a>, the last of which (YR-BRI cn 409) was delivered on 1 January 1993.\n</p><p>There were three reasons why the Rombac initiative failed. Romania\'s economy and international position deteriorated to the point where supplies needed for One-Eleven manufacturing slowed to a trickle, with <a href="/wiki/Hard_currency" title="Hard currency">hard currency</a> restrictions delaying the delivery of components sourced outside Romania;<sup id="cite_ref-database_p80-1_28-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-database_p80-1-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-pilling_p213-4_31-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pilling_p213-4-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> the market foreseen by the Romanians failed to show an interest, though some Rombac machines were leased to European operators. The One-Eleven\'s noise level and fuel economy failed to keep pace with US and West European competition.\n</p><p>Adopting a new engine would have resolved noise and fuel economy issues. Following the <a href="/wiki/Romanian_Revolution" title="Romanian Revolution">fall of the Ceau\xc5\x9fescu regime</a>, plans were made to restart production using the <a href="/wiki/Rolls-Royce_Tay_(turbofan)" class="mw-redirect" title="Rolls-Royce Tay (turbofan)">Rolls-Royce Tay</a>. British <a href="/wiki/Aircraft_lease" title="Aircraft lease">aircraft leasing</a> company Associated Aerospace agreed a $1 billion deal to purchase 50 Tay-powered One-Elevens fitted with a new electronic <a href="/wiki/Glass_cockpit" title="Glass cockpit">glass cockpit</a> for onward leasing to Western customers. The <a href="/wiki/Liquidation" title="Liquidation">liquidation</a> of Associated Aerospace in April 1991 stopped this deal. Despite this setback, Rombac continued to try to sell the One-Eleven, with US operator <a href="/wiki/Kiwi_International_Air_Lines" title="Kiwi International Air Lines">Kiwi International Air Lines</a> placing a firm order for 11 Tay-engined aircraft with options for an additional five, but these plans never happened.<sup id="cite_ref-database_p80-1_28-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-database_p80-1-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-pilling_p213-4_31-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pilling_p213-4-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Operational_history">Operational history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="767" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Line-up_of_American_Airlines_BAC_111-401AK_One-Eleven_at_LaGuardia_Airport.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Several <a href="/wiki/American_Airlines" title="American Airlines">American Airlines</a> BAC 1\xe2\x80\x9311 at <a href="/wiki/LaGuardia_Airport" title="LaGuardia Airport">LaGuardia Airport</a></div></div></div>\n<p>Once in service, the One Eleven found itself in competition with the <a href="/wiki/Douglas_DC-9" class="mw-redirect" title="Douglas DC-9">Douglas DC-9</a>, and was joined by another competitor, the <a href="/wiki/Boeing_737" title="Boeing 737">Boeing 737</a>, only a year after its introduction.<sup id="cite_ref-Gunn1999_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gunn1999-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> Advantages over the DC-9 included a lower unit cost. The DC-9 offered more seating, and its engines were interchangeable with those on the Boeing 727. These factors led to <a href="/wiki/Trans_Australia_Airlines" title="Trans Australia Airlines">Trans Australia Airlines</a> choosing to purchase the DC-9 instead.<sup id="cite_ref-Gunn1999_35-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gunn1999-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> In the US, the <a href="/wiki/Civil_Aeronautics_Board" title="Civil Aeronautics Board">Civil Aeronautics Board</a> was sceptical of smaller operators\' need for jet aircraft and withheld financing, leading to several US customers cancelling their One-Eleven orders.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Mohawk_Airlines" title="Mohawk Airlines">Mohawk Airlines</a> became both the first American operator of the type and the first airline in the US to operate jet aircraft on short haul routes.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> On 25 June 1965, Mohawk introduced its first One-Eleven into passenger service; by the end of the decade, the airline operated a fleet of 20 BAC One-Elevens.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> Buying the jets pushed Mohawk into debt and this, along with an economic downturn and <a href="/wiki/Strike_action" title="Strike action">strike action</a>, led to its forced merger with <a href="/wiki/Allegheny_Airlines" title="Allegheny Airlines">Allegheny Airlines</a> which in turn continued to operate the One-Eleven.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> In July 1963, <a href="/wiki/American_Airlines" title="American Airlines">American Airlines</a> had placed an order for 15 400-series One-Elevens for \xc2\xa314 million; this was the first time American Airlines had purchased a foreign aircraft type for its fleet.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Braniff_International_Airways" title="Braniff International Airways">Braniff International Airways</a>, another major US air carrier, also ordered the One-Eleven.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Aloha_Airlines" title="Aloha Airlines">Aloha Airlines</a> selected the One-Eleven as its first jet type for interisland service in the <a href="/wiki/Hawaiian_Islands" title="Hawaiian Islands">Hawaiian Islands</a>.\n</p><p>Several British operators, including <a href="/wiki/Dan_Air" class="mw-redirect" title="Dan Air">Dan Air</a> and <a href="/wiki/British_Caledonian" title="British Caledonian">British Caledonian</a>, made extensive use of the type. Dan Air increased the number of One-Elevens it had in service in the 1970s, eventually replacing its aging <a href="/wiki/De_Havilland_Comet" title="De Havilland Comet">de Havilland Comets</a> with the One-Eleven entirely in the 1980s. The type became the airline\'s main revenue generator.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> Dan Air would often lease One-Elevens, including Rombac-produced aircraft, to meet short-term demands. During periods of low demand Dan Air would lease its own One-Elevens to other operators. British Caledonian and Dan Air One-Elevens would often be exchanged between the two airlines on temporary and permanent arrangements. Passenger demand grew on several key One-Eleven routes, exceeding the Series 500\'s larger capacity during the 1980s, which began to force airlines to use other planes.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Prior to the formation of <a href="/wiki/British_Airways" title="British Airways">British Airways</a> (BA), some predecessor companies, British European Airways and Cambrian Airways, were operating the One-Eleven. Their fleets were inherited by BA. British Airways Regional Division found the performance of the One-Eleven more than adequate and sought to expand operations with the type, including further acquisitions, in the early 1970s in part to replace <a href="/wiki/Vickers_Viscount" title="Vickers Viscount">Vickers Viscounts</a>. BA\'s 400-Series One-Elevens were all named after areas and locations in the <a href="/wiki/The_Midlands,_England" class="mw-redirect" title="The Midlands, England">English Midlands</a>, reflecting the type\'s new base at <a href="/wiki/Birmingham_Airport" title="Birmingham Airport">Birmingham Airport</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> BA ordered more Series-500 aircraft in the late 1970s and the airline received some of the last British-made One-Elevens.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>British Caledonian had begun replacing its One-Elevens with the new <a href="/wiki/Airbus_A320" class="mw-redirect" title="Airbus A320">Airbus A320</a>, when it was merged with British Airways in 1987 due to financial problems.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> In both Caledonian and British Airways service, the One-Elevens were a dominant type for the operator\'s European routes. BA proceeded to rationalise its fleets, retiring many overlapping types, the One-Eleven being gradually phased out for the more modern A320 and <a href="/wiki/Boeing_737" title="Boeing 737">Boeing 737</a> jetliners. The last aircraft was removed from regular service with BA in October 1992.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> A number of ex-BA aircraft saw service with <a href="/wiki/Maersk_Air" title="Maersk Air">Maersk Air</a> before, in turn, being replaced by the <a href="/wiki/Bombardier_CRJ200" class="mw-redirect" title="Bombardier CRJ200">Bombardier CRJ200</a> in the late 1990s.<sup id="cite_ref-Hill_1999,_p._152_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hill_1999,_p._152-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ryanair_Bucuresti_Rombac_1-11-561RC_One-Eleven_Haafke-1.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/Ryanair_Bucuresti_Rombac_1-11-561RC_One-Eleven_Haafke-1.jpg/220px-Ryanair_Bucuresti_Rombac_1-11-561RC_One-Eleven_Haafke-1.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/Ryanair_Bucuresti_Rombac_1-11-561RC_One-Eleven_Haafke-1.jpg/330px-Ryanair_Bucuresti_Rombac_1-11-561RC_One-Eleven_Haafke-1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/Ryanair_Bucuresti_Rombac_1-11-561RC_One-Eleven_Haafke-1.jpg/440px-Ryanair_Bucuresti_Rombac_1-11-561RC_One-Eleven_Haafke-1.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="683" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ryanair_Bucuresti_Rombac_1-11-561RC_One-Eleven_Haafke-1.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A <a href="/wiki/Ryanair" title="Ryanair">Ryanair</a> One-Eleven</div></div></div>\n<p>In Ireland <a href="/wiki/Aer_Lingus" title="Aer Lingus">Aer Lingus</a> flew One-Elevens for three decades, in both the scheduled and charter markets. The aircraft was used on less-busy European routes into the 1990s.<sup id="cite_ref-Hill_1999,_p._152_48-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hill_1999,_p._152-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> The One-Eleven was important in <a href="/wiki/Budget_airline" class="mw-redirect" title="Budget airline">budget airline</a> <a href="/wiki/Ryanair" title="Ryanair">Ryanair</a>\'s early years: it had sought an aircraft for low-fare scheduled services from regional airports, and obtained One-Elevens. The first of these, a Rombac-produced 500-Series, entered service on the Dublin\xe2\x80\x93Luton route on 1 December 1986. Ryanair\'s fleet expanded to six aircraft by 1988, with three leased from Romania.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> These leased aircraft were later replaced with former BA One-Elevens. Ultimately Ryanair replaced the One-Eleven with the Boeing 737 in the mid-1990s.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In April 1966, <a href="/wiki/Philippine_Airlines" title="Philippine Airlines">Philippine Airlines</a> began operating One Elevens in revenue service. They eventually operated 12 of the later 500-Series. One of these aircraft suffered two separate in-flight bomb explosions, but was repaired each time and continued in service until 1992. In another high-profile incident on 21 May 1982, John Clearno tried to hijack a Philippine One-Eleven while on the ground, but was eventually overpowered by the cockpit crew following hours of negotiation. No passengers or crew were injured.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> Philippine Airlines replaced the type with the Boeing 737 from 1989 onwards.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Romania was a major customer for British-produced One-Elevens, with several large orders in the 1970s. These aircraft were often fitted with equipment such as engine \'<a href="/wiki/Hush_kit" title="Hush kit">hush kits</a>\'. In May 1977, a major cooperation agreement between British Aerospace and the Romanian government was signed, and this led to a gradual One-Eleven <a href="/wiki/Technology_transfer" title="Technology transfer">technology transfer</a> to Romania. Full contracts for license production under the Rombac name followed two years later. Complete airframes and components were provided to assist in the venture.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup> All Rombac One-Elevens manufactured by Romaero were delivered to <a href="/wiki/TAROM" title="TAROM">TAROM</a> and <a href="/wiki/Romavia" title="Romavia">Romavia</a>, which leased them to airlines across both Eastern and Western Europe. Rombac-produced One-Elevens would be adopted by many emerging operators globally, including <a href="/wiki/Lauda_Air" title="Lauda Air">Lauda Air</a> in Austria, and <a href="/wiki/Aero_Asia_International" title="Aero Asia International">Aero Asia International</a> in Pakistan.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>One-Elevens remained in widespread use with European operators into the 1990s. Once retired from major operators, they were often sold to smaller airlines, often in the Far East and Africa. <a href="/wiki/Nigeria" title="Nigeria">Nigeria</a> was a major operator until the type was grounded after a crash in 2002. A major factor for the withdrawal from European service of remaining One-Elevens was the <a href="/wiki/Joint_Aviation_Authorities" title="Joint Aviation Authorities">Stage III noise abatement regulations</a>, which came into effect from March 2003. Bringing the Rolls-Royce Spey engines into compliance with the noise regulations with <a href="/wiki/Hush_kit" title="Hush kit">hush kits</a> was expensive, and many European operators chose to dispose of the type from their fleets. In 2010, the <a href="/wiki/European_Aviation_Safety_Agency" title="European Aviation Safety Agency">European Aviation Safety Agency</a> accepted an <a href="/wiki/Airbus" title="Airbus">Airbus</a> request to revoke the Type Certificate for the BAC One-Eleven. As a result, BAC One-Eleven aircraft registered in any EU Member State are no longer eligible for a normal certificate of airworthiness. In December 2012, the last operational One-Eleven in the UK, which had continued to fly as a military aircraft, was retired.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> Eventually, on 7 May 2019, the last flyable One-Eleven was retired by Northrop Grumman after serving as a F-35 testbed.\n</p>\n<div 250</a> disintegrated in mid-air after flying into a severe thunderstorm near <a href="/wiki/Falls_City,_Nebraska" title="Falls City, Nebraska">Falls City, Nebraska</a>. It was en route to <a href="/wiki/Omaha,_Nebraska" title="Omaha, Nebraska">Omaha, Nebraska</a>, from <a href="/wiki/Kansas_City,_Missouri" title="Kansas City, Missouri">Kansas City, Missouri</a>. Thirty-eight passengers and four crew members were killed in the crash. Flight 40 was a regularly scheduled passenger flight between <a href="/wiki/Elmira,_New_York" title="Elmira, New York">Elmira, New York</a> and <a href="/wiki/Washington,_DC" class="mw-redirect" title="Washington, DC">Washington, DC</a>. It crashed outside <a href="/wiki/Blossburg,_Pennsylvania" title="Blossburg, Pennsylvania">Blossburg, Pennsylvania</a> with the loss of all 34 passengers and crew.<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup> The fire was traced to the <a href="/wiki/Auxiliary_power_unit" title="Auxiliary power unit">auxiliary power unit</a> (APU); as a direct result of this accident in-flight use of the APU was banned.<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>On 12 September 1969, a <a href="/wiki/Philippine_Airlines" title="Philippine Airlines">Philippine Airlines</a> BAC One-Eleven registration PI-C1131 operating <a href="/wiki/Philippine_Airlines_Flight_158" title="Philippine Airlines Flight 158">flight 158</a> en route from <a href="/wiki/Mactan%E2%80%93Cebu_International_Airport" title="Mactan\xe2\x80\x93Cebu International Airport">Mactan\xe2\x80\x93Cebu International Airport</a> in <a href="/wiki/Cebu" title="Cebu">Cebu</a>, <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a> to <a href="/wiki/Manila_International_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Manila International Airport">Manila International Airport (now Ninoy Aquino Airport)</a> in <a href="/wiki/Manila" title="Manila">Manila</a>, Philippines struck a mango tree on the hill in suburban Kula-ike, <a href="/wiki/Antipolo" title="Antipolo">Antipolo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rizal" title="Rizal">Rizal</a>, 12 nautical miles (22&#160;km) east of its destination while on a VOR approach to runway 24. There were 47 people on board, of which 45 were killed. One passenger and one flight steward survived, both were hospitalized with burns. The crash remained the worst air disaster in the Philippines until <a href="/wiki/Philippine_Airlines_Flight_206" title="Philippine Airlines Flight 206">1987</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-asn_66-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-asn-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>On 6 September 1971, a One-Eleven 515FB operating as <a href="/wiki/Paninternational_Flight_112" title="Paninternational Flight 112">Paninternational Flight 112</a> collided with a bridge during an emergency landing on the <a href="/wiki/Bundesautobahn_7" title="Bundesautobahn 7">A7 Autobahn</a> in Hamburg, Germany, shearing off both wings after a double engine failure during takeoff. The <a href="/wiki/Water_injection_(engine)" title="Water injection (engine)">water injection</a> system had inadvertently been filled with jet fuel instead of water. Twenty-two of the 121 people aboard died.<sup id="cite_ref-ASN_67-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ASN-67">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>On 18 April 1974, <a href="/wiki/Court_Line_Flight_95" title="Court Line Flight 95">Court Line Flight 95</a>, operated by One-Eleven 528 G-AXMJ was involved in a ground collision with <a href="/wiki/Piper_Aztec" class="mw-redirect" title="Piper Aztec">Piper PA-23 Aztec</a> G-AYDE at <a href="/wiki/London_Luton_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="London Luton Airport">London Luton Airport</a> due to the Aztec entering the active runway without clearance. The pilot of the Aztec was killed and his passenger was injured. All 91 on board the One-Eleven safely left the aircraft after take-off was aborted.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources.
