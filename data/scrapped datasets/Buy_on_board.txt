class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Buy_on_board&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>As the airline market in the United States became <a href="/wiki/Airline_deregulation_act" class="mw-redirect" title="Airline deregulation act">deregulated</a>, airlines began to compete by price. Airline ticket prices began to decrease, and airlines began to charge extra for services that were once included in the airfare.<sup id="cite_ref-Brancatelli1_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brancatelli1-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Starting in 2003, many <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> <a href="/wiki/Air_carriers" class="mw-redirect" title="Air carriers">air carriers</a> began eliminating free meal services in <a href="/wiki/Economy_class" title="Economy class">economy classes</a> on North American flights and replacing them with buy on board services.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> In the 2000s <a href="/wiki/US_Airways" title="US Airways">US Airways</a> (now part of <a href="/wiki/American_Airlines" title="American Airlines">American Airlines</a>) briefly charged for soft drinks, but then reversed course. By 2009, many US carriers had established buy on board as part of an <a href="/wiki/%C3%80_la_carte" title="\xc3\x80 la carte">\xc3\xa0 la carte</a> pricing movement.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Around that year, US carriers began using celebrity-named and brand name products to make their buy on board products generate more revenue.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Continental_Airlines" title="Continental Airlines">Continental Airlines</a>, the last large United States carrier to offer free meals on all domestic flights,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> announced in March 2010 that it would begin a buy on board program in fall 2010 and end many of its free meal programs on domestic flights.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Jeff Green of <i><a href="/wiki/Businessweek" class="mw-redirect" title="Businessweek">Businessweek</a></i> described the end of Continental\'s program as an "end of an era."<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, passengers increasingly began to bring their own foods on board to avoid paying for buy on board.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> \n</p><p>As of 2016, <a href="/wiki/Hawaiian_Airlines" title="Hawaiian Airlines">Hawaiian Airlines</a> remains the last U.S. legacy airline to offer free meals on board, although all of its flights are to/from <a href="/wiki/Hawaii" title="Hawaii">Hawaii</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Southwest_Airlines" title="Southwest Airlines">Southwest Airlines</a> is the only mainland U.S. airline without a buy-on-board program as of 2016. \n</p><p>Today, all three major U.S. airlines now offer free snacks in economy on board their flights, in addition to their buy-on-board menus.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Europe">Europe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Buy_on_board&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Europe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In Europe, the general increase in the number of tourists that fly, and deregulation which enabled low price carriers, has caused stiffer price competition. Low cost carriers, such as <a href="/wiki/Ryanair" title="Ryanair">Ryanair</a>, which charges for all food or drink, have forced traditional airlines to lower their costs. As of 2017 only 6 out of the 21 most popular airlines in Europe offered complimentary inflight food and drink. KLM, Lufthansa, Swiss, Austrian, Air France, Alitalia and TAP Portugal all continued to offer free snacks and beverages on their short-haul flights.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
