class="mw-headline" id="Interpreting_the_symbol">Interpreting the symbol</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ticker_symbol&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Interpreting the symbol">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Stock symbols are unique identifiers assigned to each <a href="/wiki/Security_(finance)" title="Security (finance)">security</a> traded on a particular market. A stock symbol can consist of letters, numbers, or a combination of both, and is a way to uniquely identify that stock.  The symbols were kept as short as possible to reduce the number of characters that had to be printed on the <a href="/wiki/Ticker_tape" title="Ticker tape">ticker tape</a>, and to make it easy to recognize by traders and investors.\n</p><p>The allocation of symbols and formatting convention is specific to each stock exchange.  In the US, for example, stock tickers are typically between 1 and 4 letters and represent the company name where possible.  For example, US-based computer company stock <a href="/wiki/Apple_Inc." title="Apple Inc.">Apple Inc.</a> traded on the <a href="/wiki/NASDAQ" class="mw-redirect" title="NASDAQ">NASDAQ</a> exchange has the symbol AAPL, while the motor company Ford\'s stock that is traded on the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Stock_Exchange" title="New York Stock Exchange">New York Stock Exchange</a> has the single-letter ticker F.  In Europe, most exchanges use three-letter codes; for example, Dutch consumer goods company <a href="/wiki/Unilever" title="Unilever">Unilever</a> traded on the <a href="/wiki/Euronext" title="Euronext">Amsterdam Euronext</a> exchange has the symbol UNA.  In Asia, numbers are often used as stock tickers to avoid issues for international investors when using non-<a href="/wiki/Latin_script" title="Latin script">Latin scripts</a>.  For example, the bank <a href="/wiki/HSBC" title="HSBC">HSBC</a>\'s stock traded on the <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong_Stock_Exchange" title="Hong Kong Stock Exchange">Hong Kong Stock Exchange</a> has the ticker symbol 0005.\n</p><p>Symbols sometimes change to reflect mergers. Prior to the 1999 merger with <a href="/wiki/Mobil_Oil" class="mw-redirect" title="Mobil Oil">Mobil Oil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Exxon" title="Exxon">Exxon</a> used a phonetic spelling of the company "XON" as its ticker symbol.  The symbol of the firm after the merger was "XOM".  Symbols are sometimes reused. In the US the single-letter symbols are particularly sought after as vanity symbols.  For example, since March of 2008 <a href="/wiki/Visa_Inc." title="Visa Inc.">Visa Inc.</a> has used the symbol V that had previously been used by <a href="/wiki/Vivendi" title="Vivendi">Vivendi</a> which had delisted and given up the symbol.<sup id="cite_ref-Mantell_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mantell-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>To fully qualify a stock, both the ticker and the exchange or country of listing needs to be known. On many systems both must be specified to uniquely identify the security.  This is often done by appending the location or exchange code to the ticker.\n</p>\n<table class="wikitable">\n<caption>Example <a href="/wiki/Vodafone" title="Vodafone">Vodafone Group plc</a> stock ticker symbol\n</caption>\n<tbody><tr>\n<th>Location\n</th>\n<th><a href="/wiki/Reuters_Instrument_Code" title="Reuters Instrument Code">Reuters Instrument Code</a>\n</th>\n<th><a href="/wiki/Bloomberg_L.P." title="Bloomberg L.P.">Bloomberg</a> ticker\n</th></tr>\n<tr>\n<td>London Stock Exchange\n</td>\n<td>VOD.L\n</td>\n<td>VOD LN\n</td></tr>\n<tr>\n<td>Nasdaq\n</td>\n<td>VOD.O\n</td>\n<td>VOD UQ\n</td></tr>\n<tr>\n<td>Stock Exchange of Singapore\n</td>\n<td>VOD.SI\n</td>\n<td>VOD SP\n</td></tr>\n</tbody></table>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_identifiers">Other identifiers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ticker_symbol&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Other identifiers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Although stock tickers identify a security, they are exchange dependent, generally limited to stocks and can change.  These limitations have led to the development of other codes in financial markets to identify securities for <a href="/wiki/Settlement_(finance)" title="Settlement (finance)">settlement</a> purposes.  The most prevalent of these is the <a href="/wiki/International_Securities_Identifying_Number" class="mw-redirect" title="International Securities Identifying Number">International Securities Identifying Number</a> (ISIN).<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>  An ISIN uniquely identifies a <a href="/wiki/Security_(finance)" title="Security (finance)">security</a> and its structure is defined in <a href="/wiki/ISO_6166" class="mw-redirect" title="ISO 6166">ISO 6166</a>.  Securities for which ISINs are issued include <a href="/wiki/Bond_(finance)" title="Bond (finance)">bonds</a>, <a href="/wiki/Commercial_paper" title="Commercial paper">commercial paper</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stock" title="Stock">stocks</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Warrant_(finance)" title="Warrant (finance)">warrants</a>.  The ISIN code is a 12-character alpha-numerical code that does not contain information characterizing financial instruments, but serves for uniform identification of a security at trading and settlement.\n</p><p>The ISIN identifies the security, not the <a href="/wiki/Stock_exchange" title="Stock exchange">exchange</a> (if any) on which it trades; it is therefore not a replacement for the ticker symbol. For instance, <a href="/wiki/Daimler_AG" title="Daimler AG">Daimler AG</a> stock trades on twenty-two different stock exchanges worldwide, and is priced in five different currencies; it has the same ISIN on each (DE0007100000), though not the same ticker symbol. ISIN cannot specify a particular trade in this case, and another identifier, typically the three- or four-letter exchange code (such as the <a href="/wiki/Market_Identifier_Code" title="Market Identifier Code">Market Identifier Code</a>) will have to be specified in addition to the ISIN.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Symbol_for_stock_market_indices">Symbol for stock market indices</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ticker_symbol&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Symbol for stock market indices">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>While usually a stock ticker identifies a security that can be traded, <a href="/wiki/Stock_market_index" title="Stock market index">stock market indices</a> are also sometimes assigned a symbol, even though they can generally not be traded. Symbols for indices are usually distinguished by adding a symbol in front of the name, such as a <a href="/wiki/Caret" title="Caret">caret</a> (^)<sup id="cite_ref-analyzerxl_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-analyzerxl-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> or a dot. For example, <a href="/wiki/Reuters" title="Reuters">Reuters</a> lists the <a href="/wiki/Nasdaq_Composite" title="Nasdaq Composite">Nasdaq Composite</a> index under the symbol <i>.IXIC</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-reuters_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-reuters-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Symbols_by_country">Symbols by country</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ticker_symbol&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Symbols by country">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Australia">Australia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ticker_symbol&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Australia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In Australia the <a href="/wiki/Australian_Securities_Exchange" title="Australian Securities Exchange">Australian Securities Exchange</a>  uses the following conventions:\n3 character base symbol with the first and third character being alphanumeric and the second alphabetic. class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ticker_symbol&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: United Kingdom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, prior to 1996, stock codes were known as EPICs, named after the <a href="/wiki/London_Stock_Exchange" title="London Stock Exchange">London Stock Exchange</a>\'s <a href="/w/index.php?title=Exchange_Price_Information_Computer&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Exchange Price Information Computer (page does not exist)">Exchange Price Information Computer</a> (e.g.: "MKS" for <a href="/wiki/Marks_and_Spencer" class="mw-redirect" title="Marks and Spencer">Marks and Spencer</a>).  Following the introduction of the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sequence_trading_platform&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Sequence trading platform (page does not exist)">Sequence trading platform</a> in 1996, EPICs were renamed <b>Tradable Instrument Display Mnemonics</b> (TIDM), but they are still widely referred to as EPICs.  Stocks can also be identified using their <a href="/wiki/SEDOL" title="SEDOL">SEDOL</a> (Stock Exchange Daily Official List) number or their ISIN (<a href="/wiki/International_Securities_Identification_Number" title="International Securities Identification Number">International Securities Identification Number</a>).\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ticker_symbol&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, modern letter-only ticker symbols were developed by <a href="/wiki/S%26P_Global_Ratings" title="S&amp;P Global Ratings">Standard &amp; Poor\'s</a> (S&amp;P) to bring a national standard to investing. Previously, a single company could have many different ticker symbols as they varied between the dozens of individual stock markets.  The term <i>ticker</i> refers to the noise made by the <a href="/wiki/Ticker_tape" title="Ticker tape">ticker tape</a> machines once widely used by stock exchanges.\n</p><p>The S&amp;P system was later standardized by the securities industry and modified as years passed. Stock symbols for <a href="/wiki/Preferred_stock" title="Preferred stock">preferred stock</a> have not been standardized.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Some companies use a well-known product as their ticker symbol.  Belgian brewer <a href="/wiki/AB_InBev" title="AB InBev">AB InBev</a>, the brewer of Budweiser beer, uses "BUD" as its three-letter ticker for American Depository Receipts, symbolizing its premier product in the United States.  Its rival, the <a href="/wiki/Molson_Coors_Brewing_Company" class="mw-redirect" title="Molson Coors Brewing Company">Molson Coors Brewing Company</a>, uses a similarly beer-related symbol, "TAP". Likewise, <a href="/wiki/Southwest_Airlines" title="Southwest Airlines">Southwest Airlines</a> pays tribute to its headquarters at <a href="/wiki/Dallas_Love_Field" title="Dallas Love Field">Love Field</a> in <a href="/wiki/Dallas" title="Dallas">Dallas</a> through its "LUV" symbol. <a href="/wiki/Cedar_Fair_Entertainment_Company" class="mw-redirect" title="Cedar Fair Entertainment Company">Cedar Fair Entertainment Company</a>, which operates large amusement parks in the United States, uses "FUN" as its symbol. <a href="/wiki/Harley-Davidson" title="Harley-Davidson">Harley-Davidson</a> uses "HOG", an abbreviation for the corporate-sponsored <a href="/wiki/Harley_Owners_Group" title="Harley Owners Group">Harley Owners Group</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>  <a href="/wiki/Yamana_Gold" title="Yamana Gold">Yamana Gold</a> uses "AUY", because on the <a href="/wiki/Periodic_table_of_elements" class="mw-redirect" title="Periodic table of elements">periodic table of elements</a>, "Au" is the symbol for <a href="/wiki/Gold" title="Gold">gold</a>.  Sotheby\'s (the famous auction house) uses the symbol "BID".  <a href="/wiki/Petco" title="Petco">Petco</a> uses the symbol "WOOF," a reference to the sound made by dogs.\n</p><p>While most symbols come from the company\'s name, sometimes it happens the other way around. <a href="/wiki/Yum!_Brands#Tricon_Global" title="Yum! Brands">Tricon Global</a>, owner of <a href="/wiki/KFC" title="KFC">KFC</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pizza_Hut" title="Pizza Hut">Pizza Hut</a> and <a href="/wiki/Taco_Bell" title="Taco Bell">Taco Bell</a>, adopted the symbol "YUM" to represent its corporate mission when the company was spun out of <a href="/wiki/PepsiCo" title="PepsiCo">PepsiCo</a> in 1997. In 2002, the company changed its name to match its symbol, adopting the name <a href="/wiki/Yum!_Brands" title="Yum! Brands">Yum! Brands</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Symbols sometimes change to reflect mergers. Prior to the 1999 merger with Mobil Oil, Exxon used a phonetic spelling of the company "XON" as its ticker symbol.  The symbol of the firm after the merger was "XOM". After <a href="/wiki/Hewlett-Packard" title="Hewlett-Packard">Hewlett-Packard</a> merged with <a href="/wiki/Compaq" title="Compaq">Compaq</a>, the new firm took on the ticker symbol "HPQ".  (The former symbols were HWP and CPQ.) AT&amp;T\'s ticker symbol is simply "T"; accordingly, the company is referred to simply as "Telephone" on Wall Street (the T symbol is so well known that when the company was purchased by SBC, it took the AT&amp;T name, capitalizing on its history and keeping the desired single letter symbol).\n</p><p>Some examples of US Stock symbols include:\n</p>\n<ul><li>A \xe2\x80\x93 <a href="/wiki/Agilent_Technologies" title="Agilent Technologies">Agilent Technologies</a></li>\n<li>AAPL \xe2\x80\x93 <a href="/wiki/Apple_Inc." title="Apple Inc.">Apple</a></li>\n<li>BRK.(A/B) -
