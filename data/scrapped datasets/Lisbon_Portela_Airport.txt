href="/wiki/IATA_airport_code" title="IATA airport code">IATA</a>: <b>LIS</b>, <a href="/wiki/ICAO_airport_code" title="ICAO airport code">ICAO</a>: <b>LPPT</b>), also known simply as <b>Lisbon Airport</b> or <b>Portela Airport</b>, is an <a href="/wiki/International_airport" title="International airport">international airport</a> located 7&#160;km (4.3&#160;mi) northeast from the city centre of <a href="/wiki/Lisbon" title="Lisbon">Lisbon</a>, the capital of <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-therouteshop.com_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-therouteshop.com-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The airport is the main international gateway to Portugal. It is the 20th-largest airport in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> in terms of passenger volume, having served 31,173,000 passengers in 2019, an increase over the previous year of 7.4%. As of 2019 the airport handled 141 thousand tonnes of cargo.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> It is an important European hub to <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> the largest European <a href="/wiki/Star_Alliance" title="Star Alliance">Star Alliance</a> hub to South America<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> and also a European hub to <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The airport is the main hub of Portugal\'s <a href="/wiki/Flag_carrier" title="Flag carrier">flag carrier</a> <a href="/wiki/TAP_Air_Portugal" title="TAP Air Portugal">TAP Air Portugal</a> including its subsidiary <a href="/wiki/TAP_Express" title="TAP Express">TAP Express</a> in addition to being a hub for <a href="/wiki/Low-cost_carrier" title="Low-cost carrier">low-cost airlines</a> <a href="/wiki/Ryanair" title="Ryanair">Ryanair</a> and <a href="/wiki/EasyJet" title="EasyJet">easyJet</a>. It is a focus city for <a href="/wiki/Azores_Airlines" title="Azores Airlines">Azores Airlines</a>, <a href="/wiki/EuroAtlantic_Airways" title="EuroAtlantic Airways">euroAtlantic Airways</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hi_Fly_(airline)" title="Hi Fly (airline)">Hi Fly</a>, <a href="/wiki/Orbest" title="Orbest">Orbest</a>, and <a href="/wiki/White_Airways" title="White Airways">White Airways</a>. The airport is run by <a href="/wiki/ANA_Aeroportos_de_Portugal" title="ANA Aeroportos de Portugal">ANA Aeroportos de Portugal</a>, which was concessioned to the French group <a href="/wiki/Vinci_SA" title="Vinci SA">Vinci Airports</a> in February 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-vinci-airports.com_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vinci-airports.com-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In January 2019, <a href="/wiki/Politics_of_Portugal" title="Politics of Portugal">Portugal\'s government</a> unveiled a 1.1-billion-euro plan to expand Lisbon\'s current airport and build a second one in <a href="/wiki/Montijo,_Portugal" title="Montijo, Portugal">Montijo</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-njherald.com_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-njherald.com-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> It aimed to handle around 50 million passengers a year from 2022.<sup id="cite_ref-whig.com_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-whig.com-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-news.airwise.com_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-news.airwise.com-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> The project is suspended by environmental concerns.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc" role="navigation" opened on 15 October 1942, during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, and initially operated in conjunction with the <a href="/wiki/Cabo_Ruivo_Seaplane_Base" title="Cabo Ruivo Seaplane Base">Cabo Ruivo Seaplane Base</a>: seaplanes performed transatlantic flights, and passengers were transferred onto continental flights operating from the new airport.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> As a neutral airport it was open to both German and British airlines, and was a hub for smuggling people into, out of, and across Europe. It is widely referenced in the classic film <i><a href="/wiki/Casablanca_(film)" title="Casablanca (film)">Casablanca</a></i>, whose plot revolves around an escape attempt to Lisbon airport. As such, it was heavily monitored by both <a href="/wiki/Axis_powers" title="Axis powers">Axis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Allies_of_World_War_II" title="Allies of World War II">Allied</a> spies. Although Portugal was neutral, the airport was used by allied flights en route to <a href="/wiki/Gibraltar" title="Gibraltar">Gibraltar</a>, <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">North Africa</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cairo" title="Cairo">Cairo</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-aw_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aw-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>At the end of the war the airport developed rapidly, and by 1946 was used by major airlines such as <a href="/wiki/Air_France" title="Air France">Air France</a>, <a href="/wiki/British_European_Airways" title="British European Airways">British European Airways</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iberia_(airline)" title="Iberia (airline)">Iberia</a>, <a href="/wiki/KLM" title="KLM">KLM</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sabena" title="Sabena">Sabena</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pan_American_World_Airways" class="mw-redirect" title="Pan American World Airways">Pan Am</a> and <a href="/wiki/Trans_World_Airlines" title="Trans World Airlines">Trans World Airlines</a><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>. By 1954 the number of passengers reached 100,000.<sup id="cite_ref-aw_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aw-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>A 1951\xe2\x80\x9352 airport diagram<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> shows four runways laid out at 45-degree angles: 1,350 m Runway 5, 1,024 m Runway 9, 1,203 m Runway 14, and 1,170 m Runway 18. Runways 5 and 36 were each later extended northward to a length of 1,999 m.\n</p><p>Major upgrades from 1959 to 1962 included a new runway capable of handling the first generation of jets, such as the <a href="/wiki/Boeing_707" title="Boeing 707">Boeing 707</a> and <a href="/wiki/Douglas_DC-8" title="Douglas DC-8">Douglas DC-8</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-aw_13-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aw-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> The first jet aircraft flight was an Air France <a href="/wiki/Sud_Aviation_Caravelle" title="Sud Aviation Caravelle">Caravelle</a> in 1960.<sup id="cite_ref-aw_13-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aw-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> In 1962 runway 03/21 came into use. It was 3130 m long and would allow direct transatlantic flights.<sup id="cite_ref-aw_13-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aw-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> The first direct flight to New York was operated by TWA with a Boeing 707, who later operated the first Boeing 747 service in 1970.<sup id="cite_ref-aw_13-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aw-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> When TAP ordered the 747 in 1972, five large parking bays were built, and the terminal was enlarged.<sup id="cite_ref-aw_13-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aw-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> A major upgrade to the buildings and facilities commenced in 1983, and the first air bridges were added in 1991.<sup id="cite_ref-aw_13-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aw-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Along with the airports in Porto, Faro, Ponta Delgada, Santa Maria, Horta, Flores, Madeira, and Porto Santo, the airport\'s concessions to provide support to civil aviation were conceded to <a href="/wiki/ANA_Aeroportos_de_Portugal" title="ANA Aeroportos de Portugal">ANA Aeroportos de Portugal</a> on 18 December 1998, under provisions of decree 404/98. With this concession, ANA became responsible for the planning, development and construction of future infrastructure.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Airport_expansion">Airport expansion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lisbon_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Airport expansion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The airport is now surrounded by urban development, being one of the few airports in Europe located inside a major city<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>. This led to a national debate on whether to keep the present location or to build a new airport; the last option was chosen. Initially, <a href="/wiki/Ota_(Alenquer)" title="Ota (Alenquer)">Ota</a>, a village 50&#160;km (31&#160;mi) north of Lisbon, was chosen as one of the sites for the <a href="/wiki/Ota_Airport" title="Ota Airport">new airport</a>. In 2007 an independent study coordinated by the Portuguese Industry Confederation (CIP) suggested <a href="/wiki/Alcochete_Municipality" class="mw-redirect" title="Alcochete Municipality">Alcochete</a> as an alternative location (see <a href="/wiki/New_Lisbon_Airport" title="New Lisbon Airport">Alcochete Airport</a>). A military training facility currently occupies the site, but the military agreed to abandon the location provided it could transfer its facility to a different area. A second government-contracted study led by the <a href="/wiki/Laborat%C3%B3rio_Nacional_de_Engenharia_Civil" title="Laborat\xc3\xb3rio Nacional de Engenharia Civil">National Laboratory of Civil Engineering</a> (LNEC)<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> concluded in late 2007 that Alcochete was the best location.\n</p><p>The selection of Alcochete was announced on 10 January 2008, more than 35 years after the first capacity increase studies were initiated. Portuguese government announced that Alcochete was the preliminary choice, to be finalised after public consultation.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> The location of Alcochete as the construction site of the future Lisbon Airport was confirmed by the government on 8 May 2008,<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> but the contract was shelved as part of Portugal\'s cost-cutting austerity measures, and completely dismissed from Portugal\'s transportation strategy plans in July 2013, with investment being concentrated on expanding and further improving the existing Lisbon Airport infrastructure.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In November 2006 the operating company ANA Aeroportos de Portugal, announced an expansion plan for some airport structures in order to respond to current passenger traffic growth trends, and full capacity use of the airport, which had been intended to respond to growth until the new airport was to be finished in 2017.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> This plan involved the construction of a new 2nd terminal called Terminal 2 (concluded and operational since August 2007), and expansion of Terminal 1 with new boarding gates (concluded in 2011), a large new shopping and restaurant area, new airbridges and parking positions, a more efficient use of currently existing structures, and a new underground <a href="/wiki/Lisbon_Metro" title="Lisbon Metro">Metro de Lisboa</a> station inaugurated in July 2012.\n</p><p>Terminal 2 is used by 6 scheduled low-cost flight airlines for departures to European destinations, while Terminal 1 handles all arrivals and regular scheduled and chartered flights. In October 2010, European low-cost airline easyJet officially opened a new base at Lisbon Airport, exclusively using Terminal 2 for departures to 20 destinations.<sup id="cite_ref-t2t1_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-t2t1-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> A free shuttle bus connects Terminal 1 Departures area and Terminal 2 every ten minutes.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Between 2007 and 2013, Lisbon Airport underwent several structural and equipment improvements and expansions. These included the construction of Terminal 2, lighting and baggage claim refurbishment, new cargo facilities, fuel storage, north pier and boarding lounge, north bus gate and baggage claim, enlargement of express cargo facilities, electrical refurbishments, departure lounge refurbishments and underground station and other terminal improvements all of which have been completed.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> As part of the definite solution for Lisbon Airport, in July 2013 a new commercial area was inaugurated in the Terminal 1 air side area with 20 new stores and spacious naturally lighted internal circulation areas.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> In July 2015, a significantly larger food court was introduced, catering to more tastes and delicacies.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>With the long-term concession of <a href="/wiki/ANA_Aeroportos_de_Portugal" title="ANA Aeroportos de Portugal">ANA Aeroportos de Portugal</a> to the French group Vinci Airports<sup id="cite_ref-vinci-airports.com_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vinci-airports.com-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> the project for a new airport was postponed in July 2013, and it was decided that the existing Lisbon Airport would be further upgraded to surpass 22 million passengers annually<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> and would remain the present solution for this major European gateway.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> Ryanair had predicted that it will double the number of passengers it carries from the airport in the three years from 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In January 2019, Portugal\'s government unveiled a 1.1-billion-euro ($1.26 billion) plan to expand Lisbon\'s current airport and build a second one.<sup id="cite_ref-njherald.com_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-njherald.com-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> National airports operator ANA is footing the bill to adapt a military airfield in <a href="/wiki/Montijo,_Portugal" title="Montijo, Portugal">Montijo</a>, 30&#160;km (19&#160;mi) by road from Lisbon.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> It aims to handle around 50 million passengers a year from 2022.<sup id="cite_ref-whig.com_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-whig.com-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The airports company will also pay for the expansion of the Humberto Delgado Airport in the capital. With around 29 million passengers a year, it is at full capacity.<sup id="cite_ref-news.airwise.com_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-news.airwise.com-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Naming">Naming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lisbon_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Naming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In February 2015, Lisbon city council unanimously agreed to propose that the name of Lisbon International Airport, known as Portela due to its geographical location, be changed to Humberto Delgado Airport. The proposal, tabled by the Socialist leadership under former Mayor <a href="/wiki/Ant%C3%B3nio_Costa" title="Ant\xc3\xb3nio Costa">Ant\xc3\xb3nio Costa</a>, was agreed to by councillors from across party lines.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Portuguese government under current Prime Minister Ant\xc3\xb3nio Costa, announced in February 2016 that Lisbon Portela Airport would be renamed on 15 May 2016 after <a href="/wiki/Humberto_Delgado" title="Humberto Delgado">Humberto Delgado</a>, in memory of the late Portuguese air force general and famous politician. "He was an opposition figure to the <a href="/wiki/Estado_Novo_(Portugal)" title="Estado Novo (Portugal)">dictatorship regime</a>... and had a very important role in the field of civil aviation," Minister of Planning and Infrastructure <a href="/wiki/Pedro_Marques_(politician)" title="Pedro Marques (politician)">Pedro Marques</a> said at a press conference after the meeting of Council of Ministers, stressing that it was Humberto Delgado who presided over the foundation of Portugal\'s flagship airline <a href="/wiki/TAP_Air_Portugal" title="TAP Air Portugal">TAP</a> and "so it is very fair this assignment name to the airport". 2016 marks the 110th anniversary of the birth of Humberto Delgado, who was also known as the "Fearless General" due to his staunch opposition to <a href="/wiki/Ant%C3%B3nio_de_Oliveira_Salazar" title="Ant\xc3\xb3nio de Oliveira Salazar">Salazar\'s</a> rule and his participation in the <a href="/wiki/1958_Portuguese_presidential_election" title="1958 Portuguese presidential election">1958 Portuguese presidential election</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Terminals">Terminals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lisbon_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Terminals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Lisbon Humberto Delgado Airport features two passenger terminal buildings:<sup id="cite_ref-terminals_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-terminals-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Terminal_1">Terminal 1</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lisbon_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Terminal 1">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Terminal 1 is the main building and features large landside and airside areas containing several shops and service facilities. It consists of two check-in halls, the older one has been converted into <a href="/wiki/TAP_Air_Portugal" title="TAP Air Portugal">TAP Air Portugal</a>\'s self check-in area, and the newer one housing 68 desks (37\xe2\x80\x9389 and 90\xe2\x80\x93106). The joint departures area features 47 gates (17 of which are equipped with jet-bridges) with 21 of them designated to non-Schengen destinations.<sup id="cite_ref-terminals_33-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-terminals-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> As the airport features several more apron stands, bus boarding is often used here. Most airlines use Terminal 1, including TAP Air Portugal, its <a href="/wiki/Star_Alliance" title="Star Alliance">Star Alliance</a> partners and, exceptionally, Vueling.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Terminal_2">Terminal 2</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lisbon_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Terminal 2">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Terminal 2 is a much smaller and newer of the two terminals, used solely by <a href="/wiki/Low-cost_carrier" title="Low-cost carrier">low-cost carriers</a>. It is located away from Terminal 1 on the southern border of the airport perimeter. It has 21 check-in desks (201\xe2\x80\x93222), designated to each particular low-cost carrier, and 15 departure gates (201\xe2\x80\x93215) using mainly walk boarding but also bus. There are only standard facilities, a few shops and service counters. The terminal is reachable via the free airport shuttle service from Terminal 1.<sup id="cite_ref-terminals_33-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-terminals-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> The main users of Terminal 2 are <a href="/wiki/Ryanair" title="Ryanair">Ryanair</a>, <a href="/wiki/EasyJet" title="EasyJet">easyJet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wizz_Air" title="Wizz Air">Wizz Air</a>, <a href="/wiki/Transavia" title="Transavia">Transavia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Transavia_France" title="Transavia France">Transavia France</a>.\n</p><p>It has been closed since the outbreak of COVID-19 in Europe with all airlines using terminal 1, as of March 2021.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Airlines_and_destinations">Airlines and destinations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span ANA operates several covered and open parking areas.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Valet" title="Valet">Valet</a> service, car hire, and chauffeur <a href="/wiki/Limousine" title="Limousine">limousine</a> services are also available.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Car_sharing">Car sharing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lisbon_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Car sharing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Lisbon also has car-sharing companies that operate in the city.
