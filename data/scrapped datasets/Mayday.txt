For other uses, see <a href="/wiki/Mayday_(disambiguation)" class="mw-disambig" title="Mayday (disambiguation)">Mayday (disambiguation)</a>.</div>\n<p>\n<b>Mayday</b> is an emergency <a href="/wiki/Procedure_word" title="Procedure word">procedure word</a> used internationally as a <a href="/wiki/Distress_signal" title="Distress signal">distress signal</a> in <a href="/wiki/Voice_procedure" class="mw-redirect" title="Voice procedure">voice-procedure</a> radio communications.\n</p><p>It is used to signal a life-threatening emergency primarily by aviators and mariners, but in some countries local organizations such as <a href="/wiki/Firefighters" class="mw-redirect" title="Firefighters">firefighters</a>,  <a href="/wiki/Police" title="Police">police</a> forces, and transportation organizations also use the term. Convention requires the word be repeated three times in a row during the initial emergency declaration ("Mayday mayday mayday") to prevent it being mistaken for some similar-sounding phrase under noisy conditions, and to distinguish an <i>actual</i> mayday call from a message <i>about</i> a mayday call.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc" role="navigation" aria-labelledby="mw-toc-heading"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mayday&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The "mayday" procedure word was conceived as a distress call in the early 1920s by Frederick Stanley Mockford, officer-in-charge of radio at <a href="/wiki/Croydon_Airport" title="Croydon Airport">Croydon Airport</a>, London. He had been asked to think of a word that would indicate distress and would easily be understood by all pilots and ground staff in an emergency.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Since much of the air traffic at the time was between Croydon and <a href="/wiki/Le_Bourget_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Le Bourget Airport">Le Bourget Airport</a> in Paris, he proposed the term "mayday", the phonetic equivalent of the French <i lang="fr" title="French-language text">m\'aidez</i> ("help me") or <i lang="fr" title="French-language text">m\'aider</i> (a short form of <i lang="fr" title="French-language text">venez m\'aider</i>, "come [and] help me").<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The term is unrelated to the holiday <a href="/wiki/May_Day" title="May Day">May Day</a>.\n</p><p>Following tests, the new procedure word was introduced for <a href="/wiki/English_Channel" title="English Channel">cross-Channel</a> flights in February 1923.<sup id="cite_ref-times1923_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-times1923-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The previous distress call had been the <a href="/wiki/Morse_code" title="Morse code">Morse code</a> signal <a href="/wiki/SOS" title="SOS">SOS</a>, but this was not considered suitable for voice communication, "[o]wing to the difficulty of distinguishing the letter \'S\' by telephone".<sup id="cite_ref-times1923_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-times1923-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> In 1927, the <a href="/wiki/International_Telecommunication_Union" title="International Telecommunication Union">International Radiotelegraph Convention</a> of <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington</a> adopted the voice call "mayday" as the radiotelephone distress call in addition to the SOS radiotelegraph (Morse code) signal.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mayday_calls">Mayday calls</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a The suffix "medico" used to be added by vessels in British waters to indicate a medical problem ("pan-pan medico", repeated three times), or by aircraft declaring a non-life-threatening medical emergency of a passenger in flight, or those operating as protected medical transport in accordance with the <a href="/wiki/Geneva_Conventions" title="Geneva Conventions">Geneva Conventions</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> "Pan-pan medico" is no longer in official use.<sup id="cite_ref-Tim_Bartlett_2009_53_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tim_Bartlett_2009_53-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Declaring_emergency">Declaring emergency</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mayday&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Declaring emergency">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Sometimes the phrase "declaring emergency" is used in aviation, as an alternative to calling "mayday".<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> For example, in 1998 <a href="/wiki/Swissair_Flight_111" title="Swissair Flight 111">Swissair Flight 111</a> radioed "Swissair one-eleven <a href="/wiki/Heavy_(aviation)" class="mw-redirect" title="Heavy (aviation)">heavy</a> is declaring emergency" after their situation had worsened, upgrading from the "pan-pan" which was declared earlier.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>However, the <a href="/wiki/International_Civil_Aviation_Organization" title="International Civil Aviation Organization">International Civil Aviation Organization</a> recommends the use of the standard "pan-pan" and "mayday" calls instead of "declaring an emergency".<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>  Cases of pilots using phrases other than "pan-pan" and "mayday" have caused confusion and errors in aircraft handling.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Silencing_other_communications_traffic">Silencing other communications traffic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mayday&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Silencing other communications traffic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Radio_silence" title="Radio silence">Radio silence</a></div>\n<p>"Seelonce mayday" (using an approximation of the French pronunciation of <i><i lang="fr" title="French-language text">silence</i></i>) is a demand that the channel only be used by the vessel/s and authorities involved with the distress. The channel may not be used for normal working traffic until "seelonce feenee" is broadcast. "Seelonce mayday" and "seelonce feenee" may only be sent by the controlling station in charge of the distress. The expression "stop transmitting \xe2\x80\x93 mayday" is an aeronautical equivalent of "seelonce mayday". "Seelonce distress" and "prudonce" are no longer in use since ITU WRC-07.\n</p><p>The format for a "seelonce mayday" is MAYDAY, All Stations x3 or [Interfering station] x3, this is [controlling station], SEELONCE MAYDAY. ITU RR 2016.\n</p><p>"Seelonce feenee" (from French <i><i lang="fr" title="French-language text">silence fini</i></i>, \'silence finished\') means that the emergency situation has been concluded and the channel may now be used normally. "Distress traffic ended" is the aeronautical equivalent of "seelonce feenee".<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The format for the "seelonce feenee" is MAYDAY, All stations x3, this is [controlling station] x3, date and time in UTC, distressed vessels MMSI number, distressed vessels name, distressed vessels call sign, SEELONCE FEENEE.
