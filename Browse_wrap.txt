id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">\n\t\t<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>\n\t\t<div id="contentSub"></div>\n\t\t<div id="contentSub2"></div>\n\t\t\n\t\t<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>\n\t\t<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>\n\t\t<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#searchInput">Jump to search</a>\n\t\t<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p><b>Browse-wrap</b> (also <b>Browserwrap</b> or <b>browse-wrap license</b>) is a term used in <a href="/wiki/Internet_law" class="mw-redirect" title="Internet law">Internet law</a> to refer to a <a href="/wiki/Contract" title="Contract">contract</a> or <a href="/wiki/License_agreement" class="mw-redirect" title="License agreement">license agreement</a> covering access to or use of materials on a <a href="/wiki/Web_site" class="mw-redirect" title="Web site">web site</a> or downloadable product.  In a browse-wrap agreement, the terms and conditions of use for a website or other downloadable product are posted on the website, typically as a hyperlink at the bottom of the screen.<sup id="cite_ref-Kwan120103_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kwan120103-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Overstock09_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Overstock09-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Unlike a <a href="/wiki/Clickwrap" title="Clickwrap">clickwrap</a> agreement, where the user must manifest assent to the terms and conditions by clicking on an "I agree" box, a browse-wrap agreement does not require this type of express manifestation of assent.<sup id="cite_ref-Kwan120103_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kwan120103-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Rather, a web-site user purportedly gives their consent simply by using the product \xe2\x80\x94 such as by entering the website or downloading software.<sup id="cite_ref-Kwan120103_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kwan120103-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Browse-wrap agreements, like clickwrap agreements, derive their name by analogy to the "<a href="/wiki/Shrink_wrap_contract" title="Shrink wrap contract">shrink wrap agreements</a>" included inside the sealed packaging of tangible products, where one can not see the agreement until the product has been purchased or used.<sup id="cite_ref-Specht_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Specht-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>  Courts that have ruled on the issue have held that the validity of a browse-wrap agreement primarily depends on whether a website user has actual or constructive notice of the terms and conditions prior to using the website or other product.<sup id="cite_ref-Kwan120103_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kwan120103-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc" role="navigation" aria-labelledby="mw-toc-heading"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" style="display:none" /><div class="toctitle" lang="en" dir="ltr"><h2 id="mw-toc-heading">Contents</h2><span class="toctogglespan"><label class="toctogglelabel" for="toctogglecheckbox"></label></span></div>\n<ul>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-1"><a class="tocnumber">5</span> <span class="toctext">External links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Case_law">Case law</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Browse_wrap&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Case law">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In <i><a href="/wiki/Specht_v._Netscape" class="mw-redirect" title="Specht v. Netscape">Specht v. Netscape</a></i>, the Second Circuit Court of Appeals looked at the enforceability of a browse-wrap contract entered into on the <a href="/wiki/Netscape" title="Netscape">Netscape</a> website.<sup id="cite_ref-Specht_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Specht-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Users of the site were urged to download free software available on the site by clicking on a tinted button labeled "download".<sup id="cite_ref-Specht_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Specht-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>22</span></sup>  Only if a user scrolled down the page to the next screen did he come upon an invitation to review the full terms of the program\'s <a href="/wiki/License_agreement" class="mw-redirect" title="License agreement">license agreement</a>, available by <a href="/wiki/Hyperlink" title="Hyperlink">hyperlink</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Specht_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Specht-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>23</span></sup>  The plaintiffs, who had not seen the agreement, downloaded the software and then were later sued for violations of federal privacy and computer fraud statutes arising from the use of the software.<sup id="cite_ref-Specht_3-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Specht-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>23\xe2\x80\x9325</span></sup>  The Second Circuit then noted that an essential ingredient to contract formation is the mutual manifestation of assent.<sup id="cite_ref-Specht_3-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Specht-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>29</span></sup> The court found that "a consumer\'s clicking on a download button does not communicate assent to contractual terms if the offer did not make clear to the consumer that clicking on the download button would signify assent to those terms."<sup id="cite_ref-Specht_3-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Specht-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>29\xe2\x80\x9330</span></sup> Because the plaintiffs were not put on notice of these terms they were not bound by them.<sup id="cite_ref-Specht_3-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Specht-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>30\xe2\x80\x9332</span></sup>\n</p><p>In <i><a href="/wiki/Ticketmaster_v._Tickets.com" class="mw-redirect" title="Ticketmaster v. Tickets.com">Ticketmaster v. Tickets.com</a></i>, the court looked at a <a href="/wiki/Breach_of_contract" title="Breach of contract">breach of contract</a> claim where the terms and conditions were situated at the bottom of the home page in "<a href="/wiki/Small_print" class="mw-redirect" title="Small print">small print</a>."<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>  The court ruled for the defendant in this case but did allow Ticketmaster to replead if there were facts showing that the defendant had knowledge of the terms and implicitly agreed to them.\n</p><p>In 2005, the <a href="/wiki/Illinois_Appellate_Court" title="Illinois Appellate Court">Illinois Appellate Court</a> ruled in favor of a browse-wrap agreement in <i>Hubbert v. Dell Corp.</i> In this case consumers of <a href="/wiki/Dell" title="Dell">Dell</a> products were repeatedly shown the words "All sales are subject to Dell\'s Term[s] and Conditions of Sale", including a conspicuous hyperlink, over a series of pages.  The court found that this repeated exposure and visual effect would put a reasonable person on notice of the "terms and conditions".<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In contrast, in 2014, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Court_of_Appeals_for_the_Ninth_Circuit" title="United States Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit">United States Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit</a> ruled in <i><a href="/wiki/Nguyen_v._Barnes_%26_Noble,_Inc." title="Nguyen v. Barnes &amp; Noble, Inc.">Nguyen v. Barnes &amp; Noble, Inc.</a></i> that Barnes &amp; Noble\'s 2011 Terms of Use agreement, presented in a browse-wrap manner via hyperlinks alone, was not enforceable since it failed to offer users reasonable notice of the terms.\n</p><p>Similarly, in <i><a href="/wiki/In_re_Zappos.com,_Inc.,_Customer_Data_Security_Breach_Litigation" title="In re Zappos.com, Inc., Customer Data Security Breach Litigation">In re Zappos.com, Inc., Customer Data Security Breach Litigation</a></i>, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_District_Court_for_the_District_of_Nevada" title="United States District Court for the District of Nevada">United States District Court for the District of Nevada</a> ruled against <a href="/wiki/Zappos.com" class="mw-redirect" title="Zappos.com">Zappos.com</a>\'s browsewrap terms of use, describing that its presentation was not prominent, and that no reasonable user would have read the agreement.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Summary">Summary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Browse_wrap&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Summary">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>A browse-wrap agreement can be formed by use of a web page or a hyperlink or small disclaimer on the page.  It may only be enforced if the browsing user assents to it.  For assent to occur the browse-wrap agreement should be conspicuous, state that there is an agreement, and provide where it can be located.  Courts examine the enforceability of browse-wrap agreements on a case-by-case basis, and there are no "bright-line" rules on whether a given agreement is sufficiently conspicuous.  However, based on <i>Specht</i>, some practitioners believe\n</p>\n<blockquote><p>\nthat the icon for the terms of use agreement be placed in the upper left-hand quadrant of the homepage and that all visitors be channeled through the homepage. The reason for this suggestion is that the court will take judicial notice of the fact that all Internet pages open from the upper left-hand quadrant, thus the defendant must overcome the presumption that the icon was viewed. Without this presumption, the plaintiff has the burden of proving the defendant did see the icon.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
