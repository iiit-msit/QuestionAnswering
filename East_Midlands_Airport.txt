Authority</a><sup id="cite_ref-stats_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stats-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup></div></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p><b>East Midlands Airport</b> (<a href="/wiki/IATA_airport_code" title="IATA airport code">IATA</a>: <b>EMA</b>, <a href="/wiki/ICAO_airport_code" title="ICAO airport code">ICAO</a>: <b>EGNX</b>) is an <a href="/wiki/International_airport" title="International airport">international airport</a> in the <a href="/wiki/East_Midlands" title="East Midlands">East Midlands</a> of <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>, close to <a href="/wiki/Castle_Donington" title="Castle Donington">Castle Donington</a> in northwestern <a href="/wiki/Leicestershire" title="Leicestershire">Leicestershire</a>, between <a href="/wiki/Loughborough" title="Loughborough">Loughborough</a> (10 miles (16&#160;km)), <a href="/wiki/Derby" title="Derby">Derby</a> (12.5 miles (20&#160;km)) and <a href="/wiki/Nottingham" title="Nottingham">Nottingham</a> (14 miles (23&#160;km)); <a href="/wiki/Leicester" title="Leicester">Leicester</a> is (20 miles (32&#160;km)) to the south and <a href="/wiki/Lincoln,_England" title="Lincoln, England">Lincoln</a> (43 miles (69&#160;km)) north east. It serves the whole East Midlands region of <a href="/wiki/Leicestershire" title="Leicestershire">Leicestershire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nottinghamshire" title="Nottinghamshire">Nottinghamshire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lincolnshire" title="Lincolnshire">Lincolnshire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Northamptonshire" title="Northamptonshire">Northamptonshire</a> and <a href="/wiki/Derbyshire" title="Derbyshire">Derbyshire</a>. The airfield was originally built as a <a href="/wiki/Royal_Air_Force_station" class="mw-redirect" title="Royal Air Force station">Royal Air Force station</a> known as <b>RAF Castle Donington</b> in 1943, before being redeveloped as a civilian airport in 1965.\n</p><p>East Midlands Airport has established itself as a hub for low-fare airlines such as <a href="/wiki/Jet2.com" title="Jet2.com">Jet2.com</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ryanair" title="Ryanair">Ryanair</a> and tour operators like <a href="/wiki/TUI_Airways" title="TUI Airways">TUI Airways</a>, which serve a range of domestic and European short-haul destinations. Passenger numbers peaked in 2008 at 5.6 million but had declined to around 4.5 million in 2015, making it the <a href="/wiki/Busiest_airports_in_the_United_Kingdom_by_total_passenger_traffic" class="mw-redirect" title="Busiest airports in the United Kingdom by total passenger traffic">11th-busiest airport in the UK by passenger traffic</a>. A major <a href="/wiki/Air_cargo" title="Air cargo">air cargo</a> hub, it was the second-busiest UK airport for freight traffic in 2016, after <a href="/wiki/London_Heathrow_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="London Heathrow Airport">London Heathrow</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-stats_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stats-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The airport is owned by the <a href="/wiki/Manchester_Airports_Group" class="mw-redirect" title="Manchester Airports Group">Manchester Airports Group</a> (MAG), the largest British-owned airport operator, which is controlled by the ten metropolitan borough councils of <a href="/wiki/Greater_Manchester" title="Greater Manchester">Greater Manchester</a>, with <a href="/wiki/Manchester_City_Council" title="Manchester City Council">Manchester City Council</a> retaining the controlling stake.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div id="toc" href="/w/index.php?title=East_Midlands_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="RAF_Castle_Donington">RAF Castle Donington</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=East_Midlands_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: RAF Castle Donington">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>RAF Castle Donington was opened as a <a href="/wiki/Royal_Air_Force_station" class="mw-redirect" title="Royal Air Force station">Royal Air Force station</a> in 1943, during the <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">second world war</a>. The airfield was equipped with three concrete runways, together with two hangars, and was a satellite airfield to <a href="/wiki/RAF_Wymeswold" title="RAF Wymeswold">RAF Wymeswold</a>, situated some 9 miles (14&#160;km) to the south-east. Initially the airfield was used by <a href="/wiki/No._28_OTU" class="mw-redirect" title="No. 28 OTU">28 Operational Training Unit</a>, training <a href="/wiki/RAF_Bomber_Command" title="RAF Bomber Command">RAF Bomber Command</a> crews on the <a href="/wiki/Vickers_Wellington" title="Vickers Wellington">Vickers Wellington</a>, and subsequently by <a href="/wiki/No._108_OTU" class="mw-redirect" title="No. 108 OTU">108 Operational Training Unit</a>, later renamed 1382 Transport Conversion Unit, training <a href="/wiki/RAF_Transport_Command" title="RAF Transport Command">RAF Transport Command</a> crews on the <a href="/wiki/Douglas_Dakota" class="mw-redirect" title="Douglas Dakota">Douglas Dakota</a>. The airfield closed and the air force station was decommissioned in 1946.<sup id="cite_ref-pscape_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pscape-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-emoh_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-emoh-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="East_Midlands_Airport">East Midlands Airport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=East_Midlands_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: East Midlands Airport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:G-BHWE-Boeing737-1197.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6e/G-BHWE-Boeing737-1197.jpg/220px-G-BHWE-Boeing737-1197.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="124" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6e/G-BHWE-Boeing737-1197.jpg/330px-G-BHWE-Boeing737-1197.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6e/G-BHWE-Boeing737-1197.jpg/440px-G-BHWE-Boeing737-1197.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2204" data-file-height="1240" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:G-BHWE-Boeing737-1197.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Britannia_Airways" title="Britannia Airways">Britannia Airways</a> <a href="/wiki/Boeing_737" title="Boeing 737">Boeing 737</a> operating holiday charters in 1982</div></div></div>\n<p>In 1964, the site of the former RAF station was purchased by a consortium of local government authorities, when a major programme of building work and runway investment was begun. The airfield was renamed East Midlands Airport to reflect the area it served, and it opened for passengers in April 1965.<sup id="cite_ref-pscape_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pscape-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-emoh_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-emoh-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Until 1982, when the head office moved to <a href="/wiki/Donington_Hall" title="Donington Hall">Donington Hall</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/British_Midland_International" title="British Midland International">British Midland</a> had its head office on the airport property.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> BMI also had its maintenance base at the airport.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Go_Fly" class="mw-redirect" title="Go Fly">Go Fly</a> established a hub at East Midlands, and the operation has been strengthened since the airline\'s absorption by <a href="/wiki/EasyJet" title="EasyJet">easyJet</a>. The majority of BMI operations were ceded to a new low-cost subsidiary, <a href="/wiki/Bmibaby" title="Bmibaby">bmibaby</a>, in 2002.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>In 1993 <a href="/wiki/National_Express" title="National Express">National Express</a> purchased the airport from the local councils.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> With <a href="/wiki/Bournemouth_Airport" title="Bournemouth Airport">Bournemouth Airport</a>, it was sold to <a href="/wiki/Manchester_Airports_Group" class="mw-redirect" title="Manchester Airports Group">Manchester Airports Group</a> in February 2001.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> In 2004 the airport was controversially renamed Nottingham East Midlands Airport.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The change, however, did not last long, and on 8 December 2006 the airport\'s name was reverted to East Midlands Airport.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>A major development towards the long-haul programme came in 2005 with the introduction of holiday flights to the <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Republic" title="Dominican Republic">Dominican Republic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Orlando,_Florida" title="Orlando, Florida">Orlando</a> and <a href="/wiki/Canc%C3%BAn" title="Canc\xc3\xban">Canc\xc3\xban</a> by <a href="/wiki/First_Choice_Airways" title="First Choice Airways">First Choice Airways</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Following increasing overcrowding at the <a href="/wiki/Airport_terminal" title="Airport terminal">terminal</a> building, the airport facilities were extended and remodelled. There are new short-stay car parks, but there are charges for drop-off outside the terminals. The arrivals hall has been extended, a new transport interchange has been created and a new pier has been built to reduce across-tarmac walking to aircraft.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/EasyJet" title="EasyJet">EasyJet</a> ceased operating from the airport on 5 January 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> However, it was announced on 13 April 2011 that <a href="/wiki/Bmibaby" title="Bmibaby">Bmibaby</a> would close its Manchester and Cardiff bases, moving an additional service to East Midlands Airport with increased frequencies and new routes for summer 2012. It was announced only just over a year later, on 3 May 2012, that Bmibaby would close down and cease all operations in September 2012, with a number of services being dropped from June. The parent company, <a href="/wiki/International_Airlines_Group" title="International Airlines Group">International Airlines Group</a>, cited heavy losses and the failure to find a suitable buyer as the reasons for the decision.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> In light of the announcement, Flybe and <a href="/wiki/Monarch_Airlines" title="Monarch Airlines">Monarch Airlines</a> announced they would establish a base at the airport, and low-cost airline <a href="/wiki/Jet2.com" title="Jet2.com">Jet2.com</a> confirmed they would also expand their operations from the airport, with new routes and an additional aircraft from summer 2013. From 2015, the airport announced jet2.com would base a seventh aircraft at East Midlands Airport in the summer period. Monarch Airlines shut down its base at East Midlands as well by spring 2015.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Ryanair expanded its East Midlands base with a series of new routes and frequency increases on existing routes. It now serves the airport with 9 based aircraft, 41 destinations, over 320 weekly flights and roughly 2.3 million passengers a year<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>, making it the largest airline at the airport, accounting for about 50% of passenger traffic, with East Midlands now being Ryanair\'s third-largest UK airport, after <a href="/wiki/London_Stansted_Airport" title="London Stansted Airport">London\xe2\x80\x93Stansted</a> and <a href="/wiki/Manchester_Airport" title="Manchester Airport">Manchester</a>, both now also owned by <a href="/wiki/Manchester_Airports_Group" class="mw-redirect" title="Manchester Airports Group">MAG</a>.\n</p><p>In 2016 Heathrow handled 1.54 million tonnes of freight and mail, compared with 300,100 tonnes at East Midlands.<sup id="cite_ref-stats_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stats-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/DHL_Aviation" title="DHL Aviation">DHL Aviation</a> have a large purpose-built facility at EMA, and courier companies <a href="/wiki/United_Parcel_Service" title="United Parcel Service">United Parcel Service</a> (UPS) and <a href="/wiki/TNT_N.V." class="mw-redirect" title="TNT N.V.">TNT</a> use the airport as a base to import and export freight.\n</p><p>On 4 March 2020 <a href="/wiki/Flybe" title="Flybe">Flybe</a> entered administration,<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> with EMA announcing that all flights were cancelled with immediate effect, the following day.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Airlines_and_destinations">Airlines and In May 2016, the charge was doubled to \xc2\xa32, with any stay in the area above ten minutes being charged at \xc2\xa31 per minute. Drivers needing longer can stay free for one hour in the long-term carpark, a five-minute bus ride from the terminal. Business Park, an office complex, is on the airport grounds. The now-defunct airline <a href="/wiki/Flybmi" title="Flybmi">flybmi</a> formerly had its head office at Pegasus Business Park.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Accidents_and_incidents">Accidents and incidents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=East_Midlands_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Accidents and incidents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<ul><li>On 20 February 1969, <a href="/wiki/Vickers_Viscount" title="Vickers Viscount">Vickers Viscount</a> G-AODG of <a href="/wiki/BMI_(airline)" class="mw-redirect" title="BMI (airline)">British Midland Airways</a> was damaged beyond economic repair when it landed short of the runway. There were no casualties.<sup id="cite_ref-ASN200269_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ASN200269-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>On 31 January 1986, <a href="/wiki/Aer_Lingus" title="Aer Lingus">Aer Lingus</a> Flight 328, a <a href="/wiki/Short_360" title="Short 360">Short 360</a>, en route from <a href="/wiki/Dublin" title="Dublin">Dublin</a>, struck power lines and crashed short of the runway. None of the 36 passengers and crew died but two passengers were injured in the accident.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>On 18 January 1987, a British Midland <a href="/wiki/Fokker_F27_Friendship" title="Fokker F27 Friendship">Fokker F27 Friendship</a>, on a training flight, crashed on approach to East Midlands Airport due to wing and tail surface icing. There were no fatalities.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>On 8 January 1989, British Midland <a href="/wiki/Kegworth_air_disaster" title="Kegworth air disaster">Flight BD092</a> crashed on approach to East Midlands Airport, killing 47 people. The <a href="/wiki/Boeing_737" title="Boeing 737">Boeing 737</a> aircraft had developed a fan blade failure in one of the two engines while en route from London Heathrow to <a href="/wiki/Belfast_International_Airport" title="Belfast International Airport">Belfast</a> and a decision was made to divert to East Midlands. The crew mistakenly shut down the functioning engine, causing the aircraft to lose power and crash on the embankment of the M1 Motorway just short of the runway. No one on the ground was injured and no vehicles were damaged despite the aircraft crashing on the embankment of one of the busiest sections of motorway in the UK. The investigation into the <a href="/wiki/Kegworth_air_disaster" title="Kegworth air disaster">Kegworth air disaster</a>, as the incident became known, led to considerable improvements in aircraft safety and emergency instructions for passengers.
