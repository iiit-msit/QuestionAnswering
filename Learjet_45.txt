70/75</a>\n</td></tr>\n</tbody></table>\n<p>The <b>Learjet 45</b> (LJ45) aircraft is a mid-size <a href="/wiki/Business_jet" title="Business jet">business jet</a> aircraft produced by the <a href="/wiki/Learjet" title="Learjet">Learjet Division</a> of <a href="/wiki/Bombardier_Aerospace" class="mw-redirect" title="Bombardier Aerospace">Bombardier Aerospace</a>.\n</p><p>The Model 45 was the first all-new design since the original Learjet, and significantly altered the Learjet line. Through its four primary variants \xe2\x80\x93 the original Model 45, the Model 45XR, Model 40 and Model 40XR \xe2\x80\x93 it was the Learjet Division\'s principal product from the 1990s until the introduction of the Model 75 variant in 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-swiss_army_2010_11_18_flying_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-swiss_army_2010_11_18_flying-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-used_report_2017_03_28_bca_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-used_report_2017_03_28_bca-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc" role="navigation" class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Learjet_45&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Development and production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Beauty_standing_still_(426105349).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Beauty_standing_still_%28426105349%29.jpg/220px-Beauty_standing_still_%28426105349%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="143" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Beauty_standing_still_%28426105349%29.jpg/330px-Beauty_standing_still_%28426105349%29.jpg 1.5x, <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Beauty_standing_still_(426105349).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>On ground</div></div></div>\n<p>The Model 45 was developed in the 1990s as a competitor in the "super-light" business jet category, a rival to the popular <a href="/wiki/Cessna_Citation_Excel" title="Cessna Citation Excel">Cessna Citation Excel</a> / <a href="/wiki/Cessna_Citation_XLS" class="mw-redirect" title="Cessna Citation XLS">XLS</a> \xe2\x80\x93 but sacrificing the Cessna\'s stand-up room for the Learjet family\'s traditional high-speed performance.<sup id="cite_ref-swiss_army_2010_11_18_flying_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-swiss_army_2010_11_18_flying-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-learjet_45_45XR_2007_06_bizjet_traveler_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-learjet_45_45XR_2007_06_bizjet_traveler-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-used_report_2017_03_28_bca_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-used_report_2017_03_28_bca-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Model 45 was Learjet\'s first completely new ("clean sheet") design since the company\'s first aircraft (all other models having been evolved from the original 1963 Learjet design, the <a href="/wiki/Learjet_23" title="Learjet 23">Model 23</a>). The Model 45 was developed to make Learjets fundamentally more competitive against newer designs from competing manufacturers. But, as a clean-sheet design (starting from scratch), being built to more rigorous (FAR Part 25) rules, than previous Learjets, the aircraft\'s development took substantially longer than that of previous Learjet models.<sup id="cite_ref-learjet_45_45XR_2007_06_bizjet_traveler_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-learjet_45_45XR_2007_06_bizjet_traveler-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The development of the LJ45 began in 1989,<sup id="cite_ref-learjet_45_45XR_2007_06_bizjet_traveler_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-learjet_45_45XR_2007_06_bizjet_traveler-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> but was not announced by Bombardier until September, 1992. First flight of the prototype aircraft took place on October 7, 1995 \xe2\x80\x93 the 32nd anniversary of the first flight of the original <a href="/wiki/Learjet_23" title="Learjet 23">Learjet 23</a>.  <a href="/wiki/Federal_Aviation_Administration" title="Federal Aviation Administration">FAA</a> <a href="/wiki/Type_certificate" title="Type certificate">certification</a> was delayed, and finally granted in September 1997, with the first customer aircraft subsequently delivered in mid-1998.<sup id="cite_ref-2-FirstLJ45_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2-FirstLJ45-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FirstLJ45_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FirstLJ45-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The aviation magazine <i><a href="/wiki/Flying_(magazine)" title="Flying (magazine)">Flying</a></i> reported that the Lear 45 was first certified under FAR Part 25 (transport category rules) in 1998.<sup id="cite_ref-swiss_army_2010_11_18_flying_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-swiss_army_2010_11_18_flying-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Initially, delays in production resulted in frustrated customers and lost or delayed revenues. Some customers\' orders were delayed more than two years.<sup id="cite_ref-learjet_45_45XR_2007_06_bizjet_traveler_5-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-learjet_45_45XR_2007_06_bizjet_traveler-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Deliveries_and_difficulties">Deliveries and difficulties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Learjet_45&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Deliveries and difficulties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:N300JC_Learjet_Inc_45_C-N_340_(5394534073).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/N300JC_Learjet_Inc_45_C-N_340_%285394534073%29.jpg/330px-N300JC_Learjet_Inc_45_C-N_340_%285394534073%29.jpg data-file-height="770" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:N300JC_Learjet_Inc_45_C-N_340_(5394534073).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>In flight</div></div></div>\n<p>By late 2006, Learjet had delivered over 300 Model 45s (40 of which were its improved version, the Model 45XR).<sup id="cite_ref-learjet_45_45XR_2007_06_bizjet_traveler_5-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-learjet_45_45XR_2007_06_bizjet_traveler-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> By November 2010, over 500 of the Model 45 and 45XR had been delivered.<sup id="cite_ref-swiss_army_2010_11_18_flying_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-swiss_army_2010_11_18_flying-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Initially, numerous mechanical and electrical problems began appearing in the aircraft after delivery \xe2\x80\x93  including cracked windshields, problems with pressurization, fried power distribution panels, and inappropriate alarms. These and other problems commonly forced Model 45 operators to ground their aircraft about once a month, limiting their use.<sup id="cite_ref-learjet_45_45XR_2007_06_bizjet_traveler_5-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-learjet_45_45XR_2007_06_bizjet_traveler-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Problems came to a head in August 2003, when the FAA discovered a defective fastener for the Model 45\'s horizontal stabilizer could break, sending the airplane into a fatal dive. The FAA grounded all Learjet 45s, across the nation, and they all sat on the ground for a month, while the manufacturer struggled to develop a fix and get it to aircraft in the field.<sup id="cite_ref-learjet_45_45XR_2007_06_bizjet_traveler_5-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-learjet_45_45XR_2007_06_bizjet_traveler-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-faa_grounds_2003_08_12_wash_jpost_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-faa_grounds_2003_08_12_wash_jpost-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>By 2007, Model 45 operators were said to have a "love-hate" relationship with the aircraft \xe2\x80\x93 appreciating its exceptional combination of performance, payload and economics, but frustrated by frequent maintenance problems, often grounding the aircraft, and difficulty getting prompt and adequate product support and parts.<sup id="cite_ref-learjet_45_45XR_2007_06_bizjet_traveler_5-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-learjet_45_45XR_2007_06_bizjet_traveler-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ain_2004_product_support_report_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ain_2004_product_support_report-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> However, 10 years later, a 2017 "Used Aircraft Report" by <i>Business &amp; Commercial Aviation</i> magazine indicated that owners now regard the aircraft as "gas-and-go airplane" and credit it "with great reliability," and the article\'s author, B/CA senior editor Fred George, describes it as "a rock-solid reliable workhorse."<sup id="cite_ref-used_report_2017_03_28_bca_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-used_report_2017_03_28_bca-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>By 2018, Learjet 45/45XRs were priced at $1.5\xe2\x80\x934.9 million.<sup id="cite_ref-AINdec2018_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AINdec2018-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" 2x" data-file-width="1600" data-file-height="1063" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:VH-SQD_Bombardier_Learjet_45_Singapore_Flying_College_(9527311466).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Flight deck</div></div></div>\n<p>In size, the LJ45 and LJ45XR fit between the smaller <a href="/wiki/Learjet_31" title="Learjet 31">Learjet 31</a> and <a href="/wiki/Learjet_40" title="Learjet 40">Learjet 40</a>, at the low end,  and the larger <a href="/wiki/Learjet_60" title="Learjet 60">Learjet 60</a> at the top of the Learjet product line. It offers the operating economics typical of a "light" business jet, but the performance and comfort of a "mid-size" business jet.<sup id="cite_ref-learjet_45_45XR_2007_06_bizjet_traveler_5-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-learjet_45_45XR_2007_06_bizjet_traveler-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-used_report_2017_03_28_bca_6-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-used_report_2017_03_28_bca-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>It has a 1,971-nautical-mile (3,650&#160;km; 2,268&#160;mi) range at Mach 0.81 with four passengers on board.\n</p><p>The plane was designed around a flat-floor cabin,<sup id="cite_ref-learjet_45_45XR_2007_06_bizjet_traveler_5-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-learjet_45_45XR_2007_06_bizjet_traveler-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-used_report_2017_03_28_bca_6-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-used_report_2017_03_28_bca-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> with a non-circular cross-section for additional headroom.<sup id="cite_ref-used_report_2017_03_28_bca_6-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-used_report_2017_03_28_bca-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Typical interior configuration is eight passenger seats, in a double-<a href="/wiki/Club_seating" title="Club seating">club seating</a> arrangement, and a fully enclosed toilet (usable as a 9th seat) and an adjoining 15-cubic-foot baggage area.<sup id="cite_ref-swiss_army_2010_11_18_flying_4-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-swiss_army_2010_11_18_flying-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-used_report_2017_03_28_bca_6-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-used_report_2017_03_28_bca-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> An external 50-cubic-foot baggage area is also provided.<sup id="cite_ref-used_report_2017_03_28_bca_6-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-used_report_2017_03_28_bca-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Model 45 was Learjet\'s first "clean-sheet" (all-new) design since the development of the original <a href="/wiki/Learjet_23" title="Learjet 23">Model 23</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-learjet_45_45XR_2007_06_bizjet_traveler_5-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-learjet_45_45XR_2007_06_bizjet_traveler-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> (All previous models had been derivatives of the Model 23, with the wide-fuselage <a href="/wiki/Learjet_55" title="Learjet 55">Model 55</a> and <a href="/wiki/Learjet_60" title="Learjet 60">Model 60</a> utilizing the basic wing design originating in the Model 23, but with extensions and winglets; some however, view the Model 55 as a "clean sheet" design, also.)<sup id="cite_ref-used_report_2017_03_28_bca_6-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-used_report_2017_03_28_bca-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Previous Learjet designs had used rigorous, extensive "<a href="/wiki/Fail-safe" title="Fail-safe">fail-safe</a>" structures in the wing and tail assemblies, with numerous <a href="/wiki/Wing_spar" class="mw-redirect" title="Wing spar">wing spars</a> providing a highly reinforced structure (derived from the Swiss-designed <a href="/wiki/FFA_P-16" title="FFA P-16">P-16</a> prototype fighter that was the original basis for the first Learjet)<sup id="cite_ref-lear_gene_2005_11_01_airport_journals_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lear_gene_2005_11_01_airport_journals-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> \xe2\x80\x93 but the Model 45, instead, used a lighter, less-robust structure, affording less cost in manufacture and lower aircraft empty weights, resulting in improved capacity, efficiency and some performance enhancement (for a given amount of engine power), at a competitive price.<sup id="cite_ref-swiss_army_2010_11_18_flying_4-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-swiss_army_2010_11_18_flying-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Outwardly, however, the Model 45 retained the traditional Learjet appearance, with its semi-swept wing, T-tail, sharp nose, and wrap-around windshield \xe2\x80\x93 while using engines and avionics similar to those in the <a href="/wiki/Learjet_60" title="Learjet 60">Model 60</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-swiss_army_2010_11_18_flying_4-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-swiss_army_2010_11_18_flying-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>It also incorporated two conspicuous  modifications pioneered on earlier Learjets: the "delta fins" (twin ventral fins, positioned to help stabilize the aircraft in flight, and help right the aircraft in a deep <a href="/wiki/Aerodynamic_stall" class="mw-redirect" title="Aerodynamic stall">aerodynamic stall</a>) \xe2\x80\x93 and <a href="/wiki/Winglets" class="mw-redirect" title="Winglets">winglets</a> (upturned wingtips to reduce <a href="/wiki/Induced_drag" class="mw-redirect" title="Induced drag">induced drag</a> and improve stability). Among other characteristics, the design has reportedly yielded the most benign stall characteristics of any "light" business jet to date (as of 2017).<sup id="cite_ref-swiss_army_2010_11_18_flying_4-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-swiss_army_2010_11_18_flying-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-used_report_2017_03_28_bca_6-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-used_report_2017_03_28_bca-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Model 45 cockpit is equipped with a four-screen <a href="/wiki/Honeywell_Primus" title="Honeywell Primus">Honeywell Primus</a> 1000 EFIS avionics system.,<sup id="cite_ref-swiss_army_2010_11_18_flying_4-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-swiss_army_2010_11_18_flying-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> however it uses obsolete <a href="/wiki/Cathode-ray_tube" title="Cathode-ray tube">CRT</a> screens, rather than modern <a href="/wiki/Liquid-crystal_display" title="Liquid-crystal display">LCD</a> screens \xe2\x80\x93 resulting substantial maintenance costs (though a $300,000 LCD-display upgrade is available).<sup id="cite_ref-used_report_2017_03_28_bca_6-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-used_report_2017_03_28_bca-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The aircraft is powered by two <a href="/wiki/FADEC" title="FADEC">DEEC</a>-controlled <a href="/wiki/Honeywell_TFE731" class="mw-redirect" title="Honeywell TFE731">Honeywell TFE731</a>-20-AR <a href="/wiki/Turbofan" title="Turbofan">turbofan</a> engines, rated at 3,500 pounds thrust, each, in sea-level temperatures up to 88&#160;\xc2\xb0F \xe2\x80\x93 a fuel-efficient, "understressed," derated version of the 4,435&#160;lb thrust TFE731-40 \xe2\x80\x93 developed specifically for the Learjet 45. Recommended engine TBO (Time Between Overhauls) is 5,000 hours.<sup id="cite_ref-swiss_army_2010_11_18_flying_4-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-swiss_army_2010_11_18_flying-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-airliners_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-airliners-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-learjet_45_45XR_2007_06_bizjet_traveler_5-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-learjet_45_45XR_2007_06_bizjet_traveler-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-used_report_2017_03_28_bca_6-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-used_report_2017_03_28_bca-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>At normal atmospheric conditions, zero-wind takeoff runway requirements range from 4,200 feet (for a stripped, basic plane with a <a href="/wiki/Basic_operating_weight" class="mw-redirect" title="Basic operating weight">basic operating weight</a> of 11,700 pounds) to 5,040 feet for a fully equipped model, with <a href="/wiki/Auxiliary_power_unit" title="Auxiliary power unit">auxiliary power unit</a> and other upgrades (14,200&#160;lbs).<sup id="cite_ref-used_report_2017_03_28_bca_6-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-used_report_2017_03_28_bca-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>However, the engine exhibits poor performance in high-<a href="/wiki/Density_altitude" title="Density altitude">density altitude</a> situations (such as takeoff from hot, humid or high-elevation airports), often resulting in long runway requirements. (Engine power deficiency in high-density altitudes was largely resolved by the engine upgrade to the -20BR engine variant in the Learjet 45XR variant of the Learjet 45 \xe2\x80\x93 an engine upgrade which is also available as a retrofit to the older Learjet 45, and widely recommended.)<sup id="cite_ref-swiss_army_2010_11_18_flying_4-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-swiss_army_2010_11_18_flying-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-airliners_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-airliners-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-learjet_45_45XR_2007_06_bizjet_traveler_5-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-learjet_45_45XR_2007_06_bizjet_traveler-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-used_report_2017_03_28_bca_6-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-used_report_2017_03_28_bca-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>An internal auxiliary power unit, for ground use, provides electrical and pneumatic power, permits easy starts, without ground power units, and enables aircraft cooling or heating while on the ground, without the engines operating. However, the auxiliary power unit was initially optional, rather than standard, equipment.<sup id="cite_ref-swiss_army_2010_11_18_flying_4-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-swiss_army_2010_11_18_flying-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-airliners_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-airliners-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-learjet_45_45XR_2007_06_bizjet_traveler_5-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-learjet_45_45XR_2007_06_bizjet_traveler-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-used_report_2017_03_28_bca_6-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-used_report_2017_03_28_bca-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The wing has 13 degrees of <a href="/wiki/Swept_wing" title="Swept wing">wing sweep</a> with a <a href="/wiki/Supercritical_airfoil" title="Supercritical airfoil">supercritical airfoil</a> optimized for cruise flight at Mach 0.78. Though there are no <a href="/wiki/Leading-edge_slats" class="mw-redirect" title="Leading-edge slats">leading-edge slats</a> or <a href="/wiki/Wing_fence" title="Wing fence">wing fences</a>, the wing has <a href="/w/index.php?title=Vortilons&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Vortilons (page does not exist)">vortilons</a> on the leading edge, to avoid <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanwise_flow&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Spanwise flow (page does not exist)">spanwise flow</a>, and small metal triangles on the leading edge to minimize airflow separation during flight at a high <a href="/wiki/Angle_of_attack" title="Angle of attack">angle of attack</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-swiss_army_2010_11_18_flying_4-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-swiss_army_2010_11_18_flying-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-used_report_2017_03_28_bca_6-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-used_report_2017_03_28_bca-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Lear 45 was certified under FAR Part 25 (transport category rules), rather than FAR Part 23 (often used in earlier business jets). The stricter Part 25 certification requires greater system redundancy, and requires that the airplane reliably meets the performance numbers published in the aircraft\'s FAA-approved Pilot\'s Operating Handbook (POH).<sup id="cite_ref-swiss_army_2010_11_18_flying_4-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-swiss_army_2010_11_18_flying-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-learjet_45_45XR_2007_06_bizjet_traveler_5-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-learjet_45_45XR_2007_06_bizjet_traveler-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Dual-wheel, <a href="/wiki/Trailing_link" class="mw-redirect" title="Trailing link">trailing link</a>, main landing gear is provided, with digital nosewheel steering facilitating tight turns. Single-point pressure refueling is also provided, expediting turnarounds.<sup id="cite_ref-swiss_army_2010_11_18_flying_4-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-swiss_army_2010_11_18_flying-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/De_Havilland_Canada" title="De Havilland Canada">De Havilland Canada</a> builds the LJ45\'s wings, and Bombardier subsidiary <a href="/wiki/Short_Brothers" title="Short Brothers">Short Brothers</a> of <a href="/wiki/Belfast" title="Belfast">Belfast</a>, <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>, builds the <a href="/wiki/Fuselage" title="Fuselage">fuselage</a> and <a href="/wiki/Empennage" title="Empennage">empennage</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Variants">Variants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Learjet_45&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: data-file-width="1280" data-file-height="960" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Bombardier_Learjet_45XR_-_33_-_closeup_portside_door_open.jpg" class="internal" 45, introduced in June 2004, offering substantially higher takeoff weights, faster cruise speeds and faster time-to-climb rates than the Model 45, chiefly through modifications to the engine.<sup id="cite_ref-swiss_army_2010_11_18_flying_4-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-swiss_army_2010_11_18_flying-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup class="mw-redirect" title="Gross weight">gross weight</a> 1,000 pounds greater than the original Model 45, greatly increasing payload and fuel/range options. The aircraft also has exceptionally short runway requirements for mid-size bizjets, capable of loaded flights from 4,000-foot runways.<sup id="cite_ref-swiss_army_2010_11_18_flying_4-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-swiss_army_2010_11_18_flying-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-learjet_45_45XR_2007_06_bizjet_traveler_5-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-learjet_45_45XR_2007_06_bizjet_traveler-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The 45XR\'s performance and specifications increases are due to the upgrading of the engines to the TFE731-20BR configuration, flat-rated to 3,500 pounds thrust, even at 104 degrees Fahrenheit, well above the 88-degree mark for that power from the original Model 45\'s TFE731-20 engines.<sup id="cite_ref-swiss_army_2010_11_18_flying_4-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-swiss_army_2010_11_18_flying-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-used_report_2017_03_28_bca_6-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-used_report_2017_03_28_bca-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-wingsoverkansas1_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wingsoverkansas1-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Model 45 cockpit is equipped with a four-screen <a href="/wiki/Honeywell_Primus" title="Honeywell Primus">Honeywell Primus</a> 1000 EFIS avionics system,<sup id="cite_ref-used_report_2017_03_28_bca_6-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-used_report_2017_03_28_bca-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> with <a href="/wiki/TCAS" class="mw-redirect" title="TCAS">TCAS</a> and <a href="/wiki/EGPWS" class="mw-redirect" title="EGPWS">EGPWS</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-swiss_army_2010_11_18_flying_4-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-swiss_army_2010_11_18_flying-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>LJ45 owners can upgrade their aircraft through the incorporation of several service bulletins.<sup id="cite_ref-wingsoverkansas1_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wingsoverkansas1-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-used_report_2017_03_28_bca_6-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-used_report_2017_03_28_bca-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Model_40">Model 40</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Learjet_45&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Model 40">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Learjet_40" title="Learjet 40">Learjet 40</a></div>\n<p>The Model 40, first delivered in 2003, is a two-foot (24.5 inches) shorter variant of the Model 45, with less passenger room, but otherwise comparable characteristics and performance data. The model 40 also lacked an auxiliary power unit.<sup id="cite_ref-swiss_army_2010_11_18_flying_4-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-swiss_army_2010_11_18_flying-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Model_40XR">Model 40XR</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Learjet_45&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Model 40XR">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The Model 40XR, introduced in 2004, is a higher-performance variant of the Model 40, with the same modifications as used to convert the Model 45 into the Model 45XR.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Model_75">Model 75</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Learjet_45&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Model 75">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Learjet_75" class="mw-redirect" title="Learjet 75">Learjet 75</a></div>\n<p>The Learjet Model 75 variant, first delivered in 2012, is essentially a Model 45XR with more modern engines, engine systems, and Garmin 5000 avionics, and more efficient winglets.<sup id="cite_ref-operators_learjet_70_75_bca_avweek_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-operators_learjet_70_75_bca_avweek-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>\n<sup id="cite_ref-used_report_2017_03_28_bca_6-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-used_report_2017_03_28_bca-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Model 75\'s Honeywell TFE731-40BR engines yield 10% more takeoff thrust (3,850 pounds) than the -20BR engines powering the Model 45XR, cutting required runway length 12%, allowing a sea-level, standard-day takeoff within 4,440&#160;ft., (versus the Model 45XR\'s fully equipped need for 5,040&#160;ft). In these conditions, the Model 75 can take off from a 4,500-foot runway, with a full load of passengers and fuel, and fly 1,800 nautical miles (2,070 statute miles).<sup id="cite_ref-operators_learjet_70_75_bca_avweek_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-operators_learjet_70_75_bca_avweek-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>As with the original Model 45, the Model 75 was initially delivered in a stripped-down form, with many features only offered as options, rather than as standard equipment). However, the aircraft does come equipped with an auxiliary power unit, and thrust reversers (rare in light business jets). Early units had minor quality problems and defects.<sup id="cite_ref-operators_learjet_70_75_bca_avweek_13-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-operators_learjet_70_75_bca_avweek-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Model 75 was first FAA-certified in 2013, and took the place of the Model 45XR, becoming the division\'s principal product. Since 2015 (as of 2017), it is the division\'s only product.<sup id="cite_ref-operators_learjet_70_75_bca_avweek_13-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-operators_learjet_70_75_bca_avweek-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-learjet_75_certification_2013_11_15_wichita_eagle_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-learjet_75_certification_2013_11_15_wichita_eagle-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Model_70">Model 70</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Learjet_45&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Model 70">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Learjet_75" class="mw-redirect" title="Learjet 75">Learjet 75</a></div>\n<p>The Model 70, introduced in 2013, is a shortened variant of the Model 75 \xe2\x80\x93 alternatively described as an upgraded Model 40XR, with the same modifications as used to convert the Model 45XR into the Model 75.
