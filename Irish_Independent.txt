title="Newspaper">newspaper</a>, and online publication via Independent.ie, which is owned by <a href="/wiki/Independent_News_%26_Media" title="Independent News &amp; Media">Independent News &amp; Media</a> (INM) who are a subsidiary of <a href="/wiki/Mediahuis" title="Mediahuis">Mediahuis</a>. \n</p><p>The newspaper version often includes glossy magazines.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> \n</p><p>Traditionally a <a href="/wiki/Broadsheet" title="Broadsheet">broadsheet</a> newspaper, it introduced an additional <a href="/wiki/Compact_(newspaper)" title="Compact (newspaper)">compact</a> size in 2004 and in December 2012 (following billionaire <a href="/wiki/Denis_O%27Brien" title="Denis O&#39;Brien">Denis O\'Brien</a>\'s takeover) it was announced that the newspaper would become compact only.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div id="toc" 1924</div></div></div>\n<h3><span id="Murphy_and_family_.281905.E2.80.931973.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Murphy_and_family_(1905\xe2\x80\x931973)">Murphy and family (1905\xe2\x80\x931973)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Irish_Independent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Murphy and family (1905\xe2\x80\x931973)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The <i>Irish Independent</i> was formed in 1905 as the direct successor to <i><b>The Irish Daily Independent and Daily Nation</b></i>, an 1890s pro-<a href="/wiki/Charles_Stewart_Parnell" title="Charles Stewart Parnell">Parnellite</a> newspaper, and was launched by <a href="/wiki/William_Martin_Murphy" title="William Martin Murphy">William Martin Murphy</a>, a controversial Irish nationalist businessman, staunch anti-<a href="/wiki/Charles_Stewart_Parnell" title="Charles Stewart Parnell">Parnellite</a>, and fellow townsman of Parnell\'s most venomous opponent, Bantry\'s <a href="/wiki/Timothy_Michael_Healy" class="mw-redirect" title="Timothy Michael Healy">Timothy Michael Healy</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The first issue of the <i>Irish Independent</i>, published 2 January 1905, was marked as "Vol. 14. No. 1."\n</p><p>During the <a href="/wiki/Dublin_Lockout" class="mw-redirect" title="Dublin Lockout">1913 Lockout</a> of workers, in which Murphy was the leading figure among the employers, the <i>Irish Independent</i> vigorously sided with its owner\'s interests, publishing news reports and opinion pieces hostile to the strikers, expressing confidence in the unions\' defeat and launching personal attacks on the leader of the strikers, <a href="/wiki/James_Larkin" title="James Larkin">James Larkin</a>. <i>The Irish Independent</i> described the <a href="/wiki/Easter_Rising" title="Easter Rising">1916 Easter Rising</a> as <i>"insane and criminal"</i> and famously called for the shooting of its leaders.<sup id="cite_ref-easter_rising_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-easter_rising-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> In December 1919, during the <a href="/wiki/Irish_War_of_Independence" title="Irish War of Independence">Irish War of Independence</a>, a group of twenty <a href="/wiki/Irish_Republican_Army" title="Irish Republican Army">IRA</a> men destroyed the printing works of the paper, angered at its criticism of the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Republican_Army" title="Irish Republican Army">Irish Republican Army</a>\'s attacks on members of the <a href="/wiki/Dublin_Metropolitan_Police" title="Dublin Metropolitan Police">Dublin Metropolitan Police</a> and British government officials.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> In 1924, the traditional nationalist newspaper, the <i><a href="/wiki/Freeman%27s_Journal" title="Freeman&#39;s Journal">Freeman\'s Journal</a></i>, merged with the <i>Irish Independent</i>. Until October 1986 the paper\'s masthead over the editorial contained the words "incorporating the Freeman\'s Journal".<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>For most of its history, the <i>Irish Independent</i> (also called simply the <i>Independent</i> or more colloquially, the <i>Indo</i>) was seen as a nationalist, Catholic, <a href="/wiki/Anti-Communist" class="mw-redirect" title="Anti-Communist">anti-Communist</a>, newspaper,<sup id="cite_ref-fm_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fm-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> which gave its political allegiance to the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Civil_War" title="Irish Civil War">Pro-Treaty</a> party <a href="/wiki/Cumann_na_nGaedheal" title="Cumann na nGaedheal">Cumann na nGaedheal</a> and later its successor party, <a href="/wiki/Fine_Gael" title="Fine Gael">Fine Gael</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-fm_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fm-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> During the Spanish Civil War, the <i>Irish Independent</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">&#39;</span>s coverage was strongly pro-<a href="/wiki/Francisco_Franco" title="Francisco Franco">Franco</a>; the paper criticised the De Valera government for not intervening on behalf of the <a href="/wiki/Nationalist_faction_(Spanish_Civil_War)" title="Nationalist faction (Spanish Civil War)">Spanish Nationalists</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1961, the <a href="/wiki/Harp" title="Harp">harp</a> became a symbol of the <i>Irish Independent</i>. It originally appeared in black but was changed to green in 1972.\n</p>\n<h3><span id="O.27Reilly_.281973.E2.80.932012.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="O\'Reilly_(1973\xe2\x80\x932012)">O\'Reilly (1973\xe2\x80\x932012)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Irish_Independent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: O&#039;Reilly (1973\xe2\x80\x932012)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In the 1970s, former <a href="/wiki/Heinz" title="Heinz">Heinz</a> chairman <a href="/wiki/Tony_O%27Reilly" title="Tony O&#39;Reilly">Tony O\'Reilly</a> took over the <i>Irish Independent</i>. Under his leadership, it became a more populist, <a href="/wiki/Market_liberal" class="mw-redirect" title="Market liberal">market liberal</a> newspaper\xe2\x80\x94populist on social issues, but economically right-wing. By the mid-nineties its allegiance to Fine Gael had ended. In the <a href="/wiki/1997_Irish_general_election" title="1997 Irish general election">1997 general election</a>, it endorsed <a href="/wiki/Fianna_F%C3%A1il" title="Fianna F\xc3\xa1il">Fianna F\xc3\xa1il</a> under a front-page editorial, entitled "It\'s Payback Time". While it suggested its headline referred to the fact that the election offered a chance to "pay back" politicians for their failings, its opponents suggested that the "payback" actually referred to its chance to get revenge for the refusal of the <a href="/wiki/Rainbow_Coalition_(Ireland_1992)" class="mw-redirect" title="Rainbow Coalition (Ireland 1992)">Rainbow Coalition</a> to award the company a mobile phone licence.<sup id="cite_ref-partiality_claim_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-partiality_claim-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In late 2004, Independent Newspapers moved from their traditional home in Middle Abbey Street to a new office, "Independent House" in <a href="/wiki/Talbot_Street" title="Talbot Street">Talbot Street</a>, with the printing facilities already relocated to the <a href="/wiki/Citywest" title="Citywest">Citywest</a> business park near <a href="/wiki/Tallaght" title="Tallaght">Tallaght</a>.\n</p><p>On 27 September 2005, a fortnight after the paper published its centenary edition, it was announced that editor Vinnie Doyle would step down after 24 years in the position. He was replaced by Gerry O\'Regan, who had until then been editor of the <i>Irish Independent</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">&#39;</span>s sister paper, the <i>Evening Herald</i>. The newspaper\'s previous editor <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Rae_(editor)" title="Stephen Rae (editor)">Stephen Rae</a> was also formerly editor of the <i>Evening Herald</i> and was appointed editor in September 2012. Fionnan Sheahan was appointed editor in January 2015.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span Tribune">Sunday Tribune</a></i> from 1988 to 2011. The New Irish Writing Page is "the longest-running creative writing feature of its kind in any Irish or British newspaper".<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Exam_Brief"><i>Exam
