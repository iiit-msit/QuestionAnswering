by</th><td class="infobox-data">&#8212;</td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p>The <b>Flight Compensation Regulation</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Regulation_(European_Union)" title="Regulation (European Union)">regulation</a> in <a href="/wiki/EU_law" class="mw-redirect" title="EU law">EU law</a> establishing common rules on <a href="/wiki/Damages" title="Damages">compensation</a> and assistance to passengers in the event of denied boarding, <a href="/wiki/Flight_cancellation" class="mw-redirect" title="Flight cancellation">flight cancellations</a>, or long delays of flights. It requires compensation of \xe2\x82\xac250 to \xe2\x82\xac600<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> depending on the flight distance for delays over of at least 2 hours,<sup id="cite_ref-261_2004_arts_3_and_4_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-261_2004_arts_3_and_4-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> cancellations, or being denied boarding from <a href="/wiki/Overbooking" class="mw-redirect" title="Overbooking">overbooking</a>. Delays shorter than two hours means no entitlement to any compensation of any kind even if the delay was classified as non-extraordinary. Airlines must provide refreshments and accommodation where appropriate. The <a href="/wiki/Court_of_Justice_of_the_European_Union" title="Court of Justice of the European Union">Court of Justice of the European Union</a> has interpreted passenger rights strictly, so that there are virtually no exceptions for airlines to evade their obligations for breach of contract.\n</p><p>It repealed Regulation (EEC) No 295/91, and went into effect on 17 February 2005.\n</p>\n<div class="toctext">External links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applicability">Applicability</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flight_Compensation_Regulation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Applicability">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The regulation applies to any passenger:\n</p>\n<ul><li>departing from an airport located in the territory of a Member State to which the Treaty applies;\n<dl><dd>The protection accorded to passengers departing from or to an airport located in a Member State should be extended to those leaving an airport located in a third country for one situated in a Member State, when a Community carrier operates the flight and where a community carrier is defined as any carrier licensed to operate within that community.</dd></dl></li>\n<li>departing from an <a href="/wiki/European_Economic_Area#Ratification_of_the_EEA_Agreement" title="European Economic Area">EU/EEA member state</a>, or</li>\n<li>travelling to an EU/EEA member state on an airline based in an EU/EEA member state</li></ul>\n<p>if that person:\n</p>\n<ul><li>has a confirmed reservation on the flight, and</li>\n<li>arrived in time for check-in as indicated on the ticket or communication from the airline, or, if no time is so indicated, no less than 45 minutes prior to the scheduled departure time of the flight</li></ul>\n<p>or\n</p>\n<ul><li>has been transferred from the flight for which he/she held a reservation to some other flight</li></ul>\n<p>unless\n</p>\n<ul><li>the passenger is travelling on a free or discounted ticket not available to the general public, other than a ticket obtained from a <a href="/wiki/Frequent_flyer" class="mw-redirect" title="Frequent flyer">frequent flyer</a> programme.</li></ul>\n<p>It does not apply to <a href="/wiki/Helicopter" title="Helicopter">helicopter</a> flights, to any flight not operated by a fixed-wing <a href="/wiki/Aircraft" title="Aircraft">aircraft</a>, nor to flights from <a href="/wiki/Gibraltar_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Gibraltar Airport">Gibraltar Airport</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-261_2004_arts_3_and_4_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-261_2004_arts_3_and_4-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> While Switzerland and Norway are not members of the EU, the regulation does apply to flights to and from these countries as if they were member states under bilateral agreements.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Denied_boarding">Denied boarding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flight_Compensation_Regulation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Denied boarding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Before denying passengers boarding involuntarily, the airline is required to first seek volunteers to give up their reservation in return for whatever benefit is negotiated between the airline and the volunteers. Irrespective of such negotiation, such volunteers are also entitled to reimbursement or rerouting.\n</p><p>If insufficient volunteers are obtained, the airline may then proceed to involuntarily deny passengers the right to board their flight. All passengers so denied must be offered all three types of compensation and assistance.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cancellation">Cancellation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flight_Compensation_Regulation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Cancellation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<table class="box-Unreferenced_section plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Unreferenced" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><a href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" decoding="async" width="50" height="39" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="512" data-file-height="399" /></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>does not <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">cite</a> any <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">sources</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Flight_Compensation_Regulation&amp;action=edit">improve this section</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Referencing_for_beginners" title="Help:Referencing for beginners">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. Unsourced material may be challenged and <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability#Burden_of_evidence" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">removed</a>.</span>  <small class="date-container"><i>(<span class="date">December 2015</span>)</i></small><small class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p>If a flight is cancelled, passengers are automatically entitled to their choice of\n</p>\n<ol><li>re-routing to the same destination at the earliest opportunity (under comparable conditions);</li>\n<li>later rerouting, at the passenger\'s convenience, to the same destination under comparable conditions (subject to seat availability); or</li>\n<li>a refund of the ticket as well as a return flight to the point of first departure, when relevant.</li></ol>\n<p>Any ticket refund is the price paid for the flight(s) not used, plus the cost of flights already flown in cases where the cancellation has made those flights of no purpose. Where applicable, passengers are also entitled to refreshments, communication and accommodation as described below. Where re-routing is to another airport serving the same destination, the airline must pay for onward transport to the original airport or to a close by destination agreed with the passenger. These choices, and the entitlement to refreshments, etc., apply to all cancellations, regardless of whether the circumstances are extraordinary or not.\n</p><p>It is unclear whether "the earliest opportunity" requires airlines to endorse a ticket onto another carrier.\n</p><p>The airline is also required to pay cash compensation as described below, unless one of the following conditions applies:\n</p>\n<ul><li>the airline notifies the passengers at least two weeks prior to departure</li>\n<li>the airline notifies the passengers between one and two weeks prior to departure, and re-routes passengers so that they can:\n<ul><li>depart no more than two hours earlier than scheduled, and</li>\n<li>arrive no more than four hours later than scheduled</li></ul></li>\n<li>the airline notifies the passengers less than one week prior to departure, and re-routes passengers so that they can:\n<ul><li>depart no more than one hour earlier than scheduled, and</li>\n<li>arrive no more than two hours later than scheduled</li></ul></li>\n<li>the cancellation was caused by extraordinary circumstances that could not have been avoided by any reasonable measure.</li></ul>\n<p>The airline must also provide an explanation to passengers of alternative transport.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Flight_types">Flight types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flight_Compensation_Regulation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Flight types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The requirements for an entitlement to compensation and the specific amount owed depend on the length of a flight, whereas the relevant distance is determined according to the <a href="/wiki/Great-circle_distance" title="Great-circle distance">great circle</a> method. The Flight Compensation Regulation differentiates between three types of flights: \n</p>\n<ol><li>Flights of less than 1,500&#160;km (930&#160;mi) in distance;</li>\n<li>Flights within the EU of greater than 1,500&#160;km (930&#160;mi) in distance, or any other flight of greater than 1,500&#160;km (930&#160;mi) but less than 3,500&#160;km (2,200&#160;mi) in distance;</li>\n<li>Flights not within the EU of greater than 3,500&#160;km (2,200&#160;mi) in distance.</li></ol>\n<p><i>Note: In the rest of this article, types 1, 2 and 3 are used to refer to the above thresholds.</i>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Delays">Delays</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flight_Compensation_Regulation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Delays">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Passengers are entitled to refreshments and communication if the expected delay of the arrival exceeds:<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<ul><li>two hours, in the case of a type 1 flight,</li>\n<li>three hours, in the case of a type 2 flight, or</li>\n<li>four hours, in the case of a type 3 flight.</li></ul>\n<p>Furthermore, if the flight is expected to depart on the day after the original scheduled departure time, passengers are entitled to accommodation.\n</p><p>If a flight is delayed by five hours, passengers are additionally entitled to abandon their journey and receive a refund for all unused tickets. They may also ask for a refund on tickets used already if the flight no longer serves any purpose in relation to their original travel plan, and, if relevant, a flight back to their original point of departure at the earliest opportunity.\n</p><p>Finally, various court decisions (see below) have established that a passenger experiencing a loss of time equivalent to three hours or more at the final destination is entitled to compensation as though their flight had been cancelled.\n</p><p>Flight delay is based on the scheduled arrival time. This is defined as when the doors are opened on the plane and not when it lands (as some airlines may claim).<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In case of an arrival delay of more than 3 hours, passengers are entitled to cash compensation, unless the delay is caused by extraordinary circumstances.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In October 2017, an <a href="/wiki/European_Court_of_Justice#Appeals_on_points_of_law" title="European Court of Justice">EU Court of Appeal</a> confirmed the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">UK</a> <a href="/wiki/Civil_Aviation_Authority_(United_Kingdom)" title="Civil Aviation Authority (United Kingdom)">CAA</a>\'s interpretation that the final destination must be included in the total delay. This means that, if the passenger misses a connection outside the EU and ends up with a delay longer than the times indicated above, even if the delay on the flight leaving the EU was inferior to the aforementioned times, the total delay will be used and not only the delay on leaving the EU.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Compensation_and_assistance">Compensation and assistance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flight_Compensation_Regulation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Compensation and assistance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<table class="box-Unreferenced_section plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Unreferenced" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td But if rerouting only delays the passenger by less than these thresholds, half of the specified amounts are payable as compensation.\n</p><p>Said cash payments merely serve to compensate a traveller\'s inconvenience and do not replace or form a part of any potential reimbursements for unused tickets, trips in vain, additional transport costs, meals and accommodation.\n</p><p>Airlines are not obliged to provide cash compensation in the case of extraordinary circumstances which could not have been avoided even if the airline took all reasonable precautions, according to Article 5, Paragraph 3.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rerouting_or_refunding">Rerouting or refunding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flight_Compensation_Regulation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Rerouting or refunding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Rerouting or refunding is, at the passenger\'s choice, one of the following three reimbursements:\n</p>\n<ol><li>Repayment of the cost of unused flight tickets, and for used tickets where the flight(s) taken no longer serve(s) any purpose in relation to the passenger\'s original travel plan, and where applicable, a flight back to the original point of departure at the earliest opportunity</li>\n<li>Rerouting under similar conditions to the intended final destination at the earliest opportunity</li>\n<li>Rerouting under similar conditions to the intended final destination at the passenger\'s leisure, subject to the availability of seats.</li></ol>\n<p>If a passenger\'s destination is an airport at a city with multiple airports and rerouting results in the passenger being taken to another of those airports, the airline must also pay for transport for the passenger to the original intended airport or an agreed nearby destination.\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Refreshments.2C_communication_and_accommodation"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Refreshments,_communication_and_accommodation">Refreshments, communication and accommodation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flight_Compensation_Regulation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Refreshments, communication and accommodation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>When passengers become entitled to assistance, they must be offered, free of charge,\n</p>\n<ul><li>Meals and refreshments in proportion to the waiting time</li>\n<li>Two telephone calls, fax or telex messages, or emails</li>\n<li>Hotel accommodation and transport between the airport and the hotel, if a stay of one or more nights, or a stay additional to that intended by the passenger becomes necessary</li></ul>\n<p>In the case of a delay, the airline may withdraw or abrogate these entitlements if offering them would delay the flight further.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Upgrades_and_downgrades">Upgrades and downgrades</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flight_Compensation_Regulation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Upgrades and downgrades">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<table class="box-Unreferenced_section plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Unreferenced" For the purpose of this condition, flights to the <a href="/wiki/DOM-TOM" class="mw-redirect" title="DOM-TOM">French overseas departments</a> are not considered to be within the European Union. It is unclear whether the refund relates to the price of the entire ticket, or the segment on which the passenger was downgraded.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Method_of_refund">Method of refund</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flight_Compensation_Regulation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Method of refund">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Refunds and compensations payable under this regulation may be paid in <a href="/wiki/Cash" title="Cash">cash</a>, by electronic <a href="/wiki/Bank_transfer" class="mw-redirect" title="Bank transfer">bank transfer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bank_draft" class="mw-redirect" title="Bank draft">bank draft</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Cheque" title="Cheque">cheque</a>. With the signed agreement of a passenger, they may also be paid in travel vouchers or other services.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Obligation_to_notify_passengers">Obligation to notify passengers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flight_Compensation_Regulation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Obligation to notify passengers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Airlines are obliged to display a notice at their check-in counters stating:\n</p>\n<dl><dd>"<i>If you are denied boarding or if your flight is cancelled or delayed for at least two hours, ask at the check-in counter or boarding gate for the text stating your rights, particularly with regard to compensation and assistance.</i>"</dd></dl>\n<p>Additionally, when an airline cancels a flight, denies a person boarding, or incurs a delay exceeding two hours to a flight, it is obliged to provide each passenger affected with a written notice setting out their rights under the regulation, and the contact details of the national body tasked with enforcing the regulation.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Relevant_court_cases">Relevant court cases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flight_Compensation_Regulation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Relevant court cases">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In the case <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wallentin-Hermann_v_Alitalia%E2%80%94Linee_Aeree_Italiane_SpA&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Wallentin-Hermann v Alitalia\xe2\x80\x94Linee Aeree Italiane SpA (page does not exist)">Wallentin-Hermann v Alitalia\xe2\x80\x94Linee Aeree Italiane SpA</a></i> (Case C-549/07) of 22 December 2008,<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/European_Court_of_Justice" title="European Court of Justice">European Court of Justice</a> in <a href="/wiki/Luxembourg" title="Luxembourg">Luxembourg</a> ruled on the interpretation of Article 5 of the regulation relating to cancellations, specifically paragraph 3 which states:\n</p>\n<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r996844942">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>An operating air carrier shall not be obliged to pay compensation in accordance with Article 7, if it can prove that the cancellation is caused by extraordinary circumstances which could not have been avoided even if all reasonable measures had been taken.</p></blockquote>\n<p>The Court agreed with Wallentin-Hermann that any technical issues during aircraft maintenance don\'t constitute "extraordinary circumstances" that would allow airlines to avoid paying passengers compensation for canceled flights. \nThis case therefore closed the loophole which had allowed the airlines to abuse passengers by frivolous interpretation of "technical or extraordinary circumstances"; it  further defined the phrase and limited its exploitation. The definition of "technical and/or extraordinary circumstances" by the Court now stands firm and solid: any carrier must prove that the alleged mechanical problem leading to the cancellation was "beyond its actual control", the court affirmed in a statement. In its judgment, the Fourth Chamber of the Court of Justice held: \n</p>\n<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r996844942"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>Extraordinary circumstances\xe2\x80\x9d was not defined in the 2004 Regulation, but the phrase was to be interpreted narrowly since article 5(3) constituted a derogation from the principle, indicated in recitals 1 and 2 of the preamble, of protection of consumers, in as much as cancellation of flights caused serious inconvenience to passengers.</p></blockquote>\n<p>However, what actually lies within the concept of defining what is inside or outside of the "actual control of the air carrier" is not clear and is subject to litigation in many EU-states.\n</p><p>Furthermore, in the joined cases of <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sturgeon_v_Condor&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Sturgeon v Condor (page does not exist)">Sturgeon v Condor</a></i>, and Bock v Air France (C-402/07 and C-432/07),<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> the Fourth Chamber of the European Court of Justice held on 19 November 2009 that despite no express provision in the Regulation to compensate passengers for delay, passengers are now entitled to the compensation as set out in Article 7 for any delay in excess of three hours providing the air carrier cannot raise a defence of "extraordinary circumstances".\n</p>\n<blockquote>\n<p>"Articles 5, 6 and 7 of Regulation EC 261/2004 must be interpreted as meaning that passengers whose flights are delayed may be treated, for the purposes of the application of the right to compensation, as passengers whose flights are cancelled and they may thus rely on the right to compensation laid down in Article 7 of the regulation where they suffer, on account of a flight delay, a loss of time equal to or in excess of three hours, that is, where they reach their final destination three hours or more after the arrival time originally scheduled by the air carrier."<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n</blockquote>\n<p>The fourth Chamber also ruled that under the definition of "extraordinary circumstances", technical faults within an aircraft should not be included and therefore an air carrier cannot rely on a technical fault within an aircraft as a defence from a valid claim under the Regulation, "unless that problem stems from events which, by their nature or origin, are not inherent in the normal exercise of the activity of the air carrier concerned and are beyond its actual control".<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Various passenger rights groups reported the case and encouraged passengers to bring claims against airlines in the event of a delay of over three hours.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Sturgeon ruling was reconfirmed in a ruling of the European Court of Justice on 23 October 2012 in <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nelson_v_Deutsche_Lufthansa_AG&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Nelson v Deutsche Lufthansa AG (page does not exist)">Nelson v Deutsche Lufthansa AG</a></i> and <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=R_(TUI_Travel,_British_Airways,_easyjet_and_IATA)_v_Civil_Aviation_Authority&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="R (TUI Travel, British Airways, easyjet and IATA) v Civil Aviation Authority (page does not exist)">R (TUI Travel, British Airways, easyjet and IATA) v Civil Aviation Authority</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In the case of <i>Denise McDonagh v Ryanair Ltd</i> (C-12/11), the Third Chamber of the European Court of Justice ruled that natural disasters such as the eruption of the Icelandic volcano <a href="/wiki/Eyjafjallaj%C3%B6kull" title="Eyjafjallaj\xc3\xb6kull">Eyjafjallaj\xc3\xb6kull</a> and the subsequent <a href="/wiki/2010_eruptions_of_Eyjafjallaj%C3%B6kull" title="2010 eruptions of Eyjafjallaj\xc3\xb6kull">cloud of volcanic ash in 2010</a>, which shut down most European air traffic, do constitute "extraordinary circumstances" that release air carriers from the obligation to pay compensation, but that there is no such category as "super-extraordinary circumstances" that would release them from the obligation to provide care. According to the court\'s ruling, air carriers continued to have an obligation of care towards passengers under Art. 5 and 8 of the regulation during the week-long shutdown of European airspace, and this obligation does not have a temporal or monetary limit.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In the case of <i>Jet2 vs. Huzar</i>, the English Court of Appeal ruled on 11 June 2014 that "ordinary technical problems that cause flight disruption, such as component failure and general wear and tear, should not be considered \xe2\x80\x9cextraordinary circumstances\xe2\x80\x9d".<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Therefore, general technical faults found during routine maintenance checks before departure will generally not be considered "extraordinary circumstances".\n</p><p>\nOn 4 September 2014, in the case of <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Germanwings_GmbH_v._Ronny_Henning&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Germanwings GmbH v. Ronny Henning (page does not exist)">Germanwings GmbH v. Ronny Henning</a></i> (C-452/13),<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> the Ninth Chamber of the European Court of Justice ruled that <link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r996844942"/></p><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>the concept of \xe2\x80\x98arrival time\xe2\x80\x99, which is used to determine the length of the delay to which passengers on a flight have been subject, refers to the time at which at least one of the doors of the aircraft is opened, the assumption being that, at that moment, the passengers are permitted to leave the aircraft.</p></blockquote>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Germanwings" title="Germanwings">Germanwings</a> initially refused to pay the passenger compensation, arguing the delay was 2 hours and 58 minutes when the plane touched the ground.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In September 2015, the Court of Justice of the European Union judged, regarding <a href="/w/index.php?title=Case_C-257/14&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Case C-257/14 (page does not exist)">Case C-257/14</a>:<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<dl><dd>Even in the event of a flight cancellation on account of unforeseen technical problems, air carriers are required to compensate passengers.</dd></dl>\n<dl><dd>However, certain technical problems resulting, in particular, from hidden manufacturing defects</dd></dl>\n<p>affecting the safety of flights or acts of sabotage or terrorism may exempt air carriers from their obligation to pay compensation.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Intermediaries">Intermediaries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flight_Compensation_Regulation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Intermediaries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The general difficulties claiming from airlines directly has led to the rise of online intermediaries that operate on a "no-win, no fee" basis <a href="/wiki/Contingency_fee" class="mw-redirect" title="Contingency fee">contingency fee</a>. All help filing claims against airlines, including going to court if necessary.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In response, airlines have repeatedly criticised such intermediaries for forcing more costs on the airline industry, which then passes extra costs on to passengers in the form of increased ticket prices. For example, in response to the added pressure caused by the increase in EU 261 claims in recent years, in 2011 <a href="/wiki/Ryanair" title="Ryanair">Ryanair</a> put in place a \xe2\x82\xac2.00 surcharge per ticket to compensate for its additional costs.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> In 2013 it then increased this levy to \xe2\x82\xac2.50.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Brexit_and_British_consumers">Brexit and British consumers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flight_Compensation_Regulation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Brexit and British consumers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In preparing for <a href="/wiki/Brexit" title="Brexit">Brexit</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Government of the United Kingdom">UK Government</a> announced it would convert directly-applicable EU laws into UK law in its Great Repeal Bill White Paper as early as 2018.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> Consequently, the <a href="/wiki/European_Union_(Withdrawal)_Act_2018" title="European Union (Withdrawal) Act 2018">European Union (Withdrawal) Act 2018</a>, which took effect on 30 December 2020, retained European statutes such as Regulation (EC) No 261/2004 that applied in the UK prior to its exit. This means that the Flight Compensation Regulation has been be ported over to the UK\'s domestic law by the end of the transition period. Thus, while Regulation (EC) No 261/2004 itself no longer applies to flights departing in the UK as of 1 January 2021,<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> its new equivalent in British law ("UK261") subjects British carriers and other airlines not headquartered in Europe to similar provisions on those routes.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>However, the Repeal Bill does state that the regulation will continue to apply \xe2\x80\x98until legislators in the UK decide otherwise\xe2\x80\x99. Effectively, this allows the <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Parliament of the United Kingdom">British parliament</a> to amend the regulation or to drop it from UK law entirely at a later date. Likewise, while the European Union (Withdrawal) Act 2018 has converted the ECJ\'s jurisdiction up until the end of 2020 into British case law, British courts may deviate from these previous rulings under the same principles that guide their treatment of earlier decisions by domestic judges.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Future_developments">Future developments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flight_Compensation_Regulation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Future developments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In 2013 the European Commission proposed a number of revisions to the regulation,<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> which have not yet been adopted as of 2021:\n</p>\n<ul><li>Airlines will be obliged to inform passengers about flight delays within 30 minutes after the scheduled departure time</li>\n<li>The definition of "extraordinary circumstances" will be further clarified to include natural disasters or air traffic control strikes, and to exclude technical problems identified during routine maintenance</li>\n<li>With respect to compensation for long delays, which was never explicitly stated in the original regulation but added by the European Court of Justice, a threshold of five hours for type 1 and 2 flights, 9 hours for type 3 flights up to 6,000&#160;km, and 12 hours for longer flights will be established.</li>\n<li>The right to refreshments and communication will become applicable after two hours irrespective of the length of flight.</li>\n<li>An explicit right to refreshments, communication, accommodation, and assistance will be added for circumstances where passengers experience a delay at their final destinations due to the late arrival of connecting flights.</li>\n<li>Airlines will be required to reroute passengers on another carrier if they cannot accommodate them themselves within 12 hours after the scheduled departure time.</li>\n<li>Airlines will be obliged to give passengers access to toilets, drinking water, air conditioning, and medical assistance after a tarmac delay of one hour or more and refreshments after two hours, and to permit passengers to disembark after a tarmac delay of five hours or more.</li>\n<li>Rescheduling of a flight within two weeks before its departure time will give rise to the same rights as a cancellation.</li>\n<li>Passengers will be entitled to corrections of misspelt names free of charge except within 48 hours of departure.</li>\n<li>Airlines will be prohibited from cancelling the return leg of a ticket for reason only that a passenger has failed to use the outbound leg.</li>\n<li>Airlines will be required to give passengers full compensation for mobility equipment lost or damaged during a flight, provided its value was declared at check-in. No charge may be applied for this.</li>\n<li>Forms must be provided at the airport to customers wishing to submit complaints, and these must be accepted as valid claims under the regulation.</li>\n<li>Airlines will be required to accept small musical instruments as cabin baggage and publish the conditions under which they will accept larger instruments.</li>\n<li>Airlines will be obliged to inform passengers of cabin and checked baggage limitations clearly at the time of booking and at airports.</li>\n<li>Airlines will not be required to pay for accommodation of passengers for more than three nights in the event of major disruptions out of their control causing delays or cancellations, except for passengers with reduced mobility, passengers accompanying them, unaccompanied minors, pregnant women, and passengers with specific medical needs.
