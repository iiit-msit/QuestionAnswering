Shareholders may be referred to as members of a corporation. By law, a person is not a shareholder in a corporation until their name and other details are entered in the corporation\'s register of shareholders or members.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The influence of a shareholder on the business is determined by the shareholding percentage owned. Shareholders of a corporation are legally separate from the corporation itself. They are generally not liable for the debts of the corporation and the shareholders\' liability for company debts are said to be limited to the unpaid share price unless if a shareholder has offered guarantees. The corporation is not required to record the beneficial ownership of a shareholding, only the owner as recorded on the register. When more than one person are on the record as owners of a shareholding, the first one on the record is taken to have control of the shareholding, and all correspondence and communication by the company will be with that person.\n</p><p>Shareholders may have acquired their shares in the <a href="/wiki/Primary_market" title="Primary market">primary market</a> by subscribing to the <a href="/wiki/Initial_public_offering" title="Initial public offering">IPOs</a> and thus provided <a href="/wiki/Capital_(finance)" class="mw-redirect" title="Capital (finance)">capital</a> to the corporation. However, most shareholders acquire shares in the <a href="/wiki/Secondary_market" title="Secondary market">secondary market</a> and provided no capital directly to the corporation. Shareholders may be granted special privileges depending on a <a href="/wiki/Share_class" title="Share class">share class</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Board_of_directors" title="Board of directors">board of directors</a> of a corporation generally governs a corporation for the benefit of shareholders.\n</p><p>Shareholders are considered by some to be a <a href="/wiki/Subset" title="Subset">subset</a> of <a href="/wiki/Stakeholder_(corporate)" title="Stakeholder (corporate)">stakeholders</a>, which may include anyone who has a direct or indirect interest in the <a href="/wiki/Business_entity" class="mw-redirect" title="Business entity">business entity</a>. For example, employees, suppliers, customers, the community, etc., are typically considered stakeholders because they contribute value or are impacted by the corporation.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shareholder&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>A <i>beneficial shareholder</i> is the person that has the economic benefit of ownership of the shares, while a <i>nominee shareholder</i> is the person who is on the corporation\'s register as the owner while being in fact acting for the benefit and at the direction of the beneficiary, whether disclosed or not.\n</p><p>Primarily, there are two types of shareholders.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Common_shareholders">Common shareholders</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shareholder&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Common shareholders">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>An individual or an institution can be a common shareholder who owns common shares within a company. This type of shareholding is more common. Common shareholders have the right to influence decisions concerning the company and can file class action lawsuits in case any wrongdoing happens.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Preferred_shareholders">Preferred shareholders</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shareholder&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Preferred shareholders">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In this, the shareholder is paid a fixed sum of dividend even before the common shareholders and they have no voting rights within the company.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rights">Rights</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shareholder&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Rights">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Subject to the applicable laws, the rules of the corporation and any <a href="/wiki/Shareholders%27_agreement" title="Shareholders&#39; agreement">shareholders\' agreement</a>, shareholders may have the right:\n</p>\n<ul><li>To sell their shares.<sup id="cite_ref-velasco_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-velasco-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>To vote on the directors nominated by the board of directors.<sup id="cite_ref-velasco_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-velasco-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>To nominate directors (although this is very difficult in practice because of minority protections) and propose <a href="/wiki/Shareholder_resolution" title="Shareholder resolution">shareholder resolutions</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-velasco_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-velasco-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>To vote on mergers and changes to the corporate charter.<sup id="cite_ref-velasco_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-velasco-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>To <a href="/wiki/Dividend" title="Dividend">dividends</a> if they are declared.<sup id="cite_ref-velasco_3-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-velasco-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>To access certain information; for publicly traded companies, this information is normally publicly available.<sup id="cite_ref-velasco_3-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-velasco-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>To sue the company for violation of fiduciary duty.<sup id="cite_ref-velasco_3-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-velasco-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>To purchase new shares issued by the company.</li>\n<li>To vote on &amp; file file <a href="/wiki/Shareholder_resolution" title="Shareholder resolution">shareholder resolutions</a>.</li>\n<li>To vote on management proposals.</li>\n<li>To what <a href="/wiki/Asset" title="Asset">assets</a> remain after a <a href="/wiki/Liquidation" title="Liquidation">liquidation</a>.</li></ul>\n<p>The above-mentioned rights can be generally classified into (1) cash-flow rights and (2) voting rights. While the value of shares is mainly driven by the cash-flow rights that they carry ("<a href="/wiki/Cash_is_king" title="Cash is king">cash is king</a>"), voting rights can also be valuable. The value of shareholders\' cash-flow rights can be computed by discounting future free cash flows. The value of shareholders\' voting rights can be computed by four methods:\n</p>\n<ul><li>The difference between voting shares and non-voting shares (dual-class approach).<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>The difference between the price paid in a block-trade transaction and the subsequent price paid in a smaller transaction on exchanges (block-trade approach).<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>The implied voting value obtained from option prices.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>The excess lending fee over voting events.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
