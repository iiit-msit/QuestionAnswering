The most common example of an inchoate offense is "<a href="/wiki/Attempt" title="Attempt">attempt</a>". "Inchoate offense" has been defined as the following: "Conduct deemed criminal without actual harm being done, provided that the harm that would have occurred is one the law tries to prevent."<sup class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Inchoate_offense&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Intent">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Intention_(criminal_law)" title="Intention (criminal law)">Intention (criminal law)</a></div>\n<p>Every inchoate crime or offense must have the <i><a href="/wiki/Mens_rea" title="Mens rea">mens rea</a></i> of intent or of <a href="/wiki/Recklessness_(law)" title="Recklessness (law)">recklessness</a>, typically intent.  Absent a specific law, an inchoate offense requires that the defendant have the specific intent to commit the underlying crime. For example, for a defendant to be guilty of the inchoate crime of <a href="/wiki/Solicitation_of_murder" class="mw-redirect" title="Solicitation of murder">solicitation of murder</a>, he or she must have intended for a person to die.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Attempt" title="Attempt">Attempt</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Conspiracy_(criminal)" title="Conspiracy (criminal)">conspiracy</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Solicitation" title="Solicitation">solicitation</a><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> all require <i>mens rea</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>On the other hand, committing an offence under the US <a href="/wiki/Racketeer_Influenced_and_Corrupt_Organizations_Act" title="Racketeer Influenced and Corrupt Organizations Act">Racketeer Influenced and Corrupt Organizations Act</a> merely requires "knowing",<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> that is, recklessness.  Facilitation also requires "believing",<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> yet another way of saying reckless.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>Intent may be distinguished from recklessness and <a href="/wiki/Criminal_negligence" title="Criminal negligence">criminal negligence</a> as a higher <i>mens rea</i>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Proof_of_intent">Proof of intent</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Inchoate_offense&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Proof of intent">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Specific intent may be inferred from circumstances.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>  It may be proven by the doctrine of "dangerous proximity", while the <a href="/wiki/Model_Penal_Code" title="Model Penal Code">Model Penal Code</a> requires a "substantial step in a course of conduct".<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Merger_doctrine">Merger doctrine</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Inchoate_offense&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Merger doctrine">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Lesser_included_offense" title="Lesser included offense">Lesser included offense</a></div>\n<p>The doctrine of merger has been abandoned in many jurisdictions in cases involving a conspiracy, allowing an accused to be convicted of both conspiracy and the principal offense.  However, an accused cannot be convicted of either attempt or solicitation and the principal offense.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Defenses">Defenses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Inchoate_offense&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Defenses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>A number of  defences are possible to the charge of an inchoate offense, depending on the jurisdiction and the nature of the offense.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Impossibility">Impossibility</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Inchoate_offense&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Impossibility">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Impossibility_defense" title="Impossibility defense">Impossibility defense</a></div>\n<p>Impossibility is no defence to the crime of attempt where the conditions creating the impossibility are unknown to the actor.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>Originally at common law, impossibility was a complete defence;<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>  as it was under French law at one point.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>  Indeed, the ruling in <i>Collins\'s Case</i> L. and C. 471 was that an offender cannot be guilty of an attempt to steal his own umbrella when he mistakenly believes that it belongs to another.  Although the "moral guilt" for the attempt and the actual crime were the same, there was a distinction between the harm caused by a theft and the harmlessness of an impossible act.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>  This principle was directly overruled in England with the rulings <i>R v Ring</i> and <i>R v. Brown</i><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>  The example from <i>R v Brown</i> of an attempt to steal from an empty pocket is now a classic example of illustrating the point that impossibility is no defense to the crime of attempt when the conditions creating the impossibility are unknown to the actor.  This principle has been codified in the <a href="/wiki/Model_Penal_Code" title="Model Penal Code">Model Penal Code</a>:\n</p>\n<blockquote>\n<p>A person is guilty of an attempt to commit a crime if, acting with the kind of culpability otherwise required for commission of the crime he: purposely engages in conduct which would constitute the crime <i>if the attendant circumstances were as he believes them to be.</i> MPC \xc2\xa7 5.01 (1)(a) (emphasis added).\n</p>\n</blockquote>\n<p>Consequently, the principle is universal in the United States either in Model Penal Code jurisdictions (40 states) or those remaining common law jurisdictions influenced by the reasoning in <i>R v Brown</i>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>Other cases that illustrate the case law for <a href="/wiki/Impossibility_defence" class="mw-redirect" title="Impossibility defence">impossibility defences</a> are <i><a href="/wiki/People_v._Lee_Kong" title="People v. Lee Kong">People v. Lee Kong</a></i> (CA, 1892), <i><a href="/wiki/State_v._Mitchell" title="State v. Mitchell">State v. Mitchell</a></i> (MO, 1902), and <i><a href="/wiki/United_States_v._Thomas_(1962)" title="United States v. Thomas (1962)">United States v. Thomas (1962)</a></i>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Abandonment">Abandonment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Inchoate_offense&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Abandonment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>A defendant may plead and prove, as an affirmative defense, that they:\n</p>\n<ol><li>Stopped all actions in furtherance of the crime or conspiracy</li>\n<li>Tried to stop the crime as it was ongoing</li>\n<li>Tried to convince the co-conspirators to halt such actions, or reported the crime to the police or other authorities</li></ol>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Disputes">Disputes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Inchoate_offense&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Disputes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Burglaries_as_inchoate_crimes">Burglaries as inchoate crimes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Inchoate_offense&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Burglaries as inchoate crimes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Burglary" title="Burglary">Burglary</a></div>\n<p>There is some scholarly treatment of burglaries in <a href="/wiki/Law_of_the_United_States" title="Law of the United States">American law</a> as inchoate crimes, but this is in dispute.  According to scholar <a href="/wiki/Frank_Schmalleger" title="Frank Schmalleger">Frank Schmalleger</a>, burglaries "are actually inchoate crimes in disguise."<sup id="cite_ref-Schmalleger_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Schmalleger-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Other scholars warn about the consequences of such a theory:\n</p>\n<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r996844942">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>Burglary, as a preliminary step to another crime, can be seen as an inchoate, or incomplete, offense.  As it disrupts the security of persons in their homes and in regard to their personal property, however, it is complete as soon as the intrusion is made.  This dual nature is at the heart of a debate about whether the crime of burglary ought to be abolished, leaving its elements to be covered by attempt or as aggravating circumstances to other crimes, or retained and the grading schemes reformed to reflect the seriousness of the individual offense.</p><div class="templatequotecite">\xe2\x80\x94&#8201;<cite>McCord and McCord.<sup id="cite_ref-McCord_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McCord-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup></cite></div></blockquote>\n<p>Certainly, <i>possession of burglary tools</i>, in those jurisdictions that criminalize that activity, creates an inchoate crime (<i><a href="/wiki/Going_equipped" class="mw-redirect" title="Going equipped">going equipped</a></i> in the UK).<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>  It is clear that:\n</p>\n<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r996844942"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>In effect piling an inchoate crime onto an inchoate crime, the possession of burglary tools with the intent to use them in a burglary is a serious offense, a felony in some jurisdictions.  Gloves that a defendant was trying to shake off as he ran from the site of a burglary were identified as burglar\'s tools in <i>Green v. State</i> (Fla. App. 1991).</p><div class="templatequotecite">\xe2\x80\x94&#8201;<cite>McCord and McCord.<sup id="cite_ref-McCord_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McCord-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup></cite></div></blockquote>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Examples">Examples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Inchoate_offense&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Examples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Examples of inchoate offenses include <a href="/wiki/Conspiracy_(crime)" class="mw-redirect" title="Conspiracy (crime)">conspiracy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Solicitation" title="Solicitation">solicitation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Criminal_facilitation" class="mw-redirect" title="Criminal facilitation">facilitation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Misprision_of_felony" title="Misprision of felony">misprision of felony</a> (and <a href="/wiki/Misprision" title="Misprision">misprision</a> generally), <a href="/wiki/Organized_crime" title="Organized crime">organized crime</a>, <a href="/wiki/Racketeer_Influenced_and_Corrupt_Organizations_Act" title="Racketeer Influenced and Corrupt Organizations Act">Racketeer Influenced and Corrupt Organizations Act</a> (RICO), and <a href="/wiki/Attempt" title="Attempt">attempt</a>, as well as some <a href="/wiki/Public_health" title="Public health">public health</a> crimes;
