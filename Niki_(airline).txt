href="http://www.flyniki.com">flyniki.com</a></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p><b>Niki</b> (also styled as <b>NIKI</b> or <b>flyNiki</b>, legally <i>NIKI Luftfahrt <a href="/wiki/GmbH" class="mw-redirect" title="GmbH">GmbH</a></i>) was<sup id="cite_ref-austrianaviation_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-austrianaviation-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> an <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austrian</a> <a href="/wiki/Low-cost_airline" class="mw-redirect" title="Low-cost airline">low-cost airline</a> headquartered in <i>Office Park I</i> at <a href="/wiki/Vienna_International_Airport" title="Vienna International Airport">Vienna International Airport</a> in <a href="/wiki/Schwechat" title="Schwechat">Schwechat</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> It operated scheduled and charter services to European and North African leisure destinations from <a href="/wiki/Vienna" title="Vienna">Vienna</a>, <a href="/wiki/Salzburg" title="Salzburg">Salzburg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Graz" title="Graz">Graz</a> and <a href="/wiki/Innsbruck" title="Innsbruck">Innsbruck</a> and also started services from several German airports in March 2017. Niki had a variety of owners, including two stints of ownership under <a href="/wiki/Niki_Lauda" title="Niki Lauda">Niki Lauda</a>. In January 2018, the airline was acquired by <a href="/wiki/Lauda_(airline)" title="Lauda (airline)">Lauda</a>, an airline that also had ties to Niki Lauda.<sup data-file-height="1067" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Niki_Airbus_A321-231_OE-LOS_(28593412252).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Niki <a href="/wiki/Airbus_A321-200" class="mw-redirect" title="Airbus A321-200">Airbus A321-200</a> wearing the airline\'s first livery</div></div></div>\n<p>In 2003, <a href="/wiki/Niki_Lauda" title="Niki Lauda">Niki Lauda</a>, ex-<a href="/wiki/Formula_One" title="Formula One">Grand Prix</a> driver and founder of <a href="/wiki/Lauda_Air" title="Lauda Air">Lauda Air</a>, acquired the former <a href="/wiki/Aero_Lloyd_Austria" class="mw-redirect" title="Aero Lloyd Austria">Aero Lloyd Austria</a> operation. The airline started operations on 28 November 2003.\n</p><p>Although Niki was often described as "low-cost", it provided full services, such as free refreshments, newspapers, and baggage. It offered a sandwich or a cold snack, soft drinks, juice, tea, and coffee on all short-haul flights as well as on charter routes to Greece, Tunisia, and Turkey. On mid-haul charter flights to the Canary Islands and Egypt, all passengers were offered a warm snack. Also, gourmet menus from <a href="/wiki/Demel" title="Demel">Demel</a> could be ordered before the flight. There was also a <a href="/wiki/Buy_on_board" title="Buy on board">buy on board</a> service.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Development_as_an_Air_Berlin_subsidiary">Development as an Air Berlin subsidiary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Niki_(airline)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Development as an Air Berlin subsidiary">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Airbus_A321-211,_Niki_JP6816481.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Airbus_A321-211%2C_Niki_JP6816481.jpg/220px-Airbus_A321-211%2C_Niki_JP6816481.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="149" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Airbus_A321-211%2C_Niki_JP6816481.jpg/330px-Airbus_A321-211%2C_Niki_JP6816481.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Airbus_A321-211%2C_Niki_JP6816481.jpg/440px-Airbus_A321-211%2C_Niki_JP6816481.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1200" data-file-height="810" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Airbus_A321-211,_Niki_JP6816481.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Niki <a href="/wiki/Airbus_A321-200" class="mw-redirect" title="Airbus A321-200">Airbus A321-200</a> in the livery used until 2012</div></div></div>\n<p>On 9 January 2004, Niki announced cooperation with <a href="/wiki/Air_Berlin" title="Air Berlin">Air Berlin</a>. In late 2011, Niki merged completely with <a href="/wiki/Air_Berlin" title="Air Berlin">Air Berlin</a>, sharing operations, booking systems, and aircraft. On 20 March 2012, Niki became an "affiliate member" of <a href="/wiki/Oneworld" title="Oneworld">Oneworld</a> as a subsidiary company of <a href="/wiki/Air_Berlin" title="Air Berlin">Air Berlin</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Starting in late 2012, Niki successively repainted their aircraft in the <a href="/wiki/Air_Berlin" title="Air Berlin">Air Berlin</a> paint scheme, using the Niki logo only at the nose. The new paint scheme was applied during regular <a href="/wiki/Aircraft_maintenance_checks#C_Check" title="Aircraft maintenance checks">C checks</a>.\n</p><p>In October 2014, Niki announced the cancellation of their flights from Vienna to Copenhagen, Moscow, and Frankfurt due to decreasing demand. In exchange, new leisure routes will be started by summer 2015.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Also in spring 2015, Niki started to replace its fleet of <a href="/wiki/Embraer_190" class="mw-redirect" title="Embraer 190">Embraer 190s</a> with <a href="/wiki/Airbus_A319-100" class="mw-redirect" title="Airbus A319-100">Airbus A319-100s</a> from its parent, <a href="/wiki/Air_Berlin" title="Air Berlin">Air Berlin</a>, with the used Embraers being transferred to <a href="/wiki/Helvetic_Airways" title="Helvetic Airways">Helvetic Airways</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-e190_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-e190-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sale_to_Etihad_Airways">Sale to Etihad Airways</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Niki_(airline)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Sale to Etihad Airways">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>On 5 December 2016, parent company <a href="/wiki/Air_Berlin" title="Air Berlin">Air Berlin</a> announced plans to sell its entire 49 percent stake in Niki to its own minority owner <a href="/wiki/Etihad_Airways" title="Etihad Airways">Etihad Airways</a>. Etihad Airways itself would merge Niki into a new joint-venture to be created with the German leisure carrier <a href="/wiki/TUIfly" class="mw-redirect" title="TUIfly">TUIfly</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-nikisale_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nikisale-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-nikisale2_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nikisale2-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> At the same time, Niki and Air Berlin would exchange several aircraft to harmonize each fleet.<sup id="cite_ref-nikisale_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nikisale-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> It had also been announced that Niki would take over several routes to southern European, north African, and Turkish leisure destinations from Air Berlin as part of the new joint-venture.<sup id="cite_ref-nikisale2_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nikisale2-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> However, the sale never happened.\n</p><p>Also in December 2016, Niki announced the termination of its only medium-haul route, from Vienna to <a href="/wiki/Abu_Dhabi" title="Abu Dhabi">Abu Dhabi</a>, Etihad\'s home base, by March 2017.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Niki later also announced it plans to terminate all of its 12 routes from Vienna to non-leisure destinations such as Milan or Z\xc3\xbcrich by 30 January 2017.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> In January 2017, it was also announced that Niki will cease all operations to and from <a href="/wiki/Linz_Airport" title="Linz Airport">Linz Airport</a> at short notice due to the restructuring measures. The announcement was made after the 2017 schedule had already been made available for booking.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> For the 2017 season, Niki reduced the number of flights from Austria to 56 flights a week to 20 destinations, compared to 34 destinations in 2016.<sup id="cite_ref-2017changes_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2017changes-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Shortly after, it was announced that Niki would take over several Air Berlin leisure routes to and from Germany.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> It also has been announced that Niki\'s fleet will be switched over to <a href="/wiki/Airbus_A321" title="Airbus A321">Airbus A321s</a> only while the smaller A319s and A320s are handed over to Air Berlin.<sup id="cite_ref-nikisale_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nikisale-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> It has also been announced that <a href="/wiki/TUI_fly_Deutschland" title="TUI fly Deutschland">TUI fly Deutschland</a> will additionally start to operate 15 <a href="/wiki/Boeing_737" title="Boeing 737">Boeing 737s</a> currently wet-leased to <a href="/wiki/Air_Berlin" title="Air Berlin">Air Berlin</a> for Niki.<sup id="cite_ref-tui737_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tui737-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Failed_takeover_by_Lufthansa_and_IAG">Failed takeover by Lufthansa and IAG</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Niki_(airline)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Failed takeover by Lufthansa and IAG">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>On 17 August 2017, Niki\'s parent Air Berlin announced it was filing for <a href="/wiki/Bankruptcy" title="Bankruptcy">bankruptcy</a> but that all operations would continue until further notice. On 9 October 2017, Air Berlin told its staff that it would cease all remaining operations under its own brand name, but that Niki flights would continue after this date.\n</p><p>On 12 October 2017, it was announced that German <a href="/wiki/Lufthansa" title="Lufthansa">Lufthansa</a> would purchase some of the assets from Air Berlin which included Niki and operations would continue and jobs were to be saved.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> On 24 October 2017, it was announced that Niki was to be absorbed into Lufthansa\'s low-cost subsidiary <a href="/wiki/Eurowings_Europe" title="Eurowings Europe">Eurowings Europe</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> It was announced that Lufthansa plans to cease the Niki brand by the end of 2017 altogether.<sup id="cite_ref-LHtakeover_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LHtakeover-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>However, on 13 December 2017, Lufthansa pulled out of the agreement due to signs from the <a href="/wiki/European_Commission" title="European Commission">European Commission</a> that the merger would not be approved.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Niki declared <a href="/wiki/Bankruptcy" title="Bankruptcy">bankruptcy</a> and ceased all operations on 14 December 2017.<sup id="cite_ref-austrianaviation_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-austrianaviation-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The day after, former owner Niki Lauda expressed interest in repurchasing the airline named after him.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>On 29 December 2017, it was announced that <a href="/wiki/International_Airlines_Group" title="International Airlines Group">International Airlines Group</a> (IAG), parent company of <a href="/wiki/British_Airways" title="British Airways">British Airways</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iberia" class="mw-redirect" title="Iberia">Iberia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aer_Lingus" title="Aer Lingus">Aer Lingus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vueling" title="Vueling">Vueling</a>, would acquire for 36.5 million euros major parts of Niki including 15 <a href="/wiki/Airbus_A321" title="Airbus A321">Airbus A321</a> aircraft and traffic rights in <a href="/wiki/D%C3%BCsseldorf" title="D\xc3\xbcsseldorf">D\xc3\xbcsseldorf</a>, <a href="/wiki/Munich" title="Munich">Munich</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vienna" title="Vienna">Vienna</a>, <a href="/wiki/Z%C3%BCrich" title="Z\xc3\xbcrich">Z\xc3\xbcrich</a> and <a href="/wiki/Palma_de_Mallorca" title="Palma de Mallorca">Palma de Mallorca</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ch-aviationIAG_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ch-aviationIAG-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> IAG planned to establish a new Austrian subsidiary of Spanish low-cost carrier Vueling as a replacement for Niki.<sup id="cite_ref-ch-aviationIAG_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ch-aviationIAG-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> For this purpose, a new subsidiary of Vueling in Austria was to be founded and act independently. Of the approx. 1000 Niki employees, 740 were to be retained. On January 8, 2018, a Berlin administrative court ruled that only an Austrian court had the jurisdiction to handle the insolvency of Niki.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Subsequent_takeovers">Subsequent takeovers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Niki_(airline)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Subsequent takeovers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>On January 23, 2018, the Austrian Press Agency announced that Niki Lauda\'s company <a href="/wiki/Lauda_(airline)" title="Lauda (airline)">Lauda</a> had beaten IAG in the bid for Niki, and would take over the company.<sup id="cite_ref-The_News_&amp;_Observer_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_News_&amp;_Observer-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Less than two months later, <a href="/wiki/Ryanair" title="Ryanair">Ryanair</a> agreed to purchase a majority stake in Laudamotion,<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> initially acquiring almost 25 percent for less than 50 million euros ($61.7 million) with the intent to purchase another 50 percent at a later date. The deal was subsequently approved by the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> 11 planes from Niki are being operated by Laudamotion as of April 2018.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Destinations">Destinations</span><span href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Switzerland</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline"
