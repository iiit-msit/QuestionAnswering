Wanderer</caption><tbody><tr><th scope="row" class="infobox-label"><div style="display:inline-block; padding:0.1em 0;line-height:1.2em;">Type of site</div></th><td class="infobox-data"><a updated">1993-06-30</span>)</span></td></tr><tr><th scope="row" class="infobox-label">Current&#160;status</th><td class="infobox-data category">Closed</td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p>The <b>World Wide Web Wanderer</b>, also referred to as just the <b>Wanderer</b>, was a <a href="/wiki/Perl" title="Perl">Perl</a>-based <a href="/wiki/Web_crawler" title="Web crawler">web crawler</a> that was first deployed in June 1993 to measure the size of the <a href="/wiki/World_Wide_Web" title="World Wide Web">World Wide Web</a>.   The Wanderer was developed at the <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Institute_of_Technology" title="Massachusetts Institute of Technology">Massachusetts Institute of Technology</a> by Matthew Gray, who, as of 2017, has spent a decade as a software engineer at <a href="/wiki/Google" title="Google">Google</a>.  The crawler was used to generate an index called the <i>Wandex</i> later in 1993.  While the Wanderer was probably the first <a href="/wiki/Web_robot" class="mw-redirect" title="Web robot">web robot</a>, and, with its index, clearly had the potential to become a general-purpose <a href="/wiki/Web_search_engine" class="mw-redirect" title="Web search engine">WWW search engine</a>, the author does not make this claim<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> and elsewhere<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> it is stated that this was not its purpose.  The Wanderer charted the growth of the web until late 1995.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span
