By doing so, the plaintiff seeks a <a href="/wiki/Legal_remedy" title="Legal remedy">legal remedy</a>. If this search is successful, the court will issue <a href="/wiki/Judgment_(law)" title="Judgment (law)">judgment</a> in favor of the plaintiff and make the appropriate <a href="/wiki/Court_order" title="Court order">court order</a> (e.g., an order for <a href="/wiki/Damages" title="Damages">damages</a>). "Plaintiff" is the term used in civil cases in most English-speaking jurisdictions, the notable exception being <a href="/wiki/England_and_Wales" title="England and Wales">England and Wales</a>, where a plaintiff has, since the introduction of the <a href="/wiki/Civil_Procedure_Rules" title="Civil Procedure Rules">Civil Procedure Rules</a> in 1999, been known as a "<b>claimant</b>", but that term also has other meanings. In criminal cases, the <a href="/wiki/Prosecutor" title="Prosecutor">prosecutor</a> brings the case against the defendant, but the key complaining party is often called the "<b>complainant</b>".\n</p><p>In some <a href="/wiki/Jurisdiction" title="Jurisdiction">jurisdictions</a>, a lawsuit is commenced by filing a <a href="/wiki/Summons" title="Summons">summons</a>, claim form or a <a href="/wiki/Complaint" title="Complaint">complaint</a>. These documents are known as <a href="/wiki/Pleading" title="Pleading">pleadings</a>, that set forth the alleged wrongs committed by the <a href="/wiki/Defendant" title="Defendant">defendant</a> or defendants with a demand for relief. In other jurisdictions, the action is commenced by service of <a href="/wiki/Legal_process" title="Legal process">legal process</a> by delivery of these documents on the defendant by a process server; they are only filed with the court subsequently with an <a href="/wiki/Affidavit" title="Affidavit">affidavit</a> from the process server that they had been given to the defendant according to the rules of <a href="/wiki/Civil_procedure" title="Civil procedure">civil procedure</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Terminology">Terminology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Plaintiff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Terminology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In most English-speaking jurisdictions, including <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong" title="Hong Kong">Hong Kong</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nigeria" title="Nigeria">Nigeria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> and the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, as well as in both <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Republic of Ireland</a>, the legal term "plaintiff" is used as a general term for the party taking action in a civil case.\n</p><p>The word <i>plaintiff</i> can be traced to the year 1278, and stems from the Anglo-French word <i>pleintif</i> meaning "complaining". It was identical to "<a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/plaintive" class="extiw" title="wikt:plaintive">plaintive</a>" at first and receded into legal usage with the -iff spelling in the 15th century.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>A plaintiff identified by name in a <a href="/wiki/Class_action" title="Class action">class action</a> is called a <a href="/wiki/Named_plaintiff" class="mw-redirect" title="Named plaintiff">named plaintiff</a>.\n</p><p>In most common-law jurisdictions, the term "claimant" used in England and Wales since 1999 (see below) is used only in specific, often non-judicial contexts. In particular, in American usage, terms such as "claimant" and "claim form" are limited to extrajudicial process in <a href="/wiki/Insurance" title="Insurance">insurance</a> and <a href="/wiki/Administrative_law" title="Administrative law">administrative law</a>. After exhausting remedies available through an <a href="/wiki/Insurer" class="mw-redirect" title="Insurer">insurer</a> or <a href="/wiki/Government_agency" title="Government agency">government agency</a>, an American claimant in need of further relief would turn to the courts, file a complaint (thus establishing a real court case under judicial supervision) and become a plaintiff.\n</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Law_of_England_and_Wales" class="mw-redirect" title="Law of England and Wales">England and Wales</a>, the term "claimant" replaced "plaintiff" after the <a href="/wiki/Civil_Procedure_Rules" title="Civil Procedure Rules">Civil Procedure Rules</a> came into force on 26 April 1999.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The move, which brings England and Wales out of line with general usage in English-speaking jurisdictions, was reportedly based on an assessment that the word "claimant" is more acceptable as "<a href="/wiki/Plain_English" title="Plain English">plain English</a>" than the word "plaintiff".<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Scottish_law" class="mw-redirect" title="Scottish law">Scottish law</a> a plaintiff is referred to as a "<b>pursuer</b>" and a defendant as a "defender".<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The party against whom the complaint is made is the <a href="/wiki/Defendant" title="Defendant">defendant</a>; or, in the case of a petition, a respondent. Case names are usually given with the plaintiff first, as in <i>Plaintiff v. Defendant</i>.\n</p><p>The similar term "complainant" denotes the complaining witness in a criminal proceeding.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span
