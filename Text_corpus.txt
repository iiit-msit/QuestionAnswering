href="/wiki/Language_resource" title="Language resource">language resource</a> consisting of a large and structured set of texts (nowadays usually electronically stored and processed). In <a href="/wiki/Corpus_linguistics" title="Corpus linguistics">corpus linguistics</a>, they are used to do statistical analysis and <a href="/wiki/Statistical_hypothesis_testing" title="Statistical hypothesis testing">hypothesis testing</a>, checking occurrences or validating linguistic rules within a specific language territory.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc" role="navigation" class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Text_corpus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>A corpus may contain texts in a single language (<i>monolingual corpus</i>) or text data in multiple languages (<i>multilingual corpus</i>).\n</p><p>In order to make the corpora more useful for doing linguistic research, they are often subjected to a process known as <a href="/wiki/Annotation" title="Annotation">annotation</a>. An example of annotating a corpus is <a href="/wiki/Part-of-speech_tagging" title="Part-of-speech tagging">part-of-speech tagging</a>, or <i>POS-tagging</i>, in which information about each word\'s part of speech (verb, noun, adjective, etc.) is added to the corpus in the form of <i>tags</i>. Another example is indicating the <a href="/wiki/Lemma_(morphology)" title="Lemma (morphology)">lemma</a> (base) form of each word. When the language of the corpus is not a working language of the researchers who use it, <a href="/wiki/Interlinear_gloss" title="Interlinear gloss">interlinear glossing</a> is used to make the annotation bilingual.\n</p><p>Some corpora have further <i>structured</i> levels of analysis applied. In particular, a number of smaller corpora may be fully <a href="/wiki/Parsing" title="Parsing">parsed</a>. Such corpora are usually called <a href="/wiki/Treebank" title="Treebank">Treebanks</a> or <a href="/wiki/Treebank" title="Treebank">Parsed Corpora</a>. The difficulty of ensuring that the entire corpus is completely and consistently annotated means that these corpora are usually smaller, containing around one to three million words. Other levels of linguistic structured analysis are possible, including annotations for <a href="/wiki/Morphology_(linguistics)" title="Morphology (linguistics)">morphology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Semantics" title="Semantics">semantics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pragmatics" title="Pragmatics">pragmatics</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Text_corpus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Corpora are the main knowledge base in <a href="/wiki/Corpus_linguistics" title="Corpus linguistics">corpus linguistics</a>. Other notable areas of application include:\n</p>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Language_technology" title="Language technology">Language technology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Natural_language_processing" title="Natural language processing">natural language processing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Computational_linguistics" title="Computational linguistics">computational linguistics</a>\n<ul><li>The analysis and processing of various types of corpora are also the subject of much work in <a href="/wiki/Computational_linguistics" title="Computational linguistics">computational linguistics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Speech_recognition" title="Speech recognition">speech recognition</a> and <a href="/wiki/Machine_translation" title="Machine translation">machine translation</a>, where they are often used to create <a href="/wiki/Hidden_Markov_model" title="Hidden Markov model">hidden Markov models</a> for part of speech tagging and other purposes. Corpora and <a href="/wiki/Frequency_list" class="mw-redirect" title="Frequency list">frequency lists</a> derived from them are useful for <a href="/wiki/Language_teaching" class="mw-redirect" title="Language teaching">language teaching</a>. Corpora can be considered as a type of <a href="/wiki/Foreign_language_writing_aid" title="Foreign language writing aid">foreign language writing aid</a> as the contextualised grammatical knowledge acquired by non-native language users through exposure to authentic texts in corpora allows learners to grasp the manner of sentence formation in the target language, enabling effective writing.<sup id="cite_ref-Yoon_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Yoon-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup></li></ul></li></ul>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Machine_translation" title="Machine translation">Machine translation</a>\n<ul><li>Multilingual corpora that have been specially formatted for side-by-side comparison are called <i>aligned parallel corpora</i>. There are two main types of <a href="/wiki/Parallel_corpora" class="mw-redirect" title="Parallel corpora">parallel corpora</a> which contain texts in two languages. In a <i>translation corpus</i>, the texts in one language are translations of texts in the other language. In a <i>comparable corpus</i>, the texts are of the same kind and cover the same content, but they are not translations of each other.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> To exploit a parallel text, some kind of text alignment identifying equivalent text segments (phrases or sentences) is a prerequisite for analysis. <a href="/wiki/Machine_translation" title="Machine translation">Machine translation</a> algorithms for translating between two languages are often trained using parallel fragments comprising a first language corpus and a second language corpus which is an element-for-element translation of the first language corpus.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup></li></ul></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Philology" title="Philology">Philologies</a>\n<ul><li>Text corpora are also used in the study of <a href="/wiki/Historical_document" title="Historical document">historical documents</a>, for example in attempts to <a href="/wiki/Decipherment" title="Decipherment">decipher</a> ancient scripts, or in <a href="/wiki/Biblical_scholarship" class="mw-redirect" title="Biblical scholarship">Biblical scholarship</a>. Some archaeological corpora can be of such short duration that they provide a snapshot in time. One of the shortest corpora in time may be the 15\xe2\x80\x9330 year <a href="/wiki/Amarna_letters" title="Amarna letters">Amarna letters</a> texts (<a href="/wiki/1350_BC" class="mw-redirect" title="1350 BC">1350 BC</a>). The <i>corpus</i> of an ancient city, (for example the "<a href="/wiki/K%C3%BCltepe" title="K\xc3\xbcltepe">K\xc3\xbcltepe</a> Texts" of Turkey), may go through a series of corpora, determined by their find site dates.</li></ul></li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Some_notable_text_corpora">Some notable text corpora</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span
