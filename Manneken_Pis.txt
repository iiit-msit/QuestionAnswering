It was designed by <a href="https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/J%C3%A9r%C3%B4me_Duquesnoy_l%27Ancien" class="extiw" title="fr:J\xc3\xa9r\xc3\xb4me Duquesnoy l&#39;Ancien">J\xc3\xa9r\xc3\xb4me Duquesnoy the Elder</a> (1570\xe2\x80\x931641),  and put in place in 1618 or 1619.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The current statue is a replica which dates from 1965.<sup id="cite_ref-pisstake_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pisstake-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The original is kept in the <a href="/wiki/Brussels_City_Museum" title="Brussels City Museum">Brussels City Museum</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECouvreurDeknopSymons200526_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECouvreurDeknopSymons200526-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:0_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:1_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> <i>Manneken Pis</i> is the best-known symbol of the people of Brussels. href="/w/index.php?title=Manneken_Pis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins_of_Manneken_Pis">Origins of <i>Manneken Pis</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Manneken_Pis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Origins of Manneken Pis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The earliest mention of the existence of <i>Manneken Pis</i> can be found in an administrative document, dating from 1451\xe2\x80\x9352, about the water lines supplying the fountains of Brussels.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDeligne2008_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDeligne2008-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;a&#93;</a></sup> From the beginning, the fountain played an essential role in the distribution of <a href="/wiki/Drinking_water" title="Drinking water">drinking water</a>. It stood on a column and poured water into a double rectangular basin of stone. The only representations of this first statue can be found, very schematically, in a painting by <a href="/wiki/Denis_van_Alsloot" title="Denis van Alsloot">Denis Van Alsloot</a> representing <a href="/wiki/Ommegang_of_Brussels" title="Ommegang of Brussels">Brussels\' Ommegang</a> of 1615, as well as in a preparatory drawing to this painting.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEVautierHoubrechtsVan_Sprang2012_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEVautierHoubrechtsVan_Sprang2012-11">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:0_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The first statue was replaced by a new <a href="/wiki/Bronze" title="Bronze">bronze</a> version, commissioned in 1619. This 61-centimetre-tall (24&#160;in) bronze statue, on the corner of <span lang="fr" style="font-style: normal;" title="French-language text">Rue de l\'\xc3\x89tuve</span>/<span lang="nl" style="font-style: normal;" title="Dutch-language text">Stoofstraat</span> and <span lang="fr" style="font-style: normal;" title="French-language text">Rue des Grands Carmes</span>/<span lang="nl" style="font-style: normal;" title="Dutch-language text">Lievevrouwbroerstraat</span>, was made by Brussels\' sculptor <a href="https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/J%C3%A9r%C3%B4me_Duquesnoy_l%27Ancien" class="extiw" title="fr:J\xc3\xa9r\xc3\xb4me Duquesnoy l&#39;Ancien">J\xc3\xa9r\xc3\xb4me Duquesnoy the Elder</a> (1570\xe2\x80\x931641), father of the architect and sculptor <a href="/wiki/Jer%C3%B4me_Duquesnoy_(II)" title="Jer\xc3\xb4me Duquesnoy (II)">J\xc3\xa9r\xc3\xb4me Duquesnoy the Younger</a> and the famous sculptor <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Duquesnoy" title="Fran\xc3\xa7ois Duquesnoy">Fran\xc3\xa7ois Duquesnoy</a>. It was probably cast and installed in 1620. During that time, the column supporting the statue and the double rectangular basin collecting water were completely remodeled by the stone cutter Daniel Raessens.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Gravure_Manneken-Pis.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/Gravure_Manneken-Pis.jpg/170px-Gravure_Manneken-Pis.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="232" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/Gravure_Manneken-Pis.jpg/255px-Gravure_Manneken-Pis.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/Gravure_Manneken-Pis.jpg/340px-Gravure_Manneken-Pis.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1959" data-file-height="2676" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Gravure_Manneken-Pis.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>Manneken Pis</i> fountain, depicted in an etching by <a href="/wiki/Jacobus_Harrewijn" title="Jacobus Harrewijn">Jacobus Harrewijn</a> (1697)</div></div></div>\n<p>During its history, the statue faced many hazards. It survived undamaged the <a href="/wiki/Bombardment_of_Brussels" title="Bombardment of Brussels">bombardment of Brussels</a> of 1695 by the French army, but the pipes having been affected, it could not deliver its water for some time. A <a href="/wiki/Pamphlet" title="Pamphlet">pamphlet</a> published the same year recounts this episode. This text is the oldest attesting that <i>Manneken Pis</i> had become "an object of glory appreciated by all and renowned throughout the world".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECulotHennautDemanetMierop199277\xe2\x80\x9379_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECulotHennautDemanetMierop199277\xe2\x80\x9379-12">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> It is also the first time that it served as a symbol for the people of Brussels. It is also traditionally said that after the bombardment, it was triumphantly placed again on its pedestal. On that occasion, the following passage from the <a href="/wiki/Bible" title="Bible">Bible</a> was inscribed above its head&#160;: <i lang="la" title="Latin-language text">In petra exaltavit me, et nunc exaltavi caput meum super inimicos meos</i> ("The Lord placed me on a stone base, and now I raise my head above my enemies"). As shown by an engraving by Jacques Harrewijn, dating from 1697, the fountain was no longer located on the street, but in a recess at the corner of <span lang="fr" style="font-style: normal;" title="French-language text">Rue du Ch\xc3\xaane</span>/<span lang="nl" style="font-style: normal;" title="Dutch-language text">Eikstraat</span> and <span lang="fr" style="font-style: normal;" title="French-language text">Rue de l\'\xc3\x89tuve</span>/<span lang="nl" style="font-style: normal;" title="Dutch-language text">Stoofstraat</span>.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_6-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Manneken-pis-Brussel.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/Manneken-pis-Brussel.jpg/170px-Manneken-pis-Brussel.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="256" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/Manneken-pis-Brussel.jpg/255px-Manneken-pis-Brussel.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/Manneken-pis-Brussel.jpg/340px-Manneken-pis-Brussel.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3264" data-file-height="4912" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Manneken-pis-Brussel.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>Manneken Pis</i> in its niche, fitted in 1770</div></div></div>\n<p>In 1770, the column and the double rectangular basin disappeared; the statue was integrated into a new decor, in the form of a stone niche in <i>rockery</i> style, originating from another dismantled fountain of Brussels.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDes_Marez1918144_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDes_Marez1918144-13">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The water simply flowed through a grating in the ground, which was replaced by a basin in the 19th century. In its new setting, <i>Manneken Pis</i> gives the impression of being smaller than in its original layout.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDes_Marez1918143_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDes_Marez1918143-14">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:0_6-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The whole structure is protected by railings, the last version of which dates from 1851.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHeymans2003_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHeymans2003-15">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> The latter prevented access to water, relegating the fountain to a decorative and symbolic role.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_6-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> It is also the case, around the same time, of the other fountains in Brussels. This correlates with efforts by the <a href="/wiki/City_of_Brussels" title="City of Brussels">City of Brussels</a>, starting in 1855, to allow for the distribution of drinking water in homes.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDeligne2005_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDeligne2005-16">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The figure has repeatedly been the object of theft or attempted theft. Legend has it that the statue was removed in 1745 and found in the Flemish town of <a href="/wiki/Geraardsbergen" title="Geraardsbergen">Geraardsbergen</a> (French: <i lang="fr">Grammont</i>). As a sign of their appreciation, the people of Brussels gave this city a replica of the statue. In reality, the first attempted theft was made in 1747 by a group of French <a href="/wiki/Grenadier" title="Grenadier">grenadiers</a> stationed in Brussels. The population rebelled against this deed and threatened a bloody revenge. To calm things down, the King of France, <a href="/wiki/Louis_XV_of_France" class="mw-redirect" title="Louis XV of France">Louis XV</a>, offered a gentleman\'s gown of <a href="/wiki/Brocade" title="Brocade">brocade</a>, embroidered with gold, to <i>Manneken Pis</i>. He also authorised the statue to carry the sword, and decorated it with the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_Saint_Louis" title="Order of Saint Louis">Cross of St. Louis</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_6-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The statue was stolen in 1817 by the fugitive Antoine Licas. The perpetrator was heavily punished; he was condemned to forced labour for life, and was first tied for an hour to <a href="/wiki/Stocks" title="Stocks">stocks</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Grand_Place" title="Grand Place">Grand Place</a>. The original statue was broken into 11 pieces during this abduction and was restored by a specialised <a href="/wiki/Welder" title="Welder">welder</a>, under the supervision of sculptor <a href="/wiki/Gilles-Lambert_Godecharle" title="Gilles-Lambert Godecharle">Gilles-Lambert Godecharle</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_6-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The pieces were matched and used to make a mold in which the bronze statue was poured. The statue was then screwed onto a new base marked "1620 \xe2\x80\x93 REST 1817".<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span id="20th_century.E2.80.93present"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="20th_century\xe2\x80\x93present">20th century\xe2\x80\x93present</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Manneken_Pis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 20th century\xe2\x80\x93present">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><i>Manneken Pis</i> experienced similar misadventures in the 20th century. Two attempted thefts occurred in 1955 and 1957. Some accounts say that it has been stolen up to seven times; in January 1963 by students of the <a href="/wiki/Antwerp" title="Antwerp">Antwerp</a> student association "De Wikings" of the Sint-Ignatius Handelshogeschool (Higher Business Education), now part of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Antwerp" title="University of Antwerp">Antwerp University</a>, who "hijacked" the statue for five days before handing it over to the Antwerp authorities. The local and international press covered the story, contributing to the students\' collection of funds donated to two orphanages.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Manneken_Pis_Original.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Manneken_Pis_Original.jpg/170px-Manneken_Pis_Original.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="227" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Manneken_Pis_Original.jpg/255px-Manneken_Pis_Original.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Manneken_Pis_Original.jpg/340px-Manneken_Pis_Original.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3024" data-file-height="4032" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Manneken_Pis_Original.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Original statue from 1619 kept at the <a href="/wiki/Brussels_City_Museum" title="Brussels City Museum">Brussels City Museum</a></div></div></div>\n<p>Things were more serious when it disappeared in 1965; the statue had been broken by the thief and only the feet and ankles remained.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECouvreurDeknopSymons200540_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECouvreurDeknopSymons200540-18">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> In June 1966, the Antwerp magazine <i>De Post</i> received an anonymous phone call, signaling that the body was in the <a href="/wiki/Brussels%E2%80%93Charleroi_Canal" title="Brussels\xe2\x80\x93Charleroi Canal">Charleroi Canal</a>. It was found there by divers, sent by the magazine, and was brought back to Brussels on 27 June. Restored once again, the statue was sheltered and the original version is now kept and displayed on the second floor of the Brussels City Museum, at the <a href="/wiki/Museum_of_the_City_of_Brussels" class="mw-redirect" title="Museum of the City of Brussels">Maison du Roi/Broodhuis</a>, on the <a href="/wiki/Grand_Place" title="Grand Place">Grand Place</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECouvreurDeknopSymons200526_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECouvreurDeknopSymons200526-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:0_6-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:1_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The statue now on display on the corner of <span lang="fr" style="font-style: normal;" title="French-language text">Rue du Ch\xc3\xaane</span>/<span lang="nl" style="font-style: normal;" title="Dutch-language text">Eikstraat</span> and <span lang="fr" style="font-style: normal;" title="French-language text">Rue de l\'\xc3\x89tuve</span>/<span lang="nl" style="font-style: normal;" title="Dutch-language text">Stoofstraat</span> is an identical copy.\n</p><p>In late 2018, it was discovered by city technician R\xc3\xa9gis Callens that the basin of the statue had developed a leak, leading to a reported 1,000\xe2\x80\x931,500 liters (around 300 gallons) of water being used per day.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> The leak occurred for an unknown number of years, unnoticed among the several hundred water features in the City of Brussels and was only later discovered with the help of Shayp water monitoring technology.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> The statue received a temporary fix in March 2019, with a permanent recirculating system set to be installed. The solution was announced during Brussels Water Week where city officials cited the situation as motivation to check for similar problems in other fountains.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legends">Legends</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Manneken_Pis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Legends">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Manneken_Pis_milieu_20040625.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Manneken_Pis_milieu_20040625.jpg/220px-Manneken_Pis_milieu_20040625.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Manneken_Pis_milieu_20040625.jpg/330px-Manneken_Pis_milieu_20040625.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Manneken_Pis_milieu_20040625.jpg/440px-Manneken_Pis_milieu_20040625.jpg 2x" data-file-width="677" data-file-height="451" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Manneken_Pis_milieu_20040625.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The surroundings of <i>Manneken Pis</i> give an idea of its size.</div></div></div>\n<p>There are several legends behind <i>Manneken Pis</i>, but the most famous is the one about Duke <a href="/wiki/Godfrey_III_of_Leuven" class="mw-redirect" title="Godfrey III of Leuven">Godfrey III of Leuven</a>. In 1142, the troops of this two-year-old lord were battling against the troops of the Berthouts, the lords of <a href="/wiki/Grimbergen" title="Grimbergen">Grimbergen</a>, in Ransbeke (now <a href="/wiki/Neder-Over-Heembeek" title="Neder-Over-Heembeek">Neder-Over-Heembeek</a>, a northern part of the <a href="/wiki/City_of_Brussels" title="City of Brussels">City of Brussels</a>). To give themselves courage, the soldiers placed the infant lord in a basket which they hung from a large <a href="/wiki/Oak_tree" class="mw-redirect" title="Oak tree">oak tree</a> overlooking the battlefield. While his men were in dire straits, the little duke rose up in the basket, and from his perch, urinated onto the troops of the Berthouts, who eventually lost the battle.<sup id="cite_ref-pisstake_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pisstake-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The fountain perpetuates the memory of this victory. The name of <span lang="fr" style="font-style: normal;" title="French-language text">Rue du Ch\xc3\xaane</span>/<span lang="nl" style="font-style: normal;" title="Dutch-language text">Eikstraat</span> ("Oak Tree Street"), at the corner of which the statue is located, recalls the famous tree.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELe_Roy194714_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELe_Roy194714-22">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<ul><li>Another legend states that, in the 14th century, Brussels was under siege by a foreign power. The city had held its ground for some time, so the attackers conceived of a plan to place explosive charges at the city walls. A little boy named <i lang="nl" title="Dutch-language text">Julianske</i> happened to be spying on them, as they were preparing. He urinated on the burning fuse and thus saved the city.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELe_Roy194715_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELe_Roy194715-23">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> There was, at the time (middle of the 15th century, perhaps as early as 1388), a similar statue made of stone. The statue was stolen several times.<sup id="cite_ref-pisstake_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pisstake-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>Another story, often told to tourists, tells of a wealthy merchant who, during a visit to the city with his family, had his beloved young son go missing. The merchant hastily formed a search party, which scoured all corners of the city, until the boy was found happily urinating in a small garden. The merchant, as a gift of gratitude to the locals who helped out during the search, had the fountain built.<sup id="cite_ref-pisstake_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pisstake-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>Another legend tells that a small boy went missing from his mother, when shopping in the centre of the city. The woman, panic-stricken by the loss of her child, called upon everyone she came across, including the mayor of the city. A citywide search began, and when at last the child was found, he was urinating on the corner of a small street. The story was passed down over time and the statue was erected as a tribute to the well-known legend.<sup id="cite_ref-pisstake_4-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pisstake-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>Another legend tells of the young boy who was awoken by a fire and was able to put out the fire with his urine. In the end, this helped stop the king\'s castle from burning down.<sup id="cite_ref-pisstake_4-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pisstake-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>\n<p><i>Manneken Pis</i> is sometimes given the nickname of <i lang="fr" title="French-language text">Petit Julien</i> in French or <i lang="nl" title="Dutch-language text">Julianske</i> in Dutch (both meaning "Little Julien"), which in fact refers to a now-disappeared fountain of Julian (<i>Juliaenkensborre</i>),<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDeligne2003_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDeligne2003-24">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> by mistakenly confusing two well-distinct fountains.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHenneWauters1845_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHenneWauters1845-25">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:0_6-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Traditions">Traditions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Manneken_Pis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Traditions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Costumes_and_folklore">Costumes and folklore</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Manneken_Pis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Costumes and folklore">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Brussel_MP_Dressed_Up.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Brussel_MP_Dressed_Up.jpg/220px-Brussel_MP_Dressed_Up.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="208" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Brussel_MP_Dressed_Up.jpg/330px-Brussel_MP_Dressed_Up.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Brussel_MP_Dressed_Up.jpg/440px-Brussel_MP_Dressed_Up.jpg 2x" data-file-width="800" data-file-height="757" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Brussel_MP_Dressed_Up.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>Manneken Pis</i> being dressed by the student class Technica of the <a href="/wiki/Erasmushogeschool_Brussel" title="Erasmushogeschool Brussel">Erasmus School of Brussels</a></div></div></div>\n<p><i>Manneken Pis</i> is dressed in costumes, several times each week, according to a published schedule, which is posted on the railings around the fountain. Since 1954, the costumes are managed by the non-profit association <i>The Friends of Manneken-Pis</i>, who review hundreds of designs submitted each year, and select a small number to be produced and used.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> His wardrobe consists of around one thousand different costumes, many of which may be viewed in a permanent exhibition inside the <a href="/wiki/Museum_of_the_City_of_Brussels" class="mw-redirect" title="Museum of the City of Brussels">City Museum</a>, located on the <a href="/wiki/Grand_Place" title="Grand Place">Grand Place</a>, immediately opposite the <a href="/wiki/Brussels_Town_Hall" title="Brussels Town Hall">Town Hall</a>. In February 2017, a specially designed museum, at 19, <span lang="fr" style="font-style: normal;" title="French-language text">rue du Ch\xc3\xaane</span>/<span lang="nl" style="font-style: normal;" title="Dutch-language text">Eikstraat</span>, called <i>Garderobe MannekenPis</i>, opened its doors.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Although the proliferation of costumes is of 20th-century origin, the occasional use of costumes dates back almost to the date of casting; the oldest costume on display, in the City Museum, being of 17th-century origin.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> In 1756, an inventory indicates that <i>Manneken Pis</i> had five complete costumes. From 1918 to 1940, he was offered some thirty costumes. But it was especially after 1945 that the movement took on an exceptional dimension; he had more than 400 costumes in 1994, more than 750 in 2005, and more than 950 in 2016.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> The costume change, on the figure, is a colourful ceremony, often accompanied by brass band music. Many costumes represent the national dress of nations whose citizens come to Brussels as tourists; others are the uniforms of assorted trades, professions, associations, and branches of the civil and military services.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>On certain occasions (e.g. <a href="/wiki/St_V" title="St V">Saint-Verhaegen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Meyboom" title="Meyboom">Meyboom</a> plantation), the statue is hooked up to a keg of beer. Cups are filled up with the beer flowing from the statue and given out to passers-by.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<ul class="gallery mw-gallery-traditional">\n\t\t<li class="gallerybox" style="width: 205px"><div style="width: 205px">\n\t\t\t<div class="thumb" style="width: 200px;"><div style="margin:15px auto;"><a href="/wiki/File:Manneken_Pis_dracula.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Manneken_Pis_dracula.jpg/118px-Manneken_Pis_dracula.jpg" decoding="async" width="118" height="170" He recalled the legend as "the boy who saved Brussels from fire by extinguishing it with his urine" (confusing the legend with an incident in <i><a href="/wiki/Gulliver%27s_Travels" title="Gulliver&#39;s Travels">Gulliver\'s Travels</a></i> perhaps). Some shocked shoppers made a formal complaint. Mall officials said that the waffle-shop owner did not follow procedures when he put up the statue and was therefore in violation of his lease.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In contrast, there is a <a href="/wiki/Botafogo_de_Futebol_e_Regatas#Mascots" title="Botafogo de Futebol e Regatas">similar statue</a> in <a href="/wiki/Rio_de_Janeiro" title="Rio de Janeiro">Rio de Janeiro</a>, in front of the quarters of <a href="/wiki/Botafogo_de_Futebol_e_Regatas" title="Botafogo de Futebol e Regatas">Botafogo de Futebol e Regatas</a>, a famous Brazilian football club. There, the presence of the statue is taken lightly, and it has even been adopted as a mascot by the club. Fans usually dress it with the club\'s jersey, after important wins.\n</p><p>A working replica of <i>Manneken Pis</i> stands on the platform of <a href="/wiki/Hamamatsuch%C5%8D_Station" title="Hamamatsuch\xc5\x8d Station">Hamamatsuch\xc5\x8d Station</a> in Tokyo, Japan. It feeds a small fountain. It premiered in May 1995 at the <a href="/wiki/Cannes_Film_Festival" title="Cannes Film Festival">Cannes Film Festival</a>. It received the <a href="/wiki/Andr%C3%A9_Cavens_Award" title="Andr\xc3\xa9 Cavens Award">Andr\xc3\xa9 Cavens Award</a> for Best Film and four awards at the <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Plateau_Awards" class="mw-redirect" title="Joseph Plateau Awards">Joseph Plateau Awards</a>.
