image)</div></div></div>\n<p>A <b>defendant</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Legal_personality" class="mw-redirect" title="Legal personality">person</a> accused of committing a <a href="/wiki/Crime" title="Crime">crime</a> in <a href="/wiki/Criminal_procedure" title="Criminal procedure">criminal prosecution</a> or a person against whom some type of civil relief is being sought in a <a href="/wiki/Civil_procedure" title="Civil procedure">civil case</a>.\n</p><p>Terminology varies from one <a href="/wiki/Jurisdiction" title="Jurisdiction">jurisdiction</a> to another. For example, <a href="/wiki/Scots_law" title="Scots law">Scots law</a> does not use the term "defendant"; the terms "accused" or "panel" are used instead in criminal proceedings, and "defender" in civil proceedings.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc" role="navigation" aria-labelledby="mw-toc-heading"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" style="display:none" /><div class="toctitle" lang="en" dir="ltr"><h2 id="mw-toc-heading">Contents</h2><span class="toctogglespan"><label class="toctogglelabel" for="toctogglecheckbox"></label></span></div>\n<ul>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-1"><a href="#Criminal_defendants"><span class="tocnumber">1</span> <span class="toctext">Criminal defendants</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-2"><a href="#Civil_defendants"><span class="tocnumber">2</span> <span class="toctext">Civil defendants</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-3"><a href="#England_and_Wales"><span class="tocnumber">3</span> <span class="toctext">England and Wales</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-4"><a href="#See_also"><span class="tocnumber">4</span> <span class="toctext">See also</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-5"><a href="#References"><span class="tocnumber">5</span> <span class="toctext">References</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criminal_defendants">Criminal defendants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Defendant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Criminal defendants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In a criminal trial, a defendant is a person accused (<a href="/wiki/Criminal_charge" title="Criminal charge">charged</a>) of committing an offense (a crime; an act defined as punishable under <a href="/wiki/Criminal_law" title="Criminal law">criminal law</a>). The other party to a criminal trial is usually a public <a href="/wiki/Prosecutor" title="Prosecutor">prosecutor</a>, but in some jurisdictions, private prosecutions are allowed.\n</p><p>Criminal defendants are often taken into custody by <a href="/wiki/Police" title="Police">police</a> and brought before a court under an <a href="/wiki/Arrest_warrant" title="Arrest warrant">arrest warrant</a>. Criminal defendants are usually obliged to post <a href="/wiki/Bail" title="Bail">bail</a> before being released from custody. For serious cases, such as murder, bail is often refused. Defendants must be present at every stage of the proceedings against them. (There is an exception for very minor cases such as traffic offenses in jurisdictions which treat them as crimes.)\n</p><p>If more than one person is accused, the people may be referred as "co-defendant" or "co-conspirator" in British and Common-Law courts.\n</p><p>In some jurisdictions, vulnerable defendants may be able to get access of services of a <a href="/wiki/Non-Registered_Intermediary" title="Non-Registered Intermediary">Non-Registered Intermediary</a> to assist with communication at court.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Civil_defendants">Civil defendants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Defendant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Civil defendants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In a civil <a href="/wiki/Lawsuit" title="Lawsuit">lawsuit</a>, a defendant (or a <b>respondent</b>) is also the accused <a href="/wiki/Party_(law)" title="Party (law)">party</a>, although not of an offense, but of a <a href="/wiki/Civil_wrong" title="Civil wrong">civil wrong</a> (a <a href="/wiki/Tort" title="Tort">tort</a> or a <a href="/wiki/Breach_of_contract" title="Breach of contract">breach of contract</a>, for instance). The person who starts the <a href="/wiki/Civil_law_(area)" class="mw-redirect" title="Civil law (area)">civil</a> action through filing a <a href="/wiki/Complaint" title="Complaint">complaint</a> is referred to as the <a href="/wiki/Plaintiff" title="Plaintiff">plaintiff</a> (also known as the <b>appellant</b>).\n</p><p>Defendants in civil actions usually make their first court appearance voluntarily in response to a summons. Historically, civil defendants could be taken into custody under a writ of <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Caspian_ad_respondent&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Caspian ad respondent (page does not exist)">Caspian ad respondent</a></i>. Modern-day civil defendants are usually able to avoid most (if not all) court appearances if represented by a <a href="/wiki/Lawyer" title="Lawyer">lawyer</a>.\n</p><p>Most often and familiarly, defendants are persons: either <a href="/wiki/Natural_person" title="Natural person">natural persons</a> (actual human beings) or <a href="/wiki/Legal_personality#Juridical_persons" class="mw-redirect" title="Legal personality">juridical persons</a> (<i>persona fiction</i>) under the <a href="/wiki/Legal_fiction" title="Legal fiction">legal fiction</a> of treating organizations as persons. But a defendant may be an object, in which case the object itself is the direct subject of the action. When a court has jurisdiction over an object, it is said to have <a href="/wiki/Jurisdiction_in_rem" class="mw-redirect" title="Jurisdiction in rem">jurisdiction <i>in rem</i></a>. An example of an <i>in rem</i> case is <i><a href="/wiki/United_States_v._Forty_Barrels_and_Twenty_Kegs_of_Coca-Cola" class="mw-redirect" title="United States v. Forty Barrels and Twenty Kegs of Coca-Cola">United States v. Forty Barrels and Twenty Kegs of Coca-Cola</a></i> (1916), where the defendant was not <a href="/wiki/The_Coca-Cola_Company" title="The Coca-Cola Company">The Coca-Cola Company</a> itself, but rather "Forty Barrels and Twenty Kegs of Coca-Cola". In current US legal practice, <i>in rem</i> suits are primarily <a href="/wiki/Asset_forfeiture" title="Asset forfeiture">asset forfeiture</a> cases, based on drug laws, as in <i><a href="/wiki/USA_v._$124,700" class="mw-redirect" title="USA v. $124,700">USA v. $124,700</a></i> (2006).\n</p><p>Defendants can set up an account to pay for litigation costs and <a href="/wiki/Legal_expense" class="mw-redirect" title="Legal expense">legal expenses</a>. These <a href="/wiki/Legal_defense_fund" title="Legal defense fund">legal defense funds</a> can have large membership counts where members contribute to the fund. The fund can be public or private and is set up for individuals, organizations, or a particular purpose. These funds are often used by public officials, civil-rights organizations, and public-interest organizations.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="England_and_Wales">England and Wales</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Defendant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: England and Wales">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Historically<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="This must be very old usage. E.g. H. Finch Law (1627) iv. xxxiii. 387 The Defendant confessing an enditement of felonie may accuse others. (May 2020)">when?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>, "defendant" was a legal term for a person prosecuted for <a href="/wiki/Misdemeanour" class="mw-redirect" title="Misdemeanour">misdemeanour</a>. It was not applicable to a person prosecuted for <a href="/wiki/Felony" title="Felony">felony</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span
