For other uses, see <a href="/wiki/The_Long_Haul_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="The Long Haul (disambiguation)">The Long Haul</a>.</div>\n<p>In <a href="/wiki/Aviation" title="Aviation">aviation</a>, the <b>flight length</b> is defined as the distance of a flight. Commercial flights are often categorized into long-, medium- or short-haul by <a href="/wiki/Airline" title="Airline">commercial airlines</a> based on flight length, although there is no international standard definition and many airlines use air time or geographic boundaries instead.\nRoute category lengths tend to define short-haul routes as being shorter than 600\xe2\x80\x93800&#160;nmi (1,100\xe2\x80\x931,500&#160;km), long-haul as being longer than 2,200\xe2\x80\x932,600&#160;nmi (4,100\xe2\x80\x934,800&#160;km), and medium-haul as being in-between.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc" length</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flight_length&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Absolute distance versus flight length">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Greatcircle_Jetstream_routes.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Greatcircle_Jetstream_routes.svg/400px-Greatcircle_Jetstream_routes.svg.png" decoding="async" width="400" height="184" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Greatcircle_Jetstream_routes.svg/600px-Greatcircle_Jetstream_routes.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Greatcircle_Jetstream_routes.svg/800px-Greatcircle_Jetstream_routes.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="815" data-file-height="374" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Greatcircle_Jetstream_routes.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Airline routes between <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco" title="San Francisco">San Francisco</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tokyo" title="Tokyo">Tokyo</a> following the most direct great circle (top) westward, and following a longer-distance jet stream route (bottom) when heading eastward</div></div></div>\n<p>The absolute distance between two points is the <a href="/wiki/Great-circle_distance" title="Great-circle distance">great-circle distance</a>, which is always the shortest geographical route.  In the example (right), the aircraft travelling westward from North America to Japan is following a great-circle route extending northward towards the <a href="/wiki/Arctic" title="Arctic">Arctic</a> region. The apparent curve of the route is a result of distortion when plotted onto a conventional map projection and makes the route appear to be longer than it really is.  Stretching a string between  North America and Japan on a globe will demonstrate why this really is the shortest route despite appearances.\n</p><p>The actual flight length is the length of the track flown across the ground in practice, which is usually longer than the ideal great-circle and is influenced by a number of factors such as the need to avoid bad weather, wind direction and speed, fuel economy, navigational restrictions and other requirements. In the example, easterly flights from Japan to North America are shown taking a longer, more southerly, route than the shorter great-circle; this is to take advantage of the favourable <a href="/wiki/Jet_stream" title="Jet stream">jet stream</a>, a fast, high-altitude tail-wind, that assists the aircraft along its ground track saving more time and fuel than the geographically shortest route.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Air_time_versus_schedule_times">Air time versus schedule times</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flight_length&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Air time versus schedule times">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Air time is the elapsed time that the aircraft is airborne, regardless of what <a href="/wiki/Time-zone" class="mw-redirect" title="Time-zone">time-zone</a> the flight began and ended in.\n</p><p>Schedule time is the difference between the scheduled local time at the origin and the scheduled local time at the destination and usually differs from the actual time in the air as it is affected by the local time zones. Local clock time flying westward, or "chasing the sun", is slowed,<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> while local clock time flying eastbound is sped up.  However, flights over the <a href="/wiki/International_Date_Line" title="International Date Line">International Date Line</a> located at approximately 180<sup>o</sup> E in the Pacific will subtract 24 hours from the schedule time going eastwards and add 24 hours going westwards.  For example, the eastward flight shown in the example from Japan to North America will have a scheduled time of arrival earlier than the departure time, while from North America to Japan the flight will take a whole day longer by local time; the actual flying time in both cases being the same or similar.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span
