template">e</abbr></a></li></ul></div></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p>In <a href="/wiki/Accounting" title="Accounting">accounting</a>, <b>revenue</b> is the <a href="/wiki/Income" title="Income">income</a> or increase in net <a href="/wiki/Assets" class="mw-redirect" title="Assets">assets</a><sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\nthat an entity has from its normal activities (in the case of a <a href="/wiki/Business" title="Business">business</a>, usually from the sale of <a href="/wiki/Product_(business)" title="Product (business)">goods and services</a> to customers). Commercial revenue may also be referred to as <b>sales</b> or as <b>turnover</b>. Some <a href="/wiki/Company" title="Company"> companies</a> receive revenue from <a href="/wiki/Interest" title="Interest">interest</a>, <a href="/wiki/Royalties" class="mw-redirect" title="Royalties">royalties</a>, or other <a href="/wiki/Fees" class="mw-redirect" title="Fees">fees</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> "Revenue" may refer to income in general, or it may refer to the amount, in a <a href="/wiki/Monetary_unit" class="mw-redirect" title="Monetary unit">monetary unit</a>, earned during a period of time, as in "Last year, Company X had revenue of $42 million". <a href="/wiki/Profit_(accounting)" title="Profit (accounting)">Profits</a> or <a href="/wiki/Net_income" title="Net income">net income</a> generally imply total revenue minus total expenses in a given period. In <a href="/wiki/Accountancy" class="mw-redirect" title="Accountancy">accounting</a>, in the balance statement, revenue is a subsection of the Equity section and revenue increases equity, it is often referred to as the "top line" due to its position on the <a href="/wiki/Income_statement" title="Income statement">income statement</a> at the very top. This is to be contrasted with the "bottom line" which denotes net income (gross revenues minus total expenses).<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In general usage, revenue is income received by an organization in the form of <a href="/wiki/Cash" title="Cash">cash</a> or cash equivalents. <a href="/wiki/Sales_(accounting)" title="Sales (accounting)">Sales revenue</a> is income received from selling goods or services over a period of time. <a href="/wiki/Tax_revenue" title="Tax revenue">Tax revenue</a> is income that a government receives from taxpayers. <a href="/wiki/Fundraising" title="Fundraising">Fundraising</a> revenue is income received by a <a href="/wiki/Charitable_organization" title="Charitable organization"> charity</a> from donors etc. to further its social purposes.\n</p><p>In more formal usage, revenue is a calculation or estimation of periodic income based on a particular <a href="/wiki/Standard_accounting_practice" class="mw-redirect" title="Standard accounting practice">standard accounting practice</a> or the rules established by a government or government agency. Two common <a href="/wiki/Accounting_methods" class="mw-redirect" title="Accounting methods">accounting methods</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cash_basis_accounting" class="mw-redirect" title="Cash basis accounting">cash basis accounting</a> and <a href="/wiki/Accrual_basis_accounting" class="mw-redirect" title="Accrual basis accounting">accrual basis accounting</a>, do not use the same process for measuring revenue. Corporations that offer shares for sale to the public are usually required by law to report revenue based on <a href="/wiki/Generally_accepted_accounting_principles" class="mw-redirect" title="Generally accepted accounting principles">generally accepted accounting principles</a> or on <a href="/wiki/International_Financial_Reporting_Standards" title="International Financial Reporting Standards">International Financial Reporting Standards</a>.\n</p><p>In a <a href="/wiki/Double-entry_bookkeeping_system" class="mw-redirect" title="Double-entry bookkeeping system">double-entry bookkeeping system</a>, revenue accounts are <a href="/wiki/General_ledger" title="General ledger">general ledger</a> accounts that are summarized periodically under the heading "Revenue" or "Revenues" on an <a href="/wiki/Income_statement" title="Income statement">income statement</a>. Revenue account-names describe the type of revenue, such as "Repair service revenue", "Rent revenue earned" or "Sales".<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc" role="navigation" aria-labelledby="mw-toc-heading"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Revenue&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Non-profit organizations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>For <a href="/wiki/Non-profit_organization" class="mw-redirect" title="Non-profit organization">non-profit organizations</a>, revenue may be referred to as <b>gross receipts</b>, <b>support</b>, <b>contributions</b>, etc.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> This operating revenue can include donations from individuals and corporations, support from government agencies, income from activities related to the organization\'s <a href="/wiki/Mission_statement" title="Mission statement">mission</a>, income from fundraising activities, and membership dues. Revenue (income and gains) from investments may be categorized as "operating" or "non-operating"\xe2\x80\x94but for many non-profits must (simultaneously) be categorized by <a href="/wiki/Fund_accounting" title="Fund accounting">fund</a> (along with other accounts).\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Association_dues_revenue">Association dues revenue</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Revenue&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Association dues revenue">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>For non-profits with substantial revenue from dues of its voluntary members: non-dues revenue is revenue generated through means besides association membership fees. This revenue can be found through means of <a href="/wiki/Sponsorships" class="mw-redirect" title="Sponsorships">sponsorships</a>, <a href="/wiki/Donations" class="mw-redirect" title="Donations">donations</a> or outsourcing the association\'s <a href="/wiki/Digital_media" title="Digital media">digital media</a> outlets.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Business_revenue">Business revenue</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Revenue&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Business revenue">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Business revenue is money income from activities that is ordinary for a particular corporation, company, partnership, or sole-proprietorship. For some businesses, such as <a href="/wiki/Manufacturing" title="Manufacturing">manufacturing</a> or <a href="/wiki/Grocery" class="mw-redirect" title="Grocery">grocery</a>, most revenue is from the sale of goods. Service businesses such as <a href="/wiki/Law_firm" title="Law firm">law firms</a> and <a href="/wiki/Barber" title="Barber">barber shops</a> receive most of their revenue from rendering services. Lending businesses such as <a href="/wiki/Car_rental" title="Car rental">car rentals</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bank" title="Bank">banks</a> receive most of their revenue from fees and interest generated by lending <a href="/wiki/Asset" title="Asset">assets</a> to other organizations or individuals.\n</p><p>Revenues from a business\'s primary activities are reported as <b>sales</b>, <b>sales revenue</b> or <b><a href="/wiki/Sales_(accounting)" title="Sales (accounting)">net sales</a></b>.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> This includes product returns and discounts for early payment of <a href="/wiki/Invoice" title="Invoice">invoices</a>. Most businesses also have revenue that is incidental to the business\'s primary activities, such as interest earned on deposits in a <a href="/wiki/Demand_account" class="mw-redirect" title="Demand account">demand account</a>. This is included in revenue but not included in net sales.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Sales revenue does not include <a href="/wiki/Sales_tax" title="Sales tax">sales tax</a> collected by the business.\n</p><p><b>Other revenue</b> (a.k.a. non-operating revenue) is revenue from peripheral (non-core) operations. For example, a company that manufactures and sells automobiles would record the revenue from the sale of an automobile as "regular" revenue. If that same company also rented a portion of one of its buildings, it would record that revenue as \xe2\x80\x9cother revenue\xe2\x80\x9d and disclose it separately on its income statement to show that it is from something other than its core operations. The combination of all the revenue generating systems of a business is called its <a href="/wiki/Revenue_model" title="Revenue model">revenue model</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Accounting_terms">Accounting terms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Revenue&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Accounting terms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<dl><dd><a href="/wiki/Net_sales" class="mw-redirect" title="Net sales">Net sales</a> = <a href="/wiki/Gross_sales" class="mw-redirect" title="Gross sales">gross sales</a> \xe2\x80\x93 (customer discounts, returns, and allowances)</dd>\n<dd><a href="/wiki/Gross_profit" class="mw-redirect" title="Gross profit">Gross profit</a> = <a href="/wiki/Net_sales" class="mw-redirect" title="Net sales">net sales</a> \xe2\x80\x93 <a href="/wiki/Cost_of_goods_sold" title="Cost of goods sold">cost of goods sold</a></dd>\n<dd><a href="/wiki/Operating_profit" class="mw-redirect" title="Operating profit">Operating profit</a> = <a href="/wiki/Gross_profit" class="mw-redirect" title="Gross profit">gross profit</a> \xe2\x80\x93 total <a href="/wiki/Operating_expense" title="Operating expense">operating expenses</a></dd>\n<dd><a href="/wiki/Net_profit" class="mw-redirect" title="Net profit">Net profit</a> = <a href="/wiki/Operating_profit" class="mw-redirect" title="Operating profit">operating profit</a> \xe2\x80\x93 taxes \xe2\x80\x93 interest</dd>\n<dd><a href="/wiki/Net_profit" class="mw-redirect" title="Net profit">Net profit</a> = <a href="/wiki/Net_sales" class="mw-redirect" title="Net sales">net sales</a> \xe2\x80\x93 <a href="/wiki/Cost_of_goods_sold" title="Cost of goods sold">cost of goods sold</a> \xe2\x80\x93 <a href="/wiki/Operating_expense" title="Operating expense">operating expense</a> \xe2\x80\x93 taxes \xe2\x80\x93 interest</dd></dl>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Financial_statement_analysis">Financial statement analysis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Revenue&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Financial statement analysis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Financial_statement_analysis" title="Financial statement analysis">Financial statement analysis</a></div>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Revenue_management#Forecasting" title="Revenue management">Revenue management \xc2\xa7&#160;Forecasting</a></div>\n<p>Revenue is a crucial part of financial statement analysis. The company\xe2\x80\x99s performance is measured to the extent to which its asset inflows (revenues) compare with its asset outflows (<a href="/wiki/Expenses" class="mw-redirect" title="Expenses">expenses</a>). <a href="/wiki/Net_income" title="Net income">Net income</a> is the result of this equation, but revenue typically enjoys equal attention during a standard <a href="/wiki/Earnings_call" title="Earnings call">earnings call</a>. If a company displays solid \xe2\x80\x9ctop-line growth\xe2\x80\x9d, analysts could view the period\xe2\x80\x99s performance as positive even if earnings growth, or \xe2\x80\x9cbottom-line growth\xe2\x80\x9d is stagnant. Conversely, high net income growth would be tainted if a company failed to produce significant revenue growth. Consistent revenue growth, if accompanied by net income growth, contributes to the value of an enterprise and therefore the <a href="/wiki/Stock" title="Stock">stock</a> price.\n</p><p>Revenue is used as an indication of earnings quality. There are several <a href="/wiki/Financial_ratios" class="mw-redirect" title="Financial ratios">financial ratios</a> attached to it:\n</p>\n<ul><li>The most important being <a href="/wiki/Gross_margin" title="Gross margin">gross margin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Profit_margin" title="Profit margin">profit margin</a>; also, companies use revenue to determine <a href="/wiki/Bad_debt" title="Bad debt">bad debt expense</a> using the income statement method.</li>\n<li>Price / Sales is sometimes used as a substitute for a <a href="/wiki/P/E_ratio" class="mw-redirect" title="P/E ratio">Price to earnings ratio</a> when earnings are negative and the P/E is meaningless. Though a company may have negative earnings, it almost always has positive revenue.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Gross_Margin" class="mw-redirect" title="Gross Margin">Gross Margin</a> is a calculation of revenue less <a href="/wiki/Cost_of_goods_sold" title="Cost of goods sold">cost of goods sold</a>, and is used to determine how well sales cover direct variable costs relating to the production of goods.</li>\n<li>Net income/sales, or <a href="/wiki/Profit_margin" title="Profit margin">profit margin</a>, is calculated by investors to determine how efficiently a company turns revenues into profits.</li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Government_revenue">Government revenue</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Revenue&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Government revenue">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Government_revenue" title="Government revenue">Government revenue</a></div>\n<p>Government revenue includes all amounts of money (i.e., taxes and fees) received from sources outside the government entity. Large governments usually have an <a href="/wiki/Government_agency" title="Government agency">agency</a> or <a href="/wiki/Departmentalization" title="Departmentalization">department</a> responsible for collecting government revenue from companies and individuals.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Government revenue may also include <a href="/wiki/Reserve_bank" class="mw-redirect" title="Reserve bank">reserve bank</a> <a href="/wiki/Currency" title="Currency">currency</a> which is printed. This is recorded as an advance to the retail bank together with a corresponding currency in circulation expense entry, that is, the income derived from the Official Cash rate payable by the retail banks for instruments such as 90-day bills. There is a question as to whether using generic business-based accounting standards can give a fair and accurate picture of government accounts, in that with a monetary policy statement to the reserve bank directing a positive inflation rate, the expense provision for the return of currency to the reserve bank is largely symbolic, such that to totally cancel the currency in circulation provision, all currency would have to be returned to the reserve bank and cancelled.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span
