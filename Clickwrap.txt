id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">\n\t\t<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>\n\t\t<div id="contentSub"></div>\n\t\t<div search</a>\n\t\t<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>A <b>clickwrap</b> or <b>clickthrough</b> <a href="/wiki/Contract" title="Contract">agreement</a> is a digital prompt that offers individuals the opportunity to accept or decline a digitally-mediated policy.<sup id="cite_ref-SMS_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SMS-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Privacy_policy" title="Privacy policy">Privacy policies</a>, <a href="/wiki/Terms_of_service" title="Terms of service">terms of service</a> and other user policies, as well as <a href="/wiki/Copyright" title="Copyright">copyright</a> policies commonly employ the clickwrap prompt. Clickwraps are common in signup processes for <a href="/wiki/Social_media" title="Social media">social media</a> services like <a href="/wiki/Facebook" title="Facebook">Facebook</a>, <a href="/wiki/Twitter" title="Twitter">Twitter</a> or <a href="/wiki/Tumblr" title="Tumblr">Tumblr</a>, connections to wireless networks operated in corporate spaces, as part of the installation processes of many software packages, and in other circumstances where agreement is sought using digital media. The name "clickwrap" is derived from the use of "<a href="/wiki/Shrink_wrap_contracts" class="mw-redirect" title="Shrink wrap contracts">shrink wrap contracts</a>" commonly used in boxed software purchases, which "contain a notice that by tearing open the <a href="/wiki/Shrink_wrap" title="Shrink wrap">shrinkwrap</a>, the user assents to the software terms enclosed within".<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The content and form of clickwrap agreements vary widely. Most clickwrap agreements require the <a href="/wiki/End-user" class="mw-redirect" title="End-user">end-user</a> to <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/manifest" class="extiw" title="wikt:manifest">manifest</a> his or her <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Special:Search/assent" class="extiw" title="wikt:Special:Search/assent">assent</a> by clicking an "ok" or "agree" <a href="/wiki/Button_(computing)" title="Button (computing)">button</a> on a <a href="/wiki/Dialog_box" title="Dialog box">dialog box</a> or pop-up window.  A user indicates <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/rejection" class="extiw" title="wikt:rejection">rejection</a> by clicking cancel or closing the window. If the user opts to reject the terms, they cannot use or purchase the <a href="/wiki/Product_(business)" title="Product (business)">product</a> or <a href="/wiki/Service_(economics)" title="Service (economics)">service</a>.  Classically, such a take-it-or-leave-it contract is described as a "<a href="/wiki/Contract_of_adhesion" class="mw-redirect" title="Contract of adhesion">contract of adhesion</a>, which is a contract that lacks bargaining power, forcing one party to be favored over the other."\n</p><p>The terms of service or license do not always appear on the same <a href="/wiki/Webpage" class="mw-redirect" title="Webpage">webpage</a> or window, but are always accessible before acceptance, such as through a <a href="/wiki/Hyperlink" title="Hyperlink">hyperlink</a> embedded in the product\'s webpage or a pop-up screen prior to installation. In order to be deemed to have accepted the terms of service, the purchaser must be put on notice that certain terms of service may apply. If the terms of service are not visible and/or accessible, courts have found the notice requirement to be lacking and as such, the purchaser may not be bound to the terms of the agreement. An analysis of the terms of service of major consumer websites has found that they frequently contain clauses that impede consumer rights in substantial and often unexpected ways.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc" role="navigation" aria-labelledby="mw-toc-heading"><input type="checkbox" role="button" href="/w/index.php?title=Clickwrap&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Legal consequences">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Clickwrap&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/In_the_Matter_of_Sears_Holdings_Management_Corporation" class="mw-redirect" title="In the Matter of Sears Holdings Management Corporation">In the Matter of Sears Holdings Management Corporation</a></div>\n<p>Few cases have considered the validity of clickwrap licenses. Still, in the cases that have challenged their validity, the terms of the contract have usually been upheld:\n</p>\n<ul><li><i>Feldman v. Google, Inc.</i>, 513 F.Supp.2d 229 (E.D.Pa. 2007) (upholding forum-selection clause)</li>\n<li><i>In re RealNetworks, Inc. Privacy Litigation</i>, No. No. 00-1366, 2000 WL 631341 (D. Ill. May 8, 2000) (upholding an arbitration clause)</li>\n<li><i>Hotmail Corp. v. Van$ Money Pie</i>, No. 98-20064, 1998 WL 388389 (N.D. Cal. Apr. 16, 1998) (granting <a href="/wiki/Preliminary_injunction" class="mw-redirect" title="Preliminary injunction">preliminary injunction</a> for alleged breach of <a href="/wiki/Contract" title="Contract">contract</a> for violating the <a href="/wiki/Terms_of_service" title="Terms of service">terms of service</a> by using a <a href="/wiki/Hotmail" class="mw-redirect" title="Hotmail">Hotmail</a> account to send <a href="/wiki/E-mail_spam" class="mw-redirect" title="E-mail spam">spam</a> or <a href="/wiki/Pornography" title="Pornography">pornography</a>). The court said that clicking the clickwrap button after notice gave consent.</li>\n<li><i>I. Lan Sys., Inc. v. Netscout Serv. Level Corp.</i>, 183 F. Supp. 2d 328, 336 (D. Mass. 2002) (upholding a clickwrap agreement on two grounds: first, clickwrap is simply "Money now, terms later" contract formation; second, the court found that the "additional terms" of the clickwrap license was not material under <a href="/wiki/Uniform_Commercial_Code" title="Uniform Commercial Code">UCC</a> (\xc2\xa7207(2)(b)).</li>\n<li><i>Caspi v. Microsoft, LLC</i>, held a forum selection clause in an online membership agreement was consented to when the user clicked the "I agree" symbol of the agreement in order to proceed with registration.</li>\n<li>In <i>A.V., et al. v iParadigms, LLC</i>, Judge Claude M. Hilton granted summary judgment on the students\' complaint in favor of iParadigms/<a href="/wiki/Turnitin" title="Turnitin">Turnitin</a>, because they had accepted the click-wrap agreement on the Turnitin website.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>\n<p>Even though courts have ruled some clickwrap licenses to be enforceable contracts, it does not follow that every term of every clickwrap license is enforceable. Clickwrap licenses must still meet the criteria for enforceability of a unilateral form contract. For example, see <i><a href="/wiki/Bragg_v._Linden_Research,_Inc." class="mw-redirect" title="Bragg v. Linden Research, Inc.">Bragg v. Linden Research, Inc.</a></i>, 487 F.Supp.2d 593 (E.D. Pa. 2007), in which the judge found certain aspects of the <i><a href="/wiki/Second_Life" title="Second Life">Second Life</a></i> clickwrap agreement "unconscionable, and therefore unenforceable."<a rel="nofollow" class="external autonumber" href="http://pub.bna.com/eclr/064925_053007.pdf">[1]</a>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cases_in_detail">Cases in detail</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Clickwrap&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Cases in detail">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In <a href="/wiki/Register.com,_Inc._v._Verio,_Inc." class="mw-redirect" title="Register.com, Inc. v. Verio, Inc.">Register.com, Inc. v. Verio, Inc.</a>, 356 F.3d 393 (2d. Cir. 2004), the court described a clickwrap license, even though the license in question was distinguished from a clickwrap license\n</p>\n<blockquote><p>Essentially, under a clickwrap arrangement, potential licensees are presented with the proposed license terms and forced to expressly and unambiguously manifest either assent or rejection prior to being given access to the product.</p></blockquote>\n<p>An earlier case, <i><a href="/wiki/Specht_v._Netscape_Communications_Corp." title="Specht v. Netscape Communications Corp.">Specht v. Netscape Communications Corp.</a></i>, 150 F.Supp.2d 585 (S.D.N.Y. 2001), <i>aff\'d</i>, 306 F.3d 17 (2d. Cir. 2002), gave perhaps the clearest definition of a clickwrap license.\n</p>\n<blockquote><p>A click-wrap license presents the user with a message on his or her <a href="/wiki/Computer_screen" class="mw-redirect" title="Computer screen">computer screen</a>, requiring that the user manifest his or her assent to the terms of the license agreement  by clicking on an <a href="/wiki/Icon_(computing)" title="Icon (computing)">icon</a>. n12 The product cannot be obtained or used unless and until the icon is clicked. For example, when a user attempts to obtain <a href="/wiki/Netscape" title="Netscape">Netscape</a>\'s Communicator or Navigator, a web page appears containing the full text of the Communicator / Navigator license agreement. Plainly visible on the screen is the query, "Do you accept all the terms of the preceding license agreement? If so, click on the Yes button. If you select No, Setup will close." Below this text are three button or icons: one labeled "Back" and used to return to an earlier step of the <a href="/wiki/Download" title="Download">download</a> preparation; one labeled "No," which if clicked, terminates the download; and one labeled "Yes," which if clicked, allows the download to proceed. Unless the user clicks "Yes," indicating his or her assent to the license agreement, the user cannot obtain the <a href="/wiki/Software" title="Software">software</a>.</p></blockquote>\n<p>The clickwrap method was presented to the court in <i><a href="/wiki/ProCD_v._Zeidenberg" class="mw-redirect" title="ProCD v. Zeidenberg">ProCD v. Zeidenberg</a></i>, 86 F.3d 1447 (7th Cir. 1996), where Zeidenberg purchased a CD-ROM, created by ProCD, which contained a compilation of a telephone directory database. Upon purchase of this CD-ROM, Zeidenberg installed the software onto his computer then created a website which offered to visitors the information contained on the CD-ROM at a price less than what ProCD charged for the software. Prior to his purchase of the software, Zeidenberg may not have been aware of any prohibited use or dissemination of the product without consent by ProCD. However, upon preparing to install the software onto his computer, the software license appeared on his computer screen and would not allow him to continue with the installation without indicating acceptance by clicking his assent in a dialog box. The court held that Zeidenberg did accept the offer and the terms contained within the license by clicking through the dialog box.  Zeidenberg had the opportunity to read the terms of the license prior to clicking the acceptance box. The court further stated that Zeidenberg could have rejected the terms of the contract and returned the software. (<i>Id.</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>More recently, in the 2017 opinion <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://cases.justia.com/federal/appellate-courts/ca2/16-2750/16-2750-2017-08-17.pdf?ts=1502980212">Meyer v. Uber Technologies</a>, the Second Circuit of the United States Court of Appeal held that users were on fair notice of the arbitration provision in <a href="/wiki/Uber" title="Uber">Uber\'s</a> registration process, because Uber presented the app\xe2\x80\x99s terms of service via hyperlink.  \xe2\x80\x9cWhile it may be the case that many users will not bother reading the additional terms, that is the choice the user makes,\xe2\x80\x9d Judge Chin wrote. \xe2\x80\x9cThe user is still on inquiry notice."  The Court further held that \xe2\x80\x9c[w]hen considering the perspective of a reasonable smartphone user, we need not presume that the user has never before encountered an app or entered into a contract using a smartphone..." Instead, the Court explained that \xe2\x80\x9c[a] reasonable user would know that by clicking the registration button, he was agreeing to the terms and conditions accessible via the hyperlink, whether he clicked on the hyperlink or not.\xe2\x80\x9d<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> \n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="European_Union">European Union</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Clickwrap&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: European Union">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>On 21 May 2015, the <a href="/wiki/European_Court_of_Justice" title="European Court of Justice">European Court of Justice</a> decided in the case of El Majdoub v. CarsOnTheWeb.Deutschland GmbH (case n\xc2\xb0 C-322/14), on a referral from a German court,<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> that click-wrap agreements are acceptable under certain circumstances as a "durable record" of the acceptance of general conditions within the meaning of Regulation 44/2001 (now replaced by Regulation 1215/2012, also known as the \'Brussels I Recast Regulation\').<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Clickwrap&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: See also">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Browse_wrap" title="Browse wrap">Browse wrap</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Electronic_signature" title="Electronic signature">Electronic signature</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Electronic_Signatures_in_Global_and_National_Commerce_Act" title="Electronic Signatures in Global and National Commerce Act">Electronic Signatures in Global and National Commerce Act</a> (ESIGN)</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Rudder_v._Microsoft_Corp." title="Rudder v.
