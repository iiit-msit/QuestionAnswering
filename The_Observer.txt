title="Sunday editions">published on Sundays</a>. In the same place on the <a href="/wiki/Political_spectrum" title="Political spectrum">political spectrum</a> as its sister papers <i><a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">The Guardian</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Guardian_Weekly" title="The Guardian Weekly">The Guardian Weekly</a></i>, whose parent company <a href="/wiki/Guardian_Media_Group" title="Guardian Media Group">Guardian Media Group Limited</a> acquired it in 1993, it takes a <a href="/wiki/Social_liberalism" title="Social liberalism">social liberal</a> or <a href="/wiki/Social_democracy" title="Social democracy">social democratic</a> line on most issues. First published in 1791, it is the world\'s oldest Sunday newspaper.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc" href="/w/index.php?title=The_Observer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">Origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Observer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The first issue, published on 4 December 1791 by W.S. Bourne, was the world\'s first <a href="/wiki/Sunday_newspaper" class="mw-redirect" title="Sunday newspaper">Sunday newspaper</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-theguardian.com_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-theguardian.com-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Believing that the paper would be a means of wealth, Bourne instead soon found himself facing debts of nearly \xc2\xa31,600. Though early editions purported editorial independence, Bourne attempted to cut his losses and sell the title to the government. When this failed, Bourne\'s brother (a wealthy businessman) made an offer to the government, which also refused to buy the paper but agreed to subsidise it in return for influence over its editorial content. As a result, the paper soon took a strong line against radicals such as <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Paine" title="Thomas Paine">Thomas Paine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Francis_Burdett" title="Francis Burdett">Francis Burdett</a> and <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Priestley" title="Joseph Priestley">Joseph Priestley</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="19th_century">19th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Observer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 19th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In 1807, the brothers decided to relinquish editorial control, naming <a href="/wiki/Lewis_Doxat" title="Lewis Doxat">Lewis Doxat</a> as the new editor. Seven years later, the brothers sold <i>The Observer</i> to <a href="/wiki/William_Innell_Clement" title="William Innell Clement">William Innell Clement</a>, a newspaper proprietor who owned a number of publications. The paper continued to receive government subsidies during this period; in 1819, of the approximately 23,000 copies of the paper distributed weekly, approximately 10,000 were given away as "specimen copies", distributed by postmen who were paid to deliver them to "lawyers, doctors, and gentlemen of the town."<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Yet the paper began to demonstrate a more independent editorial stance, criticising the authorities\' handling of the events surrounding the <a href="/wiki/Peterloo_Massacre" title="Peterloo Massacre">Peterloo Massacre</a> and defying an 1820 court order against publishing details of the trial of the <a href="/wiki/Cato_Street_Conspiracy" title="Cato Street Conspiracy">Cato Street Conspirators</a>, who were alleged to have plotted to murder members of the Cabinet. The <a href="/wiki/Woodcut" title="Woodcut">woodcut</a> pictures published of the stable and hayloft where the conspirators were arrested reflected a new stage of illustrated journalism that the newspaper pioneered during this time.\n</p><p>Clement maintained ownership of <i>The Observer</i> until his death in 1852. During that time, the paper supported <a href="/wiki/Reform_Act_1832" title="Reform Act 1832">parliamentary reform</a>, but opposed a broader franchise and the <a href="/wiki/Chartism" title="Chartism">Chartist</a> leadership. After Doxat retired in 1857, Clement\'s heirs sold the paper to Joseph Snowe, who also took over the editor\'s chair. Under Snowe, the paper adopted a more liberal political stance, supporting the North during the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">American Civil War</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-observerTextTimeline_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-observerTextTimeline-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> and endorsing universal manhood suffrage in 1866.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> These positions contributed to a decline in circulation during this time.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>In 1870, wealthy businessman <a href="/wiki/Julius_Beer" title="Julius Beer">Julius Beer</a> bought the paper and appointed <a href="/wiki/Edward_Dicey" title="Edward Dicey">Edward Dicey</a> as editor, whose efforts succeeded in reviving circulation. Though Beer\'s son Frederick became the owner upon Julius\'s death in 1880, he had little interest in the newspaper and was content to leave Dicey as editor until 1889. Henry Duff Traill took over the editorship after Dicey\'s departure, only to be replaced in 1891 by Frederick\'s wife, <a href="/wiki/Rachel_Beer" title="Rachel Beer">Rachel Beer</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-theguardian.com_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-theguardian.com-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> of the <a href="/wiki/Sassoon_family" title="Sassoon family">Sassoon family</a>. Though circulation declined during her tenure, she remained as editor for thirteen years, combining it in 1893 with the editorship of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Sunday_Times" title="The Sunday Times">The Sunday Times</a></i>, a newspaper that she had also bought.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="20th_century">20th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Observer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 20th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Upon Frederick\'s death in 1903, the paper was purchased by the newspaper magnate <a href="/wiki/Lord_Northcliffe" class="mw-redirect" title="Lord Northcliffe">Lord Northcliffe</a>. After maintaining the existing editorial leadership for a couple of years, in 1908 Northcliffe named <a href="/wiki/James_Louis_Garvin" title="James Louis Garvin">James Louis Garvin</a> as editor. Garvin quickly turned the paper into an organ of political influence, boosting circulation from 5,000 to 40,000 within a year of his arrival as a result. Yet the revival in the paper\'s fortunes masked growing political disagreements between Garvin and Northcliffe. These disagreements ultimately led Northcliffe to sell the paper to <a href="/wiki/William_Waldorf_Astor" title="William Waldorf Astor">William Waldorf Astor</a> in 1911, who transferred ownership to his son <a href="/wiki/Waldorf_Astor,_2nd_Viscount_Astor" title="Waldorf Astor, 2nd Viscount Astor">Waldorf Astor, 2nd Viscount Astor</a> four years later.\n</p><p>During this period, the Astors were content to leave the control of the paper in Garvin\'s hands. Under his editorship circulation reached 200,000 during the interwar years, a figure which Garvin fought to maintain even during the depths of the <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Great Depression in the United Kingdom">Great Depression</a>. Politically the paper pursued an independent <a href="/wiki/Conservative_Party_(UK)" title="Conservative Party (UK)">Conservative</a> stance, which eventually brought Garvin into conflict with Waldorf\'s more liberal son <a href="/wiki/David_Astor" title="David Astor">David Astor</a>. Their conflict contributed to Garvin\'s departure as editor in 1942, after which the paper took the unusual step of declaring itself non-partisan.\n</p><p>Ownership passed to Waldorf\'s sons in 1948, with David taking over as editor. He remained in the position for 27 years, during which time he turned it into a trust-owned newspaper employing, among others, <a href="/wiki/George_Orwell" title="George Orwell">George Orwell</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paul_Jennings_(UK_author)" class="mw-redirect" title="Paul Jennings (UK author)">Paul Jennings</a> and <a href="/wiki/C._A._Lejeune" title="C. A. Lejeune">C. A. Lejeune</a>. Under Astor\'s editorship <i>The Observer</i> became the first national newspaper to oppose the government\'s <a href="/wiki/Suez_crisis" class="mw-redirect" title="Suez crisis">1956 invasion of Suez</a>, a move which cost it many readers. In 1977, the Astors sold the ailing newspaper to US oil giant <a href="/wiki/ARCO" title="ARCO">Atlantic Richfield</a> (now called ARCO) who sold it to <a href="/wiki/Lonrho" title="Lonrho">Lonrho plc</a> in 1981.\n</p><p>It became part of the <a href="/wiki/Guardian_Media_Group" title="Guardian Media Group">Guardian Media Group</a> in June 1993, after a rival bid to acquire it by <i><a href="/wiki/The_Independent" title="The Independent">The Independent</a></i> was rejected.<sup id="cite_ref-Indy_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Indy-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Farzad_Bazoft" title="Farzad Bazoft">Farzad Bazoft</a>, a journalist for <i>The Observer</i>, was executed in <a href="/wiki/Iraq" title="Iraq">Iraq</a> in 1990 on charges of spying. In 2003, <i>The Observer</i> interviewed the Iraqi colonel who had arrested and interrogated Bazoft and who was convinced that Bazoft was not a spy.<sup id="cite_ref-Farzad1_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Farzad1-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="21st_century">21st century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Observer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 21st century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In 2003 the editorial supported the Iraq war, stating "Military intervention in the Middle East holds many dangers. But if we want a lasting peace it may be the only option."<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>On 27 February 2005, <i>The Observer</i> Blog<sup id="cite_ref-ObserverBlog1_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ObserverBlog1-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> was launched, making <i>The Observer</i> the first newspaper to purposely document its own internal decisions, as well as the first newspaper to release <a href="/wiki/Podcast" title="Podcast">podcasts</a>. The paper\'s regular columnists include <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Rawnsley" title="Andrew Rawnsley">Andrew Rawnsley</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nick_Cohen" title="Nick Cohen">Nick Cohen</a>.\n</p><p>In addition to the weekly <i>Observer Magazine</i> <a href="/wiki/Colour_supplement" title="Colour supplement">colour supplement</a> which is still present every Sunday, for several years each issue of <i>The Observer</i> came with a different free monthly magazine. These magazines had the titles <i>Observer Sport Monthly</i>, <i>Observer Music Monthly</i>, <i>Observer Woman</i> and <i>Observer Food Monthly</i>.\n</p><p>Content from <i>The Observer</i> is included in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Guardian_Weekly" title="The Guardian Weekly">The Guardian Weekly</a></i> for an international readership.\n</p><p><i>The Observer</i> followed its daily partner <i><a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">The Guardian</a></i> and converted to <a href="/wiki/Berliner_(format)" title="Berliner (format)">Berliner</a> format on Sunday 8 January 2006.<sup id="cite_ref-GuardianObserverRelaunch1_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GuardianObserverRelaunch1-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-GuardianObserverRelaunch2_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GuardianObserverRelaunch2-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><i>The Observer</i> was awarded the <i>National Newspaper of the Year</i> at the <a href="/wiki/British_Press_Awards" class="mw-redirect" title="British Press Awards">British Press Awards</a> 2007.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Editor <a href="/wiki/Roger_Alton" title="Roger Alton">Roger Alton</a> stepped down at the end of 2007, and was replaced by his deputy, <a href="/wiki/John_Mulholland_(journalist)" title="John Mulholland (journalist)">John Mulholland</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-guardian-20080103_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-guardian-20080103-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In early 2010, the paper was restyled. An article on the paper\'s website previewing the new version stated that "The News section, which will incorporate Business and personal finance, will be home to a new section, Seven Days, offering a complete round-up of the previous week\'s main news from Britain and around the world, and will also focus on more analysis and comment."<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Supplements_and_features">Supplements and features</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Observer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Supplements and features">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>After the paper was rejuvenated in early 2010, the main paper came with only a small number of supplements \xe2\x80\x93 <i>Sport</i>, <i>The Observer Magazine</i>, <i>The New Review</i> and <i>The New York Times International Weekly</i>, an 8-page supplement of articles selected from <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> that has been distributed with the paper since 2007. Every four weeks the paper includes <i>The Observer Food Monthly</i> magazine, and in September 2013 it launched <i>Observer Tech Monthly</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> a science and technology section which won the Grand Prix at the 2014 Newspaper Awards.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Previously, the main paper had come with a larger range of supplements including <i>Sport</i>, <i>Business &amp; Media</i>, <i>Review</i>, <i>Escape</i> (a travel supplement), <i>The Observer Magazine</i> and various special interest monthlies, such as <i>Observer Food Monthly</i>, <i>Observer Women monthly</i> which was launched in 2006,<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> <i>Observer Sport Monthly</i> and <i>The Observer Film Magazine</i>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Newsroom">The Newsroom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Observer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: The Newsroom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><i>The Observer</i> and its sister newspaper <i>The Guardian</i> operate a visitor centre in London called The Newsroom.  It contains their archives, including bound copies of old editions, a photographic library and other items such as diaries, letters and notebooks. This material may be consulted by members of the public.  The Newsroom also mounts temporary exhibitions and runs an educational programme for schools.\n</p><p>In November 2007, <i>The Observer</i> and <i>The Guardian</i> made their archives available over the Internet.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> The current extent of the archives available are 1791 to 2000 for <i>The Observer</i> and 1821 to 2000 for <i>The Guardian</i>.  These archives will eventually go up to 2003.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Bans">Bans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Observer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Bans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The paper was banned in <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a> in February 2008 after reprinting <a href="/wiki/Jyllands-Posten_Muhammad_cartoons_controversy" title="Jyllands-Posten Muhammad cartoons controversy">cartoons</a> allegedly insulting <a href="/wiki/Muhammad" title="Muhammad">Muhammed</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Editors">Editors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Observer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Editors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<ul><li>W.
