href="/w/index.php?title=Bremen_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_years">Early years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bremen_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The beginnings of the airport date back to the early 20th century. The <i>Bremer Verein f\xc3\xbcr Luftschiffahrt</i>, a local aerospace club, conducted the first experimental flights at the present site in the summer of 1910, on what was then the parade ground of the local garrison. The <a href="/wiki/Senate_of_Bremen" title="Senate of Bremen">Senate of Bremen</a> supported the establishment of an airfield to connect Bremen to the growing airship route network. Official permission for the opening of an airport was granted on 16 May 1913. The initial infrastructure was geared towards aircraft operations instead of the initially envisaged airships. Several wooden hangars were erected.<sup id="cite_ref-Relikte_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Relikte-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>During <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>, the airport was taken into military administration, and civilian operations ceased. The military erected a wooden hangar, but conducted only a small number of operations from the airfield.<sup id="cite_ref-Relikte_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Relikte-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> After the war, the airport only reopened on 18 July 1920, with Dutch airline <a href="/wiki/KLM" title="KLM">KLM</a> beginning scheduled flights to <a href="/wiki/Amsterdam" title="Amsterdam">Amsterdam</a> soon thereafter. In the same year, the <a href="/wiki/Weimar_National_Assembly" title="Weimar National Assembly">Weimar National Assembly</a> authorised investment into upgraded facilities at the airport. Administration of the airport was transferred to the newly founded <i>Bremer Flughafengesellschaft</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Airport_history_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Airport_history-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> In 1923, the aeroplane manufacturer <a href="/wiki/Focke-Wulf" title="Focke-Wulf">Focke-Wulf</a> was founded on a site adjacent to the airfield.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="World_War_II">World War II</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bremen_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: World War II">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In the 1930s, several new terminal buildings and hangars were constructed, with the largest to date being completed in 1937. In the same year, four new runways were built. These were arranged in a star-like pattern. The increasing military buildup under the rule of the <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Party" title="Nazi Party">Nazis</a> also began to show itself at the airport, with the <a href="/wiki/Luftwaffe" title="Luftwaffe">Luftwaffe</a> establishing a flight training base there. Civilian operations again came to a standstill with the beginning of <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>. For a short period between November 1939 and June 1940, the airport served as the base for a squadron of <a href="/wiki/Focke-Wulf_Fw_200" class="mw-redirect" title="Focke-Wulf Fw 200">Focke-Wulf Fw 200</a> bombers. In the later stages of the war, the airport came under repeated bombardment due to co-location with the Focke-Wulf plant. This left most of the infrastructure destroyed or severely damaged by the end of the war.<sup id="cite_ref-Relikte_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Relikte-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army" title="United States Army">United States Army</a> took over the airport and the adjacent aircraft plant in 1945 for use as an airbase. After conducting the necessary repairs, it operated mostly transport aircraft into and out of the American enclave within otherwise British-occupied northern Germany. Control was handed back to the Bremen authorities in 1949. Civilian operations resumed that year with <a href="/wiki/Scandinavian_Airlines" title="Scandinavian Airlines">Scandinavian Airlines</a> using Bremen Airport as a stopover on routes from <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">Scandinavia</a> to <a href="/wiki/Geneva" title="Geneva">Geneva</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vienna" title="Vienna">Vienna</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-SAS_Timetable_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SAS_Timetable-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Runway 09-27 was extended to 2.000 m.<sup id="cite_ref-Relikte_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Relikte-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Development_since_the_1950s">Development since the 1950s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bremen_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Development since the 1950s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In the mid-1950s, the terminal buildings were reconstructed and <a href="/wiki/Lufthansa" title="Lufthansa">Lufthansa</a> began scheduled flights to the airport. The German airline also established its pilot training operations (<a href="/wiki/Lufthansa_Flight_Training" class="mw-redirect" title="Lufthansa Flight Training">Lufthansa Flight Training</a>) at the airport. During the 1960s, scheduled jet flights began to be operated at Bremen. In 1971, a large radar system was installed on the southern perimeter of the airport.<sup id="cite_ref-Relikte_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Relikte-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>1989 was the first year that the airport had more than one million passengers. The current terminal building was opened in 1998.<sup id="cite_ref-redesign_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-redesign-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In January 2016 the airport\'s operator announced that the main terminal building would undergo major redesign and renovation works until 2018. Terminal sections 1, 2 and 3 were merged amongst several other changes.<sup id="cite_ref-redesign_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-redesign-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> In May 2016 the airport introduced its new <a href="/wiki/Brand" title="Brand">brand</a> with <i>BRE Bremen Airport</i> replacing <i>City Airport Bremen</i> .<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In February 2017 <a href="/wiki/British_Airways" title="British Airways">British Airways</a> announced it would end its flights from Bremen to <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a> and <a href="/wiki/Manchester" title="Manchester">Manchester</a>, which were both operated by <a href="/wiki/Sun-Air_of_Scandinavia" title="Sun-Air of Scandinavia">SUN-AIR</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> SUN-AIR maintained a base for the routes at Bremen Airport. In April 2017 the airport announced it was changing its name to Bremen Airport <a href="/wiki/Hans_Koschnick" title="Hans Koschnick">Hans Koschnick</a>, after the former mayor and honorable citizen of Bremen.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In October 2018, <a href="/wiki/Ryanair" title="Ryanair">Ryanair</a> announced it would be closing its base at the airport on 5 November 2018.<sup id="cite_ref-closure_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-closure-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In February 2021, <a href="/wiki/Lufthansa_Aviation_Training" title="Lufthansa Aviation Training">Lufthansa Aviation Training</a> announced it would relocate its entire German practical training from Bremen Airport to <a href="/wiki/Rostock_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Rostock Airport">Rostock Airport</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Facilities">Facilities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bremen_Airport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Facilities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The airport consists of one main passenger terminal building, split into sections <i>Terminal 1, 2</i> and <i>3</i><sup id="cite_ref-maps_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-maps-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> that features several shops, restaurants and service facilities as well five aircraft stands equipped with <a href="/wiki/Jet_bridge" title="Jet bridge">jet bridges</a> and some additional stands for mid-sized aircraft on the apron. The main building contains the check-in counters 5\xe2\x80\x9319 and 21\xe2\x80\x9338.<sup id="cite_ref-maps_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-maps-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Ryanair" title="Ryanair">Ryanair</a> uses another more basic facility to the west of the main terminal called <i>Terminal E</i> which only features walk-boarding and features the check-in counters 1E-4E.<sup id="cite_ref-maps_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-maps-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <i>Bremenhalle</i> inside the airport hosts a small aviation and space exploration museum, displaying the <a href="/wiki/Junkers_W33" class="mw-redirect" title="Junkers W33">Junkers W33</a> <a href="/wiki/Bremen_(aircraft)" title="Bremen (aircraft)"><i>Bremen</i></a> and the first <a href="/wiki/Spacelab" title="Spacelab">Spacelab</a> module.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Airlines_and_destinations">Airlines and destinations</span><span
