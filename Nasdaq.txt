id="1971.E2.80.932000"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1971\xe2\x80\x932000">1971\xe2\x80\x932000</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nasdaq&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: 1971\xe2\x80\x932000">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:NASDAQ.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/9e/NASDAQ.svg/220px-NASDAQ.svg.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="44" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/9e/NASDAQ.svg/330px-NASDAQ.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/9e/NASDAQ.svg/440px-NASDAQ.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="200" data-file-height="40" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:NASDAQ.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Former logo used from 1971 to 2014</div></div></div>\n<p>"Nasdaq" was initially an <a href="/wiki/Acronym" title="Acronym">acronym</a> for the National Association of Securities Dealers Automated Quotations.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> It was founded in 1971 by the National Association of Securities Dealers (NASD), now known as the <a href="/wiki/Financial_Industry_Regulatory_Authority" title="Financial Industry Regulatory Authority">Financial Industry Regulatory Authority</a> (FINRA).<sup id="cite_ref-history_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> On February 8, 1971, the Nasdaq stock market began operations as the world\'s first electronic stock market.<sup id="cite_ref-history_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> At first, it was merely a "quotation system" and did not provide a way to perform electronic trades.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The Nasdaq Stock Market helped lower the <a href="/wiki/Bid%E2%80%93ask_spread" title="Bid\xe2\x80\x93ask spread">bid\xe2\x80\x93ask spread</a> (the price difference between sellers and buyers), but was unpopular among brokers as it reduced their profits.\n</p><p>The NASDAQ Stock Market eventually assumed the majority of major trades that had been executed by the <a href="/wiki/Over-the-counter_(finance)" title="Over-the-counter (finance)">over-the-counter</a> (OTC) system of trading, but there are still many securities traded in this fashion. As late as 1987, the Nasdaq exchange was still commonly referred to as "OTC" in media reports<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> and also in the monthly Stock Guides (stock guides and procedures) issued by <a href="/wiki/Standard_%26_Poor%27s" class="mw-redirect" title="Standard &amp; Poor&#39;s">Standard &amp; Poor\'s</a> Corporation.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Over the years, it became more of a stock market by adding trade and volume reporting and automated trading systems. In 1981, Nasdaq traded 37% of the U.S. securities markets\' total of 21&#160;billion shares. By 1991, Nasdaq\'s share had grown to 46%.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> In 1992, the Nasdaq Stock Market joined with the <a href="/wiki/London_Stock_Exchange" title="London Stock Exchange">London Stock Exchange</a> to form the first intercontinental linkage of <a href="/wiki/Capital_market" title="Capital market">capital markets</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> In 1998, it became the first stock market in the United States to trade online, using the slogan "the stock market for the next hundred years".<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> The Nasdaq Stock Market attracted many companies during the <a href="/wiki/Dot-com_bubble" title="Dot-com bubble">dot-com bubble</a>.\n</p><p>Its main index is the <a href="/wiki/NASDAQ_Composite" class="mw-redirect" title="NASDAQ Composite">NASDAQ Composite</a>, which has been published since its inception. The QQQ <a href="/wiki/Exchange-traded_fund" title="Exchange-traded fund">exchange-traded fund</a> tracks the large-cap <a href="/wiki/NASDAQ-100" class="mw-redirect" title="NASDAQ-100">NASDAQ-100</a> index, which was introduced in 1985 alongside the <a href="/wiki/NASDAQ_Financial-100" title="NASDAQ Financial-100">NASDAQ Financial-100</a> Index, which tracks the largest 100 companies in terms of market capitalization.\n</p>\n<h3><span id="2000.E2.80.93present"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="2000\xe2\x80\x93present">2000\xe2\x80\x93present</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nasdaq&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 2000\xe2\x80\x93present">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Nasdaq_Composite_dot-com_bubble.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/Nasdaq_Composite_dot-com_bubble.svg/400px-Nasdaq_Composite_dot-com_bubble.svg.png" decoding="async" width="400" height="224" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/Nasdaq_Composite_dot-com_bubble.svg/600px-Nasdaq_Composite_dot-com_bubble.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/Nasdaq_Composite_dot-com_bubble.svg/800px-Nasdaq_Composite_dot-com_bubble.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="500" data-file-height="280" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Nasdaq_Composite_dot-com_bubble.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>During the <a href="/wiki/Dot-com_bubble" title="Dot-com bubble">dot-com bubble</a>, the <a href="/wiki/NASDAQ_Composite" class="mw-redirect" title="NASDAQ Composite">NASDAQ Composite</a> index spiked in the late 1990s. It then fell sharply as the bubble burst.</div></div></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:NASDAQ_Market_Site_201506.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/NASDAQ_Market_Site_201506.jpg/250px-NASDAQ_Market_Site_201506.jpg" decoding="async" width="250" height="167" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/NASDAQ_Market_Site_201506.jpg/375px-NASDAQ_Market_Site_201506.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/NASDAQ_Market_Site_201506.jpg/500px-NASDAQ_Market_Site_201506.jpg 2x" data-file-width="5472" data-file-height="3648" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:NASDAQ_Market_Site_201506.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Studio</div></div></div>\n<p>On March 10, 2000, the <a href="/wiki/NASDAQ_Composite" class="mw-redirect" title="NASDAQ Composite">NASDAQ Composite</a> <a href="/wiki/Stock_market_index" title="Stock market index">stock market index</a> peaked at 5,132.52, but fell to 3,227 by April 17,<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> and, in the following 30 months, fell 78% from its peak.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In a series of sales in 2000 and 2001, FINRA sold its stake in the Nasdaq. On July 2, 2002, Nasdaq Inc. became a <a href="/wiki/Public_company" title="Public company">public company</a> via an <a href="/wiki/Initial_public_offering" title="Initial public offering">initial public offering</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> In 2006, the status of the Nasdaq Stock Market was changed from a stock market to a licensed national securities exchange.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> In 2007, it merged with OMX, a leading exchange operator in the Nordic countries, expanded its global footprint, and changed its name to the NASDAQ OMX Group.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>To qualify for listing on the exchange, a company must be registered with the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Securities_and_Exchange_Commission" class="mw-redirect" title="United States Securities and Exchange Commission">United States Securities and Exchange Commission</a> (SEC), must have at least three <a href="/wiki/Market_maker" title="Market maker">market makers</a> (financial firms that act as brokers or dealers for specific securities) and must meet minimum requirements for assets, capital, public shares, and shareholders.\n</p><p>In February 2011, in the wake of an announced merger of <a href="/wiki/NYSE_Euronext" title="NYSE Euronext">NYSE Euronext</a> with <a href="/wiki/Deutsche_B%C3%B6rse" title="Deutsche B\xc3\xb6rse">Deutsche B\xc3\xb6rse</a>, speculation developed that NASDAQ OMX and <a href="/wiki/Intercontinental_Exchange" title="Intercontinental Exchange">Intercontinental Exchange</a> (ICE) could mount a counter-bid of their own for NYSE. NASDAQ OMX could be<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (October 2016)">when?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> looking to acquire the American exchange\'s cash equities business, ICE the <a href="/wiki/Derivative_(finance)" title="Derivative (finance)">derivatives</a> business. At the time, "NYSE Euronext\'s market value was $9.75 billion. Nasdaq was valued at $5.78 billion, while ICE was valued at $9.45 billion."<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Late in the month, Nasdaq was reported to be considering asking either ICE or the <a href="/wiki/Chicago_Mercantile_Exchange" title="Chicago Mercantile Exchange">Chicago Mercantile Exchange</a> to join in what would probably have to be, if it proceeded, an $11\xe2\x80\x9312&#160;billion counterbid.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In December 2005, NASDAQ acquired <a href="/wiki/Instinet" title="Instinet">Instinet</a> for $1.9&#160;billion, retaining the <a href="/wiki/Inet" title="Inet">Inet</a> ECN and subsequently selling the agency brokerage business to <a href="/wiki/Silver_Lake_Partners" class="mw-redirect" title="Silver Lake Partners">Silver Lake Partners</a> and Instinet management.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The European Association of Securities Dealers Automatic Quotation System (EASDAQ) was founded as a European equivalent to the Nasdaq Stock Market. It was purchased by NASDAQ in 2001 and became NASDAQ Europe. In 2003, operations were shut down as a result of the burst of the <a href="/wiki/Dot-com_bubble" title="Dot-com bubble">dot-com bubble</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> In 2007, NASDAQ Europe was revived first as Equiduct and was acquired by <a href="/wiki/B%C3%B6rse_Berlin" title="B\xc3\xb6rse Berlin">B\xc3\xb6rse Berlin</a> later that year.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>On June 18, 2012, Nasdaq OMX became a founding member of the United Nations <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_Stock_Exchanges_Initiative" title="Sustainable Stock Exchanges Initiative">Sustainable Stock Exchanges Initiative</a> on the eve of the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Conference_on_Sustainable_Development" title="United Nations Conference on Sustainable Development">United Nations Conference on Sustainable Development</a> (Rio+20).<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In November 2016, <a href="/wiki/Chief_operating_officer" title="Chief operating officer">chief operating officer</a> <a href="/wiki/Adena_Friedman" title="Adena Friedman">Adena Friedman</a> was promoted to <a href="/wiki/Chief_executive_officer" title="Chief executive officer">chief executive officer</a>, becoming the first woman to run a major exchange in the U.S.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 2016, Nasdaq earned $272&#160;million in listings-related revenues.<sup id="cite_ref-wsj-new_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wsj-new-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In October 2018, the SEC ruled that the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Stock_Exchange" title="New York Stock Exchange">New York Stock Exchange</a> and Nasdaq did not justify the continued price increases when selling market data.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In December 2020, NASDAQ announced that it would strip its indexes of four Chinese companies in response to <a href="/wiki/Executive_Order_13959" title="Executive Order 13959">Executive Order 13959</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Quote_availability">Quote availability</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nasdaq&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Quote availability">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Nasdaq quotes are available at three levels:\n</p>\n<ul><li>Level 1 shows the highest bid and lowest ask\xe2\x80\x94inside quote.</li>\n<li>Level 2 shows all public quotes of <a href="/wiki/Market_maker" title="Market maker">market makers</a> together with information of market dealers wishing to buy or sell stock and recently executed orders.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>Level 3 is used by the <a href="/wiki/Market_maker" title="Market maker">market makers</a> and allows them to enter their quotes and execute orders.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Trading_schedule">Trading schedule</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nasdaq&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Trading schedule">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The Nasdaq Stock Market sessions, with times in the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Time_Zone" title="Eastern Time Zone">Eastern Time Zone</a> are:\n</p><p>7:00 a.m. to 9:30 a.m.: <a href="/wiki/Extended-hours_trading" title="Extended-hours trading">extended-hours trading</a> session (premarket)\n</p><p>9:30 a.m. to 4:00 p.m.: normal trading session\n</p><p>4:00 p.m. to 8:00 p.m.: extended-hours trading session (postmarket)<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Nasdaq Stock Market averages about 253 <a href="/wiki/Trading_day" title="Trading day">trading days</a> per year.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2020)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Market_tiers">Market tiers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nasdaq&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Market tiers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The Nasdaq Stock Market has three different market tiers:\n</p>\n<ul><li><b>Capital Market</b> (NASDAQ-CM <a href="/wiki/Market_capitalization" title="Market capitalization">small cap</a>) is an equity market for companies that have relatively small levels of market capitalization. Listing requirements for such "small cap" companies are less stringent than for other Nasdaq markets that list larger companies with significantly higher market capitalization.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li><b>Global Market</b> (NASDAQ-GM <a href="/wiki/Mid_cap" class="mw-redirect" title="Mid cap">mid cap</a>) is made up of stocks that represent the Nasdaq Global Market. The Global Market consists of 1,450 stocks that meet Nasdaq\'s strict financial and liquidity requirements, and corporate governance standards. The Global Market is less exclusive than the Global Select Market.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li><b>Global Select Market</b> (NASDAQ-GS <a href="/wiki/Large_cap" class="mw-redirect" title="Large cap">large cap</a>) is a market capitalization-weighted index made up of US-based and international stocks that represent the Global Select Market Composite. The Global Select Market consists of 1,200 stocks that meet Nasdaq\'s strict financial and liquidity requirements and corporate governance standards. The Global Select Market is more exclusive than the Global Market. Every October, the Nasdaq Listing Qualifications Department reviews the Global Market Composite to determine if any of its stocks have become eligible for listing on the Global Select Market.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>\n<h2><span
