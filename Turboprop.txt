right</div></div></div>\n<p>A <b>turboprop</b> engine is a <a href="/wiki/Turbine_engine" class="mw-redirect" title="Turbine engine">turbine engine</a> that drives an aircraft <a href="/wiki/Propeller_(aeronautics)" title="Propeller (aeronautics)">propeller</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>A turboprop consists of an intake, reduction gearbox, <a href="/wiki/Gas_compressor" class="mw-redirect" title="Gas compressor">compressor</a>, <a href="/wiki/Combustor" title="Combustor">combustor</a>, <a href="/wiki/Turbine" title="Turbine">turbine</a>, and a <a href="/wiki/Propelling_nozzle" title="Propelling nozzle">propelling nozzle</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Air is drawn into the intake and compressed by the compressor. Fuel is then added to the compressed air in the combustor, where the fuel-air mixture then <a href="/wiki/Combustion" title="Combustion">combusts</a>. The hot combustion gases expand through the turbine. Some of the power generated by the turbine is used to drive the compressor.\n</p><p>The rest is transmitted through the reduction gearing to the propeller. Further expansion of the gases occurs in the propelling nozzle, where the gases exhaust to atmospheric pressure. The propelling nozzle provides a relatively small proportion of the thrust generated by a turboprop.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In contrast to a <a href="/wiki/Turbojet" title="Turbojet">turbojet</a>, the engine\'s <a href="/wiki/Exhaust_gas" title="Exhaust gas">exhaust gases</a> do not contain enough energy to create significant thrust, since almost all of the engine\'s power is used to drive the propeller.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc" role="navigation" aria-labelledby="mw-toc-heading"><input type="checkbox" role="button" links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Technological_aspects">Technological aspects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Turboprop&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Technological aspects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Turboprop_operation-en.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Turboprop_operation-en.svg/220px-Turboprop_operation-en.svg.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="128" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Turboprop_operation-en.svg/330px-Turboprop_operation-en.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Turboprop_operation-en.svg/440px-Turboprop_operation-en.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="2149" data-file-height="1252" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Turboprop_operation-en.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Schematic diagram showing the operation of a turboprop engine</div></div></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Gas_turbine_efficiency.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Gas_turbine_efficiency.png/220px-Gas_turbine_efficiency.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="157" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Gas_turbine_efficiency.png/330px-Gas_turbine_efficiency.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Gas_turbine_efficiency.png/440px-Gas_turbine_efficiency.png 2x" data-file-width="700" data-file-height="500" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Gas_turbine_efficiency.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Propulsive efficiency comparison for various gas turbine engine configurations</div></div></div>\n<p>Exhaust thrust in a turboprop is sacrificed in favour of shaft power, which is obtained by extracting additional power (up to that necessary to drive the compressor) from turbine expansion. Owing to the additional expansion in the turbine system, the residual energy in the exhaust jet is low.<sup id="cite_ref-glennTur_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-glennTur-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-glennThr_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-glennThr-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-lyle_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lyle-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Consequently, the exhaust jet produces about 10% of the total thrust.<sup id="cite_ref-srm_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-srm-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> A higher proportion of the thrust comes from the propeller at low speeds and less at higher speeds.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Turboprops have <a href="/wiki/Bypass_ratio" title="Bypass ratio">bypass ratios</a> of 50-100<sup id="cite_ref-kroo_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kroo-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Spak_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Spak-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> although the propulsion airflow is less clearly defined for propellers than for fans.<sup id="cite_ref-glennProp_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-glennProp-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-walsh_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-walsh-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The propeller is coupled to the turbine through a <a href="/wiki/Reduction_gear" class="mw-redirect" title="Reduction gear">reduction gear</a> that converts the high <a href="/wiki/Revolutions_per_minute" title="Revolutions per minute">RPM</a>/low <a href="/wiki/Torque" title="Torque">torque</a> output to low RPM/high torque. The propeller itself is normally a <a href="/wiki/Constant-speed_propeller" class="mw-redirect" title="Constant-speed propeller">constant-speed (variable pitch) propeller</a> type similar to that used with larger aircraft <a href="/wiki/Reciprocating_engine" title="Reciprocating engine">reciprocating engines</a>, except that the propeller control requirements are very different.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Unlike the small diameter fans used in <a href="/wiki/Turbofan" title="Turbofan">turbofan</a> jet engines, the propeller has a large diameter that lets it accelerate a large volume of air. This permits a lower airstream velocity for a given amount of thrust. As it is more efficient at low speeds to accelerate a large amount of air by a small degree than a small amount of air by a large degree,<sup id="cite_ref-bevil_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bevil-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-bensen_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bensen-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> a low <a href="/wiki/Disc_loading" class="mw-redirect" title="Disc loading">disc loading</a> (thrust per unit disc area) increases the aircraft\'s energy efficiency, and this reduces the fuel use.<sup id="cite_ref-wayne_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wayne-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-step_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-step-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Propellers work well until the flight speed of the aircraft is high enough that the airflow past the blade tips reaches the speed of sound. Beyond that speed the proportion of the power that drives the propeller that is converted to propeller thrust falls dramatically. For this reason turboprop engines are not used on aircraft<sup id="cite_ref-glennTur_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-glennTur-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-glennThr_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-glennThr-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-lyle_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lyle-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> that fly faster than 0.6\xe2\x80\x930.7 <a href="/wiki/Mach_number" title="Mach number">Mach</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-srm_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-srm-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> However, <a href="/wiki/Propfan" title="Propfan">propfan</a> engines, which are very similar to turboprop engines, can cruise at flight speeds approaching 0.75 Mach. To maintain propeller efficiency across a wide range of airspeeds, turboprops use constant-speed (variable-pitch) propellers. The blades of a constant-speed propeller increase pitch as aircraft speed increases. Another benefit of this type of propeller is that it can also be used to generate reverse thrust to reduce stopping distance on the runway. Additionally, in the event of an engine failure, the propeller can be <a href="/wiki/Feathering_(propeller)#Feathering" class="mw-redirect" title="Feathering (propeller)">feathered</a>, thus minimizing the drag of the non-functioning propeller.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>While the power turbine may be integral with the gas generator section, many turboprops today feature a free power turbine on a separate coaxial shaft. This enables the propeller to rotate freely, independent of compressor speed.<sup id="cite_ref-pt6nation_2_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pt6nation_2-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Turboprop&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Alan_Arnold_Griffith" title="Alan Arnold Griffith">Alan Arnold Griffith</a> had published a paper on compressor design in 1926. <a href="/wiki/Turbojet_development_at_the_RAE" title="Turbojet development at the RAE">Subsequent work at the Royal Aircraft Establishment</a> investigated axial compressor-based designs that would drive a propeller. From 1929, <a href="/wiki/Frank_Whittle" title="Frank Whittle">Frank Whittle</a> began work on centrifugal compressor-based designs that would use all the gas power produced by the engine for jet thrust.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The world\'s first turboprop was designed by the <a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">Hungarian</a> <a href="/wiki/Mechanical_engineering" title="Mechanical engineering">mechanical engineer</a> <a href="/wiki/Gy%C3%B6rgy_Jendrassik" title="Gy\xc3\xb6rgy Jendrassik">Gy\xc3\xb6rgy Jendrassik</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Jendrassik published a turboprop idea in 1928, and on 12 March 1929 he patented his invention. In 1938, he built a small-scale (100 Hp; 74.6&#160;kW) experimental gas turbine.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> The larger <a href="/wiki/Jendrassik_Cs-1" title="Jendrassik Cs-1">Jendrassik Cs-1</a>, with a predicted output of 1,000 bhp, was produced and tested at the <a href="/wiki/Ganz_Works" title="Ganz Works">Ganz Works</a> in <a href="/wiki/Budapest" title="Budapest">Budapest</a> between 1937 and 1941. It was of axial-flow design with 15 compressor and 7 turbine stages, annular combustion chamber. First run in 1940, combustion problems limited its output to 400&#160;bhp. In 1941, the engine was abandoned due to war, and the factory was turned over to conventional engine production.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Rolls-Royce_RB50_Trent_Turboprop_On_Test_Rig_At_Hucknall.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/5/5e/Rolls-Royce_RB50_Trent_Turboprop_On_Test_Rig_At_Hucknall.jpg/220px-Rolls-Royce_RB50_Trent_Turboprop_On_Test_Rig_At_Hucknall.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="181" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/5/5e/Rolls-Royce_RB50_Trent_Turboprop_On_Test_Rig_At_Hucknall.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="300" data-file-height="247" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Rolls-Royce_RB50_Trent_Turboprop_On_Test_Rig_At_Hucknall.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A Rolls-Royce RB.50 <i>Trent</i> on a test rig at <a href="/wiki/Hucknall" title="Hucknall">Hucknall</a>, in March 1945</div></div></div>\n<p>The first mention of turboprop engines in the general public press was in the February 1944 issue of the British aviation publication <i><a href="/wiki/Flight_International" title="Flight International">Flight</a></i>, which included a detailed cutaway drawing of what a possible future turboprop engine could look like.  The drawing was very close to what the future Rolls-Royce Trent would look like.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> The first British turboprop engine was the <a href="/wiki/Rolls-Royce_Limited" title="Rolls-Royce Limited">Rolls-Royce</a> <a href="/wiki/Rolls-Royce_Trent_(turboprop)" class="mw-redirect" title="Rolls-Royce Trent (turboprop)">RB.50 Trent</a>, a converted <a href="/wiki/Rolls-Royce_Derwent" title="Rolls-Royce Derwent">Derwent II</a> fitted with reduction gear and a <a href="/wiki/Rotol" class="mw-redirect" title="Rotol">Rotol</a> 7&#160;ft 11&#160;in (2.41&#160;m) five-bladed propeller.  Two Trents were fitted to <a href="/wiki/Gloster_Meteor" title="Gloster Meteor">Gloster Meteor</a> <i>EE227</i> \xe2\x80\x94 the sole "Trent-Meteor" \xe2\x80\x94 which thus became the world\'s first turboprop-powered aircraft, albeit a test-bed not intended for production.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> It first flew on 20 September 1945.  From their experience with the Trent, Rolls-Royce developed the <a href="/wiki/Rolls-Royce_Clyde" title="Rolls-Royce Clyde">Rolls-Royce Clyde</a>, the first turboprop engine to be fully <a href="/wiki/Type_certificate" title="Type certificate">type certificated</a> for military and civil use,<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Rolls-Royce_Dart" title="Rolls-Royce Dart">Dart</a>, which became one of the most reliable turboprop engines ever built. Dart production continued for more than fifty years.  The Dart-powered <a href="/wiki/Vickers_Viscount" title="Vickers Viscount">Vickers Viscount</a> was the first turboprop aircraft of any kind to go into production and sold in large numbers.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> It was also the first four-engined turboprop. Its first flight was on 16 July 1948.  The world\'s first single engined turboprop aircraft was the <a href="/wiki/Armstrong_Siddeley_Mamba" title="Armstrong Siddeley Mamba">Armstrong Siddeley Mamba</a>-powered <a href="/wiki/Boulton_Paul_Balliol" title="Boulton Paul Balliol">Boulton Paul Balliol</a>, which first flew on 24 March 1948.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Kuznetsov_NK-12M_turboprop_on_Tu-95.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Kuznetsov_NK-12M_turboprop_on_Tu-95.jpg/220px-Kuznetsov_NK-12M_turboprop_on_Tu-95.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="150" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Kuznetsov_NK-12M_turboprop_on_Tu-95.jpg/330px-Kuznetsov_NK-12M_turboprop_on_Tu-95.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Kuznetsov_NK-12M_turboprop_on_Tu-95.jpg/440px-Kuznetsov_NK-12M_turboprop_on_Tu-95.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1810" data-file-height="1234" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Kuznetsov_NK-12M_turboprop_on_Tu-95.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Kuznetsov_NK-12" title="Kuznetsov NK-12">Kuznetsov NK-12</a> is still the most powerful turboprop</div></div></div>\n<p>The Soviet Union built on German World War II turboprop preliminary design work by Junkers Motorenwerke, while BMW, <a href="/wiki/Heinkel" title="Heinkel">Heinkel-Hirth</a> and <a href="/wiki/Daimler-Benz" class="mw-redirect" title="Daimler-Benz">Daimler-Benz</a> also worked on projected designs.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> While the Soviet Union had the technology to create the airframe for a jet-powered strategic bomber comparable to Boeing\'s <a href="/wiki/B-52_Stratofortress" class="mw-redirect" title="B-52 Stratofortress">B-52 Stratofortress</a>, they instead produced the <a href="/wiki/Tupolev_Tu-95" title="Tupolev Tu-95">Tupolev Tu-95</a> Bear, powered with four <a href="/wiki/Kuznetsov_NK-12" title="Kuznetsov NK-12">Kuznetsov NK-12</a> turboprops, mated to eight <a href="/wiki/Contra-rotating_propellers" title="Contra-rotating propellers">contra-rotating propellers</a> (two per nacelle) with supersonic tip speeds to achieve maximum cruise speeds in excess of 575&#160;mph, faster than many of the first jet aircraft and comparable to jet cruising speeds for most missions. The Bear would serve as their most successful long-range combat and surveillance aircraft and symbol of Soviet power projection throughout the end of the 20th century.  The USA used turboprop engines with contra-rotating propellers, such as the <a href="/wiki/Allison_T40" title="Allison T40">Allison T40</a>, on some experimental aircraft during the 1950s. The T40-powered <a href="/wiki/Convair_R3Y_Tradewind" title="Convair R3Y Tradewind">Convair R3Y Tradewind</a> flying-boat was operated by the U.S. Navy for a short time.\n</p><p>The first American turboprop engine was the <a href="/wiki/GE_Aviation" title="GE Aviation">General Electric</a> <a href="/wiki/General_Electric_T31" title="General Electric T31">XT31</a>, first used in the experimental <a href="/wiki/Consolidated_Vultee_XP-81" title="Consolidated Vultee XP-81">Consolidated Vultee XP-81</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> The XP-81 first flew in December 1945, the first aircraft to use a combination of turboprop and <a href="/wiki/Turbojet" title="Turbojet">turbojet</a> power.  The technology of Allison\'s earlier T38 design evolved into the <a href="/wiki/Allison_T56" title="Allison T56">Allison T56</a>, used to power the <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_L-188_Electra" title="Lockheed L-188 Electra">Lockheed Electra</a> airliner, its military maritime patrol derivative the <a href="/wiki/P-3_Orion" class="mw-redirect" title="P-3 Orion">P-3 Orion</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/C-130_Hercules" class="mw-redirect" title="C-130 Hercules">C-130 Hercules</a> military transport aircraft.\n</p><p>The first turbine-powered, shaft-driven helicopter was the <a href="/wiki/Kaman_K-225" title="Kaman K-225">Kaman K-225</a>, a development of <a href="/wiki/Charles_Kaman" title="Charles Kaman">Charles Kaman</a>\'s K-125 <a href="/wiki/Synchropter" class="mw-redirect" title="Synchropter">synchropter</a>, which used a <a href="/wiki/Boeing_T50" title="Boeing T50">Boeing T50</a> turboshaft engine to power it on 11 December 1951.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Usage">Usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Turboprop&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Lockheed_C-130_Hercules.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/Lockheed_C-130_Hercules.jpg/220px-Lockheed_C-130_Hercules.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="157" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/Lockheed_C-130_Hercules.jpg/330px-Lockheed_C-130_Hercules.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/Lockheed_C-130_Hercules.jpg/440px-Lockheed_C-130_Hercules.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3000" data-file-height="2142" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Lockheed_C-130_Hercules.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A <a href="/wiki/Military_transport_aircraft" title="Military transport aircraft">military transport aircraft</a>, over 2,500 <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_C-130_Hercules" title="Lockheed C-130 Hercules">Lockheed C-130 Hercules</a> have been built</div></div></div>\n<p>Compared to <a href="/wiki/Turbofan" title="Turbofan">turbofans</a>, turboprops are most efficient at flight speeds below 725&#160;km/h (450&#160;mph; 390 knots) because the jet velocity of the propeller (and exhaust) is relatively low. Modern turboprop airliners operate at nearly the same speed as small <a href="/wiki/Regional_jet" title="Regional jet">regional jet</a> airliners but burn two-thirds of the fuel per passenger.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> However, compared to a <a href="/wiki/Turbojet" title="Turbojet">turbojet</a> (which can fly at high altitude for enhanced speed and <a href="/wiki/Fuel_efficiency" title="Fuel efficiency">fuel efficiency</a>) a propeller aircraft has a lower ceiling.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Svensk_flygambulans97.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/Svensk_flygambulans97.jpg/220px-Svensk_flygambulans97.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/Svensk_flygambulans97.jpg/330px-Svensk_flygambulans97.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/Svensk_flygambulans97.jpg/440px-Svensk_flygambulans97.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="429" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Svensk_flygambulans97.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Beech_King_Air" class="mw-redirect" title="Beech King Air">Beech King Air</a> and <a href="/wiki/Super_King_Air" class="mw-redirect" title="Super King Air">Super King Air</a> are the most-delivered turboprop <a href="/wiki/Business_aircraft" title="Business aircraft">business aircraft</a>, with a combined 7,300 examples as of May 2018<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div>\n<p>Compared to piston engines, their greater <a href="/wiki/Power-to-weight_ratio" title="Power-to-weight ratio">power-to-weight ratio</a> (which allows for shorter takeoffs) and reliability can offset their higher initial cost, maintenance and fuel consumption. As <a href="/wiki/Jet_fuel" title="Jet fuel">jet fuel</a> can be easier to obtain than <a href="/wiki/Avgas" title="Avgas">avgas</a> in remote areas, turboprop-powered aircraft like the <a href="/wiki/Cessna_Caravan" class="mw-redirect" title="Cessna Caravan">Cessna Caravan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Quest_Kodiak" title="Quest Kodiak">Quest Kodiak</a> are used as <a href="/wiki/Bush_airplane" class="mw-redirect" title="Bush airplane">bush airplanes</a>.\n</p><p>Turboprop engines are generally used on small subsonic aircraft, but the <a href="/wiki/Tupolev_Tu-114" title="Tupolev Tu-114">Tupolev Tu-114</a> can reach 470 <a href="/wiki/Knot_(unit)" title="Knot (unit)">kn</a> (870&#160;km/h, 541&#160;mph). Large <a href="/wiki/Military_aviation" title="Military aviation">military aircraft</a>, like the <a href="/wiki/Tupolev_Tu-95" title="Tupolev Tu-95">Tupolev Tu-95</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Civil_aviation" title="Civil aviation">civil aircraft</a>, such as the <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_L-188_Electra" title="Lockheed L-188 Electra">Lockheed L-188 Electra</a>, were also turboprop powered. The <a href="/wiki/Airbus_A400M" class="mw-redirect" title="Airbus A400M">Airbus A400M</a> is powered by four <a href="/wiki/Europrop_TP400" title="Europrop TP400">Europrop TP400</a> engines, which are the second most powerful turboprop engines ever produced, after the 11&#160;MW (15,000&#160;hp) <a href="/wiki/Kuznetsov_NK-12" title="Kuznetsov NK-12">Kuznetsov NK-12</a>.\n</p><p>In 2017, the most widespread turboprop <a href="/wiki/Airliner" title="Airliner">airliners</a> in service were the <a href="/wiki/ATR_42" title="ATR 42">ATR 42</a>/<a href="/wiki/ATR_72" title="ATR 72">72</a> (950 aircraft), <a href="/wiki/Bombardier_Q400" class="mw-redirect" title="Bombardier Q400">Bombardier Q400</a> (506), <a href="/wiki/De_Havilland_Canada_Dash_8" title="De Havilland Canada Dash 8">De Havilland Canada Dash 8</a>-100/200/300 (374), <a href="/wiki/Beechcraft_1900" title="Beechcraft 1900">Beechcraft 1900</a> (328), <a href="/wiki/De_Havilland_Canada_DHC-6_Twin_Otter" title="De Havilland Canada DHC-6 Twin Otter">de Havilland Canada DHC-6 Twin Otter</a> (270), <a href="/wiki/Saab_340" title="Saab 340">Saab 340</a> (225).<sup id="cite_ref-2017census_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2017census-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> Less widespread and older airliners include the <a href="/wiki/BAe_Jetstream_31" class="mw-redirect" title="BAe Jetstream 31">BAe Jetstream 31</a>, <a href="/wiki/Embraer_EMB_120_Brasilia" title="Embraer EMB 120 Brasilia">Embraer EMB 120 Brasilia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fairchild_Swearingen_Metroliner" title="Fairchild Swearingen Metroliner">Fairchild Swearingen Metroliner</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dornier_328" title="Dornier 328">Dornier 328</a>, <a href="/wiki/Saab_2000" title="Saab 2000">Saab 2000</a>, <a href="/wiki/Xian_MA60" title="Xian MA60">Xian MA60</a>, <a href="/wiki/Xian_MA600" title="Xian MA600">MA600</a> and <a href="/wiki/Xian_MA700" title="Xian MA700">MA700</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fokker_27" class="mw-redirect" title="Fokker 27">Fokker 27</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fokker_50" title="Fokker 50">50</a>.\n</p><p>Turboprop <a href="/wiki/Business_aircraft" title="Business aircraft">business aircraft</a> include the <a href="/wiki/Piper_Meridian" class="mw-redirect" title="Piper Meridian">Piper Meridian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Socata_TBM" class="mw-redirect" title="Socata TBM">Socata TBM</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pilatus_PC-12" title="Pilatus PC-12">Pilatus PC-12</a>, <a href="/wiki/Piaggio_P.180_Avanti" title="Piaggio P.180 Avanti">Piaggio P.180 Avanti</a>, <a href="/wiki/Beechcraft_King_Air" title="Beechcraft King Air">Beechcraft King Air</a> and <a href="/wiki/Super_King_Air" class="mw-redirect" title="Super King Air">Super King Air</a>. In April 2017, there were 14,311 business turboprops in the worldwide fleet.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reliability">Reliability</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Turboprop&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Reliability">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Between 2012 and 2016, the <a href="/wiki/Australian_Transport_Safety_Bureau" title="Australian Transport Safety Bureau">ATSB</a> observed 417 events with turboprop aircraft, 83 per year, over 1.4 million flight hours: 2.2 per 10,000 hours.\nThree were "high risk" involving engine malfunction and unplanned landing in single\xe2\x80\x91engine <a href="/wiki/Cessna_208_Caravan" title="Cessna 208 Caravan">Cessna 208 Caravans</a>, four "medium risk" and 96% "low risk".\nTwo occurrences resulted in minor injuries due to engine malfunction and terrain collision in <a href="/wiki/Agricultural_aircraft" title="Agricultural aircraft">agricultural aircraft</a> and five accidents involved aerial work: four in agriculture and one in an <a href="/wiki/Air_ambulance" class="mw-redirect" title="Air ambulance">air ambulance</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-AIN25jun2018C_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AIN25jun2018C-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Current_engines">Current engines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span
