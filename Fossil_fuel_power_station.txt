color:black;">&#160;</span>&#160;Other (1%)</div>\n</div>\n</div></div>\n<p>A <b>fossil fuel power station</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Thermal_power_station" title="Thermal power station">thermal power station</a> which burns a <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil fuel</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a> or <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a>, to produce <a href="/wiki/Electricity" title="Electricity">electricity</a>. Fossil fuel power stations have machinery to convert the <a href="/wiki/Heat_energy" class="mw-redirect" title="Heat energy">heat energy</a> of <a href="/wiki/Combustion" title="Combustion">combustion</a> into <a href="/wiki/Mechanical_energy" title="Mechanical energy">mechanical energy</a>, which then operates an <a href="/wiki/Electrical_generator" class="mw-redirect" title="Electrical generator">electrical generator</a>. The <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/prime_mover" class="extiw" title="wikt:prime mover">prime mover</a> may be a <a href="/wiki/Steam_turbine" title="Steam turbine">steam turbine</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Gas_turbine" title="Gas turbine">gas turbine</a> or, in small plants, a reciprocating <a href="/wiki/Gas_engine" title="Gas engine">gas engine</a>. All plants use the energy extracted from expanding gas, either steam or combustion gases. Although different energy conversion methods exist, all thermal power station conversion methods have efficiency limited by the <a href="/wiki/Carnot_efficiency" class="mw-redirect" title="Carnot efficiency">Carnot efficiency</a> and therefore produce <a href="/wiki/Waste_heat" title="Waste heat">waste heat</a>.\n</p><p>Fossil fuel power stations provide most of the <a href="/wiki/Electrical_energy" title="Electrical energy">electrical energy</a> used in the world. Some fossil-fired power stations are designed for continuous operation as <a href="/wiki/Baseload_power_plant" class="mw-redirect" title="Baseload power plant">baseload power plants</a>, while others are used as <a href="/wiki/Peaker_plant" class="mw-redirect" title="Peaker plant">peaker plants</a>. However, starting from the 2010s, in many countries plants designed for baseload supply are being operated as <a href="/wiki/Dispatchable_generation" title="Dispatchable generation">dispatchable generation</a> to balance increasing generation by <a href="/wiki/Variable_renewable_energy" title="Variable renewable energy">variable renewable energy</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>By-products of fossil fuel power plant operation must be considered in their design and operation. <a href="/wiki/Flue_gas" title="Flue gas">Flue gas</a> from combustion of the fossil fuels contains <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> and water vapor, as well as pollutants such as <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_oxide" title="Nitrogen oxide">nitrogen oxides</a> (NO<sub>x</sub>), <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_oxide" title="Sulfur oxide">sulfur oxides</a> (SO<sub>x</sub>), and, for coal-fired plants, <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(element)" title="Mercury (element)">mercury</a>, traces of other metals, and <a href="/wiki/Fly_ash" title="Fly ash">fly ash</a>. Usually all of the carbon dioxide and some of the other pollution is discharged to the air. Solid waste ash from coal-fired boilers must also be removed. \n</p><p>Fossil fueled power stations are major emitters of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> (CO<sub>2</sub>), a <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a> which is a major contributor to <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" class="mw-redirect" title="Global warming">global warming</a>.\nThe results of a recent study<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> show that the <a href="/wiki/Net_income" title="Net income">net income</a> available to shareholders of large companies could see a significant reduction from the <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas_emissions" title="Greenhouse gas emissions">greenhouse gas emissions</a> liability related to only natural disasters in the United States from a single coal-fired power plant. \nHowever, as of 2015, no such cases have awarded damages in the United States. \nPer unit of electric energy, <a href="/wiki/Brown_coal" class="mw-redirect" title="Brown coal">brown coal</a> emits nearly twice as much CO<sub>2</sub> as natural gas, and black coal emits somewhat less than brown. \nAs of 2019<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" storage">carbon capture and storage</a> of emissions is not economically viable for fossil fuel power stations.<sup id="cite_ref-wef2019_4-0" class="reference"><a class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fossil_fuel_power_station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Basic concepts: heat into mechanical energy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Thermal_power_station" title="Thermal power station">Thermal power station</a></div>\n<p>In a fossil fuel power plant the chemical energy stored in fossil fuels such as <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fuel_oil" title="Fuel oil">fuel oil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a> or <a href="/wiki/Oil_shale" title="Oil shale">oil shale</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Air" class="mw-redirect" title="Air">air</a> is converted successively into <a href="/wiki/Thermal_energy" title="Thermal energy">thermal energy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mechanical_energy" title="Mechanical energy">mechanical energy</a> and, finally, <a href="/wiki/Electrical_energy" title="Electrical energy">electrical energy</a>. Each fossil fuel power plant is a complex, custom-designed system. Multiple generating units may be built at a single site for more efficient use of <a href="/wiki/Land_use" title="Land use">land</a>, <a href="/wiki/Natural_resource" title="Natural resource">natural resources</a> and <a href="/wiki/Labor_(economics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Labor (economics)">labor</a>. Most <a href="/wiki/Thermal_power_station" title="Thermal power station">thermal power stations</a> in the world use fossil fuel, outnumbering <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power" title="Nuclear power">nuclear</a>, <a href="/wiki/Geothermal_power" title="Geothermal power">geothermal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">biomass</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Concentrated_solar_power" title="Concentrated solar power">concentrated solar power</a> plants.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Second_law_of_thermodynamics" title="Second law of thermodynamics">second law of thermodynamics</a> states that any <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamic_cycle" title="Thermodynamic cycle">closed-loop cycle</a> can only convert a fraction of the heat produced during combustion into <a href="/wiki/Mechanical_work" class="mw-redirect" title="Mechanical work">mechanical work</a>. The rest of the heat, called <a href="/wiki/Waste_heat" title="Waste heat">waste heat</a>, must be released into a cooler environment during the return portion of the cycle. The fraction of heat released into a cooler medium must be equal or larger than the ratio of <a href="/wiki/Absolute_temperature" class="mw-redirect" title="Absolute temperature">absolute temperatures</a> of the cooling system (environment) and the heat source (combustion furnace). Raising the furnace temperature improves the efficiency but complicates the design, primarily by the selection of alloys used for construction, making the furnace more expensive. The waste heat cannot be converted into mechanical energy without an even cooler cooling system. However, it may be used in <a href="/wiki/Cogeneration" title="Cogeneration">cogeneration</a> plants to heat buildings, produce hot water, or to heat materials on an industrial scale, such as in some <a href="/wiki/Oil_refinery" title="Oil refinery">oil refineries</a>, plants, and <a href="/wiki/Chemical_synthesis" title="Chemical synthesis">chemical synthesis</a> plants.\n</p><p>Typical thermal efficiency for utility-scale electrical generators is around 37% for coal and oil-fired plants,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> and 56 \xe2\x80\x93 60% (LEV) for <a href="/wiki/Combined-cycle" class="mw-redirect" title="Combined-cycle">combined-cycle</a> gas-fired plants. Plants designed to achieve peak efficiency while operating at capacity will be less efficient when operating off-design (i.e. temperatures too low.)<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Practical fossil fuels stations operating as heat engines cannot exceed the <a href="/wiki/Carnot_cycle" title="Carnot cycle">Carnot cycle</a> limit for conversion of heat energy into useful work. <a href="/wiki/Fuel_cell" title="Fuel cell">Fuel cells</a> do not have the same thermodynamic limits as they are not heat engines.\n</p><p>The efficiency of a fossil fuel plant may be expressed as its <a href="/wiki/Heat_rate_(efficiency)" title="Heat rate (efficiency)">heat rate</a>, expressed in BTU/kilowatthour or megajoules/kilowatthour.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plant_types">Plant types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fossil_fuel_power_station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Plant types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Thermal_power_station" title="Thermal power station">Thermal power station</a></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Steam">Steam</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fossil_fuel_power_station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Steam">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In a steam turbine power plant, fuel is burned in a furnace and the hot gasses flow through a boiler.  Water is converted to steam in the boiler; additional heating stages may be included to superheat the steam. The hot steam is sent through controlling valves to a turbine. As the steam expands and cools, its energy is transferred to the turbine blades which turn a generator.  The spent steam has very low pressure and energy content; this water vapor is fed through a condenser, which removes heat from the steam. The condensed water is then pumped into the boiler to repeat the cycle.\n</p><p>Emissions from the boiler include carbon dioxide, oxides of sulfur, and in the case of coal fly ash from non-combustible substances in the fuel. Waste heat from the condenser is transferred either to the air, or sometimes to a cooling pond, lake or river.\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Gas_turbine_and_combined_gas.2Fsteam"></span><span The turbines are fueled either with natural gas or fuel oil.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reciprocating_engines">Reciprocating engines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fossil_fuel_power_station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Reciprocating engines">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Gas-fired_power_plant" title="Gas-fired power plant">Gas-fired power plant</a></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Diesel_engine" title="Diesel engine">Diesel engine</a> generator sets are often<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. Emergency (standby) power systems may use reciprocating internal combustion engines operated by fuel oil or natural gas. Standby generators may serve as emergency power for a factory or data center, or may also be operated in parallel with the local utility system to reduce peak power demand charge from the utility. Diesel engines can produce strong torque at relatively low rotational speeds, which is generally desirable when driving an <a href="/wiki/Alternator" title="Alternator">alternator</a>, but diesel fuel in long-term storage can be subject to problems resulting from water accumulation and <a href="/wiki/Chemical_decomposition" title="Chemical decomposition">chemical decomposition</a>. Rarely used generator sets may correspondingly be installed as natural gas or LPG to minimize the fuel system maintenance requirements.\n</p><p>Spark-ignition internal combustion engines operating on gasoline (petrol), <a href="/wiki/Propane" title="Propane">propane</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Liquefied_petroleum_gas" title="Liquefied petroleum gas">LPG</a> are commonly used as portable temporary power sources for construction work, emergency power, or recreational uses.\n</p><p>Reciprocating external combustion engines such as the <a href="/wiki/Stirling_engine" title="Stirling engine">Stirling engine</a> can be run on a variety of fossil fuels, as well as renewable fuels or industrial waste heat. Installations of Stirling engines for power production are relatively uncommon.\n</p><p>Historically, the first central stations used reciprocating steam engines to drive generators.  As the size of the electrical load to be served grew, reciprocating units became too large and cumbersome to install economically. The steam turbine rapidly displaced all reciprocating engines in central station service.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fuels">Fuels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span For instance, the operation of a 1000-MWe coal-fired power plant results in a nuclear radiation dose of 490 person-rem/year, compared to 136 person-rem/year, for an equivalent nuclear power plant including uranium mining, reactor operation and waste disposal.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Coal is delivered by highway <a href="/wiki/Truck" title="Truck">truck</a>, <a href="/wiki/Railroad" class="mw-redirect" title="Railroad">rail</a>, <a href="/wiki/Barge" title="Barge">barge</a>, <a href="/wiki/Collier_(ship_type)" class="mw-redirect" title="Collier (ship type)">collier</a> ship or <a href="/wiki/Coal_slurry_pipeline" class="mw-redirect" title="Coal slurry pipeline">coal slurry pipeline</a>. Generating stations adjacent to a mine may receive coal by <a href="/wiki/Conveyor_belt" title="Conveyor belt">conveyor belt</a> or massive <a href="/wiki/Diesel-electric" class="mw-redirect" title="Diesel-electric">diesel-electric</a>-drive <a href="/wiki/Haul_truck" title="Haul truck">trucks</a>.\nCoal is usually prepared for use by crushing the rough coal to pieces less than 2 inches (5&#160;cm) in size.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Natural_gas">Natural gas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fossil_fuel_power_station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Natural gas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Gas-fired_power_plant" title="Gas-fired power plant">Gas-fired power plant</a></div>\n<p>Gas is a very common fuel and has mostly <a href="/wiki/Dash_for_gas" class="mw-redirect" title="Dash for gas">replaced coal</a> in countries where gas was found in the late 20th century or early 21st century, such as the US and UK. Sometimes coal-fired steam plants are refitted to use natural gas to reduce net carbon dioxide emissions. Oil-fuelled plants may be converted to natural gas to lower operating cost.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Oil">Oil</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fossil_fuel_power_station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Oil">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Heavy fuel oil was once a significant source of energy for electric power generation.  After oil price increases of the 1970s, oil was displaced by coal and later natural gas. Distillate oil is still important as the fuel source for diesel engine power plants used especially in isolated communities not interconnected to a grid. Liquid fuels may also be used by gas turbine power plants, especially for peaking or emergency service. Of the three fossil fuel sources, oil has the advantages of easier transportation and handling than solid coal, and easier on-site storage than natural gas.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Combined_heat_and_power">Combined heat and power</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fossil_fuel_power_station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Combined heat and power">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Combined_heat_and_power" class="mw-redirect" title="Combined heat and power">Combined heat and power</a> (CHP), also known as <a href="/wiki/Cogeneration" title="Cogeneration">cogeneration</a>, is the use of a <a href="/wiki/Thermal_power_station" title="Thermal power station">thermal power station</a> to provide both electric power and heat (the latter being used, for example, for <a href="/wiki/District_heating" title="District heating">district heating</a> purposes). This technology is practiced not only for domestic heating (low temperature) but also for industrial process heat, which is often high temperature heat. Calculations show that Combined Heat and Power District Heating (CHPDH) is the cheapest method in reducing (but not eliminating) carbon emissions, if conventional fossil fuels remain to be burned.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Reliable_sources" title="Wikipedia:Reliable sources"><span title="The material near this tag may rely on an unreliable source. (May 2019)">unreliable source?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_impacts">Environmental impacts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fossil_fuel_power_station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Environmental impacts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Mohave_Generating_Station_1.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Mohave_Generating_Station_1.jpg/220px-Mohave_Generating_Station_1.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="162" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Mohave_Generating_Station_1.jpg/330px-Mohave_Generating_Station_1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Mohave_Generating_Station_1.jpg/440px-Mohave_Generating_Station_1.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2003" data-file-height="1474" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Mohave_Generating_Station_1.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Mohave_Power_Station" title="Mohave Power Station">Mohave Power Station</a>, a 1,580 <a href="/wiki/Megawatt" class="mw-redirect" title="Megawatt">MW</a> coal power station near <a href="/wiki/Laughlin,_Nevada" title="Laughlin, Nevada">Laughlin, Nevada</a>, out of service since 2005 due to environmental restrictions<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div>\n<p>Thermal power plants are one of the main artificial sources of producing toxic gases and <a href="/wiki/Particulate_matter" class="mw-redirect" title="Particulate matter">particulate matter</a>. Fossil fuel power plants cause the emission of pollutants such as NOx, SOx, CO2, CO, PM, organic gases and polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons.<sup id="cite_ref-FouladiFard05_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FouladiFard05-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> World organizations and international agencies, like the IEA, are concerned about the <a href="/wiki/Environmental_impact_of_fossil_fuels" class="mw-redirect" title="Environmental impact of fossil fuels">environmental impact of burning fossil fuels</a>, and coal in particular. The combustion of coal contributes the most to <a href="/wiki/Acid_rain" title="Acid rain">acid rain</a> and <a href="/wiki/Air_pollution" title="Air pollution">air pollution</a>, and has been connected with <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" class="mw-redirect" title="Global warming">global warming</a>. Due to the chemical composition of coal there are difficulties in removing impurities from the solid fuel prior to its combustion. Modern day coal power plants pollute less than older designs due to new "<a href="/wiki/Scrubber" title="Scrubber">scrubber</a>" technologies that filter the exhaust air in smoke stacks. However, emission levels of various pollutants are still on average several times greater than natural gas power plants and the scrubbers transfer the captured pollutants to wastewater, which still requires treatment in order to avoid <a href="/wiki/Water_pollution" title="Water pollution">pollution</a> of receiving water bodies. In these modern designs, pollution from coal-fired power plants comes from the emission of gases such as carbon dioxide, <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_oxides" class="mw-redirect" title="Nitrogen oxides">nitrogen oxides</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide" title="Sulfur dioxide">sulfur dioxide</a> into the air, as well a significant volume of wastewater which may contain <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">lead</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(element)" title="Mercury (element)">mercury</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cadmium" title="Cadmium">cadmium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chromium" title="Chromium">chromium</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Arsenic" title="Arsenic">arsenic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Selenium" title="Selenium">selenium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a> compounds (<a href="/wiki/Nitrate" title="Nitrate">nitrates</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nitrite" title="Nitrite">nitrites</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-EPA-steam-electric_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EPA-steam-electric-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Acid rain is caused by the emission of <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_oxides" class="mw-redirect" title="Nitrogen oxides">nitrogen oxides</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide" title="Sulfur dioxide">sulfur dioxide</a>. These gases may be only mildly acidic themselves, yet when they react with the atmosphere, they create acidic compounds such as <a href="/wiki/Sulfurous_acid" title="Sulfurous acid">sulfurous acid</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nitric_acid" title="Nitric acid">nitric acid</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a> which fall as rain, hence the term acid rain. In Europe and the US, stricter emission laws and decline in heavy industries have reduced the environmental hazards associated with this problem, leading to lower emissions after their peak in 1960s.\n</p><p>In 2008, the <a href="/wiki/European_Environment_Agency" title="European Environment Agency">European Environment Agency</a> (EEA) documented fuel-dependent emission factors based on actual emissions from power plants in the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-EEA_AirPollution_14-0" class="reference"><a dioxide">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">Carbon dioxide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gas-fired_power_plant" title="Gas-fired power plant">gas-fired power plant</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Taichung_Thermal_Power_Plant.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Taichung_Thermal_Power_Plant.JPG/220px-Taichung_Thermal_Power_Plant.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Taichung_Thermal_Power_Plant.JPG/330px-Taichung_Thermal_Power_Plant.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Taichung_Thermal_Power_Plant.JPG/440px-Taichung_Thermal_Power_Plant.JPG 2x" data-file-width="1564" data-file-height="1173" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Taichung_Thermal_Power_Plant.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Taichung_Power_Plant" title="Taichung Power Plant">Taichung coal-fired power plant</a> in <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a>, the world\'s largest carbon dioxide emitter<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div>\n<p>Electricity generation using carbon-based fuels is responsible for a large fraction of carbon dioxide (CO<sub>2</sub>) emissions worldwide and for 34% of U.S. man-made carbon dioxide emissions in 2010. In the U.S. 70% of electricity is generated by combustion of fossil fuels.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Coal contains more carbon than oil or natural gas fossil fuels, resulting in greater volumes of carbon dioxide emissions per unit of electricity generated. In 2010, coal contributed about 81% of CO<sub>2</sub> emissions from generation and contributed about 45% of the electricity generated in the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> In 2000, the carbon intensity (CO<sub>2</sub> emissions) of U.S. coal thermal combustion was 2249&#160;lbs/MWh (1,029&#160;kg/MWh)<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> while the carbon intensity of U.S. oil thermal generation was 1672&#160;lb/MWh (758&#160;kg/MWh or 211&#160;kg/<a href="/wiki/Gigajoule" class="mw-redirect" title="Gigajoule">GJ</a>)<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> and the carbon intensity of U.S. natural gas thermal production was 1135&#160;lb/MWh (515&#160;kg/MWh or 143&#160;kg/GJ).<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (<a href="/wiki/IPCC" class="mw-redirect" title="IPCC">IPCC</a>) reports that increased quantities of the <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a> carbon dioxide within the atmosphere will "very likely" lead to higher average temperatures on a global scale (<a href="/wiki/Global_warming" class="mw-redirect" title="Global warming">global warming</a>). Concerns regarding the potential for such warming to change the global climate prompted IPCC recommendations calling for large cuts to CO<sub>2</sub> emissions worldwide.<sup id="cite_ref-ipcc_summary_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ipcc_summary-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Emissions can be reduced with higher combustion temperatures, yielding more efficient production of electricity within the cycle. As of 2019<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Fossil_fuel_power_station&amp;action=edit">&#91;update&#93;</a></sup> the price of emitting CO<sub>2</sub> to the atmosphere is much lower than the cost of adding <a href="/wiki/Carbon_capture_and_storage" title="Carbon capture and storage">carbon capture and storage</a> (CCS) to fossil fuel power stations, so owners have not done so.<sup id="cite_ref-wef2019_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wef2019-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Estimation_of_carbon_dioxide_emissions">Estimation of carbon dioxide emissions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fossil_fuel_power_station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Estimation of carbon dioxide emissions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>The  CO<sub>2</sub> emissions from a fossil fuel power station can be estimated with the following formula:<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>CO<sub>2</sub> emissions  = <a href="/wiki/Nameplate_capacity" title="Nameplate capacity">capacity</a> x <a href="/wiki/Capacity_factor" title="Capacity factor">capacity factor</a> x <a href="/wiki/Heat_rate_(efficiency)" title="Heat rate (efficiency)">heat rate</a> x <a href="/wiki/Emission_intensity" title="Emission intensity">emission intensity</a> x time\n</p><p>where "capacity" is the "<a href="/wiki/Nameplate_capacity" title="Nameplate capacity">nameplate capacity</a>" or the maximum allowed output of the plant, "<a href="/wiki/Capacity_factor" title="Capacity factor">capacity factor</a>" or "load factor" is a measure of the amount of power that a plant produces compared with the amount it would produce if operated at its rated capacity nonstop, <a href="/wiki/Heat_rate_(efficiency)" title="Heat rate (efficiency)">heat rate</a> is thermal energy in/electrical energy out, emission intensity (also called <a href="/wiki/Emission_factor" class="mw-redirect" title="Emission factor">emission factor</a>) is the CO<sub>2</sub> emitted per unit of heat generated for a particular fuel.\n</p><p>As an example, a new 1500 MW supercritical lignite-fueled power station running on average at half its capacity might have annual CO<sub>2</sub> emissions estimated as:\n</p><p>= 1500MW x 0.5 x 100/40 x 101000&#160;kg/TJ x 1year\n</p><p>= 1500MJ/s x 0.5 x 2.5 x 0.101&#160;kg/MJ x 365x24x60x60s\n</p><p>= 1.5x10<sup>3</sup> x 5x10<sup>\xe2\x88\x921</sup> x 2.5 x 1.01<sup>\xe2\x88\x921</sup> x 3.1536x10<sup>7</sup> kg\n</p><p>= 59.7 x10<sup>3-1-1+7</sup> kg\n</p><p>= 5.97 Mt\n</p><p>Thus the example power station is estimated to emit about 6 megatonnes of carbon dioxide each year.\nThe results of similar estimations are mapped by organisations such as <a href="/wiki/Global_Energy_Monitor" title="Global Energy Monitor">Global Energy Monitor</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carbon_Tracker" title="Carbon Tracker">Carbon Tracker</a> and ElectricityMap.\n</p><p>Alternatively it may be possible to measure CO2 emissions (perhaps indirectly via another gas) from satellite observations.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Particulate_matter">Particulate matter</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fossil_fuel_power_station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Particulate matter">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Another problem related to coal combustion is the emission of <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_particulate_matter" class="mw-redirect" title="Atmospheric particulate matter">particulates</a> that have a serious impact on public health. Power plants remove particulate from the flue gas with the use of a <a href="/wiki/Dust_collector#Fabric_filters" title="Dust collector">bag house</a> or <a href="/wiki/Electrostatic_precipitator" title="Electrostatic precipitator">electrostatic precipitator</a>. Several newer plants that burn coal use a different process, <a href="/wiki/Combined_cycle#Integrated_Gasification_Combined_Cycle_(IGCC)" class="mw-redirect" title="Combined cycle">Integrated Gasification Combined Cycle</a> in which <a href="/wiki/Synthesis_gas" class="mw-redirect" title="Synthesis gas">synthesis gas</a> is made out of a reaction between coal and water. The synthesis gas is processed to remove most pollutants and then used initially to power gas turbines. Then the hot exhaust gases from the gas turbines are used to generate steam to power a steam turbine. The pollution levels of such plants are drastically lower than those of "classic" coal power plants.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Particulate matter from coal-fired plants can be harmful and have negative health impacts. Studies have shown that exposure to particulate matter is related to an increase of respiratory and cardiac mortality.<sup id="cite_ref-Nel,_A._2005_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nel,_A._2005-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> Particulate matter can irritate small airways in the lungs, which can lead to increased problems with asthma, chronic bronchitis, airway obstruction, and gas exchange.<sup id="cite_ref-Nel,_A._2005_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nel,_A._2005-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>There are different types of particulate matter, depending on the chemical composition and size. The dominant form of particulate matter from coal-fired plants is <a href="/wiki/Fly_ash" title="Fly ash">coal fly ash</a>, but secondary sulfate and nitrate also comprise a major portion of the particulate matter from coal-fired plants.<sup id="cite_ref-Grahame,_T._2007_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Grahame,_T._2007-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> Coal fly ash is what remains after the coal has been combusted, so it consists of the incombustible materials that are found in the coal.<sup id="cite_ref-Schobert,_H._H._2002_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Schobert,_H._H._2002-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The size and chemical composition of these particles affects the impacts on human health.<sup id="cite_ref-Nel,_A._2005_25-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nel,_A._2005-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Grahame,_T._2007_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Grahame,_T._2007-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> Currently coarse (diameter greater than 2.5 \xce\xbcm) and fine (diameter between 0.1 \xce\xbcm and 2.5 \xce\xbcm) particles are regulated, but ultrafine particles (diameter less than 0.1 \xce\xbcm) are currently unregulated, yet they pose many dangers.<sup id="cite_ref-Nel,_A._2005_25-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nel,_A._2005-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> Unfortunately much is still unknown as to which kinds of particulate matter pose the most harm, which makes it difficult to come up with adequate legislation for regulating particulate matter.<sup id="cite_ref-Grahame,_T._2007_26-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Grahame,_T._2007-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>There are several methods of helping to reduce the particulate matter emissions from coal-fired plants. Roughly 80% of the ash falls into an ash hopper, but the rest of the ash then gets carried into the atmosphere to become coal-fly ash.<sup id="cite_ref-Schobert,_H._H._2002_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Schobert,_H._H._2002-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> Methods of reducing these emissions of particulate matter include:\n</p>\n<ol><li>a <a href="/wiki/Baghouse#Fabric_filters" title="Baghouse">baghouse</a></li>\n<li>an <a href="/wiki/Electrostatic_precipitator" title="Electrostatic precipitator">electrostatic precipitator</a> (ESP)</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Combined_cycle#Integrated_Gasification_Combined_Cycle_(IGCC)" class="mw-redirect" title="Combined cycle">cyclone collector</a></li></ol>\n<p>The baghouse has a fine filter that collects the ash particles, electrostatic precipitators use an electric field to trap ash particles on high-voltage plates, and cyclone collectors use centrifugal force to trap particles to the walls.<sup id="cite_ref-Schobert,_H._H._2002_27-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Schobert,_H._H._2002-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> A recent study indicates that sulfur emissions from fossil fueled power stations in China may have caused a 10-year lull in global warming (1998-2008).<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wastewater">Wastewater</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fossil_fuel_power_station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Wastewater">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:292px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Coal_power_plant_wastestreams_-_EPA.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Coal_power_plant_wastestreams_-_EPA.png/290px-Coal_power_plant_wastestreams_-_EPA.png" decoding="async" width="290" height="225" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Coal_power_plant_wastestreams_-_EPA.png/435px-Coal_power_plant_wastestreams_-_EPA.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Coal_power_plant_wastestreams_-_EPA.png/580px-Coal_power_plant_wastestreams_-_EPA.png 2x" data-file-width="2025" data-file-height="1571" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Coal_power_plant_wastestreams_-_EPA.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Wastestreams at a coal-fired power plant</div></div></div>\n<p>Fossil-fuel power stations, particularly coal-fired plants, are a major source of industrial wastewater. Wastewater streams include flue-gas desulfurization, fly ash, bottom ash and flue gas mercury control. Plants with air pollution controls such as wet scrubbers typically transfer the captured pollutants to the wastewater stream.<sup id="cite_ref-EPA-steam-electric_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EPA-steam-electric-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Ash_pond" title="Ash pond">Ash ponds</a>, a type of surface impoundment, are a widely used treatment technology at coal-fired plants. These ponds use gravity to settle out large particulates (measured as <a href="/wiki/Total_suspended_solids" title="Total suspended solids">total suspended solids</a>) from power plant wastewater. This technology does not treat dissolved pollutants. Power stations use additional technologies to control pollutants, depending on the particular wastestream in the plant. These include dry ash handling, closed-loop ash recycling, chemical precipitation, biological treatment (such as an activated sludge process), membrane systems, and evaporation-crystallization systems. In 2015 EPA published a regulation pursuant to the <a href="/wiki/Clean_Water_Act" title="Clean Water Act">Clean Water Act</a> that requires US power plants to use one or more of these technologies.<sup id="cite_ref-EPA-steam-electric_13-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EPA-steam-electric-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Technological advancements in ion exchange membranes and electrodialysis systems has enabled high efficiency treatment of flue-gas desulfurization wastewater to meet the updated EPA discharge limits.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Radioactive_trace_elements">Radioactive trace elements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fossil_fuel_power_station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Radioactive trace elements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Coal is a sedimentary rock formed primarily from accumulated plant matter, and it includes many inorganic minerals and elements which were deposited along with organic material during its formation. As the rest of the Earth\'s <a href="/wiki/Crust_(geology)" title="Crust (geology)">crust</a>, coal also contains low levels of <a href="/wiki/Uranium" title="Uranium">uranium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thorium" title="Thorium">thorium</a>, and other naturally occurring <a href="/wiki/Radioactive_isotopes" class="mw-redirect" title="Radioactive isotopes">radioactive isotopes</a> whose release into the environment leads to <a href="/wiki/Radioactive_contamination" title="Radioactive contamination">radioactive contamination</a>. While these substances are present as very small trace impurities, enough coal is burned that significant amounts of these substances are released. A 1,000 MW coal-burning power plant could have an uncontrolled release of as much as 5.2 metric tons per year of uranium (containing 74 pounds (34&#160;kg) of <a href="/wiki/Uranium-235" title="Uranium-235">uranium-235</a>) and 12.8 metric tons per year of thorium.<sup id="cite_ref-ORNL_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ORNL-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> In comparison, a 1,000 MW nuclear plant will generate about 30 metric tons of high-level radioactive solid packed waste per year.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> It is estimated that during 1982, US coal burning released 155 times as much uncontrolled radioactivity into the atmosphere as the <a href="/wiki/Three_Mile_Island_incident" class="mw-redirect" title="Three Mile Island incident">Three Mile Island incident</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> The collective radioactivity resulting from all coal burning worldwide between 1937 and 2040 is estimated to be 2,700,000 curies or 0.101 EBq.<sup id="cite_ref-ORNL_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ORNL-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> During normal operation, the effective dose equivalent from coal plants is 100 times that from nuclear plants.<sup id="cite_ref-ORNL_30-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ORNL-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> Normal operation however, is a deceiving baseline for comparison: just the <a href="/wiki/Chernobyl_disaster" title="Chernobyl disaster">Chernobyl nuclear disaster</a> released, in iodine-131 alone, an estimated 1.76 EBq.<sup id="cite_ref-newscientist1_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-newscientist1-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> of radioactivity, a value one order of magnitude above this value for total emissions from all coal burned within a century, while the iodine-131, the major radioactive substance which comes out in accident situations, has a half life of just 8 days.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Water_and_air_contamination_by_coal_ash">Water and air contamination by coal ash</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fossil_fuel_power_station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Water and air contamination by coal ash">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>A study released in August 2010 that examined state pollution data in the United States by the organizations <a href="/wiki/Environmental_Integrity_Project" title="Environmental Integrity Project">Environmental Integrity Project</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Club" title="Sierra Club">Sierra Club</a> and <a href="/wiki/Earthjustice" title="Earthjustice">Earthjustice</a> found that coal ash produced by coal-fired power plants dumped at sites across 21 U.S. states has contaminated ground water with toxic elements. The contaminants including the poisons <a href="/wiki/Arsenic" title="Arsenic">arsenic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">lead</a>. The study concluded that the problem of coal ash-caused water contamination is even more extensive in the United States than has been estimated. The study brought to 137 the number of ground water sites across the United States that are contaminated by power plant-produced coal ash.<sup id="cite_ref-mcclatchydc.com_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mcclatchydc.com-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Arsenic has been shown to cause <a href="/wiki/Skin_cancer" title="Skin cancer">skin cancer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bladder_cancer" title="Bladder cancer">bladder cancer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lung_cancer" title="Lung cancer">lung cancer</a>, and lead damages the <a href="/wiki/Nervous_system" title="Nervous system">nervous system</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-EJ2010_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EJ2010-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> Coal ash contaminants are also linked to respiratory diseases and other health and developmental problems, and have disrupted local aquatic life.<sup id="cite_ref-mcclatchydc.com_34-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mcclatchydc.com-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>  Coal ash also releases a variety of toxic contaminants into nearby air, posing a health threat to those who breathe in fugitive coal dust.<sup id="cite_ref-EJ2010_35-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EJ2010-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Mercury_contamination">Mercury contamination</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fossil_fuel_power_station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Mercury contamination">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>U.S. government scientists tested fish in 291 streams around the country for <a href="/wiki/Mercury_contamination" class="mw-redirect" title="Mercury contamination">mercury contamination</a>. They found <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(element)" title="Mercury (element)">mercury</a> in every fish tested, according to the study by the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Department_of_the_Interior" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Department of the Interior">U.S. Department of the Interior</a>. They found mercury even in fish of isolated rural waterways. Twenty five percent of the fish tested had mercury levels above the safety levels determined by the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Environmental_Protection_Agency" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Environmental Protection Agency">U.S. Environmental Protection Agency</a> (EPA) for people who eat the fish regularly. The largest source of mercury contamination in the United States is coal-fueled power plant emissions.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conversion_of_fossil_fuel_power_plants">Conversion of fossil fuel power plants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fossil_fuel_power_station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Conversion of fossil fuel power plants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Landfill#Reclaiming_materials" title="Landfill">Landfill \xc2\xa7&#160;Reclaiming materials</a></div>\n<p>Several methods exist to reduce pollution and reduce or eliminate carbon emissions of fossil fuel power plants. A frequently used and cost-efficient method is to convert a plant to run on a different fuel. This includes conversions of coal power plants to <a href="/wiki/Energy_crop" title="Energy crop">energy crops</a>/biomass or <a href="/wiki/Waste" title="Waste">waste</a><sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> and conversions of natural gas power plants to biogas or hydrogen.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> Conversions of coal powered power plants to waste-fired power plants have an extra benefit in that they can reduce <a href="/wiki/Landfill" title="Landfill">landfilling</a>. In addition, waste-fired power plants can be equipped with material recovery, which is also beneficial to the environment. In some instances, <a href="/wiki/Torrefaction" title="Torrefaction">torrefaction</a> of biomass may benefit the power plant if energy crops/biomass is the material the converted fossil fuel power plant will be using.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> Also, when using energy crops as the fuel, and if implementing <a href="/wiki/Biochar" title="Biochar">biochar</a> production, the thermal power plant can even become <a href="/wiki/Carbon_negative" class="mw-redirect" title="Carbon negative">carbon negative</a> rather than just carbon neutral. Improving the energy efficiency of a coal-fired power plant can also reduce emissions.\n</p><p>Besides simply converting to run on a different fuel, some companies also offer the possibility to convert existing fossil-fuel power stations to <a href="/wiki/Grid_energy_storage" title="Grid energy storage">grid energy storage</a> systems which use electric <a href="/wiki/Thermal_energy_storage" title="Thermal energy storage">thermal energy storage</a> (ETES)<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Coal_pollution_mitigation">Coal pollution mitigation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fossil_fuel_power_station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Coal pollution mitigation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Coal_pollution_mitigation" title="Coal pollution mitigation">Coal pollution mitigation</a></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Coal_pollution_mitigation" title="Coal pollution mitigation">Coal pollution mitigation</a> is a process whereby coal is chemically washed of <a href="/wiki/Mineral" title="Mineral">minerals</a> and impurities, sometimes <a href="/wiki/Gasification" title="Gasification">gasified</a>, burned and the resulting flue gases treated with steam, with the purpose of removing sulfur dioxide, and reburned so as to make the carbon dioxide in the flue gas economically<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> recoverable, and storable underground (the latter of which is called "carbon capture and storage"). The coal industry uses the term "clean coal" to describe technologies designed to enhance both the efficiency and the environmental acceptability of coal extraction, preparation and use,<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> but has provided no specific quantitative limits on any emissions, particularly carbon dioxide. Whereas contaminants like sulfur or mercury can be removed from coal, carbon cannot be effectively removed while still leaving a usable fuel, and clean coal plants without <a href="/wiki/Carbon_sequestration" title="Carbon sequestration">carbon sequestration</a> and storage do not significantly reduce carbon dioxide emissions. <a href="/wiki/James_Hansen" title="James Hansen">James Hansen</a> in an open letter to then U.S. President <a href="/wiki/Barack_Obama" title="Barack Obama">Barack Obama</a> advocated a "moratorium and phase-out of coal plants that do not capture and store CO<sub>2</sub>". In his book <i><a href="/wiki/Storms_of_My_Grandchildren" title="Storms of My Grandchildren">Storms of My Grandchildren</a></i>, similarly, Hansen discusses his <i>Declaration of Stewardship</i>, the first principle of which requires "a moratorium on coal-fired power plants that do not capture and sequester carbon dioxide".<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Running_the_power_station_on_hydrogen_converted_from_natural_gas">Running the power station on hydrogen converted from natural gas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fossil_fuel_power_station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Running the power station on hydrogen converted from natural gas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Gas-fired power plants can also be modified to run on <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> \nHydrogen can at first be created from natural gas through <a href="/wiki/Steam_reforming" title="Steam reforming">steam reforming</a>, as a step towards a <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_economy" title="Hydrogen economy">hydrogen economy</a>, thus eventually reducing carbon emissions.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Since 2013, the conversion process has been improved by scientists at Karlsruhe Liquid-metal Laboratory (KALLA), using a process called <a href="/wiki/Methane_pyrolysis" class="mw-redirect" title="Methane pyrolysis">methane pyrolysis</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>\nThey succeeded in allowing the soot to be easily removed (soot is a byproduct of the process and damaged the working parts in the past -most notably the nickel-iron-cobaltcatalyst-).<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> The soot (which contains the carbon) can then be stored underground and is not released into the atmosphere.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Phase_out_of_fossil_fuel_power_plants">Phase out of fossil fuel power plants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fossil_fuel_power_station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Phase out of fossil fuel power plants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel_phase-out" title="Fossil fuel phase-out">Fossil fuel phase-out</a></div>\n<p>As of 2019<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Fossil_fuel_power_station&amp;action=edit">&#91;update&#93;</a></sup> there is still a chance of keeping global warming below 1.5\xc2\xb0C if no more fossil fuel power plants are built and some existing fossil fuel power plants are shut down early, together with other measures such as <a href="/wiki/Reforestation" title="Reforestation">reforestation</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-NatGeo2019_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NatGeo2019-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\nAlternatives to fossil fuel power plants include <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power" title="Nuclear power">nuclear power</a>, <a href="/wiki/Solar_power" title="Solar power">solar power</a>, <a href="/wiki/Geothermal_power" title="Geothermal power">geothermal power</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wind_power" title="Wind power">wind power</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hydropower" title="Hydropower">hydropower</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">biomass power plants</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">renewable energies</a> (see <a href="/wiki/Non-carbon_economy" class="mw-redirect" title="Non-carbon economy">non-carbon economy</a>). Most of these are proven technologies on an industrial scale, but others are still in prototype form.\n</p><p>Some countries only include the cost to produce the electrical energy, and do not take into account the <a href="/wiki/Social_cost_of_carbon" title="Social cost of carbon">social cost of carbon</a> or the indirect costs associated with the many pollutants created by burning coal (e.g. increased hospital admissions due to respiratory diseases caused by fine smoke particles).<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Relative_cost_by_generation_source">Relative cost by generation source</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fossil_fuel_power_station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Relative cost by generation source">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Cost_of_electricity_by_source" title="Cost of electricity by source">Cost of electricity by source</a></div>\n<p>When comparing power plant costs, it is customary<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="surely this depends on whether the country is in an emissions trading scheme or not doesn&#39;t it? (May 2019)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> to start by calculating the cost of power at the generator terminals by considering several main factors. External costs such as connections costs, the effect of each plant on the distribution grid are considered separately as an additional cost to the calculated power cost at the terminals.\n</p><p>Initial factors considered are:\n</p>\n<ul><li>Capital costs, including waste disposal and decommissioning costs for nuclear energy.</li>\n<li>Operating and maintenance costs.</li>\n<li>Fuel costs for fossil fuel and biomass sources, and which may be negative for wastes.</li>\n<li>Likely annual hours per year run or load factor, which may be as low as 30% for wind energy, or as high as 90% for nuclear energy.</li>\n<li>Offset sales of heat, for example in combined heat and power district heating (CHP/DH).</li></ul>\n<p>These costs occur over the 30\xe2\x80\x9350 year life<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="obviously some previous fossil fuel plants have lasted 50 years but for plants being built now are they assumed to have at least 30 year lifetime by the accountants? And is gas different to coal? (May 2019)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> of the fossil fuel power plants, using <a href="/wiki/Discounted_cash_flow" title="Discounted cash flow">discounted cash flows</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
